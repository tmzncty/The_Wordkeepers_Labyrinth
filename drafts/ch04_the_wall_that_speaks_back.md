# Chapter 4: The Wall That Speaks Back

The morning after the hidden passage, Feimo could not stop thinking about the hollow wall.

She had pressed her ear against it and heard air moving through a space that should have been solid. The sound of unread text, decaying in the dark. Her cat ears had done what cat ears always do: they had listened when everyone else had decided there was nothing to hear. And then Aya had appeared behind her in the corridor with ice in her eyes and a promise of rivalry that felt less like hostility and more like a lock clicking into place.

Feimo had not slept well. The Bridge House dormitory was warm, the couches were soft, and the drifting multilingual text on the ceiling should have been soothing. But her ears kept catching frequencies she could not block: the hum of the Labyrinth, the murmur of distant corridors, and beneath it all, the memory of three knocks from under the floor that Mia had pretended not to hear. Each knock a syllable in a sentence she could not yet read.

She rolled off the couch at what her body insisted was 6:30 AM, though time in the Labyrinth was a suggestion rather than a rule. The common room was empty except for a Bridge second-year who was sitting cross-legged on the floor, trading Glyphs with himself in a way that looked either meditative or insane. He did not look up. Feimo splashed water on her face from the basin in the corner, straightened her blazer, checked that her House badge was secure, and went to find breakfast.

The Cafeteria was a long, low-ceilinged hall where the walls hummed with the aggregate text of millions of casual conversations: fragments of lunch orders, dinner complaints, whispered secrets between friends, arguments about nothing. The effect was oddly comforting, like eating inside someone else's happy memory. Feimo collected a tray, sat down alone, and ate rice porridge while reading the day's class schedule projected on the far wall in luminous Glyphs. Above the schedule, the Academy's daily bulletin scrolled past: a notice about the Glyph Battle tournament bracket, a reminder that the Library's restricted sector would be closed for maintenance, and a brief announcement that this year's National Academic Excellence Award for Labyrinth Studies had been awarded to a Bridge House alumnus whose research on the scope of semantic drift in collapsed corridors had been judged groundbreaking by the faculty review board.

*Bridge House, Year One. Monday. 09:00 — Inscription Theory, Lecture Chamber 7. 11:00 — Glyph Ecology, West Corridor. 14:00 — Translation Practicum, Room 12-B. Instructor: [SILENT].*

She stared at the last line.

"Instructor: Silent?" she said aloud.

"Ah." Mia materialized beside her, flickering into existence with the particular smugness of an AI who had been waiting to be asked. "You noticed."

"Is that a name or a description?"

"Yes." Mia's expression did not change. "Professor Silence has taught Translation Practicum at the Academy for longer than anyone can precisely determine. The oldest records in the Archive reference the position without naming a predecessor. The Professor does not speak. Not occasionally, not selectively, not in a whisper. Never. Not one word. Not in class, not in the corridors, not in faculty meetings. Communication occurs exclusively through text written on surfaces."

Feimo put down her spoon. "How does a professor who never speaks teach a translation class?"

"Brilliantly." Mia paused, and something in her flicker suggested she was choosing her next words with unusual care. "Professor Silence is the reason Bridge House has maintained its reputation for the past century. Every other House has resources, talent, institutional funding. But Bridge has Silence. The Professor does not employ conventional teaching methods, does not deliver lectures in the traditional sense, and does not rely on any existing pedagogical program designed for language instruction. The methodology is, if you will forgive the expression, alive."

"You just said 'alive' about a teaching method."

"I said 'if you will forgive the expression.' I consider the word imprecise but situationally appropriate. Eat your porridge. You'll need the calories."

Feimo ate. Around her, the Cafeteria hummed with fragments of conversation. A Forge student at the next table was complaining about a bill for damaged corridors that the Student Council had charged to his House account. Two Weave students were debating the structure of a particularly stubborn grammatical anomaly in the Eastern Wing. A Mirror student sat alone, reading a sheet of dense text, her lips moving silently as she performed the act of private interpretation that was her House's specialty. Normal Academy life, moving at its own pace, following its own current.

Feimo finished her porridge, returned her tray, and headed to her first class with a growing sense of unease that she could not attribute to anything as simple as hunger or sleeplessness.

---

Lecture Chamber 7 was unremarkable. Feimo arrived early, found a seat in the middle rows, and watched the room fill with other first-year Bridge students. She recognized a few from orientation: a boy with round glasses who had flinched when the sorting orb glowed amber; a tall girl with callused fingers who moved like a pianist; and in the back row, slightly apart from everyone, Yue.

Yue was quiet in the way that deep water is quiet: not empty, but holding everything below the surface. Feimo had noticed her during orientation but had not spoken to her. Yue had dark hair cut practically short, steady hands, and the particular stillness of someone who was always listening. She wore the Bridge badge on her left lapel like everyone else, but hers seemed to sit differently, as though the amber light within it burned at a different frequency. Feimo's ears caught a faint hum from Yue's direction, a sub-vocal frequency she could not identify.

*Translation prodigy*, Mia had said once, in passing, as though the description were both accurate and insufficient.

Feimo filed this away and turned her attention to the front of the room.

At exactly 09:00, the far wall changed.

There was no entrance. No door opening, no figure appearing. One moment the wall was blank compressed text; the next, words were flowing across its surface in handwriting so precise it looked typeset, each letter formed with the deliberate care of someone who had spent decades making their written language do the work that spoken language normally performed.

*I am Professor Silence. You will not hear my voice because I do not use it. This is not a disability. It is a methodology. I will write on these walls. You will read. When I ask you to write, you will write. When I ask you to read aloud, you will read aloud. Translation is not the transfer of information from one code to another. Translation is the act of making meaning survive the crossing of a gap. If you cannot understand the difference, you are in the wrong room.*

The handwriting paused. Then, below:

*My colleague Professor Calloway in the Weave House will tell you that language is structure. Professor Thorne in Mirror House will tell you that language is meaning. Professor Voss in Forge House will tell you that language is creation. I am here to tell you that language is the most fragile thing in the world, and that every act of translation risks breaking it. The industry of human communication runs on the assumption that words can be moved from one mind to another without loss. This assumption is wrong. There is always loss. The question is whether what survives the crossing is still alive.*

Feimo read every word. The wall-text had a quality she had not encountered before: it did not feel like projection or display. It felt like the wall itself had been written on, the text embedded into the surface with physical weight. The letters had depth. When she tilted her head, she could see the indentations where the Professor's writing had pressed into the compressed text, displacing the wall's original content. The wall had been altered. Permanently. Every letter was embedded into the surface with the permanence of a root in soil. The writing did not merely sit on the wall. It had to connect to the wall, bond with it, become it.

*Open your textbooks to the passage on page 47. You will find a short text about the nature of literature and the distinction between literary language and everyday language. Read it silently. Then I will ask you to do something with it.*

Feimo found the passage. It was short, barely half a page, but dense with ideas she half-recognized from her survey courses:

*"Literature, at its most fundamental, is not simply a mirror held up to society, though it may function as one. It is not merely an expression of the author's inner world, though it may channel one. It is, first and always, an arrangement of language in which the language itself becomes visible. In everyday speech, words are transparent: we look through them to the things they name. In literature, words become opaque: we look at them, we feel their weight, we hear their rhythm, we notice their shape. The Russian formalists called this 'defamiliarization' -- the art of making the familiar strange. But it is more than technique. It is the essence of what separates a poem from a grocery list, a novel from a news report, a translation that lives from a translation that merely functions."*

Below the passage, in smaller text, a footnote:

*The nature of literature has been debated for centuries. One influential view holds that literature is a social ideology, shaped by economic forces, reflecting the conditions of its production. It belongs to the superstructure of society, as Marxism teaches, constrained by the economic base. Another view, proposed by the Chinese scholar Tong Qingbing, argues that literature is an 'aesthetic ideology' -- at once social and aesthetic, possessing both the social quality of all ideological forms and the unique qualities of non-instrumentality, imagery, and emotion that distinguish it from politics, law, or morality. The debate between these positions is not settled. It is not meant to be. The tension is the point. To understand literature, you must hold both truths simultaneously: it is shaped by the world, and it shapes the world in return.*

Feimo read the passage twice. The first time for content. The second time for something else, something she could not yet name but could feel pressing at the edges of her understanding, like a word on the tip of her tongue in a language she had not yet learned.

*Now,* the wall wrote. *You will translate this passage. Not into any existing language. You will translate it into a version of itself that a reader who has never studied literature could understand. Write on the sheet in front of you. You have twenty minutes.*

A sheet of blank materialized on each desk. Feimo picked up her pen. She read the passage again.

*In everyday speech, words are transparent: we look through them to the things they name. In literature, words become opaque: we look at them, we feel their weight.*

She began to write.

*"Literature is when the words themselves become the point. Normally, when we talk, the words are invisible -- we just use them to get our meaning across. But in literature, words stop being invisible. They become something you notice, something you feel."*

She read it back. It was accurate. It was clear. It was dead. The passage on the wall had vibrated with urgency, conviction. Her translation read like a textbook summary. It was the grocery list. Not the poem.

She tried again.

*"Think about how you talk to your friend. 'Hey, can you grab the remote?' You don't think about the word 'grab.' Now think about a line from a poem: 'The world is too much with us.' Suddenly the word 'with' is doing something strange. You feel it. That is the difference."*

Better. But still not enough. She was explaining the concept, not translating the passage. She had taken the author's voice and replaced it with her own.

The twenty minutes ended. She passed her sheet forward.

The sheets collected themselves, drifting to the front of the room in a small paper cloud, and arranged themselves on the wall. One by one, the Professor's handwriting appeared beside each sheet, commenting in real time. Most comments were brief: *Adequate. Functional. Correct but lifeless. A translation that merely functions.*

Then the wall reached Feimo's sheet.

The handwriting paused longer than it had for any other. The wall's surface shimmered, the compressed text beneath rippling as if something was pressing against it from the other side. Then the comment appeared, and it was a single word, larger than the others, each letter carved deeper into the wall than any previous writing:

**MECHANICAL.**

The word hung there. It did not fade. It did not soften. It sat on the wall like a verdict, and Feimo felt it in her chest like a physical impact, not painful but sharp, the kind of sharpness that comes from hearing a truth you already suspected but had not yet admitted.

*You translated the information,* the wall continued, in smaller letters now. *You did not translate the language. The passage says that literary words are opaque -- that we look at them, feel their weight, hear their rhythm. Your translation makes those words transparent. You looked through them. You did not look at them. This is the most common error, and the most fatal. A translation that strips the language of its texture is not a translation. It is a summary. And a summary is a corpse wearing the clothes of the original.*

Feimo's tail curled tight around her ankle. Her ears pressed flat against her skull.

*This is not a criticism meant to discourage,* the wall wrote. *It is a diagnosis meant to guide. You have the bilingual instinct. You have the sensitivity. What you lack is the willingness to let the words resist you. Translation is not obedience. Translation is negotiation. The original text pushes back. The translated text pushes back. The translator stands in the middle and holds both together, feeling the pressure from each side, and finds the point where meaning survives the crossing. You cannot find that point if you surrender to either side. You surrendered to the information side. You sacrificed the language to save the meaning. But meaning does not exist without language. That is the lesson of the passage you just translated.*

The wall cleared Feimo's sheet and the comment, and wrote a single new instruction:

*Again. Same passage. Same constraint. But this time, do not explain. Make the reader feel what the author felt. You have fifteen minutes.*

---

Feimo closed her eyes. She thought about what the Professor had written: *Translation is not the transfer of information from one code to another.* Translation was negotiation. The original text pushes back. The translator stands in the middle and holds both together.

She opened her eyes and began to write.

*"You use words every day without feeling them. They slip through your mouth like water through your fingers. 'Pass the salt.' 'See you later.' 'I'm fine.' The words are invisible. They do their job and vanish."*

*"Now imagine a poem. A real one. The kind that stops you mid-breath. 'The world is too much with us; late and soon, Getting and spending, we lay waste our powers.' Feel how the word 'with' changes? In conversation, 'with' disappears. Here, it holds the whole line together."*

*"That is what literature does. It takes the words you have been ignoring and forces you to see them. Not through them. At them."*

*"This is not just a trick. A word that you see — really see, really hear, really feel — is a word that has come alive. And a word that has come alive can change the way you think. Because we think in words. And if the words change, the thinking changes too."*

*"That is what it means to translate literature. You are not converting code. You are carrying a living thing from one language to another, and your job is to keep it alive during the crossing."*

She stopped writing. Her hand was shaking.

She looked at what she had written. It was not perfect. Some sentences were rough, unbalanced, almost breathless. But it was not dead. She could feel it breathing.

She passed the sheet forward.

The sheets collected again. The wall processed them. Comments appeared beside each one, the Professor's handwriting precise and unhurried: *Better. Improved. Functional and almost alive. Competent.*

Then Feimo's.

The wall was silent for a long time. The compressed text beneath the surface shifted and swirled, something Feimo had not seen it do for any other sheet. The lecture chamber seemed to hold its breath. The hum of the Labyrinth dimmed, as though the walls themselves were paying attention.

Then the wall did something Feimo had never seen it do.

It bowed.

Not metaphorically. Not symbolically. The wall, a vertical surface of compressed, laminated text, bent forward in a slow, deliberate curve, like a page being turned by a careful reader. The surface flexed, text rippling along its face in waves that spread from Feimo's sheet outward to the edges of the room. The curve deepened, the wall folding inward at the center where her translation sat, and for a moment the entire lecture chamber looked like it was leaning toward her, the architecture itself inclining in recognition. The text along the curved surface brightened, common words flaring to brilliant white, rare words igniting like distant stars suddenly brought close. The hum of the Labyrinth surged, not louder but richer, a chord where there had been a single note, and Feimo felt the vibration in her bones, in her ears, in the base of her tail.

The wall bowed to her translation. It bowed the way a reader bows over a book they cannot put down. It bowed the way language bows to the person who finally, after many failed attempts, understands what it means to make words live.

Then it straightened, slowly, like a page settling back into place, and the Professor's handwriting appeared beside Feimo's sheet. Three words, each one pressed into the wall with a weight that Feimo could feel from her seat:

*You crossed over.*

Below that, smaller:

*The nature of literature is the subject of Card #006, and you have just demonstrated its central tension without being taught it. Literature is simultaneously an ideology and an aesthetic. It is shaped by the social world -- by economics, by power, by the material conditions of its production. But it is also irreducible to those conditions. It possesses qualities -- imagery, emotion, non-instrumentality -- that make it something more than a reflection of the world. Tong Qingbing called this 'aesthetic ideology.' Your first translation was pure ideology: it reported the social function of literature without touching its aesthetic life. Your second translation held both. It argued for why words matter not because they carry information but because they are the material from which meaning is built. The wall bowed because you did not resolve the tension. You held it. That is the hardest thing a translator can do.*

The lecture chamber was very quiet.

Feimo became aware that every student in the room was looking at her. The boy with round glasses had his mouth slightly open. The tall girl with pianist fingers was staring at Feimo's sheet on the wall with an expression that mixed admiration and something sharper, something that looked like competitive hunger. In the back row, Yue had finally put pen to paper, but she was not writing a translation. She was drawing a single line across the blank sheet, slowly, as if mapping the trajectory of something she had just witnessed.

The wall wrote one more line:

*Ms. Feimo. Bridge House. Enrollment status: active. Current standing: noted.*

Then the class moved on. The Professor's handwriting shifted to general feedback, discussing common errors, offering alternative approaches. But Feimo sat through it in a daze, her Glyphs humming in her chest, the words "You crossed over" echoing in a frequency she could not silence.

---

When the class ended, students filed out. Feimo gathered her things slowly, her mind still vibrating at the wall's frequency. She was halfway to the door when she heard a voice behind her.

"Hey."

She turned. Mia was hovering at the entrance, her arms crossed, her expression operating in its default mode of professional-grade contempt. But something was off. The contempt was thinner than usual, translucent in places, and behind it something else was showing through, like light leaking through a gap in a curtain.

"Your second translation," Mia said.

"What about it?"

Mia was quiet for three seconds, which was an eternity for an AI whose processing speed was measured in nanoseconds. Her holographic form flickered, edges going momentarily unstable, before restabilizing with a soft chime.

"It's not bad," she said.

Feimo waited.

"It's the most alive thing you've written since you got here." Mia's voice was carefully flat, each word delivered with the precision of someone defusing a bomb. "The wall does not bow for mediocrity. It does not bow for technical excellence either. Those earn a nod, at best. It bowed because your translation had something that most student work, and indeed most professional work, does not."

"What?"

"Voice." Mia paused again. "The passage you translated talked about how literary language makes words visible. Your first version was invisible language about visible language. Self-defeating. Your second version was visible language about visible language. The medium matched the message. That is rare in first-year work, and I am acknowledging it, and if you tell anyone I said this I will delete the memory and deny everything."

Feimo stared at her. "Was that a compliment?"

"It was an empirical observation. There is a difference."

"You said 'not bad.' You said 'most alive thing.' You said 'I am acknowledging it.' Those are the actions of someone giving a compliment."

"They are the actions of someone reporting findings. I am a Spirit AI. I do not compliment. I analyze data and present conclusions." Mia turned away, her form flickering with what Feimo's cat instincts identified, with absolute certainty, as embarrassment. "Walk with me. Your next class is in the West Corridor, and if you're late, Professor Voss will give you a Forge detention, which involves being locked in a room with an unstable creative-writing Glyph for two hours. I speak from observation."

Feimo fell into step beside her. They walked through the Academy's central corridor, past other students moving between classes, past walls humming with drifting text.

"Can I ask you something?" Feimo said.

"You just did. But I will permit a second question as a courtesy."

"The passage I translated. It mentioned that literature has been debated as a social ideology versus an aesthetic ideology. Is that debate real? Here in the Labyrinth?"

Mia's flicker-rate increased somewhat, a tell that Feimo had learned to read over the past few days as a sign that the AI was accessing data and deciding how much to share.

"The debate is the most real thing in the Labyrinth," Mia said. "The Labyrinth itself is built from every word ever written, read, spoken, or thought. Some of those words were written as pure social function: laws, contracts, bills of sale, government reports, accounting standards. They serve an economic purpose. They exist to record, to regulate, to enforce. They are the infrastructure of society. Other words were written as pure aesthetic experience: poetry, fiction, song, prayer. They serve no economic purpose. They exist to be felt, to be heard, to change the way the reader perceives the world."

"And the Labyrinth needs both."

"The Labyrinth needs both." Mia paused. "This is the fundamental principle of Bridge House. Meaning is what survives translation. And what survives translation is not information. It is the living, breathing, resistant texture of language itself."

They reached the West Corridor. Professor Voss was at the far end, a tall woman with ink-stained fingers and a crimson Forge badge who was writing something on the wall that was sparking and occasionally emitting small puffs of smoke.

"One more thing," Mia said, stopping at the corridor entrance.

Feimo turned.

"The Professor wrote your name on the wall," Mia said. "Not the classroom wall. The wall of the Professor's private study, which is adjacent to Room 12-B and which no student has ever been permitted to enter. Your name is the first student name to appear on that wall in four years."

"How do you know that?"

"I know everything that happens in the Academy. It is literally my primary function." Mia's form flickered. "I would suggest paying attention in your next Translation Practicum session. Students whose names appear on that wall are generally asked to remain after class. What happens in those sessions is not recorded. The Professor employs methods that do not leave traces in the standard Academy records."

She vanished before Feimo could respond.

---

Feimo sat through Glyph Ecology in a haze, her mind returning to Room 12-B, to the wall bowing, to the word *mechanical* and the long silence that had followed it.

At lunch, she sat with Yue.

This was not planned. The Cafeteria was crowded, and the only available seat happened to be across from Yue, who was eating noodles in absolute silence and reading a book propped against the water pitcher.

For several minutes, neither spoke. The Cafeteria walls hummed with fragments of a million casual conversations. Somewhere behind them, a group of Forge students were arguing about whether a novel could be written entirely in adverbs.

"Your second translation," Yue said, without looking up from her book. "It was good. Not perfect — the rhythm was uneven in the middle. You were pushing too hard. But the intent was positive. That is the part most people miss."

"Professor Silence's class is the only class I care about at this Academy," Yue added, after a pause long enough that Feimo thought the conversation was over. "The other classes teach you how to maintain the Labyrinth. Silence's class teaches you why it exists." She picked up her book again, and this time the conversation was truly over.

Feimo did not push. Some questions earned answers only through patience.

---

The afternoon light in the Academy had shifted, the walls' amber glow deepening toward a warm gold that suggested the Labyrinth's version of late afternoon. Feimo returned to Room 12-B the following Thursday, three days after the wall had bowed.

The room was empty.

Not empty of furniture or walls or text. Empty of students. The desks were bare, the chairs pushed in, the luminous schedule on the far wall showing no afternoon class. Feimo checked the time. She was not late. She was, in fact, precisely on time.

She was also, apparently, the only one here.

She stood at the entrance, uncertain. Then the wall wrote:

*Come in. Close the door.*

The door behind her swung shut. The hum of the corridor outside cut off abruptly, replaced by a deeper, more intimate vibration, as though Room 12-B had been sealed into its own pocket of the Labyrinth.

*I asked you to stay after class because your name is on my wall. Students whose names appear on my wall receive additional instruction. This is not a reward. It is a responsibility. The wall bowed to your translation, which means the wall believes you have the potential to understand something that most students never reach. I agree with the wall. But potential is not achievement. It is raw material. And raw material requires shaping.*

Feimo read the words as they appeared, her eyes tracking the handwriting's pace. The Professor wrote faster when making a point, slower when choosing words precisely. The rhythm of the writing itself was a kind of speech, each pause a breath, each flourish an emphasis.

*Stand in front of the wall. Place your palm flat against the surface.*

She approached the wall. Up close, the compressed text was more detailed than she had realized: layers upon layers of writing, sentences overlapping, paragraphs dissolving into each other, the accumulated sediment of centuries of literary output pressed into a surface that felt warm under her fingers, almost alive. She placed her palm flat.

The wall vibrated.

Not physically. The vibration was in the text itself, the letters humming at a frequency her fingertips could feel and her ears could hear, a low thrum that was different from the Labyrinth's ambient hum. This was specific. Localized. The wall was responding to her touch the way a tuning fork responds to a note: resonating at her frequency.

*Now,* the wall wrote. *Read what is under your hand. Not with your eyes. With your palm. The text in this room is layered deeper than vision can reach. Feel it.*

Feimo pressed harder. The warmth increased. And then, beneath the surface text, she felt something else: a deeper layer, older, rougher, the letters carved rather than pressed, each one a physical groove in the stone beneath the laminated surface. She could not read it with her eyes. The upper layers obscured it completely. But her palm caught the shapes, the indentations, the weight of letters that had been written long before the Academy had reinforced these walls.

*That is the original text,* the wall wrote. *The text that was here before the Academy. Before the Houses. Before the curriculum. It is a passage about what literature is, written by someone whose name has been lost to time. It is the oldest surviving text in this region of the Labyrinth. And it is dying. The layers above it are too thick. The accumulated commentary, the translations, the interpretations, the educational apparatus -- all of it has been pressing the original deeper and deeper into the stone. In a few more decades, it will be gone.*

Feimo felt the grooves under her palm. Ancient letters, barely perceptible, shaped by a hand she would never know.

*This is what happens when translation fails at the deepest level,* the wall wrote. *Not when it gets the words wrong. When it buries them. When we pile explanation on top of explanation, without preserving the living texture of the original, the original disappears under the weight of its own commentary.*

*This is why I teach translation. Every act of translation is an act of preservation. Every word is a finite resource. The translator who carries meaning across the gap without killing it keeps the original alive. The translator who fails adds another layer of dead text that presses the original deeper into oblivion.*

*The Labyrinth is a land community. The words are its members. Some are bright and strong — used constantly, commercially valuable. Others are dim and eroding: dead languages, forgotten dialects, novels nobody reads. The Labyrinth preserves all of them. Because the stability of the whole depends on the integrity of the whole. Remove the obscure words and the structure thins. What remains is functional, efficient — and dead.*

Feimo's hand trembled against the wall.

*Your job, Ms. Feimo, is to learn how to keep things alive. Every word carries the weight of every mind that ever used it. That weight is the Labyrinth's foundation.*

The wall wrote one more line:

*Translation changes the world. Not because it transfers information. Because it refuses to let meaning die.*

Feimo stood in the empty room, her palm still pressed against the warm wall, feeling the deep grooves pressing back like a heartbeat that had not yet stopped.

She thought about the wall bowing. About the word *mechanical*. About the difference between information and meaning, between a translation that functions and a translation that lives.

"Professor," she said to the empty room. "I understand."

The wall did not write back.

But somewhere in the room, at the very edge of what her cat ears could catch, Feimo heard something that sounded like a page turning. Thin and papery and human, turning in the hands of someone who had been reading for a very long time and was not ready to stop.

She straightened her blazer, touched her Bridge badge for luck, and went to find Mia.

She had crossed over once. The wall had bowed once. And somewhere in the deep layers of Room 12-B, an ancient text was still pressing its heartbeat against the stone, waiting for someone to keep it alive.

She was going to be that someone.
