# Chapter 35: The Academy Defensive

The alarm came at 4:17 in the morning.

Feimo was already awake. She had been lying in the dark for eleven minutes, staring at the ceiling, replaying the argument she had overheard between Cain and Professor Silence two floors below. She could not make out the words. She did not need to. The cadence was enough: Cain's voice rising in controlled increments, each sentence a scalpel, and the Professor's responses arriving after deliberate pauses, each one a wall. The argument had ended with a door closing. Not a slam. A click. The kind of sound that means the conversation is over because one party has decided it is over, and the other has not yet realized it.

Then the alarm.

Feimo sat up. The sound was not the Academy's standard fire drill tone, which was a flat two-note oscillation designed to be heard and ignored in equal measure. This was different: a sustained high-frequency pulse, the kind of noise that bypassed the conscious mind and went straight to the adrenal cortex. She had heard it once before, three weeks ago, when a Fragmenter had breached the sub-basement's eastern corridor. The alarm meant something was inside.

She dressed in the dark: trousers, boots, the jacket with the reinforced collar that Noah had given her last month, saying nothing about where she had gotten it or why it smelled of copper and ozone. The jacket fit like a second skin. Feimo had learned not to question gifts from her sister. Noah's generosity operated on a logic that made sense only in retrospect.

The corridor outside her room was empty. The emergency lighting had activated, casting everything in a flat amber that stripped surfaces of depth and shadow. She moved toward the stairwell, counting doors: three, five, seven. Mia's room was the seventh. The door was open.

Mia was already in the corridor, leaning against the wall with her arms crossed and her tail wrapped once around her left thigh, the posture she adopted when she was processing information faster than her body could express it. She was wearing what she had clearly slept in: a dark shirt, loose trousers, no shoes. Her hair was down. Feimo had never seen her hair down outside of a combat situation.

"Sub-basement," Mia said, without preamble. "Level four. The alarm is keyed to the motion sensors Cain installed last week. Something triggered three of them simultaneously."

"Fragmenter?"

"Multiple. The sensor pattern is wrong for a single entity. It's a cluster."

Feimo processed this. A single Fragmenter was dangerous. A cluster meant coordination. Fragmenters did not coordinate. They were attracted to textual residue the way moths were attracted to light: individually, chaotically, without pattern. If they were moving in formation, something had changed.

"Where is everyone?" Feimo asked.

"Noah went down eight minutes ago. I heard her window open. Aya is in the east stairwell, moving toward the same target. Yue is..." Mia paused. "I don't know where Yue is."

That was unusual. Mia always knew where Yue was. Not because she tracked her, but because Yue left traces in the Academy's ambient text field, faint resonances that Mia's sensitivity could detect the way a seismograph detects tremors too small for human perception. If Yue was invisible to Mia's sense, she was either very far away or deliberately suppressing her presence.

"Silence?" Feimo asked.

"In the War Room. I saw the light under the door."

The War Room. Cain's surveillance center, the windowless box on the third floor with its eleven monitors and its four security personnel and its permanent smell of fresh paint and electronics. The Professor should not have been there at four in the morning. The War Room was Cain's territory. The Professor's territory was the seminar room, the library, the quiet spaces where knowledge was made rather than monitored.

"She's watching," Mia said. "She's been watching since the alarm started. She's not intervening."

"Why?"

Mia looked at her. In the amber light, her eyes were the color of old copper, and the expression in them was the one that Feimo had come to recognize as Mia's analytical mode collapsing into something less controlled: not fear, exactly, but the awareness that the variables in an equation had exceeded the model's capacity.

"Because she wants to see what you'll do," Mia said.

---

They descended.

The stairwell was a narrow concrete shaft that spiraled downward through the Academy's bones, each landing marked by a door and a number painted in stencil: 3, 2, 1, B1, B2. Below B2, the numbers stopped. The Deep Labyrinth began where the Academy's architecture gave up its pretense of order and yielded to something older: stone walls that predated the building by centuries, corridors that followed geometries no architect had drawn, and silence so complete it registered as pressure against the eardrums.

Feimo counted her steps. One hundred and twelve from B2 to the threshold of level three, where the emergency lighting ended and the Labyrinth's own illumination began: a faint phosphorescence that clung to the walls like the memory of light rather than light itself. The luminescence came from the text embedded in the stone, characters in half a dozen scripts that glowed with the residual energy of centuries of reading. The Labyrinth's walls remembered what they had been read, and the remembering was visible.

"Level four," Mia said. She had moved ahead, her bare feet silent on the stone, her tail unwound now and hanging straight, the posture of alertness. "The sensors are clustered around the Deep Archive entrance."

The Deep Archive. Feimo had been there once, with Mia, during the unauthorized breach that Cain had cited in his War Room presentation. The Archive was a sealed section of the Labyrinth's lower levels, accessible through a door that should not have existed: a door with Chinese characters carved into its frame, characters that Feimo had read and that had opened at her reading. She had not told Cain about the characters. She had not told anyone except Mia, and Mia had not told anyone because Mia's loyalty, once granted, was not a social contract but a physical law.

They reached the entrance. The door was open.

Not the way it had been open during their first visit, when Feimo had spoken the inscription and the door had responded with the slow, grinding reluctance of a mechanism that had not been activated in decades. This door had been forced. The stone frame was cracked on the right side, a jagged fracture that ran from floor to lintel, and the characters carved into the wood were damaged: three of them sheared off entirely, their outlines visible only as darker patches where the surrounding stone had been worn smooth by the passage of fingers or eyes or time.

Feimo felt the damage as a physical sensation, a wrongness in the texture of the air. The Labyrinth's text field had been disrupted. Where the characters had been, there was now a gap in the field's continuity, a silence in a language she had not known she was listening to until it stopped.

"Fragmenters did this?" she asked.

"No." Mia was examining the fracture, her fingers hovering over the cracked stone without touching it. "Fragmenters feed on text. They don't destroy it. They absorb it, metabolize it, leave behind residue. This is different. This was done by something that wanted the text gone, not consumed."

A sound from inside the Archive. Not the skittering of Fragmenters, which sounded like dry leaves in a wind tunnel, but something heavier. A rhythm. Footsteps, but wrong: too many, too synchronized, too deliberate.

Feimo and Mia looked at each other.

"That's not a cluster," Mia said. "That's a formation."

---

The Deep Archive was a cathedral of text.

Feimo had seen it once before, briefly, in the chaos of their first breach. She had not had time to take in the scale of it. Now, moving through the entrance with Mia at her shoulder, she understood that what she had seen was a fraction of what the space contained.

The Archive was a single room, but the word "room" failed it the way the word "book" failed the Library of Alexandria. The walls rose to a ceiling she could not see, lost in the phosphorescent haze that the text field generated at altitude. Every surface was covered in writing: characters, equations, diagrams, maps, musical notation, marginalia in a dozen languages, the accumulated annotations of centuries of scholars who had come here to read and could not resist writing back. The floor was stone, polished smooth by the passage of bodies, and the air was thick with the particular silence of a place where reading had been happening for so long that the silence itself had become a form of content.

In the center of the room, something was moving.

There were seven of them. They were not Fragmenters. Fragmenters were small, insectile, drawn to textual residue with the mindless persistence of a compass needle finding north. These were larger: humanoid in outline but wrong in proportion, their limbs too long, their joints articulated at angles that no skeleton supported. They moved in unison, seven bodies executing the same motion at the same time, as if controlled by a single nervous system. They were not reading the walls. They were erasing them. Each entity pressed a hand against the stone, and where the hand touched, the text vanished: not faded, not obscured, but removed, as if the characters had never existed.

Feimo watched one of them press its palm against a section of the western wall. A line of classical Chinese dissolved. The text had been a passage from the *Shijing*, one of the Book of Songs, a verse about a woman waiting at a river crossing. Feimo had read it during her first visit. She remembered the words: *By the islet in the river, the maiden who is handsome and good.* The words were gone now. The wall was blank where they had been, a silence in the shape of poetry.

"Text Eaters," Mia breathed. "I've read about them. I didn't think they were real."

"What are they?"

"A theory. A hypothesis in the Labyrinth's theoretical framework. The idea that the system could produce entities that didn't just feed on text but eliminated it. The opposite of Fragmenters. Fragmenters consume and leave residue. Text Eaters consume and leave nothing. The text ceases to have existed."

Feimo felt something cold settle in her chest. Not fear. Something worse than fear: the recognition that the Labyrinth was capable of producing threats she did not have a framework for understanding. Fragmenters were dangerous, but they were comprehensible. They were pests. They could be fought, contained, managed. Text Eaters were something else. They were erasure. They were the physical embodiment of forgetting.

"We have to stop them," Feimo said.

"We have to not die first," Mia replied. "Seven Text Eaters. Two of us. The math is not favorable."

A voice from the entrance behind them: "Make it three."

Aya.

She stood in the doorway, breathing hard, her dark hair pulled back and her hands bare. She was wearing the Academy's standard-issue training clothes, the kind worn for physical exercises, and there was a cut on her left forearm that she had not bothered to bandage, the blood already drying in a dark line from elbow to wrist. She had come fast. She had come fighting something on the way.

"The east stairwell was blocked," Aya said, stepping into the Archive. "Two of them were in the corridor. I got through."

"Two Fragmenters?" Mia asked.

Aya shook her head. "Smaller. Not Text Eaters either. Something new. Something I haven't seen before."

Feimo looked at her. In the Archive's phosphorescent light, Aya's face was a study in controlled urgency: jaw set, eyes moving, cataloguing the room with the methodical speed of someone trained to assess a space for threats before she assessed it for anything else. There was no panic in her expression. There was something else, something that sat behind the urgency like a bass note beneath a melody, and Feimo recognized it because she had felt it herself in the corridor outside Mia's room: the awareness that the situation had exceeded the parameters of the expected, and that the response required not just competence but judgment.

"The Text Eaters," Aya said. "How long have they been here?"

"Minutes," Mia said. "Maybe ten."

Aya looked at the wall where the *Shijing* passage had been. "They're not attacking randomly. Look at the pattern. They started at the perimeter and moved inward. They're clearing the outer walls first. Working toward something."

Mia followed her gaze. Her eyes narrowed. "The center. There's something in the center."

Feimo looked. At the heart of the Archive, behind the seven moving figures, there was a pedestal. She had not noticed it during her first visit; the chaos of the Fragmenter encounter had drawn her attention to the periphery, to the threats and the walls and the door. But the pedestal had always been there, a waist-high column of dark stone, and on it, an object.

A book.

It was not an impressive object. It was small, perhaps the size of a paperback, bound in dark cloth that had faded to the color of old tea. There was no lettering on the spine. No ornamentation on the cover. It sat on the pedestal with the self-effacing modesty of a thing that had been waiting so long it had forgotten it was waiting.

But the Text Eaters were moving toward it.

"They're not erasing randomly," Aya said again. "They're clearing a path. They want the book."

"Then we don't let them reach it," Feimo said.

---

The fight was not like fighting Fragmenters.

Fragmenters could be disrupted. They were unstable constructs, held together by the textual residue they consumed, and a strong enough counter-resonance could destabilize them, causing them to collapse into inert powder. Feimo and Mia had developed the technique during their weeks of covert Labyrinth exploration: a coordinated reading, aloud and in unison, of a text dense enough to generate a resonance field that overwhelmed the Fragmenters' absorption capacity. It was, in essence, a text-based weapon: words deployed not as communication but as force.

Text Eaters did not respond to counter-resonance. Feimo discovered this when she and Mia tried their usual technique, reading a passage from the 2021 cloze section aloud, their voices overlapping in the Archive's stone chamber. *Fluid intelligence is the type of intelligence that has to do with short-term memory and the ability to think quickly, logically, and abstractly in order to solve new problems.* The words filled the space, creating a resonance field that should have destabilized any text-consuming entity within its radius.

The Text Eaters did not even pause. They continued their slow, synchronized advance toward the pedestal, their long limbs moving with the mechanical patience of automata, their hands pressing against the walls and erasing everything they touched. The cloze passage's resonance washed over them like water over stone.

"It's not working," Mia said, her voice tight.

"I can see that."

"They're not absorbing the text. They're ignoring it. They're not text consumers. They're text nullifiers. The resonance doesn't disrupt them because they don't operate on the same principle. Fragmenters metabolize text. Text Eaters negate it. It's a different mechanism entirely."

Feimo felt the cold in her chest spread outward, reaching her arms, her hands, the tips of her fingers. She was watching centuries of accumulated writing disappear, one wall at a time. A section of mathematical notation, gone. A margin filled with Arabic calligraphy, gone. A diagram she did not recognize, annotated in a script she had never seen, gone. Each erasure was a small death, a diminishment of the Archive's accumulated knowledge, and she felt each one as a subtraction from something that belonged to her, even though she had not known it existed until moments ago.

"Try the reading passage," Aya said.

Feimo turned. Aya was standing three meters from the nearest Text Eater, her posture balanced, her hands raised in the guard position that the Academy's combat training prescribed but that Aya had clearly modified to suit her own style. The cut on her arm had started bleeding again. She was not looking at Feimo. She was looking at the Text Eaters.

"The 2021 reading section," Aya said. "Text 3. The Victorians. About why they didn't smile."

Feimo remembered the passage. She had read it during the exam session, three days ago, seated by the window while the rain fell and Cain's cameras watched. The passage was about photography, about exposure times and dental hygiene and social convention, about the way a society's relationship to its own image was mediated by technology and shame. It was also about something else, something the passage had not stated but implied: that the act of being seen, of having your image captured and fixed, was a form of vulnerability, and that the refusal to smile was a refusal to be vulnerable.

"Why that one?" Feimo asked.

"Because it's about resistance," Aya said. "About people who refused to perform for the technology that was watching them. Read it. Read it like you mean it."

Feimo opened her mouth. The words came out not as recitation but as something closer to invocation: "I've become preoccupied with looking for photographs that show our Victorian ancestors smiling. What better way to shatter the image of nineteenth-century prudery?"

The Text Eaters stopped.

Not all of them. Three of the seven faltered, their synchronized motion breaking for a fraction of a second, as if the passage had introduced a dissonance into their coordination. The other four continued, but their pace slowed, and the nearest one turned its head toward Feimo with a motion that was not human but was, unmistakably, an act of attention.

"They hear it," Mia said. "They're not absorbing it, but they're processing it. The passage is creating interference in their coordination signal."

Feimo kept reading. "Of course, I need to concede that my collection of smiling Victorians makes up only a tiny percentage of the vast catalogue of photographic portraiture created between 1840 and 1900, the majority of which show sitters posing miserably and stiffly in front of painted backdrops."

The three Text Eaters that had faltered now stood motionless. The remaining four had stopped advancing. The Archive was quiet except for Feimo's voice, and in that quiet, the text on the walls seemed to glow brighter, as if the passage's resonance was recharging the phosphorescence that the Text Eaters' erasure had dimmed.

"It's not the content," Mia said, her voice hushed with the particular reverence she reserved for moments when a hypothesis was confirmed. "It's the structure. The passage is about the tension between concealment and revelation. Between the desire to be seen and the fear of what being seen reveals. That tension is generating a frequency that interferes with the Text Eaters' negation field. They can erase text, but they can't erase contradiction."

Feimo continued. The passage moved through the history of Victorian photography, through exposure times and daguerreotypes and Box Brownies, through dentistry and class and the social etiquette of the mouth. She read about Mark Twain, who said there could be "nothing more damning than a silly, foolish smile fixed forever," and as she read, the Text Eaters retreated. Not far. Not far enough. But they moved backward, away from the pedestal, their synchronized motion broken into individual, hesitant steps, as if each entity was being forced to make a choice it had not been designed to make.

Then the nearest one lunged.

It moved fast, faster than a Fragmenter, faster than anything that size had a right to move, its elongated arm reaching for Feimo with the blind precision of a mechanism that had identified its target. Feimo stumbled backward, her voice breaking off mid-sentence, and the Text Eater's hand passed within centimeters of her face. She felt the negation field like a cold wind: the sensation of words being pulled out of her, not the words she had been reading but the words she was, the textual substrate of her consciousness, the accumulated language that made her Feimo and not merely a body.

She hit the ground. The Text Eater's hand came down.

And Aya was there.

She moved between Feimo and the Text Eater with the fluid efficiency of someone who had trained for precisely this geometry: body interposed, weight forward, both hands raised, not to strike but to block. The Text Eater's hand struck Aya's forearm and the negation field flared, visible now as a ripple in the air, a distortion that blurred everything it touched. Aya absorbed the impact. Her feet slid backward on the stone floor, leaving two parallel tracks in the dust, but she held.

"Aya," Feimo said.

"Don't," Aya said. Her voice was strained. The negation field was pressing against her, and Feimo could see it working: the text on Aya's skin, the faint luminous characters that every student at the Academy carried as a byproduct of prolonged exposure to the Labyrinth's text field, was dimming. The Text Eater was erasing her. Not killing her. Not hurting her. Erasing the text that constituted her presence in the Labyrinth's system.

"I don't want you to fall," Aya said.

The words were quiet. They were not dramatic. They were not a declaration or a confession or a speech. They were the kind of thing a person says when they are standing between someone they care about and a threat that is actively consuming them, and they have no time for the luxury of choosing their words carefully. They were instinct, not intention. The raw output of a system that had calculated, faster than conscious thought, that the person behind them was worth protecting at cost to itself.

Aya's forearm was glowing. Not with the text field's phosphorescence but with something brighter, a focused emission that pulsed in time with her heartbeat. The Text Eater recoiled. Its hand withdrew, and the negation field broke, and Aya dropped to one knee, breathing hard, her forearm still pulsing with light that faded as Feimo watched, dimming from white to amber to the faint residual glow that all Academy students carried.

The Text Eater fell back. The other six had stopped advancing entirely. They stood in a loose semicircle around the pedestal, motionless, their synchronized coordination shattered by the combination of Feimo's reading and Aya's intervention. They were not defeated. They were recalibrating.

"We need to move," Mia said. "Now. While they're processing. Whatever Aya did, it bought us minutes, not hours."

Feimo got to her feet. Her hands were shaking. She looked at Aya, who was still on one knee, her head bowed, her breathing audible in the Archive's silence.

"Can you stand?" Feimo asked.

"I can stand," Aya said. She proved it, rising with the controlled economy of movement that characterized everything she did. The cut on her arm had reopened fully now, and the blood ran down to her wrist in a thin, dark line. She did not look at it.

"The book," Mia said. "Get the book."

Feimo moved toward the pedestal. The Text Eaters did not stop her. They watched, their heads turning in unison, tracking her movement with the patient attention of predators that have been delayed but not deterred. She reached the pedestal and looked down.

The book was even smaller than it had appeared from a distance. Its dark cloth cover was worn soft by time, and the pages, visible at the edges, were yellowed but intact. There was no title on the spine, no author's name, no publisher's mark. It was, in every external respect, anonymous.

Feimo opened it.

The first page was covered in handwriting. Chinese characters, small and precise, written in ink that had faded from black to a deep brown. The hand was careful, each stroke placed with the deliberation of someone for whom the act of writing was both communication and art. Feimo recognized the hand. She had seen it before, on the door frame of the Archive's entrance, on the walls of the Labyrinth's corridors, on fragments of text that surfaced in her dreams. The hand belonged to whoever had built the Labyrinth, or to whoever had written the instructions for its construction.

She turned the page.

The second page was a map.

Not a single map. A composite: seven diagrams arranged in a pattern that Feimo's mind initially processed as random but quickly recognized as deliberate. Each diagram showed a different section of the Labyrinth, rendered in the same precise hand, with annotations in Chinese that labeled corridors, chambers, thresholds, and nodes. At the center of the composite, where the seven diagrams converged, there was a single character: 門. Gate.

She turned more pages. Each one contained a diagram of a different Labyrinth section, annotated in Chinese, with marginal notes that referenced texts, dates, and what appeared to be a set of instructions. The fourth page was different from the others. At the top, in characters larger than the rest, was an inscription:

打開一道門，打開全部。

*Open one door, open all.*

Feimo read the inscription and felt the words land not on her eyes but in her chest, in the same place where the cold had settled when she watched the Text Eaters erase the *Shijing* passage. The words were a key. Not a metaphorical key, not a symbol, not an allusion. A literal key: a sequence of characters that, when read aloud or silently, activated something in the Labyrinth's architecture. She could feel it working, the way you can feel a lock turning even when you cannot see the mechanism.

"Feimo." Mia's voice, sharp. "What does it say?"

Feimo looked up. Mia was standing three meters away, positioned between Feimo and the nearest Text Eater, her posture the one she adopted when she was ready to move in any direction. Aya stood beside her, bleeding, glowing faintly, her eyes fixed on Feimo with an expression that was hard to read.

"It's a map," Feimo said. "Of all seven Labyrinths."

Silence.

"Seven?" Mia said.

"All seven. Every section. Every corridor. Every threshold. The entire system, mapped and annotated in Chinese. Whoever built this place left instructions, and this book is the instruction manual."

She turned to the inscription page again. "And this. This is the master key. 'Open one door, open all.' Whoever reads this and understands it gains access to every sealed section of the Labyrinth. Every door, every archive, every hidden passage."

Mia's tail went rigid. It was the physiological response Feimo had learned to associate with Mia's highest level of alarm: not fear but the recognition that a variable had entered the equation that changed everything.

"Close the book," Mia said.

"What?"

"Close it. Now."

Feimo closed the book. The Text Eaters, which had been standing motionless, resumed their slow rotation around the pedestal, their heads tracking the book in Feimo's hands with the focused attention of something that had been programmed for a single purpose.

"Don't tell them you can read it," Mia said.

"Who?"

"Anyone. Cain. The security council. The faculty. Anyone." Mia stepped closer, her voice dropping to the register she used for information that was too dangerous to speak at normal volume. "Cain has cameras everywhere. He has sensors, monitoring equipment, the entire surveillance infrastructure. If he finds out that you can read Chinese, that you can access the Labyrinth's master key, he won't just restrict your movement. He'll use you. He'll put you in a room and he'll make you read every inscription in this place, and he'll use what you find to consolidate power in ways that none of us can reverse."

Feimo looked at the book in her hands. It weighed almost nothing.

"He doesn't know I can read Chinese," she said.

"He suspects. He's been watching you since the door incident. He doesn't have proof, and he's not the kind of person who acts on suspicion when he can wait for confirmation. But this book is confirmation. If anyone sees you reading it, if anyone sees you react to those characters, the suspicion becomes certainty, and the game changes."

Feimo understood. The understanding was not comfortable. It came with the weight of a responsibility she had not asked for and could not refuse: the knowledge that her ability to read Chinese was not a skill but a weapon, and that weapons, once revealed, attracted the attention of everyone who wanted to wield them.

She tucked the book inside her jacket, against her ribs, where the reinforced collar would conceal its outline.

"We go," Mia said. "Now. While they're still recalibrating."

---

They made it to level two before the second wave hit.

These were Fragmenters, not Text Eaters: smaller, faster, and drawn to the concentration of textual energy that the three of them carried after their exposure to the Deep Archive. They came from the eastern corridor in a skittering mass, dozens of them, their chitinous bodies clicking against the stone floor like a handful of marbles poured onto a table.

Feimo and Mia fell into their practiced rhythm: reading aloud, coordinated, their voices overlapping to create a resonance field that destabilized the Fragmenters on contact. But there were too many, and the resonance field had a radius, and the Fragmenters were coming from outside it, circling around the edges, probing for gaps.

Aya fought differently. She did not read. She moved. Her body became the weapon: precise, economical, each strike targeting the junction between a Fragmenter's head and thorax, the point where its text-absorption mechanism was most vulnerable. She fought with the efficiency of someone who had been trained not to read the Labyrinth but to survive it, and the distinction mattered. Reading was a slow weapon, requiring concentration and voice. Fighting was fast, requiring only the body and the willingness to use it.

They cleared the corridor in four minutes. Feimo was breathing hard. Mia's voice was hoarse. Aya's cut had stopped bleeding, replaced by a thin line of luminous scarring that pulsed faintly in the corridor's half-light.

They reached the stairwell. Noah was there, descending from above, her expression the calm mask she wore when the situation was bad enough to require her presence but not bad enough to require her to talk about it. She took one look at the three of them: Feimo clutching her ribs where the book sat, Mia barefoot and shaking, Aya bleeding and glowing.

"Report," Noah said.

"Text Eaters in the Deep Archive," Mia said. "Seven. Coordinated. They were trying to reach a book on the central pedestal."

"A book."

"A map of the Labyrinth. All seven sections. Written in Chinese."

Noah's eyes moved to Feimo. The look lasted exactly two seconds. Feimo could not read it. Noah's expressions were encrypted in a cipher that her sister had never shared.

"The book is secure?" Noah asked.

"Secure," Feimo said.

"Good. Get to the War Room. The Professor needs to know."

"She already knows," Mia said. "She's been watching since the alarm started."

Noah paused. Something shifted in her expression, a microadjustment that Feimo would have missed if she had not spent a lifetime reading her sister's face for the signs that preceded decisions.

"Then she's waiting for you," Noah said. "Go."

---

The War Room smelled the way it always did: paint, electronics, the chemical sharpness of a space that had been built for control rather than comfort. The eleven monitors glowed on the far wall, each displaying a different section of the Academy. Feimo could see the sub-basement corridor on the center screen: empty now, the Text Eaters gone, the Archive's entrance damaged but intact.

Professor Silence stood in front of the monitors with her back to the door. She did not turn when they entered.

"Sit," she said.

They sat. Mia took the chair nearest the door, the tactician's position: closest to escape. Aya sat beside her, her bleeding forearm resting on the table, leaving a small dark stain on the surface. Feimo sat across from them, the book pressing against her ribs, a weight that was simultaneously negligible and immense.

The Professor turned. She looked at each of them in sequence: Mia, Aya, Feimo. Her expression was the one she wore in seminars, the one that communicated nothing except the expectation of honesty.

"The Text Eaters," the Professor said. "You encountered seven."

"Yes," Mia said.

"You engaged them."

"We tried counter-resonance. It didn't work. The cloze passage had no effect."

"But a reading passage did. Text 3. The Victorians."

Mia's jaw tightened. "You were watching."

"I was listening. The War Room's audio feeds are more reliable than its video. I heard the passage. I heard the result." She paused. "I also heard what Aya said."

The room went quiet. Feimo felt her pulse in her throat.

"I don't want you to fall," the Professor repeated, her voice neutral, as if quoting a line from a text she was about to analyze. "An interesting choice of words. Not 'I won't let them hurt you.' Not 'Stand behind me.' Not any of the conventional formulations of protection. Instead, a statement about the self: *I* don't want. The protector's motivation, not the protected's safety."

Aya said nothing. She was looking at the table, at the blood stain, at the place where her arm met the surface. Her expression was the one Feimo had seen in the tournament: the face of someone who has been caught doing something sincere and is trying to calculate the cost of sincerity.

"The Text Eaters are a new phenomenon," the Professor continued. "They were not in the Labyrinth's original design. They are an emergent property, produced by the system in response to a stimulus I have not yet identified. Their appearance tonight suggests that the Labyrinth is changing. Growing, perhaps. Adapting."

She looked at Feimo.

"As are you."

Feimo felt the book against her ribs. She said nothing.

"The exam we administered three days ago," the Professor said, "was the 2021 paper. The cloze passage was about fluid intelligence: the type of cognition that peaks in young adulthood and then generally declines. *While aging is inevitable, scientists are finding out that certain changes in brain function may not be.* The passage describes the accumulation of abdominal fat and its association with cognitive decline. It prescribes aerobic exercise and a Mediterranean diet."

She paused.

"The passage is about maintenance. About the body's tendency toward entropy and the interventions that can delay it. It is, in the context of the Academy's current situation, a text about the Labyrinth itself. The system is aging. The Text Eaters are symptoms of that aging: the emergence of processes that consume rather than preserve. The question the passage poses is whether the decline can be delayed, and if so, by what means."

Mia leaned forward. "The reading section. Text 2. The CCT program."

The Professor nodded. "Conditional cash transfers. A poverty-alleviation program in Indonesia that, as a side effect, reduced deforestation by thirty percent. The program was not designed to protect forests. It was designed to protect people. But by giving the rural poor a financial buffer against bad harvests, it removed the incentive to clear land for emergency planting. The environmental benefit was an emergent property of a social intervention."

"You think the Labyrinth works the same way," Feimo said.

"I think the Labyrinth is a system, and systems behave according to principles that are transferable across domains. The CCT program shows that a targeted intervention in one area can produce beneficial effects in another. It also shows that the relationship between cause and effect is not always direct. Sometimes the most effective action is the one that appears, to a superficial observer, to be unrelated to the problem."

Feimo thought about this. She thought about the Text Eaters and the passage that had stopped them. Not a passage about combat or defense or the Labyrinth itself. A passage about Victorian photography. About smiling. About the refusal to perform for the camera. An intervention that appeared unrelated to the problem but had, through a mechanism she did not fully understand, disrupted the Text Eaters' coordination and bought them time.

"Text 3," Feimo said. "The Victorians. Why did it work?"

The Professor sat down. It was the first time Feimo had seen her sit during a briefing. The gesture was not casual. It was a decision, a deliberate lowering of authority, a signal that what followed was not instruction but conversation. She did not mention the monitors. She did not mention the cameras, or the feed from the sub-basement, or the fact that she had been watching the entire engagement and had chosen not to intervene. The omissions were their own kind of statement.

"The passage is about the tension between being seen and refusing to be seen," the Professor said. "The Victorians had the technology to smile. By the 1890s, exposure times were fast enough. But they still didn't smile. Not because they couldn't, but because smiling meant vulnerability. It meant revealing something real behind the performance. And the passage's author, who collects smiling Victorian photographs, is engaged in an act of resistance: showing that the stern, posed, unsmiling image is the performance, and the smile is the truth."

She looked at the monitors. The center screen still showed the empty corridor.

"The Text Eaters erase text. They negate meaning. They are, in a sense, the ultimate form of control: the elimination of content, not through censorship but through annihilation. The text does not become forbidden. It becomes nonexistent. And what the Victorian passage does, what all acts of genuine expression do, is assert that content exists. That there is something behind the mask. The smile behind the sternness. The truth behind the performance. The Text Eaters cannot negate that assertion because it is not a text to be erased. It is a contradiction to be inhabited."

Mia was writing. She had produced a notebook from somewhere and was recording the Professor's words with the speed of someone who recognized that what she was hearing was not a lecture but a thesis.

"Text 4," Mia said without looking up. "Net neutrality."

The Professor's mouth moved. It was not quite a smile. It was the nearest thing to a smile that Feimo had ever seen on her face: a slight reconfiguration of the muscles around her eyes, a softening that lasted less than a second.

"The FCC," the Professor said. "An agency created to regulate a system, gradually captured by the entities it was supposed to regulate, until its primary function became the deregulation of those entities. The 2017 order eliminated net neutrality rules and rejected the commission's authority to require broadband providers to do much of anything. Judge Millett's concurring opinion noted that the result was 'unhinged from the realities of modern broadband service.'"

She paused.

"Does this remind you of anything?"

"Cain," Feimo said.

"Not only Cain. The Academy itself. An institution created to manage the Labyrinth, gradually captured by the logic of control, until its primary function becomes the restriction of access rather than the facilitation of exploration. Cain's War Room, his cameras, his security detail, his curfews and authorization requirements: these are the Academy's version of the FCC's deregulatory order. They are not protecting the system. They are preventing the system from being used."

Feimo thought of the translation passage, the one from the exam's translation section. She had read it during the test session, her pencil moving through the nested clauses with the halting precision of someone navigating a sentence that resisted rendering. The passage was about higher education: about the post-war expansion that doubled enrollment in five-year periods, about the new staff who defined the norms of academic life, about the growth rates that increased the chances for academic innovation while weakening the forms and processes of admission.

*High growth rates increased the chances for academic innovation; they also weakened the forms and processes by which teachers and students are admitted into a community of scholars during periods of stability or slow growth.*

That was the Labyrinth now. A period of high growth. The emergence of new phenomena, new entities, new textual structures. And the forms and processes of the old system, the admission requirements and authorization protocols and security frameworks, were being weakened by the very growth they were supposed to manage.

"The writing prompt," Feimo said suddenly. "The Part B essay."

The Professor raised an eyebrow.

"The picture prompt. A foreign friend graduating from college and wanting to find a job in China. The task was to write suggestions."

"Go on."

"The passage was about advice. About the kind of advice that is useful and the kind that is not. In the context of everything else in the exam, the prompt is asking: what does a newcomer need to know to navigate a system they don't understand?"

She looked at the book pressing against her ribs.

"That's what this is. The Chinese book. It's not just a map. It's a set of suggestions for navigating the Labyrinth. Left by someone who understood the system and wanted to help others who would come after."

"Or," Mia said, looking up from her notebook, "it's a trap. Left by someone who understood that the most effective way to control a system is to give its users the illusion of a guide."

The Professor stood. She walked to the monitors and pressed a sequence on the control panel. The center screen changed: the empty corridor was replaced by a view of the Deep Archive's interior. The damage was visible: the cracked door frame, the blank sections of wall where the Text Eaters had erased centuries of text. The pedestal stood empty at the center, dark and still.

"The exam's Part B section," the Professor said, "was about artificial intelligence. About how AI is changing companies and the economy in ways that don't involve mass unemployment. The matching exercise asked you to identify where specific statements about AI's capabilities fit within the text. The answers were about hiring practices, marketing optimization, cost reduction, and infrastructure maintenance."

She turned to face them.

"AI is a tool. Like the Labyrinth. Like language itself. The question the exam poses is not whether AI is good or bad but whether it is used well or poorly. The same technology that removes unconscious bias from hiring can also be used to surveil employees. The same algorithm that optimizes marketing can also be used to manipulate consumers. The tool is neutral. The intent is not."

She looked at each of them.

"Cain's intent is control. My intent is understanding. These are not the same, and the Labyrinth knows the difference. The Text Eaters appeared because the system is under stress. They are a symptom, not a cause. The cause is the restriction of the Labyrinth's natural function: the flow of text, the accumulation of meaning, the generation of new knowledge from the interaction of old knowledge."

She paused at the door.

"The book you recovered is significant. But its significance depends on what you do with it. A map in the hands of an explorer is a tool for discovery. A map in the hands of a conqueror is a tool for domination. The same object. Different outcomes."

She opened the door.

"Decide which one you are."

---

The door closed. The three of them sat in the War Room, surrounded by monitors, bathed in the cold light of screens showing corridors and stairwells and rooms that were, at that moment, empty.

Feimo reached inside her jacket and withdrew the book. She held it in both hands, feeling the weight of it, the cloth cover soft against her palms. She opened it to the first page, the one with the inscription in the careful, precise hand.

打開一道門，打開全部。

She could read it. She could feel the key working, the mechanism in the Labyrinth's architecture responding to the sequence of characters, preparing to open pathways that had been sealed for longer than anyone in the Academy could remember.

"Don't tell them," Mia said again, quieter now. Not a command. A request. The kind of request that comes from someone who has analyzed the situation from every angle and arrived at a conclusion that contradicts every instinct of openness and trust. "Not because I don't trust them. Not because I think they'd betray you. But because the moment this becomes known, it stops being yours. It becomes a weapon in someone else's war."

Feimo looked at Mia. In the monitor light, her face was sharp angles and deep shadows, the face of someone who had chosen, long ago, to protect herself by understanding everything and trusting nothing. And yet here she was, trusting Feimo. Asking Feimo to trust her in return.

"Okay," Feimo said.

Aya stood. She looked at the blood on the table, at the luminous scar on her forearm, at Feimo and the book. Her expression was the one she had worn in the Archive, the one that sat behind the urgency and the competence and the physical courage: not warmth, not yet, but the acknowledgment that warmth existed, somewhere, in a frequency she had not yet learned to hear.

"I need to bandage this," Aya said. She gestured at her arm.

"I'll help," Feimo said.

Aya looked at her. Something shifted in her eyes, a movement that was too quick and too quiet to be called a change but was, nonetheless, a change.

"Okay," Aya said.

They left the War Room together. The monitors watched them go, each screen showing a different empty corridor, a different angle on a building that was still standing, still illuminated, still functioning, despite everything that was growing inside it.

The exam paper sat on the table, untouched. The writing prompt stared up at the ceiling: *A foreign friend of yours has recently graduated from college and intends to find a job in China. Write him or her an email to make some suggestions.*

Feimo had not written her essay during the exam. She had run out of time, distracted by the passages, by Cain's surveillance, by the knowledge that every sentence she read was also being read by the walls, by the system, by whatever consciousness the Labyrinth possessed and deployed in its own defense.

Now, walking down the corridor with Mia on one side and Aya on the other, she thought about what she would have written. What suggestions would she give a stranger entering a system they did not understand?

Learn to read the walls. Learn to hear the silence between words. Learn to distinguish between protection and control, between a door that locks to keep you safe and a door that locks to keep you contained. Learn that the system you are entering has a history that predates your arrival and a future that will outlast your stay.

And above all, learn to smile for the camera.

Not because the camera deserves it. But because the smile is the one thing the system cannot erase. It is the text behind the text, the meaning beneath the meaning, the human expression that survives every attempt to reduce it to data.

Feimo touched the book through her jacket. The cloth cover was warm against her skin, or perhaps it was her skin that was warm against the cover. She could not tell where her body ended and the book began, and the inability to distinguish the boundary was, she thought, the point.

Open one door. Open all.

She walked on.
