# Chapter 1: The Cat-Ear Girl and the Cruelest Spirit

The textbook was talking to her.

Feimo knew that was impossible. Textbooks did not talk. They sat on desks, they collected dust, they accumulated coffee rings and the faint ghost-smell of cheap paper. They did not whisper. And yet, as her cheek pressed flat against the open page of *Introduction to English Lexicology*, Third Edition, she could hear it: a low, murmuring hum rising from the words themselves, syllables overlapping like water running over stones.

She told herself it was a dream. She had fallen asleep in Study Room 4B again. The fluorescent lights were probably still buzzing overhead. Her boba tea was definitely cold. In about four hours the cleaning auntie would come in, see the cat-eared girl drooling on her textbook, and sigh with the weariness of someone who had seen worse.

The hum grew louder.

Feimo opened her eyes.

She was not in Study Room 4B.

The ceiling above her was vaulted and impossibly high, its surface covered in text so small it looked like texture, like grain in wood. The words shifted as she watched, rearranging themselves in a slow, organic drift, sentences dissolving and reforming like ink in water. The floor beneath her was polished stone, cool against her palms, and embedded in its surface were faintly glowing lines, like veins of light pulsing in rhythm with that ever-present hum. The air smelled of old paper and something else, something she could not name, the scent of meaning itself if meaning had a scent.

She sat up. The room was vast. Corridors branched off in four directions, each lined with walls of compressed text that seemed to breathe, expanding and contracting in a cadence that matched her heartbeat. In the distance, a vast atrium opened: sixteen doors, each bearing a year, stretching from 2010 to 2025, standing in patient silence like chapters waiting to be opened. No fluorescent lights. The illumination came from the words themselves, a warm amber glow that made the air feel thick and lived-in, like the inside of a library that had been burning steadily for a thousand years. The light was not uniform: common words glowed bright and steady, while rarer words flickered like distant stars embedded in the stone.

"This isn't real," Feimo said aloud.

Her voice echoed. The walls rippled. Somewhere far away, she heard what might have been laughter, or the turning of a page, or both.

She got to her feet, and that was when she noticed two things simultaneously.

First: her textbook was still in her hands, open to the same page. The chapter heading read "Polysemy and Context-Dependent Meaning." She had been studying it before she fell asleep. Or she was still studying it. The line between the two had become uncomfortably thin.

Second: there was a mirror.

It was set into the wall at the junction of two corridors, a perfect oval of reflective surface surrounded by a frame of compressed, unreadable script that moved if she looked at it from the corner of her eye. Feimo stepped toward it, and her reflection stepped to meet her.

The girl in the mirror had her face. Her dark hair, perpetually messy from falling asleep in weird positions. Her slightly-too-large eyes, amber-brown, with the particular quality of seeming half-asleep even when she was paying attention. Her Academy uniform, rumpled, the silver-trimmed blazer that she wore every day without ever quite making it look official.

But perched on top of her head, half-hidden by messy bangs, were two triangular ears covered in dark fur. They swiveled as she stared, catching the ambient hum of the walls and rotating toward it like satellite dishes. And behind her, swaying gently with a will of its own, was a long, thin tail, its tip twitching in a rhythm she could not consciously control.

Feimo stared. Her tail twitched. The reflection's tail twitched in perfect synchrony.

She reached up and touched her head. The ear flicked under her fingers. It was warm. It was real. Or as real as anything in this place. She pressed it down; it sprang back up. She pressed the other one; the first one rose. It was like a game she could not win.

"I've officially lost it," she whispered. "Midterm psychosis. I knew I should have slept more."

"Congratulations on your first accurate observation of the evening."

The voice came from nowhere and everywhere, crisp and sharp as a paper cut. A column of pale blue light coalesced three feet to Feimo's left, swirled like frost forming on glass, solidified into a floating sphere, and then unfolded into the translucent projection of a young woman.

She was beautiful in the way that a precisely engineered equation is beautiful: every line deliberate, every proportion exact. Dark hair cut to an immaculate bob that did not move, because holographic hair apparently did not need to obey physics. Eyes like polished obsidian, currently narrowed in what appeared to be professional-grade contempt. She wore a variant of the Academy uniform Feimo had never seen before, crisp and unwrinkled, with silver circuit-like patterns running along the cuffs. She hovered six inches above the floor with her arms crossed.

The holographic girl looked Feimo up and down. Her gaze lingered on the ears, the tail, the coffee-stained textbook, and the small patch of drool still drying on Feimo's cheek.

"You're the new transfer?" she said.

"I don't know what I am," Feimo said honestly.

"Terrific. Another one." The girl pinched the bridge of her nose, a gesture so human it was almost startling against her translucent form. "I don't mind telling you that your file is the most confusing one I've processed this cycle. My designation is Mia. I am the Academy's Spirit AI, assigned to guide and assist newly enrolled students. You will address me by name, not by title, not by 'hey you,' and absolutely not by 'that floating lady.'" She paused. "What's your name?"

"Feimo."

"Feimo." Mia tasted the word, tilting her head. "Crimson ink. Dramatic. Were your parents optimists or just fans of calligraphy?"

"It's a family name," Feimo said, which was technically true and mostly a way to avoid explaining that she had no idea why her parents chose it either.

Mia's eyes flickered, the obsidian surface scrolling with faint lines of data too fast to read. "Scanning... Feimo. Demi-kind. Nekomimi variant. Enrollment status: confirmed. House: unsorted." She looked up. "You haven't been sorted yet."

"Sorted into what?"

"The Four Houses. The Academy's organizational structure." Mia's tone carried the cadence of someone reciting information for the hundredth time, with the weary precision of a flight attendant explaining seatbelt mechanics. "Weave, Mirror, Bridge, Forge. Each represents a different approach to language, a different way of understanding the words that form this place." She gestured at the walls around them. "I'll explain it in short: the Labyrinth Academy is the material substrate of human literary consciousness. Every word ever written, read, spoken, or thought has structural presence here. The walls are text. The floors are grammar. The light is meaning. The hum you hear is the aggregate of billions of minds reading, all at once, all the time. Each House leads a different area of expertise."

Feimo processed this for approximately two seconds.

"I was studying for a midterm."

"Yes, well. You studied too hard. Or, more accurately, you studied just enough. Labyrinth Sensitivity correlates with deep reading habits, bilingualism, and genuine literary engagement. For example, students who read across multiple languages seem to develop it more readily. Congratulations: your insomnia and poor life choices have qualified you for interdimensional education." Mia's lips curved into something that was not quite a smile. "Don't look so panicked. It's unbecoming. Also, your left ear is flat against your head. That means you're scared. I consider that a reasonable response, all things considered."

Feimo clapped a hand over her ear. "That's a reflex!"

"Sure it is." Mia turned, her form drifting toward the corridor to the left. "Come along. The sorting ceremony starts in twelve minutes, and you look like you slept in a textbook."

"I *did* sleep in a textbook."

"I know. I can smell the cheap paper from here. And the boba tea. When was the last time you washed that blazer?"

Feimo chose not to answer that question and followed instead, because what else was there to do? The corridor opened into a wider hall, and the hum of the walls grew louder, more structured, resolving into something almost musical. Other students were appearing, stepping through shimmering portals or simply materializing mid-stride, each wearing the same dark blazer with silver trim. Some had animal ears. Most did not. A group of students in deep blue Weave badges walked past in tight formation, comparing notes. A tall girl with silver-gray hair followed behind them, her eyes fixed on a book she was reading while walking, her expression one of absolute, terrifying focus. Behind her, a boy with paint-stained fingers and a crimson House badge was drawing something on a blank patch of wall, sketching Glyphs that sparked under his pen like tiny fireworks. The sketch seemed to draw energy from the wall itself, each stroke lighting up the surrounding text.

None of them looked at Feimo twice.

"The sorting determines your dominant linguistic aptitude," Mia continued without turning around. "Weave students specialize in grammar and structure. They fix collapsed corridors and repair corrupted text. Mirror students analyze and interpret. They're the ones who argue about symbolism at dinner and write twelve-page essays on a single sentence." A pause. "Bridge students translate and connect across languages. They're the Labyrinth's diplomats. Forge students create new text entirely. They add to the Labyrinth rather than just maintaining it. They're the most talented and the most insufferable."

"Which one am I?"

"That's what the ceremony determines. Your test results suggest Bridge, but the Labyrinth has opinions of its own."

They reached a circular chamber. The ceiling here was higher still, open to a vault that disappeared into darkness above, and the walls were covered in four enormous banners, each bearing a House crest. A threaded needle piercing three Glyphs for Weave, deep blue against stone. An eye reflected in a mirror for Mirror, silver-gray and unsettling. An arch bridge spanning a chasm for Bridge, warm amber like the light of the walls themselves. A hammer striking an anvil for Forge, crimson as fresh ink.

A floating lectern at the center of the chamber held a crystalline orb the size of a basketball, its surface swirling with pale light. One by one, students approached, placed their hands on the orb, and were enveloped in colored light before walking toward their House banner.

Feimo watched a boy touch the orb and be surrounded by deep blue. Weave. He walked toward the needle banner with quiet satisfaction, already pulling out a notebook. A girl with thick glasses received silver-gray light and practically sprinted toward Mirror, clutching a notebook to her chest with both arms.

"Step forward when ready," said a calm, automated voice from the lectern.

Feimo stepped forward. She placed her hand on the orb.

It was warm. The hum of the Labyrinth intensified, rising from a murmur to a roar, and the world dissolved into golden light. The other students, the banners, the chamber itself, all of it vanished, replaced by a vast expanse of luminous text flowing in every direction like a river of light. Somewhere in the distance, she heard a voice, clear and authoritative, as if reading from a textbook:

*"The discipline of literary studies divides into three branches. Literary theory seeks universal principles governing the nature, creation, and reception of literature. Literary history traces the development of literature across time, mapping how genres rise and fall, how movements influence one another, how individual works emerge from and reshape their traditions. Literary criticism analyzes specific works and phenomena, applying theoretical frameworks to produce interpretive insight. The three are interdependent: theory provides the methodology that guides historical and critical inquiry, while history and criticism generate the empirical material from which new theory grows."*

The voice was clinical and precise, like a lecturer reading from notes, but it resonated in Feimo's chest like a second heartbeat. She felt the information settle into her awareness, not as memorized facts, but as something she understood from the inside. Three ways of looking at the same object. Three perspectives that could not exist without each other.

The light shifted. A new voice, different timbre, warmer:

*"The American scholar M.H. Abrams, in* The Mirror and the Lamp, *proposed that all literary activity involves four elements: the universe that literature describes, the work that is produced, the artist who creates it, and the audience that receives it. These four elements form a relational map. Any literary theory can be positioned on this map by identifying which element it privileges. Mimetic theories center the universe. Expressive theories center the artist. Objective theories center the work. Pragmatic theories center the audience. The four-element framework is the fundamental coordinate system of literary thought."*

The amber light pulsed, and Feimo felt something shift inside her, a recognition, as if a door she had always walked past without noticing had suddenly opened. The four elements: world, work, artist, audience. A map. Every theory she had ever half-understood in her survey courses suddenly had a place to live.

The light faded. Feimo found herself standing in the warm amber glow of a new corridor. The Bridge House banner hung above her, its crest shimmering. On her left lapel, a small badge had appeared: an arch bridge spanning a chasm, Glyphs flowing across it in miniature, warm to the touch.

"Bridge House," Mia said from behind her. There was something in her voice, a note that had not been there before. Not quite respect. More like recalibration, the sound of an equation being rebalanced. "The Labyrinth thinks you understand how words cross gaps. How meaning survives translation."

"I don't even understand how I got here."

"That's approximately the right attitude for Bridge." Mia floated ahead, but slower now, as if she was waiting for Feimo to keep up. "Come on. Your orientation challenge is about to begin, and if you're late, I have to file paperwork. You do not want to know how much paperwork."

The Hawthorne Corridor was narrow and old.

Unlike the broad, well-lit halls of the Academy's central chambers, this passage was dim, its walls covered in text that was raw and unpressed, letters overlapping and half-eroded like pages left out in the rain for decades. The hum here was different, strained and discordant, a wrong note in the Labyrinth's otherwise steady frequency. The air felt thicker, charged with a tension that made Feimo's ears pin back involuntarily and her tail wrap around her own ankle.

"This is a Weave challenge," Mia explained, hovering at the corridor's entrance. Her form flickered at the edges, reacting to the instability. "Every new student must complete one orientation challenge from each House. We're starting with Weave because it's the most structured. Weave challenges test your ability to restore damaged or incomplete text. You'll encounter a passage with gaps. Fill the gaps correctly, and the corridor stabilizes. Fill them wrong, and the text erodes further. You lose a Glyph for every incorrect answer."

"What happens when I run out of Glyphs?"

"You don't have any Glyphs to lose. You just arrived." Mia's expression was unreadable. "In your case, incorrect answers cause localized corridor collapse. The ceiling comes down. I would suggest not being wrong."

"Helpful. Thanks."

"I'm a Spirit AI. Helpfulness is a feature I was not specifically programmed for."

Feimo stepped into the corridor. The moment she crossed the threshold, the walls shuddered. Text streamed across their surface, fragmented, breaking apart like ice cracking on a river. A section of wall to her right crumbled, letters scattering like dust motes in a sunbeam, each one dimming and going dark as it hit the floor.

And then the words appeared, floating in the air before her, filling the corridor from wall to wall in clean, sharp type:

*"In 1924 America's National Research Council sent two engineers to supervise a series of experiments at a telephone-parts factory called the Hawthorne Plant near Chicago. It hoped they would learn how shop-floor lighting ___ workers' productivity."*

Four options materialized beneath the blank, each one a small crystalline shard:

**[A] affected** **[B] achieved** **[C] extracted** **[D] restored**

Feimo frowned. She knew this. She had studied this passage, or something like it, during her exam prep. The Hawthorne experiments, the study of factory workers, the relationship between observation and behavior. The researchers had gone in expecting to find a simple correlation between lighting and output. They found something much stranger. The answer was *affected*. Lighting affected productivity.

She reached out and touched option A. The Glyph for "affect" flared gold in her palm, warm and heavy with the accumulated weight of every context in which that word had ever been used, and the blank in the wall filled itself in. The corridor groaned but held, the text stabilizing around the restored word like a wound closing.

*"Instead, the studies ended ___ giving their name to the 'Hawthorne effect,' the extremely influential idea that the very ___ of being experimented upon changed subjects' behavior."*

More blanks appeared. Eighteen more, each one a fracture in the corridor's structure, each one a small crisis demanding resolution. Feimo moved through them steadily, her cat-eyes reading the dim text faster than any human eye could, her ears catching the sub-audible frequencies of the Glyphs as they pulsed in the walls.

**ended ___ giving their name**: "up." End up doing something. The studies did not plan to become famous. They did not set out to name a phenomenon. They just did. Sometimes the most important results are the ones nobody expected.

**the very ___ of being experimented upon**: "act." Not truth, not sight, not proof. The act itself. The mere fact of observation was enough to change the observed. She touched the Glyph and felt it resonate: the weight of every experiment, every watched moment, every situation where knowing you were being studied altered what you did.

The corridor was shaking now, dust falling from the ceiling in fine streams. Feimo's tail bristled. Her ears swiveled, catching frequencies she could not name, and she felt rather than heard the next section of the passage straining to hold together, the text flickering between legible and corrupted.

**The idea arose because of the ___ behavior of the women in the plant**: "perplexing." Because their output rose when lighting was increased, but also when it was dimmed. Nothing made sense. That was the point. The word carried the researchers' bewilderment like a scent.

**According to ___ of the experiments, their hourly output rose when lighting was increased**: "accounts." Records, reports, the testimony of the engineers who were there. What people said happened.

**It did not ___ what was done in the experiment**: "matter." It did not matter what they changed. The workers' behavior shifted regardless of the direction of the change. The Glyph of "matter" pulsed with quiet insistence: *this counts. This is important. Don't dismiss it.*

**___ something was changed, productivity rose**: "so long as." As long as something, anything, was different, output went up. The condition was absurdly simple and deeply unsettling. The workers did not need better conditions. They needed *any* change at all.

**A(n) ___ that they were being experimented upon seemed to be ___ to alter workers' behavior ___ itself**: "awareness." "enough." "by itself." The workers knew they were being watched, and that knowledge alone was enough to change how they worked. No other variable mattered. Observation was the only cause that held.

The corridor buckled. Feimo turned back to her corridor. The remaining blanks hovered before her like a gauntlet.

**After several decades, the same data were ___ to econometric analysis**: "subjected." Subject something to analysis. The data were put through a new lens, decades later, examined by economists instead of psychologists. The word carried a particular weight: to subject something is to force it to endure scrutiny it did not choose.

**___ the descriptions on record, no systematic ___ was found that levels of productivity were related to changes in lighting**: "Contrary to." "evidence." Contrary to what everyone believed, the evidence did not support the original conclusions. The descriptions on record said one thing; the reanalysis said another. The gap between narrative and data, between what we believe happened and what the numbers show, was a canyon.

The corridor groaned. Feimo was sweating now, her ears flat against her skull, her tail wrapped tight around her leg. A chunk of ceiling cracked and fell, shattering on the floor into a shower of dimming letters.

**lighting was always changed on a Sunday**: The passage was providing context now, explaining why the original interpretation was misleading. The peculiar timing of the experiments, always shifting conditions on a rest day, had created a confounding variable no one had noticed for decades.

**output ___ rose compared with the previous Saturday and ___ to rise for the next couple of days**: "duly." "continued." Output rose on schedule, as if obeying a natural rhythm. Not because of the lighting change. Because it was Monday. Workers tended to be more productive at the start of the week. The effect had nothing to do with the experiment at all.

**a comparison with data for weeks when there was no experimentation showed that output always went up on Mondays**: "However." The real insight. The punchline of a decades-long joke. The productivity increase happened with or without experimentation. The alleged Hawthorne effect, the extremely influential idea that had shaped research methodology for generations, was hard to pin down. Perhaps it had never existed at all, not in the way anyone believed.

**Workers ___ to be diligent for the first few days of the week in any case, before ___ a plateau and then slackening off**: "tended." "hitting." They hit a natural plateau mid-week. Then they slackened off. The rhythm was human, ordinary, completely unrelated to observation or awareness or the grand theories that had been built on top of it.

Feimo filled the final blank. The corridor shuddered once, twice, and then fell still. The text on the walls solidified, letters aligning into clean, legible script. The hum that had been discordant and strained resolved into a steady, calm frequency, like a heartbeat returning to rest.

She let out a breath she had not realized she was holding.

"Not terrible," Mia said. She had been watching from the corridor entrance the entire time, her holographic form flickering slightly at the edges from the corridor's instability. "You completed the passage in four minutes and thirty-seven seconds. Average completion time for first-year Bridge students is six minutes twelve seconds. Average error rate: 2.3. Yours: zero."

"You timed me?"

"I time everyone. It's called data collection. I have spreadsheets." Mia paused. "Your cat instincts helped. You were reading the Glyph frequencies in the walls before you consciously processed the words. That's not cheating, despite what some Weave students would say."

"It felt like cheating."

"Good. That means you're self-aware. The truly insufferable ones don't notice they have advantages." Mia's expression softened by approximately one microexpression, a shift so subtle that a less observant person would have missed it entirely. "The corridor is stable. You should rest."

Feimo sat down on the cool stone floor and leaned against the restored wall. Her hands were shaking. Not from exhaustion, exactly, but from the residual charge of channeling that many Glyphs in sequence. Each word she had chosen had pulsed through her like a small electric shock, and the accumulated effect left her feeling like she had been rewired from the inside. Her three active Glyphs settled into her awareness: "affect," "matter," "continued." Small words, common words, but each one carried the weight of every context in which it had ever been used. She was not able to block them out; they had become part of her. "Affect" felt like the workers' quiet surprise. "Matter" felt like the moment something dismissed as trivial turns out to count. "Continued" felt like persistence — the stubborn refusal of things to stop just because someone declared them finished.

She noticed other text on the walls of the restored corridor, fragments from passages that had been here long before her challenge. Near the floor, half-eroded, a sentence about birds and the land community:

*"We have at least drawn nearer the point of admitting that birds should continue as a matter of intrinsic right, regardless of the presence or absence of economic advantage to us."*

Below it, another fragment, different hand, different era:

*"It tends to ignore, and thus eventually to eliminate, many elements in the land community that lack commercial value, but that are essential to its healthy functioning."*

Feimo traced the words with her fingers. They felt significant, though she could not say why. Something about value, about what we choose to preserve and what we let disappear. About rights that exist independent of usefulness. The Labyrinth was built from every word ever written, but not every word survived equally. Some corridors were bright and strong, maintained by the constant attention of readers and students. Others, like this one, were dim and eroding, their text fading because nobody read it anymore.

"Reading the walls again?" Mia hovered closer. "Deep Archive echoes. Old text." She paused, and something in her voice shifted, a note that was not quite reverence. "The Labyrinth preserves everything, but not everything gets maintained. Words that people stop reading grow thin."

Feimo looked at the fragments. Birds. Intrinsic rights. Elements essential to healthy functioning. She did not know who had written them or when. But the words felt like the Labyrinth itself was speaking, confessing something it was afraid to say aloud: that preserving was not the same as protecting, and that some things were only valued after they were already gone.

"The corridor is restored," Mia said, turning away. "I'll escort you to Bridge House dormitory."

Feimo stood, stretched, and followed. But as she took her first step, she heard something.

Faint. Rhythmic. Coming from somewhere beneath the polished stone.

*Tap. Tap. Tap.*

Not random. Not mechanical. Deliberate. Like someone below was rapping their knuckles against the underside of the floor, patiently, rhythmically, as if trying to get her attention. It seemed to happen in patterns: three knocks, pause, three knocks, pause.

Feimo's ears rotated, triangulating the source. The sound was coming from directly below her, maybe ten feet down, maybe more. She pressed her palm flat against the stone. The knocking vibrated through her bones, steady as a metronome.

"Do you hear that?" she whispered.

Mia was silent for a long moment. Her holographic form flickered, the edges going momentarily unstable, static rippling across her features before re-stabilizing. She did not look down.

"Hear what?" she said.

The knocking stopped.

Feimo stared at the floor. The stone was seamless, unmarked, exactly like every other section of the corridor. There was no crack, no seam, no indication that anything lay beneath it but more stone and more text and the endless, humming dark. But her ears did not lie. Something had been down there. Something had been knocking.

"Nothing," she said slowly. "Forget it."

Mia watched her for three more seconds, then turned away. "This way."

They walked through the restored corridor in something that was not silence, because the Labyrinth was never silent, but was quieter than conversation. The walls hummed around them, a steady, warm frequency that sounded almost like breathing, like the Labyrinth itself was resting. Feimo's new badge caught the light, the Bridge crest gleaming amber against her dark blazer.

The Hawthorne workers had been changed by the act of being watched. The wall fragments spoke of things preserved because they mattered, not because they were useful. And the knocking beneath the floor had not stopped.

"Mia," she said.

"What?"

"What's beneath the Academy? What state is it in?"

Mia did not slow down. "The Deep Archive. Restricted. Off-limits. Not your concern."

"But something's down there. Something was knocking."

"I didn't hear anything."

"You hesitated before you said that."

"I'm a Spirit AI. I process data. Processing takes time. It was not hesitation. It was computation." Mia's voice was perfectly level, perfectly controlled, and perfectly unconvincing. "Do not lead yourself into conclusions you cannot support with evidence."

Feimo let it go. For now.

The Bridge House dormitory was warm. That was the first thing her cat instincts registered, and the only thing that mattered after a night of cool corridors. The walls were covered in text from a dozen languages, flowing across the surface in a constant, gentle migration, each language weaving around the others without collision. The common room had couches — soft, warm, shimmering with faint Glyph-light. Feimo made a beeline for the nearest one and collapsed into it with a sound dangerously close to a purr.

"Don't even think about sleeping," Mia said, hovering above her with her arms crossed. "Your first class is in six hours. You need to review the Glyphs you collected from the Weave challenge. Your Resonance is at baseline, which means you can hold approximately twenty active Glyphs simultaneously. You currently have three. That is pathetic."

"I just got here."

"Excuses are the refuge of the unprepared. And the unprepared are the first to lose their Glyphs in a corridor collapse."

Feimo rolled onto her back and stared at the ceiling, which was also covered in drifting text, sentences from a dozen traditions flowing overhead like clouds. Somewhere in the common room, two students were trading Glyphs in low voices, holding crystalline shards up to the light and comparing their facets. The hum of the Labyrinth was gentler here, warmer, like the murmur of a conversation you could almost but not quite make out.

"Hey, Mia."

A long-suffering sigh, so perfectly rendered that it was easy to forget it came from a hologram. "*What?*"

"That sorting ceremony. When the orb was sorting me, I heard voices. They talked about literary theory. Three branches, four elements. Like a lecture. I can't seem to remember every piece of it, but the main ideas are clear."

Mia was quiet for a moment. "The orb plays fragments of the Academy's foundational texts during sorting. An orientation mechanism." She paused. "The four-element framework comes from M.H. Abrams. *The Mirror and the Lamp*, 1953. Universe, work, artist, audience — every literary theory can be mapped to one of those four centers. It's the fundamental coordinate system." Her voice had lost its edge entirely, becoming something closer to a teacher who genuinely cared about the material. She caught herself. "It's foundational knowledge. You will be tested on it. Study before you sleep."

"I don't even know if I can sleep here."

"You can. Your body is still in the real world, sitting in Study Room 4B with your face in a textbook. Your consciousness is here. When you fall asleep in the Labyrinth, your consciousness returns to your body. You will wake up at your desk." Mia's expression was unreadable. "It is the reverse of how you got in. The door swings both ways."

Feimo closed her eyes. The couch was soft. The hum was warm. The three Glyphs she had collected pulsed faintly in her awareness. The Labyrinth had chosen her, and she did not yet know why.

"Hey, Mia."

"*What now?*"

"Thank you. For the healing. For the guided tour. And for not letting me die in a collapsing corridor."

A very long silence.

Then: "The Academy cannot afford to lose a student on orientation day. It's standard protocol. Also, your Glyph reflexes are above average for a first-year. It would be a waste to let you get crushed before you've had a chance to prove whether that's real talent or just animal instinct." A pause. "Go to sleep, Feimo."

Feimo smiled into the couch cushion. Her tail curled around her ankle, warm and surprisingly comfortable, and the Bridge House badge on her lapel pulsed softly, keeping time with the hum of the walls.

As her consciousness began to dissolve, pulling back toward the real world and the cold desk in Study Room 4B, she heard it one more time. Faint. Rhythmic. Rising from somewhere deep beneath the Bridge House dormitory, from beneath the entire Academy, from the dark and unmaintained corridors of the Deep Archive where the Labyrinth's oldest text lay eroding in silence.

*Tap. Tap. Tap.*

Someone was knocking.

Feimo opened her eyes. Study Room 4B. Fluorescent lights. Cold boba tea. Her textbook was still open to "Polysemy and Context-Dependent Meaning." The clock on the wall read 3:47 AM. The cleaning auntie had not arrived yet. Nothing had changed.

On her left lapel, invisible to everyone but her, a small badge glowed amber in the shape of an arch bridge.

And from somewhere far away, so far that she might have imagined it, the knocking continued.
