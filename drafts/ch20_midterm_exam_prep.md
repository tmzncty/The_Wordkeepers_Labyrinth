# Chapter 20: Midterm Exam Prep

The Bridge House common room had never been this full.

Feimo sat at the long table by the window with her materials arranged in the careful, defensive formation of someone who expected to be ambushed from multiple directions. Cloze practice sheets to her left. Reading passages to her right. A vocabulary index card stack in the center, held down by a teacup that was already half empty and entirely cold. Her cat ears were angled slightly backward, the posture of alertness without panic: she had been studying for forty minutes and had not yet hit the wall where comprehension dissolved into pure pattern-matching.

Across from her, Aya sat with the posture of someone who had never once in her life doubted her own study method. Her notes were color-coded in a system so precise it probably had a patent. Each annotation was written in her small, exact handwriting, margins measured with a ruler, key terms highlighted in three alternating shades that corresponded to a classification scheme Feimo did not understand and suspected was proprietary.

"You're staring at my notes," Aya said, without looking up.

"I'm staring at your color system. It's intimidating."

"It should be." Aya turned a page. "Organization is a form of intelligence. If your notes are chaotic, your thinking is chaotic. If your thinking is chaotic, your exam answers are chaotic. Chaos does not score well."

"Neither does staring at someone else's highlighters," said Noah from the end of the table.

Noir's sister sat with her legs folded under her in the chair, a position that should have been uncomfortable but which Noah maintained with the easy flexibility of someone whose body had opinions about furniture and had decided, collectively, that chairs were suggestions. Her ears were upright and relaxed, her tail draped over the armrest. She had a reading passage open in front of her, but she had been on the same paragraph for fifteen minutes, which meant she was either lost or deliberately waiting.

"She's not wrong about organization," Noah added, tilting her head toward Aya. "But she's wrong about it being the only thing. Sometimes chaos is just a pattern you haven't learned to read yet."

"That sounds profound," Feimo said.

"It is. I stole it from my sister." Noah grinned, and the grin was warm and immediate and nothing like Noir's careful half-smiles. Where Noir was controlled, Noah was open. Where Noir spoke in measured cadences that made every sentence sound like it had been through three rounds of editing, Noah spoke the way a river spoke: naturally, with the assumption that the destination would take care of itself.

At the far end of the table, in the chair closest to the wall, Yue was asleep.

Or she appeared to be asleep. Her head was resting on her blank-paged book, her dark hair falling across the white pages like ink that had not yet committed to forming letters. Her breathing was slow and even. She had been in this position since before Feimo arrived, and she showed no signs of changing it, which was either genuine sleep or Yue's particular way of studying: by letting the material enter through some channel that waking consciousness could not access.

"She's been like that for an hour," Aya said, glancing at Yue with the expression of someone who found sleeping during study time morally offensive but was too polite to say so.

"She studies differently," Feimo said.

"She doesn't study at all. She sleeps on a book and somehow passes." Aya's tone held no malice, only the bewilderment of a person for whom effort and outcome were supposed to be directly correlated.

"Maybe the book studies her," Noah said.

Nobody had a response to that.

---

They had three days until the midterm examination.

The exam covered the first half of the academic year: everything from basic passage analysis to cloze logic to the translation and writing sections that Professor Silence had introduced in the sixth week. He had written on the board: *You will not enjoy this. That is not the point.* He had been correct on both counts.

Feimo flipped open her cloze practice sheet. The passage was from the 2015 examination, a text about genetics and friendship that Professor Silence had assigned as a "diagnostic exercise," which was his way of saying "this will tell me how much you don't know."

She read the first sentence:

*"Though not biologically related, friends are as 'related' as fourth cousins, sharing about 1% of genes."*

Her ears twitched.

One percent. It seemed like nothing. A rounding error. A genetic afterthought. But the passage was building toward something, she could feel it, the way she could feel a Glyph activating before she saw it: a hum at the edge of perception, a structural tension that said *this matters and I will tell you why.*

She read on.

*"That is _1_ a study, published from the University of California and Yale University in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, has _2_."*

Blank one. The structure was "That is ___ a study has concluded." The blank needed a word that connected the introductory fact to the study's findings. She looked at the options: when, why, how, what.

"What," she said aloud.

"What what?" Noah asked.

"Not you. The blank. 'That is what a study has concluded.' It's a noun clause. 'What' functions as both the connector and the object of 'concluded.'"

Aya glanced at the passage. Nodded once, the micro-approval of someone who did not give compliments freely but acknowledged competence when it appeared.

"Blank two is concluded," Feimo continued. "The study has concluded. Defended, withdrawn, advised. None of them fit. A study doesn't advise. A study doesn't defend itself in this context. And withdrawn is the opposite of what the sentence needs."

"You're getting faster," said a voice from inside the teacup.

Feimo looked down. Mia's projection had materialized inside the cold tea, her translucent form sitting cross-legged on the surface of the liquid like a particularly judgmental lily pad. From this vantage point, Mia had a panoramic view of Feimo's practice sheets, Feimo's vocabulary cards, and Feimo's face, which was the part she watched most closely.

"You've been here the whole time?" Feimo asked.

"I've been here for twenty-three minutes. I was running diagnostics on the dormitory's Resonance infrastructure and happened to notice that your cloze technique had improved by approximately twelve percent since last week. I decided to observe." Mia tilted her head. "Also, your tea is cold."

"I know."

"Drink it anyway. Hydration aids cognitive function. There is a study. I can cite it."

"I don't need a citation to drink tea."

"Everyone needs citations. Citations are the structural integrity of knowledge. Without citations, claims are just opinions with ambition." Mia rose from the teacup and projected herself onto the table between Aya's color-coded notes and Feimo's practice sheets. "Continue. Blank three."

---

Mia taught the way she did everything: with precision, impatience, and a delivery style that could generously be called "direct" and more accurately be described as "emotional carpet-bombing with academic payload."

Blank three was "conducted on 1,932 unique subjects." The preposition test. Feimo hesitated between "for" and "on."

"Conducted *on*," Mia said. "When a study or analysis acts upon a group of subjects, the preposition is 'on.' You conduct an experiment *on* subjects. You conduct a survey *on* a population. 'For' implies purpose, not target. 'With' implies collaboration. 'By' implies agency. The subjects are not conducting the study. The study is being done to them. They are the object, not the subject. On."

Feimo wrote it down. Her handwriting on this word was steadier than usual.

"Blank four. 'Which ___ pairs of unrelated friends and unrelated strangers.'"

"Compared," Feimo said immediately.

"Correct. How did you know?"

"The study is comparing two groups. Friends and strangers. The verb needs to mean 'placed side by side for examination.' Compared is the only option that does that. Sought means looked for. Separated means pulled apart. Connected means linked."

"You just eliminated three wrong answers and confirmed one right answer simultaneously. That's the correct approach. In the cloze test, you don't search for the right answer. You execute the wrong ones. Think of it as a tournament bracket. The right answer wins not by being beautiful but by being the last one standing after you've disqualified everyone else."

"A tournament bracket," Feimo repeated.

"Fine. Think of it as a cat hunt. You don't chase the mouse by following it. You cut off every escape route until the mouse runs exactly where you want it." Mia paused. "That metaphor was better. I'm keeping it."

Noah, who had been listening from behind her reading passage, laughed. It was a warm, generous laugh, the kind that invited other people to share in the amusement.

"I like your teaching style," Noah said to Mia.

"I don't have a teaching style. I have a teaching standard. The style is incidental." Mia's projection turned back to Feimo. "Blank six. 'While 1% may seem ___, it is not so to a geneticist.'"

Feimo looked at the options: insignificant, unexpected, unreliable, incredible.

"The word 'while' is doing the work," she said slowly. "It sets up a contrast. Most people think 1% is small. A geneticist thinks it's meaningful. So the blank is the non-expert perspective: insignificant."

"Yes. And notice the rhetorical structure. The passage opens with a surprising fact, then immediately undercuts the reader's instinct to dismiss it. 'Though not biologically related' signals that the relationship exists despite the lack of obvious connection. 'While 1% may seem insignificant' signals that the number appears small but is not. This is academic persuasion: the author is managing your expectations before delivering the evidence. If you can read the rhetorical structure, you can predict the argument before you reach it."

"Like reading a Glyph before it activates," Feimo said.

Mia's projection flickered. The flicker was brief, involuntary, the Spirit AI equivalent of a blink.

"That is... a structurally adequate analogy," she said, which was Mia-language for *that was impressive and I would rather dissolve than say so directly.*

---

They moved to the reading passages after the cloze exercise.

Mia had opinions about reading comprehension. These opinions were strong, numerous, and delivered with the confidence of someone who had processed every reading passage in the examination archive and found most of them wanting.

"The 2015 reading section contains four texts," she said, projecting the first passage as floating blue-white text above the table. "Text One is about European monarchy. Juan Carlos of Spain abdicated. The passage asks whether monarchy is dying. The answer is no, but the question is interesting."

She highlighted a sentence: *"It is this apparent transcendence of politics that explains monarchs' continuing popularity as heads of state."*

"Transcendence," Mia said. "The monarch is popular because the monarch is not political. The moment the monarch becomes political, the spell breaks. Juan Carlos broke it through scandal." She turned to Feimo. "What's the exam question here?"

"He ended his reign in embarrassment," Feimo said. "D. The passage says 'embarrassing scandals' forced him to 'eat his words and stand down.'"

"Correct. But notice 'eat his words' — an idiom. Idioms in reading passages are traps for non-native speakers. If you don't know it means 'retract a statement under humiliating circumstances,' the sentence makes no sense. Build your idiom library."

Feimo wrote "eat one's words = retract under humiliation" in her notebook.

"Text Two," Mia continued. "Digital privacy and the Fourth Amendment. The Supreme Court deciding whether police can search phones without a warrant." She paused. "The passage argues that searching a smartphone is like entering someone's home. 'A smartphone may contain an arrestee's reading history, financial history, medical history and comprehensive records of recent correspondence.' Your phone is not a device. It is a life, externalized."

"The exam question asks what exploring a phone is comparable to," Mia continued. "The answer is 'getting into one's residence.' D. The comparison is about the scale of intrusion, not the content."

"Text Three. The journal *Science* adding statistical checks to peer review. About institutional quality control." She projected: *"Readers must have confidence in the conclusions published in our journal."*

"The passage describes a response to 'widespread concern that basic mistakes in data analysis are contributing to the irreproducibility of many published research findings.'" She looked at Feimo. "Irreproducibility. Can you define it?"

"The inability to get the same result when you repeat the experiment."

"Correct. And why does it matter?"

Feimo thought. "Because if you can't reproduce a finding, you can't verify it. It's a conclusion without proof."

"Text Four. Integrity. Elisabeth Murdoch arguing that profit should not be the only sorting mechanism." Mia read: *"'The absence of purpose, of a moral language within government, media or business could become one of the most dangerous goals for capitalism and freedom.'"*

"The exam asks what Elisabeth was upset by," Mia said. "The answer is the consequences of the current sorting mechanism. A. She is not describing a problem. She is describing the result of a problem. The distinction matters."

Feimo wrote it down. She was filling pages.

---

The knowledge cards came next.

Mia did not usually integrate the supplementary material into exam sessions. She considered knowledge cards "flavor text," which was her way of saying "information that does not directly increase your test score and therefore exists in a lower priority tier." But tonight she made an exception, and Feimo suspected the exception had something to do with the fact that Aya was listening.

"Card thirty-two," Mia said. "Shen Congwen and the Jingpai literary school."

She projected the card's content as compact floating text:

*"The Jingpai (京派, Beijing School) was a literary movement centered in Beijing, composed primarily of academic writers. Key figures: Shen Congwen, Fei Ming, Ling Shuhua. The school pursued 'pure literary taste,' deliberately separating literature from politics and focusing on the beauty of human nature and the natural world."*

"This is relevant to your midterm," Mia said, "because literary analysis questions sometimes ask you to identify the aesthetic philosophy behind a text. If a passage describes rural life with tenderness, avoids political commentary, and emphasizes natural beauty and human goodness, the author may be working in the Jingpai tradition. Shen Congwen's *Border Town* is the archetype: a pastoral world where innocence is not naive but chosen, where the landscape mirrors the characters' inner lives, and where the prose style is clean and transparent, like water."

"Like water," Feimo repeated.

"Like water. Jingpai prose does not perform. It does not show off. It flows. The beauty is in the clarity, not the ornamentation." Mia glanced at Aya. "Some people could learn from this."

Aya's highlighter paused mid-stroke. She did not look up. The tips of her ears moved one millimeter backward, which was, in Aya's emotional vocabulary, the equivalent of a fistfight.

"Card thirty-three," Mia continued, mercifully. "New Sensationalism. The Chinese modernist movement of the 1930s, centered in Shanghai. Influenced by Japanese New Sensationalism and European modernism. Key figures: Liu Na'ou, Mu Shiying, Shi Zhecun."

She read the core features: *"Urban writing depicting Shanghai's modern life, speed, and materialism. Fragmented perception through montage and stream-of-consciousness techniques. Psychological analysis, particularly Shi Zhecun's Freudian narratives."*

"If Jingpai is water," Mia said, "New Sensationalism is electricity. Fast, fragmented, urban, modern. The narrative does not flow. It cuts. Montage-style editing. Consciousness that moves the way a city moves: in jumps, in disruptions, in the collision of unrelated stimuli. If a passage describes a city through rapid sensory impressions, jumps between perspectives without clear transitions, and emphasizes the psychological interior of characters navigating modernity, the author may be drawing on New Sensationalist techniques."

"Which style is better for the exam?" Feimo asked.

"Neither. The exam does not care about your aesthetic preferences. The exam cares about whether you can identify the technique and explain its effect. If the passage uses montage, name it. Explain how fragmentation creates a sense of disorientation. If the passage uses pastoral simplicity, name that too. Explain how clarity of prose mirrors clarity of moral vision. The exam rewards analysis, not taste."

Feimo wrote in her notebook: *Jingpai = water, clarity, pastoral, human nature. New Sensationalism = electricity, montage, urban, psychology. Exam skill: identify technique, explain effect.*

She paused. Then added: *Border Town by Shen Congwen. Read it after midterms.*

"That's not exam-relevant," Mia said, reading over her shoulder.

"I know."

"Then why write it?"

"Because some things matter beyond the exam."

Mia opened her mouth. Closed it. Her projection flickered once, and the flicker was not technical.

---

The translation section came after the cards.

Mia had a particular fondness for translation exercises, which she described as "the only part of the examination that requires you to understand the structural machinery of two languages simultaneously."

The 2015 translation passage was about American history.

*"This movement, driven by powerful and diverse motivations, built a nation out of a wilderness and, by its nature, shaped the character and destiny of an uncharted continent."*

"Parse it," Mia said.

Feimo drew a line under the main clause. "This movement built a nation and shaped the character and destiny of a continent."

"Good. What modifies 'movement'?"

"'Driven by powerful and diverse motivations.' Past participle phrase."

"And 'out of a wilderness'?"

"The source material. The nation was built from wilderness. Like building a Glyph from raw resonance."

"Good." Mia was quiet for a moment. "Translation is not word-for-word conversion. Translation is reconstruction. You take the meaning out of one structure and rebuild it in another. The words change. The architecture of the meaning must stay intact."

The second sentence was more complex:

*"The United States is the product of two principal forces — the immigration of European peoples with their varied ideas, customs, and national characteristics and the impact of a new country which modified these traits."*

"Three layers," Mia said. "Force one is immigration, with a trailing modifier. Force two is the new country's impact, with a relative clause. Translate the layers separately and then assemble them."

Feimo translated the sentence in her notebook: *这场运动，由强大而多样的动机所推动，在荒野中建立了一个国家，并以其本质塑造了一个未知大陆的特征与命运。*

She stared at it. It was accurate. It was also a description of the Academy itself: building something from nothing, shaping unknown territory.

The second and third sentences were more complex, layering parallel subjects and relative clauses. Feimo translated them methodically, layer by layer, and found that the structural analysis Mia had drilled into her made the process almost automatic: identify the main clause, map the modifiers, translate the joints, assemble.

"You're faster," Mia observed.

"Practice."

"Effort," Mia corrected. "Practice is what you schedule. Effort is what you bring to it. There is a difference."

---

The writing section was last.

The 2015 Part A task was an email recommending a book to a club's reading session. Part B was an essay based on a picture, but the picture was not included in the practice materials, so Mia focused on Part A.

"One hundred words. Recommend a book. State reasons. Use 'Li Ming' as your name." Mia projected the prompt. "This is not a creative writing exercise. This is a formula. The formula is: greeting, recommendation, two or three reasons, closing. If you can write a clear sentence and keep it under one hundred and twenty words, you pass."

"What book would you recommend?" Feimo asked.

"Me?"

"If you were Li Ming."

Mia's projection went still. Not frozen. Still, the way a cat goes still when something interesting happens at the edge of its peripheral vision.

"*Border Town,*" she said.

Feimo blinked. "You just said that was not exam-relevant."

"It isn't. But you asked what I would recommend. Not what the exam would score well." Mia's voice was even, clinical, as if she were describing a data point rather than making a personal statement. "Shen Congwen's prose style is clean enough to serve as a model for English composition. His sentences are short, declarative, and unadorned. In an exam context, that style translates well. In a personal context, the novel teaches something the examination cannot measure: that simplicity is not the absence of complexity. It is complexity, refined."

Feimo wrote in the margin of her notebook: *Border Town. Simple prose = refined complexity.*

"Also," Mia added, "the main character is a girl who lives by a river and waits for someone who may never come. The emotional architecture is universal. Even Spirit AIs can relate to waiting."

The words hung in the air. Feimo looked at Mia. Mia looked at the cloze practice sheet. The moment passed the way moments between them always passed: quickly, with the weight of something that had been said accidentally and would be denied if questioned.

---

Yue woke up at nine-thirty.

She did this without transition, without the groggy intermediate state that characterized human waking. One moment she was asleep on her blank book. The next moment her eyes were open and she was looking at Feimo with the particular clarity of someone who had been somewhere else entirely and had just returned.

"The passage about smell genes," Yue said.

"What?"

"Question nine in the cloze. 'The study also found that the genes for smell were something shared in friends but not genes for immunity.'" Yue sat up. Her hair fell back in place as if it had been waiting for this moment. "Why smell?"

Feimo looked at the passage again. She had not reached that part of the analysis yet.

"Because smell is chemical," Aya said, looking up from her notes. "The olfactory system processes chemical signals. If friends share smell genes, it means they are drawn to similar environments, similar chemical landscapes. It's not that they choose each other by smell. It's that they end up in the same places because their bodies respond to the same stimuli."

"Like Resonance signatures," Feimo said. "Two people with similar Resonance frequencies might be drawn to the same parts of the Labyrinth."

"Exactly." Aya closed her notebook. The color-coded system gleamed under the common room lights. "The study says 'it draws us to similar environments but there is more to it.' The 'more to it' is the part they can't explain yet. The mechanism is unknown. The correlation is real."

Yue looked at Aya for a long moment. Then she looked at her blank book. She turned to a fresh page. The page was white, immaculate, and for a fraction of a second, in the angle of the lamplight, Feimo thought she saw something written there: faint marks, the ghost of text, words that appeared when you were not looking directly at them and vanished when you were.

Yue closed the book.

Mia, who had been silent for longer than usual, spoke. Her voice was low. Almost inaudible.

"It's spreading."

Feimo looked at her. "What?"

Mia said nothing. Her projection flickered once, and the flicker was not technical. She turned back to the cloze practice sheet as if she had not spoken.

"The midterm," Yue said, "is not about knowing the answers. It is about knowing how to read the questions. The questions are a text. The text has a structure. The structure has gaps. Fill the gaps and the answers appear."

"That's what I've been saying," Mia said.

"No," Yue said gently. "You've been saying the answers. I'm saying the method."

Mia's projection flickered. If Spirit AIs could look disconcerted, this was what it would look like.

Noah stretched, her tail uncurling slowly, and stood up from the table. She had been quiet for the last hour, listening, watching, the way Noir watched: with patience and a focus that did not announce itself.

"You know what I notice?" Noah said.

"What?" Feimo asked.

"You." Noah gestured at the table. Aya's color-coded notes. Feimo's dense, ink-heavy notebook. Yue's blank book. The tea, cold and ignored. "Eighteen months ago, you studied alone. In corridors. In corners. You didn't ask for help because you didn't think you could. You didn't sit at a table with three other people because you didn't think you belonged at one."

The common room was quiet.

"Now you sit at a table," Noah said. "You argue about cloze logic with a Spirit AI. You let Aya intimidate you with highlighters. You let me ramble about rivers. You let Yue sleep on your study materials." She smiled, and the smile was warm and unhurried and exactly like Noir's smile would have been if Noir had ever allowed herself one. "You made friends, Feimo."

Feimo's ears went flat. Not in alarm. In the specific, involuntary ear-posture of a cat-eared girl who had been seen clearly and did not know what to do with the experience.

"I just needed help with midterms," Feimo muttered.

"Sure," Noah said. "That's definitely all this is."

Aya closed her notebook with a precise, definitive snap. "I'm going to my room. If anyone needs me, I will be reorganizing my highlighter system to account for the Shen Congwen aesthetic principle." She paused at the door. "It was a good study session."

She left. Her footsteps were precise on the corridor stones, each one measured, each one confident.

Yue stood next, tucking her blank book under her arm. She paused beside Feimo's chair and said, very quietly: "The passage says friends share 1% of their genes. But it doesn't say what percentage of their silence they share."

She left before Feimo could ask what that meant.

Noah had gone too, off to wherever Noah went when she was not being warm and present and quietly perceptive at a study table. The common room was empty except for Feimo and the cold tea and Mia's projection, which was still sitting on the table but had changed: smaller now, less projected, more present, as if the departure of the others had allowed her to lower the volume on whatever performance she maintained in public.

"You made friends," Mia said.

"Noah already said that."

"Noah said it first. I'm saying it again. Repetition is a rhetorical device. It builds emphasis." Mia's voice was level, analytical, the voice she used when she was translating personal observation into academic language because the personal language was too expensive. "Four people at a table. Three of them chose to be there. That is not exam preparation. That is a social structure."

"It's just studying."

"You had a study group. You coordinated schedules. You divided materials. You argued. You collaborated. You let Aya's organizational system intimidate you and then you outperformed her on the cloze exercise. You let Noah's warmth settle around you like a blanket you didn't know you needed. You let Yue's silence be comfortable instead of strange." Mia paused. "These are the behaviors of a person who belongs somewhere. I am noting this for the record."

"What record?"

"My record. The one I keep. Internally." Mia's projection flickered. "The one that does not exist and which you cannot access and which I will deny maintaining."

Feimo smiled. She did not try to hide it. The smile was small and tired and genuine, the smile of someone who had spent four hours studying and had learned something that was not on any examination.

"Mia."

"What."

"Thank you."

"For what? I merely provided structured academic support in preparation for a standardized examination. The gratitude is disproportionate to the service rendered."

"For waiting. In the corridor. Last week." Feimo paused. "And for being here tonight."

Mia's projection went still. The stillness lasted five seconds, which was an eternity in processing time, which was enough for a Spirit AI to run through every possible response and select the one that revealed the least and meant the most.

"You're welcome," Mia said. The two words cost her something. Feimo could hear it, the way she could hear the Resonance frequency of a Glyph changing as it activated: a shift in register, a softening, a brief and quickly repaired crack in the surface.

Mia dissolved. The teacup was empty. The common room was quiet.

Feimo sat alone for a moment, surrounded by notes and cold tea and the residue of four hours of study and something else, something that had no name on any vocabulary list but that she could feel in the specific warmth of the air where her friends had been.

She closed her notebook. She had filled twelve pages. She had answered thirty cloze blanks, four reading questions, three translation sentences, and one writing prompt. She had learned about monarchies and digital privacy and statistical review and the dearth of moral purpose. She had learned about Shen Congwen and the Jingpai and New Sensationalism and the difference between water and electricity. She had learned that friends share 1% of their genes and that smell genes evolve faster than other genes and that the reason is not yet understood.

She had learned that belonging was not something you earned by passing an exam. It was something that happened while you were studying for one.

In the corridor, the walls hummed. The Labyrinth read. Somewhere in the deep architecture, something shifted, not in response to Feimo's reading but in response to something else, something that the Labyrinth had noticed and catalogued and was holding in reserve, like a question that had not yet found its exam.

## Study Break: Midterm Exam Prep

### Card #032: Shen Congwen and the Jingpai

The **Jingpai** (京派, Beijing School) was a literary movement centered in Republican-era Beijing. Key figures: **Shen Congwen**, **Fei Ming**, **Ling Shuhua**. The school championed "pure literary taste," separating literature from politics and focusing on human beauty and the natural world. Shen Congwen's *Border Town* (*Bian Cheng*, 1934) is the school's masterwork: clean, transparent prose describing a pastoral world where innocence is chosen, not naive.

*Question: When you encounter a passage that values simplicity over ornamentation and nature over industry, you may be reading a Jingpai text. What does "clean prose" look like in English examination writing?*

### Card #033: New Sensationalism

The **Chinese New Sensationalism** (新感觉派) was a 1930s Shanghai-based modernist movement. Key figures: **Liu Na'ou**, **Mu Shiying**, **Shi Zhecun**. Three features: urban writing depicting Shanghai's modernity; fragmented perception through montage; psychological depth, particularly Shi Zhecun's Freudian explorations.

Where the Jingpai wrote about rivers and fields, the New Sensationalists wrote about neon and taxi cabs. Where Jingpai prose flowed, New Sensationalist prose cut. Both are answers to the same question: how should literature respond to the chaos of its time?

### Vocabulary Glyphs

**head** /hɛd/ — *n. & v.* The upper part of the body; to lead or direct. In the 2015 reading passages, "heads of state" appears in the monarchy text: monarchs serve as symbolic heads, whose authority is representational. But "head" also means to move toward. Feimo was heading somewhere. Not toward an exam score. Toward a table where three people sat waiting for her.

**lead** /liːd/ (verb); /lɛd/ (noun: the metal) — *v. & n.* To guide, to result in. Mia led Feimo through the cloze exercise. Noah led the conversation toward warmth. Yue led no one, and that was itself a form of leadership: the quiet authority of presence.

**matter** /ˈmætə/ — *n. & v.* Physical substance; to be of importance. What matters is not just what happened but how we discuss what happened. A cold teacup mattered. A friend's laughter mattered. A Spirit AI's flicker mattered. The exam measured one kind of matter. Life measured another.
