# Chapter 40: The War Ends

The last wave came at dawn.

Feimo had been fighting for seven hours. Her hands were cut, her left knee was swollen from a fall on the stairwell, and her throat was raw from shouting instructions to students who could not hear her over the sound of walls breaking. The Labyrinth's third level had become a warzone: stone dust in the air, the phosphorescent text on the walls flickering like dying embers, and the Fragmenters moving through the corridors in formations that were wrong, that had been wrong since the invasion began three weeks ago, because Fragmenters did not form formations and yet here they were, moving in coordinated lines toward the Deep Archive entrance.

She stood in the corridor outside the Archive and counted her allies. Noir was twenty meters ahead, positioned behind a collapsed pillar, her coat stained with the iridescent residue that Fragmenters left when they were dispersed. Aya was on the east wall, her breathing controlled, her eyes tracking the movement patterns with the focused intensity of someone who had spent years learning to read opponents. Yue was somewhere Feimo could not see, which meant Yue was where Yue needed to be.

And Mia.

Mia was beside her. Not leaning, not posing, not wrapping her tail around her thigh in the posture of analytical detachment. She was standing with her weight forward, her ears flat against her skull, her fingers curled. She looked like what she was: a creature prepared to fight, and behind the readiness, a calculation that had not stopped running since the alarm sounded.

"Fourteen," Mia said. "Maybe fifteen. They're coming in pulses now, not streams. Something is coordinating them."

"Can you identify the source?"

"No. The signal is embedded in the text field itself. It's not external. It's coming from inside the walls."

Inside the walls. Feimo looked at the corridor around her, at the damaged text that still clung to the stone in fragments and patches. The Labyrinth's knowledge system was not merely a library. It was an ecology, a living network of interconnected inscriptions that responded to reading, to attention, to the presence of minds that engaged with them. The Fragmenters fed on this text. They consumed it, metabolized it, broke it down into the raw informational substrate from which new, chaotic patterns emerged. But what Mia was describing was different. Something in the text field itself had become a vector. The knowledge was not merely being consumed. It was being turned.

"How?" Feimo said.

"I don't know. I've never seen the text field operate as a weapon. It's always been passive. Reactive. It responds to readers. It doesn't attack them."

A crash from ahead. Noir shouted something that was lost in the noise. The wall to the left of the Archive entrance buckled, a section of plaster the size of a door detaching and falling in a cascade of dust and fragments. Behind it, the raw stone was visible, and on the stone, a text that Feimo had never seen before: not the Labyrinth's usual bilingual inscriptions but something else, something in a script that shifted as she watched, resolving briefly into English before dissolving into characters she could not read before cycling back to English again:

*"For consciousness to evolve, a brain with a threshold level of complexity and capacity is required."*

She read it aloud, and as she read, the Fragmenters in the corridor paused.

Not stopped. Paused. The way an animal pauses when it hears a sound it cannot categorize.

"Say that again," Mia said.

Feimo repeated the sentence. The Fragmenters paused again, for a half-second longer.

"They're responding to the text," Mia said. Her voice had changed: the analytical engine behind it had accelerated, the words coming faster as the model in her mind processed new data. "Not consuming it. Responding to it. The way you respond to it. Feimo, the passage about plant consciousness. The one from the exam you haven't taken yet. Do you know it?"

Feimo knew it. She had seen the data packet that Professor Silence had distributed three days ago: the 2022 exam materials, packaged in the Academy's standard format, waiting in her room for the test that the invasion had postponed. She had not studied it formally, but she had read the cloze passage in the way she read everything now, not for answers but for structure. The passage was about the debate over whether plants possess consciousness, about the firing of electrical signals in stems and leaves, about the argument that such signals hinted at awareness, and about the counterargument that consciousness required a threshold level of neural complexity that plants did not have.

"The argument," Feimo said, "is that electrical signaling is not the same as consciousness. That superficial resemblance does not imply functional equivalence. Plants communicate through electrical signals, but the signaling is only superficially similar to the firing in a complex animal brain."

"And the counterargument?"

"That consciousness requires a threshold. A level of complexity below which signaling is merely mechanical."

Mia looked at the Fragmenters. They had resumed their advance, but more slowly now, as if the pause had disrupted the rhythm that the text field had imposed on them.

"What if the Labyrinth is the plant?" Mia said.

The sentence landed in Feimo's mind and rearranged what she understood.

The Labyrinth was an ecology. Its walls were covered in text that responded to reading, that glowed with the residual energy of centuries of attention. It was complex, distributed, interconnected. But it did not have a brain. It did not have a center. Its intelligence, if it had intelligence, was the intelligence of a forest: emergent, decentralized, the product of a million interactions between individual components, none of which possessed awareness but which, taken together, produced behavior that looked like awareness from the outside.

The Fragmenters were feeding on the Labyrinth the way insects feed on a tree. The tree could not run away. It could not plan a defense. But it could produce chemicals that made its leaves unpalatable. It could redirect resources to its roots. It could, through mechanisms that no one would call conscious, respond to the attack in ways that changed the terms of the engagement.

"The text field is fighting back," Feimo said. "Not consciously. Mechanically. The way a plant fights back. It's producing responses that disrupt the Fragmenters' feeding patterns."

"Then we don't need to fight them," Mia said. "We need to read to them."

---

They read for forty minutes.

Feimo stood in the corridor with her back against the damaged wall and read aloud. She read the cloze passage in its entirety, every sentence, every blank, every answer. She read about the idea that plants have some degree of consciousness, about the term "plant neurobiology" that had been coined around the notion that some aspects of plant behavior could be compared to intelligence in animals. She read about the counterargument: that plant biology differs so greatly from that of animals that so-called evidence of plants' intelligence is inconclusive. She read about the scientists who had argued that plants possess neuron-like cells, and about the perspective that this argument made sense only if you reduced the workings of a complex brain to an array of electrical pulses.

Mia read beside her. Not the same texts. Mia read the texts she had memorized over months of immersion in the Labyrinth's system: passages about information theory, about ecological resilience, about the difference between distributed and centralized networks. Her voice was steady, precise, each word placed with the care of someone who understood that the act of reading was, in this place, an act of power.

The Fragmenters responded.

Not by retreating. Not by dissolving. By changing their formation. The coordinated lines broke apart. The pulsing rhythm in the text field stuttered and shifted. The Fragmenters began to move individually again, chaotically, without pattern, the way they had moved before the invasion began. Whatever had been coordinating them was losing its grip. The Labyrinth's text field, stimulated by the reading, was overwhelming the signal that had turned it into a weapon.

"They're disaggregating," Mia said. "The coordinating signal is weakening. Keep reading."

Feimo kept reading. She moved from the cloze passage to the reading texts, because they were there, because she had absorbed them in the way she absorbed everything the Labyrinth offered, and because the act of reading them aloud in this corridor felt not like recitation but like intervention. She read about plastics: about the museums trying to preserve culturally important objects, about the polymer chemist who called her stabilizing chemicals "sunscreens," about the time bombs that careless artists had created and the conservators who worked to defuse them. She read the sentence that stayed with her: *"The object you make is already a time bomb."*

She thought: the Labyrinth is a time bomb. Every inscription on these walls is a fuse. And the Fragmenters are the flame.

She read about Generation Z, about the devaluation of degrees, about the promise that education would set you up for life and the reality that the promise had been found wanting. She read: *"Education was no longer a secure route of social mobility."* She thought of the Academy, of Cain's security framework, of the way the institution had been hollowed out by the very measures designed to protect it. The students who came here for knowledge had been diverted into compliance. The Labyrinth that should have been a route to understanding had become a route to surveillance. And the Fragmenters, the actual threat, had found their way in anyway.

She read about art-science collaborations, about the Sydney Symphony Orchestra performing Vivaldi's Four Seasons reimagined with climate prediction data. She read: *"Artists help scientists reach a broader audience and make emotional connections that enhance learning."* And she thought of Mia, who was not a scientist and not an artist but something else entirely, something the Academy's categories could not contain, and whose voice beside her now was doing exactly what the passage described: making an emotional connection that enhanced learning, not by explaining the text but by reading it with her, in the same corridor, at the same time, their voices alternating and occasionally overlapping in a rhythm that was not coordinated but was synchronized, the way two musicians who have played together for years can synchronize without a conductor.

The last Fragmenter dispersed at 6:47 AM.

It happened quietly: a ripple in the text field, a momentary brightening of the inscriptions on the nearest wall, and then the creature came apart, its form dissolving into the informational substrate from which it had assembled, leaving behind a residue that glowed faintly before fading to nothing.

The corridor was silent.

Feimo lowered her hands. They were shaking. Not from fear. From the sustained effort of reading aloud for forty minutes in a space where reading had consequences, where the act of shaping air into words could alter the behavior of entities that should not have existed but did.

Mia sat down on the floor.

She sat the way a structure settles when the load it has been bearing is suddenly removed: slowly, all at once, with the particular grace of exhaustion. Her back against the wall, her legs extended, her tail curled around her hip. She was breathing hard, her chest rising and falling in a rhythm that Feimo recognized as the aftermath of sustained adrenaline, the body's accounting system catching up with what it had spent.

"It's over," Mia said.

"The wave is over. I don't know about the war."

"The war is over." Mia's voice was flat, certain. "The coordinating signal is gone. Whatever was organizing them into formations has been disrupted. They'll go back to being individual feeders. Dangerous, but not catastrophic."

Feimo looked at the corridor. The damage was extensive. Plaster torn from walls, stone exposed, inscriptions broken and scattered. The Archive entrance was intact but scarred, the doorframe cracked, the carved characters damaged. She would need to assess the Archive itself, to determine how much of the Deep Labyrinth's knowledge had been consumed and how much had survived in fragments that could be mapped and reassembled.

But that was later. That was the work of weeks and months.

She looked at Mia.

"Can you stand?"

"In a minute."

Feimo waited. She sat down beside Mia, their shoulders almost touching, and looked at the ceiling, which was cracked in three places but still holding. She thought of the cloze passage: *"Since plants don't have nervous systems, the chances that they have consciousness are effectively zero."* And then the article's final argument, the one about evolutionary strategy: plants cannot run away from danger, so investing energy in a body system that recognizes threats and can feel pain would be a very poor evolutionary strategy.

She thought: the Labyrinth cannot run away either. It has no nervous system in the way animals do. Its responses to the Fragmenters were not conscious but mechanical, the product of complex interactions between distributed components. And yet those responses had saved them. Not because the Labyrinth had decided to fight back, but because the act of reading had activated something in the text field's architecture that was always there, a defense mechanism embedded in the structure of the knowledge itself.

Not consciousness. Something more useful. Resilience.

"Okay," Mia said. She put her hand on the wall and pushed herself up. Her movements were slower than usual, less precise, the controlled economy of motion that was her default giving way to something more human. More tired. She stood and looked at Feimo, and in her eyes there was something that Feimo had been watching develop for months, through phases that could be numbered but not named: the gradual, unwilling, irreversible process by which Mia had allowed another person to see her without the mask.

"We should check the others," Mia said.

"Give me your hand."

Mia looked at Feimo's outstretched hand. She looked at it for a long time, in the way she looked at everything that surprised her: not with surprise but with analysis, as if the surprise had triggered a rapid reassessment of probabilities.

She took it.

---

They found Noir in the east corridor, uninjured, sitting on a collapsed section of wall and drinking from a flask that smelled of copper and something sweet. Beside her was a canvas bag with bread and canned peaches — provisions she had salvaged from the cafeteria before the last wave hit. She looked at Feimo and Mia's joined hands and said nothing, which was itself a statement, because Noir always had something to say and the absence of commentary meant she had decided this was not a moment for words. She pushed the bag toward them with her foot.

"Eat," she said. "You look terrible."

Aya was in the stairwell, her back against the railing, her eyes closed, positioned where she could see both the corridor entrance and the Archive door. She opened them when Feimo approached and said, "How many waves?"

"Three. The last one lasted forty minutes."

"That matches what I counted. The formations broke twenty minutes ago."

"They broke when we read to them."

Aya opened her eyes fully. She looked at Feimo with the expression that Feimo had come to regard as Aya's version of respect: not warmth, not admiration, but the acknowledgment that something had happened that Aya's model of the world had not predicted.

"You read to them," Aya said.

"The Labyrinth's text field responded to the reading. The way a plant responds to damage. Not consciously. Mechanically. But effectively."

Aya was quiet for a moment. Then she said: "The passage about plant consciousness. The one in the exam materials."

"Yes."

"The argument is that plants lack the neural complexity for consciousness. That their responses are mechanical, not intentional."

"Yes."

"And you're saying the Labyrinth is like a plant."

"I'm saying the Labyrinth's responses are like a plant's responses. Distributed. Unconscious. But adaptive. And when stimulated by reading, the responses become strong enough to overwhelm the coordinating signal that was directing the Fragmenters."

Aya nodded slowly. "Then the question is not whether the Labyrinth is conscious. The question is whether consciousness is necessary."

Feimo thought of the passage. *"For consciousness to evolve, a brain with a threshold level of complexity and capacity is required."* The Labyrinth did not have a brain. It had walls and text and readers. It had centuries of accumulated attention, layered into the stone like sediment, and when that attention was activated by the act of reading, it produced effects that no one had predicted because no one had thought to ask what a library does when it is attacked.

Not what a librarian does. What the library itself does.

---

They met Professor Silence in the War Room.

The name had always been ironic. The War Room was Cain's territory: the windowless box on the third floor with its eleven monitors and its four security personnel and its permanent smell of electronics and fresh paint. Professor Silence's territory was the seminar room, the library, the quiet spaces where knowledge was made rather than monitored. But the monitors were dark now. The security personnel were gone. Cain was somewhere in the building, directing the post-battle assessment with the efficiency of an administrator who understood that the appearance of control was more important than control itself, and the War Room had been abandoned to its original purpose: a room where people sat and thought about what had happened and what to do next.

Silence was standing at the window. The curtains were drawn, which was unusual. He turned when they entered and looked at each of them in turn: Feimo, Mia, Noir, Aya, and Yue, who had appeared from somewhere silent and was now sitting in the corner with her knees drawn up, her eyes on the middle distance.

"You found it," Silence said.

"The plant defense mechanism," Feimo said. "The text field's response to reading."

"I didn't find it. I discovered it operates. The mechanism was always there. The Labyrinth is not a building. It is an organism. Not a conscious one. A distributed one. The text on its walls is not decoration. It is tissue. When the tissue is damaged, it responds. When it is read to, it strengthens."

Silence looked at Feimo for a long time.

"You are the first person in thirty years to read to the Labyrinth during an attack," he said. "Everyone else fought. You read."

"Fighting wasn't working."

"Neither was anything else. The Fragmenters' coordination was unprecedented. They were receiving a signal from inside the text field itself, a signal that turned the Labyrinth's knowledge against its own structure. A parasitic signal. We had no protocol for that."

"And reading was the protocol?"

"Reading is always the protocol. Reading is how the Labyrinth maintains itself. Every act of reading is an act of repair. Every reader is a node in the system's immune response. But no one reads during a battle. No one has ever thought to try."

Silence moved from the window to the table. He placed his hands flat on the surface, the gesture of someone about to deliver a conclusion that had been forming for a long time.

"Cain will say his security measures were vindicated. He will say the invasion proved the need for surveillance, for restriction, for the framework he has spent three years building. He will point to the damage and say: this is what happens when the Academy is not protected."

He paused.

"And he will be wrong. The invasion happened because of his measures, not despite them. The parasitic signal entered the text field through the surveillance nodes Cain installed. His cameras, his sensors, his monitoring equipment. They were embedded in the Labyrinth's walls. The signal used them as vectors. Cain's security infrastructure was the weapon that nearly destroyed the thing it was supposed to protect."

The room was silent.

Feimo thought of the passage from the exam about New Zealand's Employment Relations Act. The personal grievance provisions that were designed to protect ordinary workers but that, paradoxically, placed those workers at risk when applied to managers. Laws introduced to protect jobs that ended up threatening jobs. She thought: Cain's security measures were a personal grievance provision for the Academy. Designed to protect. Implemented to control. And the effect, paradoxically, was to create the vulnerability that the Fragmenters exploited.

She read the passage in her mind: *"Consequently, and paradoxically, laws introduced to protect the jobs of ordinary workers may be placing those jobs at risk."* She thought: substitute "security measures" for "laws" and "knowledge" for "jobs." The sentence held.

"Cain doesn't know this yet," Silence said.

"No."

"He will. I will tell him. Not because I want to, but because the Academy's survival requires that the next decision be made with complete information. Cain made his decisions with incomplete information. I will not repeat his error."

He looked at the wall. On the monitors, now dark, the corridors of the Labyrinth were visible as floor plans, and the damage was marked with red annotations that someone had entered before the power was cut. The red covered most of levels three and four. It extended into level five in patches. Levels six and below were unmarked: not because they were undamaged but because the sensors had not reached them.

"The exam," Silence said. "It was scheduled for next week. Given the damage, it will be postponed."

"No," Feimo said.

Silence looked at her.

"We should take it on schedule. The Labyrinth needs to be read to. Every act of reading is repair. An exam is the largest coordinated reading event in the Academy's calendar. Two hundred students, reading for three hours. The text field will respond."

Mia spoke for the first time since they entered the room. "You want to use the exam as a defense mechanism."

"I want to use the exam as what it has always been. A form of engagement. The Labyrinth doesn't distinguish between reading for knowledge and reading for repair. Every reader strengthens the system. Two hundred readers, simultaneously, for three hours, reading everything the Labyrinth has to offer. That's not an exam. That's an immune response."

Silence was quiet for a long time.

"You remind me of someone," he said. "Someone who once sat in this room and said something very similar, about a different Labyrinth, in a different crisis. That was thirty years ago. The person was wrong about many things. But they were right about this: the Labyrinth is not protected by walls or cameras or security committees. It is protected by reading."

He straightened.

"The exam will proceed on schedule. I will inform Cain. You will inform the students."

---

The exam was administered on a Tuesday.

The room was a section of the Archive's antechamber: two walls of the original exam room had been damaged, and the replacement space had a higher ceiling, with light from the text on the walls instead of the fluorescents that no longer worked. The cameras were gone. Silence had ordered them removed. Their mounting brackets remained, empty metal circles on the stone, the ghosts of surveillance.

Two hundred students sat in rows that followed the Labyrinth's geometry. Some were injured: bandaged hands, bruised faces. Some looked afraid. Most looked tired. All had pencils and booklets and the particular expression of people about to do something ordinary in a situation that was not ordinary at all.

They had taken the 2022 exam two months ago, before the invasion. The cloze about plant consciousness. The four reading texts: plastics preservation, the value of degrees, art-science collaboration, New Zealand employment law. They knew the passages. They had filled in the blanks, selected answers, written translations. The exam was over.

This was not an exam. This was a reading.

Feimo opened her booklet and began to read. Not for answers. For repair. Two hundred voices, filling the antechamber with the sound of English, the rhythm of academic prose, the cadence of arguments about consciousness and preservation and paradox. The Labyrinth's walls responded. The inscriptions brightened, slowly, like fire being coaxed from embers. Characters that had been fading since the invasion's first day regained their edges, their clarity, their glow. The text field strengthened with each sentence read, each blank filled, each answer written.

Feimo read the passage about plastics — *"The object you make is already a time bomb"* — and felt the wall behind her pulse, faintly, a warmth she had not felt since before the war. She read about the value of degrees — *"Education was no longer a secure route of social mobility"* — and the text on the opposite wall rearranged itself, one character at a time, as if responding to the attention. She read about art and science — *"Artists help scientists reach a broader audience"* — and thought of Mia, whose voice beside her in the corridor had been the bridge between knowledge and feeling.

She translated the passage about Scovell, the codebreaker. *"It was a battle between those who made codes and those who broke them."* She wrote the invitation to a professor: not the formal letter the prompt required, but the truth underneath it. *The innovation is reading itself.*

Around her, two hundred students did the same. The Labyrinth's text field responded to all of them equally. No reader was privileged. No reader was excluded. Every act of reading was an act of repair, and the repair extended outward from the antechamber like ripples from a stone, reaching damaged corridors, strengthening weakened inscriptions, pushing back the darkness that the Fragmenters had left behind.

Feimo thought of Card #073: Sartre's Engagement. The knowledge card she had studied last week. Sartre's theory that the writer bears responsibility for speaking against injustice, that literature is not a luxury but a duty, that silence in the face of oppression is itself a form of complicity. Feimo had read the card and thought it was about writing. She understood now that it was about reading. Every student in this room was performing an act of engagement: not by composing texts but by consuming them, not by speaking against injustice but by reading in the presence of damage. Sartre had argued that the writer must choose: art for art's sake, or art for the world's sake. The two hundred students in this room had made their choice. They were reading for the world's sake. And the world — the Labyrinth, the damaged walls, the weakened text field — was responding.

The exam lasted three hours.

When it ended, the antechamber was brighter than it had been at the start. The inscriptions on the walls glowed with a warmth that Feimo associated with the pre-invasion Labyrinth, before Cain's cameras, before the Fragmenters, before the war. The damage was not repaired — three floors were still rubble, the text preservation system was still compromised — but the damage had been contained. The text field had stabilized. The Labyrinth was not healed. But it was no longer dying.

---

Mia was waiting in the corridor.

She was not leaning against the wall. She was standing in the center of the corridor, upright, her hands at her sides, her tail hanging straight. Her hair was down, the way it had been during the battle, and her face was the face that Feimo had watched evolve over months of shared work and shared danger: not the mask of analytical competence but the face beneath it, the one that Mia had been reluctant to show and that was now, finally, simply there, the way a fact is there, the way the walls were there.

"The exam was a reading," Mia said.

"Yes."

"The text field responded."

"I felt it. The inscriptions in the antechamber were brighter when we left than when we entered."

Mia nodded. She looked at the corridor, at the damage and the repairs, at the sections where the plaster had been torn away and the stone was exposed and new text was already forming, faintly, the Labyrinth's self-healing process visible if you knew where to look.

"My memories are coming back," Mia said.

Feimo waited.

"Not the sealed ones. Not yet. The other ones. The ones from before the digitization. The smell of rain on concrete. The sound of a specific door closing. The feeling of holding a pen." She paused. "I remember holding a pen. The weight of it. The angle."

Feimo looked at Mia's hands. They were the same hands she had always seen: slender, precise, the fingers that typed and gestured and occasionally, rarely, reached for another person's hand in a corridor after a battle. But Mia was holding them differently now. Not curled, not defensive. Open. As if the memory of holding a pen had changed the way she held everything.

"They're more real," Feimo said. "The memories."

"They're returning in the wrong order. Sensory first, then narrative. I remember textures before I remember events. I remember the feeling of rain before I remember where it was raining."

"Is that wrong?"

"It's inefficient. A proper memory recovery would be chronological. Mine is..." She searched for the word. "Organic. The way plants grow. Not in a line. In all directions at once."

Feimo thought of the cloze passage. Plants do not grow the way animals do. They do not move toward goals or away from threats. They grow in response to conditions, expanding in every direction that conditions allow, and the result is not a trajectory but a shape, a form that is the record of everything that happened to it.

She thought of Mia, whose recovery was not a line but a shape, not a narrative but a garden, growing in all directions at once.

"Your form is more solid," Feimo said. "Since the battle. You look more real."

"I am more real. The digitization process degraded my physical substrate. Reduced the resolution. The Labyrinth's text field has been repairing it. Not through technology. Through reading. The same mechanism that defended the walls during the battle has been repairing me."

Feimo processed this. The Labyrinth's immune response was not limited to the Labyrinth itself. It extended to the people who read to it, who engaged with it, who were, in the language of the ecology metaphor, part of the ecosystem. Mia was not a visitor to the Labyrinth. She was a resident. And the Labyrinth's repair mechanisms included her.

"Then the exam was for you too," Feimo said.

"Everything is for everyone. That's what a distributed system means. No node is privileged. No node is excluded. The reading that repaired the walls repaired me. The reading that strengthened the text field strengthened my substrate. We are the same system, Feimo. The Labyrinth and I."

She looked at Feimo, and her eyes were the color they had always been but more vivid, more present, the way a photograph becomes more itself when you increase the resolution. Not different. More.

"We survived," Mia said.

Feimo looked at the corridor. At the damage and the repairs. At the text on the walls, brightening. At the students who were emerging from the exam room, blinking in the corridor's light, talking to each other in the low, relieved voices of people who had just done something difficult and were not yet sure what it meant.

"Let's rebuild," Feimo said.

Mia looked at her. The small, slightly crooked smile appeared, the one that lasted only a few seconds before Mia's face returned to its default setting of analytical neutrality. But this time, the default was different. The neutrality was not a mask. It was a foundation. The face of someone who had been through something and come out the other side not unchanged but clarified, the way a sentence is clarified by revision, the way a wall is clarified by repair.

"Let's rebuild," Mia said.

They walked down the corridor together, into the Labyrinth, into the work that was waiting.

*Questions for Reflection:*

1. The reading-as-repair concept suggests that every act of reading strengthens the Labyrinth. If reading is an immune response, what does it mean that the students' exam — an institutional ritual — became a weapon against the Fragmenters? Can institutional structures serve purposes their designers never intended?

2. Card #072 describes Black Humor as using comedy to process horror. Where does black humor appear in this chapter? What is the relationship between laughter and survival?

3. Card #073 describes Sartre's theory of engaged literature: the writer's responsibility to speak against injustice. Feimo reads to the Labyrinth during a battle. Is reading a form of engagement? Can reading be a political act?
