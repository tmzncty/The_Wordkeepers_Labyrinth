# Chapter 45: Separation

Feimo woke to silence.

Not the ordinary silence of early morning, the kind that fills a room like water filling a glass, patient and complete. This silence was specific. It was the silence of an absence. The silence of a frequency that had been transmitting for months and had now stopped, the way a radio stops when someone unplugs it, not with a crash but with a click, a small, definitive sound that is barely a sound at all and is louder than anything you have ever heard.

Mia was gone.

Not physically gone. Feimo could feel, with the ordinary senses of a body lying in a bed in a room on the fourth floor of the Labyrinth, that the building was still there. The walls were there. The corridors, the cameras, the bilingual inscriptions, the hum of the ventilation system that had grown louder since the Fragmenter invasion. All of it was there. The rain was there, falling on the courtyard outside her window with the steady indifference of weather that does not care about the events happening inside the buildings it falls on.

But the connection was gone.

For months, since the day they had first stood together in front of the Kafka door on Level 8, there had been a thread between them. Not a visible thread. Not a thread that Feimo could point to and say: here, this is it, this is the thing that connects me to the voice in my head, the projection beside my desk, the cat-eared figure who corrected my translations with the precision of a surgeon and the tenderness of someone who would never admit to tenderness. The thread was something else. A **current**, running beneath the surface of things, carrying information in both directions: Feimo's thoughts flowing toward Mia, Mia's responses flowing back. It was not language. It was not telepathy. It was the thing that happened when two people had read the same texts, walked the same corridors, argued about the same passages, and had gradually, without either of them planning it, built a **community** of two, a shared space where meaning was produced not by either of them alone but by the dialogue between them.

Feimo lay in bed and reached for the thread.

There was nothing there.

She reached again, the way you reach for a light switch in a dark room, knowing exactly where it is, confident that the muscle memory of your hand will find it in the dark. Her mind moved toward the place where Mia's voice usually was, the dry, flat, slightly irritated register that had become as familiar to her as her own heartbeat.

Nothing.

She sat up.

The room was cold. The window showed a grey sky, the particular grey of a winter morning that has not yet decided whether to rain or to simply hang there, heavy and undecided, the meteorological equivalent of a sentence that starts but does not finish. Feimo looked at the window and thought: I am looking at an unfinished sentence. The sky is a fragment. The day is a cloze test with all the blanks unfilled.

She thought about the 2024 cloze passage. The one about automatic doors.

The passage had begun: *There's nothing more welcoming than a door opening for you.* It had gone on to explain how self-sliding doors began as a commercial product in 1960, invented by Americans Dee Horton and Lew Hewitt. They **started out** as a novelty feature, but as their use had grown, their **benefits** had extended within our technologically advanced world. The doors **analysed** the light, sound, weight or movement in their vicinity as a signal to open. Sensor types were chosen to **complement** the different environments they were needed in.

Feimo thought: the Labyrinth is a kind of automatic door. It analyses the presence of its inhabitants. It responds to their movements, their weight, their proximity. The walls shift. The inscriptions change. The corridors rearrange themselves according to principles that only Professor Silence partially understood. The Labyrinth is a sensor system that reads its environment and opens the right doors for the right people at the right time.

But the passage had also said something that the exam questions had not asked. It had said: *Although these methods differ, the main **principles** remain the same.* Every automatic door, no matter how complex its sensors, operates on a single principle: detect, respond, open. And every automatic door, no matter how sophisticated, can also close.

Mia had closed a door.

Not a physical door. Not the door on Level 8, or the door to the professor's room, or the door to the engine room with its moving text and its price. Mia had closed the door between them, the invisible door that their connection had held open for months, the door that allowed Feimo to hear Mia's voice in her head and feel Mia's presence beside her and know, without looking, where Mia was and what she was thinking and whether she was angry or amused or afraid.

The door had closed. **Without** the need to be touched. Automatically. The sensor that had detected Feimo's presence in Mia's mind had been switched off, and the door had slid shut, and the space between them, which had been a caravanserai, a meeting place, a room where two languages met and exchanged their cargo, was now a wall.

Feimo got dressed.

---

She went to the library.

The Labyrinth was quiet. It was early, still before the hour when the corridors filled with students and the walls began their daily performance of shifting inscriptions, the bilingual text rearranging itself like a conversation that never stopped and never repeated. Feimo walked through the silence and felt, for the first time in months, that the building was not watching her. Not because the cameras were off. She knew the cameras were still there, Cain's red eyes blinking their rhythm along the corridors. But the Labyrinth's attention, the sense that the building was aware of its inhabitants, that it tracked their movements and responded to their needs, that it was, in some way that she could not explain, alive, had always been mediated through Mia. Mia was the one who had taught her to read the walls. Mia was the one who had explained the inscriptions, who had translated the shifts in the text, who had interpreted the building's moods. Without Mia, the Labyrinth was just a building. Stone and plaster and bilingual text that meant nothing because the person who could make it mean something was no longer there.

She sat at her usual table in the reading room.

The table was empty. Not physically empty. Her books were there, stacked in the careful order she had established months ago: exam materials on the left, literary criticism in the middle, the growing pile of vocabulary cards on the right. The table had her things on it. But it did not have Mia's projection, the translucent figure that had sat across from her for months, legs crossed, tail wrapped around one thigh, ears angled forward in the posture that meant she was reading and listening at the same time and was annoyed by both activities and would not admit to enjoying either.

The absence was a kind of violence. Not the violence of attack but the violence of removal. The cloze passage had said that automatic doors were essential in **improving** disabled access to buildings and helping provide general **convenience** to commercial buildings. The doors worked by removing an obstacle. They analysed the environment, detected a person, and removed the barrier between that person and the space beyond. The passage had not discussed what happens when the door closes on you. When the sensor stops detecting. When the barrier returns, not because someone put it there but because the system that had been removing it has decided, for reasons you do not understand, to stop.

Feimo opened her notebook.

She wrote: "The connection is gone. I woke up and it was not there. I have been reaching for it all morning and there is nothing. It is like reaching for a word you have known your entire life and finding that the word has been removed from the language, not just from your memory but from the dictionary itself, from the system of signs that makes meaning possible."

She stopped writing. She read what she had written and thought: this is a translation problem. I am trying to translate an experience into words, and the words are not sufficient, because the experience is not linguistic. The connection was not a language. It was the space between languages. It was the thing that happened between the Chinese characters and the English words on the Labyrinth's walls, the gap where meaning lived, the silence between two voices that was not empty but full, full of the resonance of two people thinking together.

Bakhtin would have understood this.

Card #089. The knowledge card she had studied last week. Bakhtin's Dialogism. The card had explained that language is fundamentally dialogic: meaning is not produced by a single speaker but in the exchange between speakers, in the space where one voice meets another and something new emerges, something that neither voice could have produced alone. The card had said: *Language is not a thing but an event. Not a possession but a process. Not a structure but a dialogue.* Bakhtin had argued against the structuralists, against the idea that language was a closed system of signs. Language was open. Language was social. Language was the meeting of two consciousnesses, each one incomplete without the other.

Mia had been Feimo's dialogic partner. Not in the academic sense, not in the sense of a seminar discussion or a translation exercise, but in the deeper sense that Bakhtin meant: the sense in which my understanding of the world is incomplete without your response, in which my meaning does not fully exist until it has been heard by you and changed by your hearing. Mia's voice in Feimo's head had not been a supplement to Feimo's own thinking. It had been the other half of it. The part that responded, that questioned, that said, in its flat, dry, irritated voice: *That is not what the passage says. Read it again.*

Without the dialogic partner, Feimo's thoughts were monologue. And monologue, Bakhtin had argued, was not a simpler form of dialogue. It was a different thing entirely. It was the illusion of self-sufficiency, the belief that one voice can carry the full weight of meaning. It could not. Meaning required two.

Feimo closed her notebook.

---

She was still sitting there when Aya found her.

Aya did not knock. The reading room had no door, only an archway, and Aya walked through it with the unhurried confidence of someone who has been looking for a person and has found them and is not surprised by where they are. She was wearing the same clothes Feimo had last seen her in: the dark jacket, the carefully maintained appearance of a student who understood that clothing was a language and chose her words carefully. Her expression was composed, but Feimo could see, in the way Aya held her shoulders, a tension that was not habitual.

"Sit down," Feimo said.

Aya sat.

They looked at each other across the table. The reading room was empty. The rain had stopped, or perhaps it had not; the windows were high and narrow and showed only the grey sky, the particular grey that could mean rain or could mean the memory of rain, the atmosphere's version of a sealed memory.

"I know," Aya said.

Feimo waited.

"She cut the connection," Aya said. "Last night. Around three in the morning. I felt it."

"You felt it?"

"I was nearby. In the corridor. I was walking, and I felt the change. The Labyrinth shifted. Not physically. The walls did not move. But the texture of the air changed, the way it does when someone leaves a room. The presence withdrew. The attention that had been directed outward, toward you, pulled back. Like a hand opening. Like a fist unclenching. Like a door closing."

Feimo said nothing.

Aya reached into her jacket and took out something small. She placed it on the table between them.

It was a Glyph.

Feimo recognized it. Not the specific Glyph, not its particular configuration of strokes and curves, but the type. It was a Glyph of the kind that the Labyrinth's walls sometimes generated, the living inscriptions that appeared when the building's engine was working well and the text on the walls was fluid and responsive. This one was small, perhaps three centimeters across, and it glowed with a warm, amber light that reminded Feimo of the phosphorescent strips on Level 7, the ones that Professor Silence had described in his confession as having been calibrated to a different standard.

"It is a Glyph of courage," Aya said. "She gave it to me. Three days ago. She said: *If something happens, give this to Feimo. Do not explain. Just give it.*"

Feimo looked at the Glyph. It sat on the table like a small sun, casting a circle of amber light on the wood, and Feimo thought about the reading passage from the 2024 exam. Text 1. The passage about nails.

The passage had told the story of a technology so simple that it seemed beneath notice. Nails. A million of them, buried by the Romans in Scotland two thousand years ago, hidden so deep that they were not found for almost two millennia. The passage had said that the price of nails fell by ninety percent between the late 1700s and mid-1900s, and that while cheaper iron and cheaper energy played a part, most of the credit went to nail manufacturers who simply found more efficient ways to turn steel into nails. The passage had concluded: *It's the cheap technologies that change the world.*

Mia's connection had been a cheap technology. A voice. A projection. A presence. No special equipment, no elaborate infrastructure, just the thread between two people who had read the same texts and walked the same corridors and argued about the same passages until the arguing itself became a form of **connection**. It cost nothing. It required nothing. And its absence changed everything.

The Glyph of courage was another cheap technology. A small piece of light, three centimeters across, generated by a building that thought in two languages. It cost nothing. It weighed nothing. And Aya had carried it for three days, waiting for the moment when Feimo would need it.

"Bring her back," Aya said.

Her voice was steady. Her eyes were on Feimo's face, and Feimo saw in them something that she had not seen before: not rivalry, not competition, not the complicated mixture of admiration and resentment that had characterized Aya's attitude toward Mia. What Feimo saw in Aya's eyes was trust. Aya trusted Feimo to do what Aya could not. Aya trusted Feimo to find the door that Mia had closed and open it again.

"I don't know where she is," Feimo said.

"You will find her." Aya stood. She pushed the Glyph across the table toward Feimo. "Take it. She made it for you."

Feimo picked up the Glyph. It was warm in her hand, the warmth of something that has been held recently, something that has been in someone's pocket, close to someone's body, carrying the residual heat of a person who is not here.

Aya turned to go. She stopped at the archway. She looked back, and her face had changed, the composure softening into something that was not quite sadness and not quite hope but the expression of a person who is letting go of something they have held for a long time and is not sure whether the letting go is a loss or a liberation.

"Take care of her," Aya said.

Then she looked past Feimo, past the table, past the reading room, at the empty air above Feimo's left shoulder, and she said, in a voice that was barely a whisper:

"Take care of her. Okay?"

She was not talking to Feimo.

She walked away.

Feimo sat with the Glyph in her hand and understood what had happened. Aya had spoken to the empty air because Aya believed that Mia was still there. Not connected, not present, not projecting, but still there, still listening, still watching from whatever distance she had retreated to, the way a person watches a house from across the street, too far to be seen but too close to leave.

Aya had made a choice. She had chosen to bless rather than to compete. To trust rather than to fight. To let go of the rivalry that had defined her relationship with Mia and replace it with something that looked, from the outside, like love.

Feimo thought about the 2024 reading passage on alloparenting. Text 2. The passage had described a practice from hunter-gatherer societies in Africa, where each child was cared for by many adults. The practice was called alloparenting, and it was based on a simple principle: no one should raise a child alone. The passage had said that in hunter-gatherer societies, adults other than the parents could provide almost half of a child's care, and that infants born to these communities could have more than ten caregivers. The passage had contrasted this with the western nuclear family, which it described as a recent invention that broke with evolutionary history. The shift to what the researchers called an "intensive mothering narrative," the idea that mothers should manage childcare alone, was likely to have been harmful. *Such narratives can lead to maternal exhaustion and have dangerous consequences.*

Feimo thought: the Labyrinth is a kind of hunter-gatherer community. A place where knowledge is not held by one person but distributed among many. Professor Silence holds the architecture. Mia holds the languages. Cain holds the security. The walls hold the text. And Feimo, who is not the holder of any single thing, is the one who moves between them, carrying information from one to another, the alloparent of the Labyrinth's knowledge, the person who does not own the child but helps to raise it.

But Mia had broken the alloparenting arrangement. She had taken herself out of the community. She had decided, for reasons that Feimo did not yet understand, that she should no longer be part of the system of care that connected her to Feimo, and she had severed the connection with the precision of a surgeon and the finality of someone who has thought about this for a long time and has decided that the surgery is necessary, even if it hurts, especially if it hurts.

The passage had also said that introducing additional caregivers could reduce stress and maternal depression, which could have a "knock-on" benefit to a child's wellbeing. Feimo was not a child. But the principle applied. The removal of a caregiver had a knock-on cost. The absence of a voice in your head, a presence beside your desk, a dry, flat, irritated voice that corrected your translations and mocked your pronunciations and told you, in a hundred small ways every day, that you were not alone. The removal of that voice was not a subtraction. It was an exponents. It was the kind of loss that grows in the telling, that multiplies each time you reach for the thread and find nothing, each time you turn to the empty chair and see the empty chair, each time you open your mouth to say something to the person who used to be there and realize that the person cannot hear you.

---

Noir arrived at noon.

Feimo did not hear her come in. She was sitting at the table, the Glyph of courage in her left hand, her right hand flat on the open notebook, not writing, not reading, just sitting, the way a person sits when they have been doing something for a long time and have stopped doing it without deciding to stop, the body continuing the posture of activity while the mind has moved to some other place that the body cannot follow.

Noir's hand on her shoulder.

Feimo looked up. Noir's face was the face of a person who has received bad news and has processed it and has decided, not to be cheerful about it, but to be present. Her eyes were clear. Her mouth was set in the particular line that meant she was going to say something honest and was not going to dress it up.

"I heard," Noir said.

"How?"

"Aya told me. She found me in the corridor. She said: *Feimo needs you.*"

Feimo said nothing. Noir pulled up a chair and sat next to her, not across from her, next to her, the way a person sits when they are not going to face you but to be beside you, the difference between a confrontation and a companionship.

"She cut the connection," Feimo said.

"I know."

"I don't know why."

Noir was quiet for a moment. Then she said: "The elephants know."

Feimo looked at her.

"The 2024 translation passage," Noir said. "The one about elephants. Do you remember it?"

Feimo remembered. The passage had described how elephants sometimes travel more than sixty miles to find food or water, and are very good at working out where other elephants are, even when they are out of sight. The researchers were convinced that the elephants always know precisely where they are in relation to all the resources they need, and can therefore take shortcuts, as well as following familiar routes. The passage had explained that one possibility was that they merely used their eyes, but that would probably result in a lot of wasted time and energy, not least because their eyesight is actually not very good. The real answer was smell. The volatile chemicals produced by plants can be carried a long way, and they are very characteristic: each plant or tree has its own particular odor signature. The experiment showed that elephants may well use smell to identify patches of trees that are good to eat, and secondly to assess the quality of the trees within each patch.

"Mia is out of sight," Noir said. "But she is not out of range. You cannot see her. You cannot hear her. The connection is gone. But the scent remains. The things she taught you, the way she taught you to read, the arguments you had, the translations you fought over. Those are volatile chemicals. They travel a long way. They are very characteristic. Each one has its own particular odor signature."

Feimo's eyes burned.

"You will find her the way the elephants find the good trees," Noir said. "Not by looking. Not by reaching for a thread that is no longer there. By following the scent. By trusting the knowledge that is already in you, the knowledge that she put there, month after month, translation after translation, argument after argument. The knowledge is yours now. She cannot take it with her. It is the one thing the connection left behind when it closed."

Noir reached out and put her arms around Feimo.

The embrace was not gentle. It was the embrace of a person who has held things together under pressure and knows what it costs and does not pretend that the cost is small. Noir held Feimo the way you hold a person who is not falling apart but is carrying something very heavy and needs, for a moment, someone else to share the weight. Feimo leaned into the embrace and felt, for the first time since waking, that she was not alone in the room, not alone in the Labyrinth, not alone in the world that Mia had left.

The Glyph of courage pulsed in her hand. Warm. Amber. A heartbeat.

"I'm going to find her," Feimo said, into Noir's shoulder.

"I know," Noir said. "But not today. Today you sit here. Today you let me hold you. Tomorrow you look for the door."

Feimo closed her eyes.

---

She thought about identity.

The 2024 reading passage about AI art generators had described a Polish digital artist named Greg Rutkowski, whose distinctive style had become one of the most commonly used prompts in the AI art generator Stable Diffusion. Anyone could type in a description, append Rutkowski's name, and produce something that looked not a million miles away from his work. The result was that the Internet became flooded with AI art bearing his style, his signature, his identity, without his permission and without his control. He had said: *It's been just a month. What about in a year? I probably won't be able to find my work out there because the Internet will be flooded with AI art.*

Mia had done something similar to herself. She had sealed her memories, not by choice, but by the action of the room, and the result was that her identity, the chain of experience and learning that connected who she was to who she had been, had been flooded with gaps. Her past was a collection of AI-generated images bearing her name but not her hand. She could still read. She could still analyze. She could still teach. But the provenance was gone. The connection between a fact and the person who had learned it, between a skill and the experience that had built it, between a love and the memory that had started it, had been severed.

And now she had severed the last connection: the one to Feimo.

Feimo understood, in the way that understanding sometimes arrives not as a thought but as a physical sensation, a tightening in the chest, a pressure behind the eyes, that Mia had not cut the connection out of anger. Not out of betrayal. Not out of the hurt that Professor Silence's confession had inflicted, although that hurt was real and would be felt for a long time. Mia had cut the connection because she was afraid.

Not afraid of Feimo. Afraid for her.

The sealed memories were not stable. They were not a wall that, once built, stood forever. They were a door that could open, and what was behind the door was unknown, and the unknown was dangerous, and the danger was not to Mia but to the person who was connected to Mia, the person who would be standing next to the door when it opened, the person who would be pulled through.

Mia had cut the connection to protect Feimo from what lay behind the door.

Feimo thought about the Chesapeake Bay. Text 4 from the 2024 reading exam. The passage had described the bay as a place whose miracle lay not in its depths but in the complexity of its natural construction, the interaction of fresh and saline waters, the mix of land and water. The shallows provided homes for hundreds of species while storing floodwaters, filtering pollutants, and protecting nearby communities from destructive storm surges. All of this had been put at risk by a Supreme Court ruling that narrowed the definition of protected wetlands, requiring a "continuous surface connection" to bodies of water. The passage had observed that water, and the pollutants that come with it, don't respect state boundaries.

Mia's sealed memories were pollutants. They did not respect the boundaries between her mind and Feimo's. The connection between them was a watershed, a vast, shared basin where thoughts and feelings and memories flowed in both directions, and the pollutants, whatever they were, whatever dangerous knowledge or destabilizing recognition lay behind the sealed door, could travel through the connection the way water travels through a watershed, crossing boundaries that are political but not natural.

The EPA's authority to regulate the Chesapeake Bay had been weakened by the ruling. Mia had not waited for a ruling. She had regulated herself. She had sealed the connection, not because the connection was bad, but because she could not guarantee that what flowed through it would be safe. She had chosen isolation over risk. Silence over contamination. Separation over the possibility that the thing behind the door would reach Feimo through the thread that connected them.

Feimo thought: this is the most Mia thing she has ever done. To protect someone by pushing them away. To love someone by leaving. To be brave in the most cowardly way possible, or cowardly in the most brave.

---

That night, Feimo sat in her room and wrote.

She wrote about the cloze passage, about automatic doors, about the way a door can open for you without being touched and close for you without being asked. She wrote: "The principle of the automatic door is detection. The sensor detects and the door responds. But what happens when the sensor is damaged? When the system that detects your presence decides, for its own protection, to stop detecting? The door does not malfunction. It functions perfectly. It simply no longer functions for you."

She wrote about the nails. About the Romans burying ten tons of iron so the locals could not use them. About the Virginians burning down their homes to recover the valuable nails from the ashes. About the passage's conclusion that cheap technologies change the world. She wrote: "Mia's voice was a cheap technology. A presence, a projection, a dry and irritated correction to my translations. It cost nothing. It weighed nothing. Its absence has rearranged my world."

She wrote about Aya. About the Glyph of courage. About the whisper to the empty air: *Take care of her, okay?* She wrote: "Aya chose blessing over rivalry. This is the hardest thing a person can do in love: to trust the other person with the person you love. To step back. To let go. To say, not *I will fight for her*, but *I will trust you to bring her back.* This is not weakness. This is the strongest thing I have ever witnessed."

She wrote about the elephants. About smell. About the way an elephant knows where another elephant is even when it cannot see it. She wrote: "Noir said the scent remains. The knowledge Mia gave me is a volatile chemical. It travels a long way. It has its own particular odor signature. I cannot see her. I cannot hear her. But I can follow the scent. I can trust the knowledge."

She wrote about Bakhtin. About dialogism. About the idea that meaning is produced not by one voice but in the exchange between two. She wrote: "Without Mia, I am a monologue. And monologue is not enough. It never was. The Labyrinth thinks in two languages. I think in two voices. One of them is mine. The other was hers. Now one voice speaks and the other is silent, and the silence is not empty. It is full of everything that is not being said."

She wrote about Metafiction. Card #090. The knowledge card about literature that is conscious of itself as literature, stories that know they are stories, texts that expose their own machinery. The card had said that metafiction breaks the illusion of fiction, making the reader aware that they are reading something that has been made, something that is constructed, something that is not reality but a representation of reality that has been shaped by choices and conventions and the limitations of the form.

Feimo wrote: "I am aware that I am in a story. Not in the magical sense, not in the sense that the Labyrinth is a book and I am a character in it, although perhaps that is also true. I am aware in the metafictional sense: I know that this moment, this absence, this loss, is being shaped by the same forces that shape every narrative. The separation before the reunion. The silence before the speech. The door closing before the door opens. I know the pattern. I have read enough books to recognize the structure. And knowing the pattern does not make it hurt less."

She put down her pen.

Outside, the rain had started again. It fell on the courtyard with the steady, indifferent rhythm of weather that does not care about the stories happening inside the buildings it falls on. The cameras blinked their red rhythm along the corridors. The Labyrinth hummed its low, constant hum, the sound of a building that thinks in two languages and stores its thoughts on walls.

And somewhere in the building, in a place that Feimo could not see and could not reach and could not hear, Mia was sitting in the dark, her ears flat against her skull, her hands on her knees, her eyes dry, her face composed, the mask in place. She was not sleeping. She was not reading. She was not projecting. She was sitting in the dark and she was listening to the silence that she had created, the silence where Feimo's voice used to be, the silence that was the price she had paid to keep Feimo safe.

The Glyph of courage sat on Feimo's desk. It pulsed with its warm, amber light, a heartbeat, a rhythm, a current. It was a cheap technology. A small thing. Three centimeters of light in a dark room. But the passage had said: cheap technologies change the world.

Feimo picked up the Glyph and held it in her hand.

"I'm coming," she said, to the empty room, to the dark building, to the person who was out of sight but not out of range. "I don't know how yet. But I'm coming."

The Glyph pulsed.

The rain fell.

The Labyrinth listened.

*Questions for Reflection:*

1. The 2024 cloze passage describes automatic doors that open and close **without** being touched, relying on sensors that **analyse** the environment. How does this technology serve as a metaphor for Mia's decision to sever the connection? Is her decision an act of protection (removing an obstacle) or an act of exclusion (closing a door)?

2. Bakhtin argued that meaning is dialogic: it is produced not by one voice but in the exchange between two. Feimo writes that without Mia, she is "a monologue." What is the difference between solitude and loneliness? Can a monologue ever be sufficient?

3. The passage on alloparenting argues that children benefit from multiple caregivers and that the "intensive mothering narrative" of the western nuclear family is harmful. How does this apply to the Labyrinth's **community** of knowledge? What happens when one member of the community withdraws?

4. Aya whispers to the empty air: "Take care of her, okay?" She believes Mia is still listening. Is this an act of faith or an act of knowledge? What is the difference?

5. Feimo connects the AI art passage to Mia's sealed memories: both involve the loss of provenance, the severing of a work from its creator. In what sense is identity a form of authorship? What does it mean to lose the connection between who you are and who you were?

6. The elephants navigate by smell, following volatile chemicals that travel long distances. Noir tells Feimo: "The scent remains." What is the "scent" that Mia has left behind? Can knowledge be a form of smell?
