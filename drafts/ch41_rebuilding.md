# Chapter 41: Rebuilding

The first thing Feimo noticed was the absence of sound.

Not silence. Silence implies a listener. This was the absence itself: the hum of the Labyrinth's ventilation system, the murmur of text shifting on the walls, the low electrical drone of the fluorescent strips that had lit every corridor since the day she arrived. All gone. The building had been stripped to its bones, and the bones were quiet.

She stood in the courtyard at six in the morning on the first day of 2023. The air was cold, the kind of cold that felt less like temperature and more like a verdict, a final assessment of what had survived the war and what had not. The east wing was a ruin. Three floors of structural damage, the walls caved inward, the inscriptions that had once covered them now rubble on the ground. The library's lower level had flooded during the final assault, and the water had been pumped out but the stains remained, dark tide marks along the shelves that held the periodicals nobody read anymore. The cafeteria was intact. That was something.

Noir was already there, standing near the fountain that no longer worked, her arms crossed, her breath forming small clouds in the January air. She wore a coat that was too large for her, military surplus, and her hair was pulled back in the severe knot she adopted when she was thinking about infrastructure rather than aesthetics.

"You're early," Noir said.

"I didn't sleep."

"None of us did."

They stood together in the courtyard and looked at the damage. The cameras were still operational; Cain had seen to that. Two of them had been destroyed in the fighting, but replacements had arrived within days, installed by a maintenance crew that worked under armed escort. The new cameras were a different model, sleeker, with wider lenses. Feimo had the uncomfortable sense that the war had given Cain an excuse to upgrade.

"The reconstruction committee meets at nine," Noir said. "Cain's chairing it."

"Of course he is."

"He has a plan for the underground."

Feimo looked at her. "What kind of plan?"

Noir's expression did not change, but something in the set of her jaw shifted, a micro-adjustment that Feimo had learned to read over three years of shared meals, shared exams, and shared silences. Noir was angry. Not the hot, performative anger of a confrontation but the cold, architectural anger of someone who had seen a structure being built and knew it was the wrong one.

"He wants to convert it," Noir said. "The underground arena. Turn it into a legitimate facility. A competitive academic venue, sanctioned by the administration, with rules and schedules and oversight."

"And you?"

"I think he's right."

---

The committee met in the lecture hall on the second floor, the one with the cracked ceiling and the projector that took forty-five seconds to warm up. Twelve people sat around a long table that had been assembled from cafeteria trays and library desks. Cain stood at the head, dressed in the pressed shirt and dark trousers that constituted his uniform, his posture the posture of a man who had spent the war winning and intended to spend the peace consolidating.

Feimo sat between Noir and Yue. Mia was absent. She had been absent from most formal gatherings since the fighting ended, appearing only for meals and for the quiet evening walks she took along the perimeter, her tail low, her ears flat, her expression the expression of someone counting things that could not be counted.

Cain spoke for thirty minutes. He outlined the damage: four floors uninhabitable, the text preservation system compromised on levels three through seven, the water damage to the periodical collection estimated at forty percent loss. He described the funding: a combination of institutional grants, alumni donations, and what he called "alternative revenue streams," a phrase that made Feimo's hand tighten around her pen.

"The underground," Cain said, "represents an untapped resource."

He clicked the projector. A blueprint appeared on the wall, rendered in clean digital lines: the old arena, redesigned. The fighting pit was now a "competitive platform." The spectator galleries were now "tiered seating with capacity for two hundred." The weapons rack was now an "equipment station." Everything had been renamed. The violence had been scrubbed from the vocabulary, replaced by the language of institutional legitimacy.

"This facility," Cain said, "will host inter-academic competitions. Debate tournaments. Examination simulations. Competitive knowledge events. It will generate revenue, attract external participants, and position our Academy as a leader in academic competition."

Feimo watched the room. Noir's face was unreadable. Yue was looking at the ceiling. Aya, sitting at the far end, was sketching something in her notebook that was probably not related to the proposal.

"The underground was never sanctioned," Feimo said. "You spent two years trying to shut it down."

"I spent two years trying to control it," Cain replied. "Control and elimination are different objectives. The war made the distinction clear. You cannot eliminate a system that people need. You can only give it a form that serves your purposes."

"And if people don't want your form?"

"Then they are free to not participate."

The vote was eight to four in favor. Noir voted yes. Feimo voted no. The reconstruction would begin in February.

---

After the meeting, Feimo found Noir in the corridor outside the lecture hall. Noir was leaning against the wall, her arms still crossed, her eyes fixed on a point somewhere in the middle distance.

"You voted yes," Feimo said.

"I did."

"Why?"

Noir pushed herself off the wall and faced Feimo. In the corridor's half-light, her features were sharp, angular, the face of someone who had been carved by circumstances into something harder than she had been designed to be.

"Because the underground was dying," Noir said. "The war killed it. The participants are scattered. The equipment is destroyed. The rules were never written down, and the people who enforced them are gone. Cain's plan is not what I would have designed, but it is a plan, and a plan is better than a ruin."

"He'll control it."

"He'll control the structure. The content is ours."

"Since when has that distinction held?"

Noir was quiet for a moment. Then she said: "My grandfather built things. Houses, bridges, small dams. He never saw the point of preserving something that couldn't be used. He used to say, 'A wall that doesn't hold anything up is just a place for weeds to grow.'"

She paused.

"The underground was a wall that held up nothing. It was beautiful and it was ours and it held up nothing. Cain is offering to make it hold something. I don't like him. I don't trust him. But I **rely** on the principle that a structure should serve a function, and if the function changes, the structure should change with it."

Feimo heard the word. **Rely.** It came from the 2023 reading passage about Waterstones and Penguin Random House. The mid-list authors who normally **rely** on Waterstones staff's passion for promoting books by lesser-known writers. Feimo had read the passage three days ago, in the preliminary materials Professor Silence had distributed, and the word had lodged in her mind with the persistence of a splinter. **Rely**: to depend on with full trust and confidence. The mid-list authors relied on the staff's passion. Noir relied on the principle of function. Feimo relied on neither. She relied on the text. On the walls. On the knowledge that persisted when structures failed.

"If he saw what you're building," Feimo said, and stopped.

Noir waited.

"If your grandfather saw what you're building. An arena with rules written by someone else. A competition sanctioned by the same authority that tried to destroy the competition that existed before."

Noir's jaw tightened. Then she said: "He would say it doesn't matter. What matters is now. Whether the structure holds. Whether the people inside it are served. My grandfather didn't build for the past. He built for the next storm."

She walked away. Feimo watched her go and thought about reliance, about the difference between depending on a person and depending on a principle, about the mid-list authors who would be praying for an end to a dispute they had not created and could not resolve.

---

The 2023 exam was administered on a Tuesday.

The room had been repaired. Fresh plaster covered the scars where shrapnel had marked the walls, and the desks were new, purchased with funds from one of Cain's alternative revenue streams. The windows were clean. The cameras blinked their red pulse from the ceiling, patient and unblinking, the eyes of a system that had survived its own destruction and emerged, somehow, more intact than the people it observed.

Feimo opened the booklet.

The cloze passage was about caravanserais.

She read it slowly, the way she had learned to read: not just the words but the architecture beneath them. *Caravanserais were roadside inns that were built along the Silk Road in areas including China, North Africa and the Middle East.* The passage described a network of structures, **located** outside the walls of cities, funded by local governments or **privately**, designed to shelter travelers from the dangers of the road. The word itself was a **combination** of Persian roots: *karvan*, a group of travelers, and *sardy*, a palace. A palace for travelers. A shelter built not for permanence but for passage.

She filled in the blanks with the methodical precision of someone assembling a machine from instructions she had already memorized. **Located**, because the sentence required a verb of placement, and the other options displayed, occupied, equipped all carried connotations of active use that contradicted the passive positioning of a building that existed only to be found. **Privately**, because the conjunction "or" demanded a contrast with "local governments," and private funding was the only option that provided institutional opposition. **Combination**, because the passage was etymological, tracing a word to its components, and combination was the only noun that described the relationship between two source words and their product.

She moved through the passage, filling blanks, mapping connections. The caravanserais increased in **construction** as routes developed. Travelers **faced** the possibility of attack or being **subjected** to extreme weather. The buildings were strategically placed **so that** they could be reached in a day's travel. They served as informal **meeting** points, and **as a result** became centers for cultural **exchange**, with travelers sharing knowledge, **as well as** taking it with them, greatly **influencing** the development of civilizations.

The passage was about infrastructure. About the structures that make passage possible. About the relationship between shelter and journey, between the temporary and the permanent, between the individual traveler and the network that supports the traveler's movement from one point to another.

Feimo thought of the Labyrinth. Of the corridors she walked every day, the walls that carried knowledge in two languages, the rooms that had been damaged and repaired and damaged again. The Labyrinth was a caravanserai. A shelter for travelers who were passing through a landscape of ideas, stopping to rest, exchanging what they knew, carrying fragments of understanding to the next station. The knowledge on the walls was not the destination. It was the road.

She completed the cloze and turned to the reading section.

---

Text 1 was about climate change education in Texas.

The passage described a debate at the State Board of Education, where a board member named Pat Hardy resisted proposed changes to science standards. "There are as many scientists working against all the panic of global climate change as there are those who are pushing it," Hardy claimed. "Texas is an energy state and we need to recognise that. You need to remember where your bread is buttered."

Feimo read the sentence and thought of Cain. *Where your bread is buttered.* The phrase was a warning disguised as a reminder: your survival depends on the system that feeds you, and the system that feeds you has interests that may not align with truth. Hardy was not making a scientific argument. She was making an economic one. The truth of climate change was irrelevant; what mattered was the financial structure of the state that employed her.

The study cited in the passage had given barely half of US states a grade of B+ or higher for their treatment of climate change in science classes. Texas received the lowest grade. An F. And Texas's textbooks were widely sold elsewhere, which meant that the state's failures had a disproportionate influence on the rest of the country.

Feimo thought about this. About the way a single institution's choices could reshape the knowledge landscape of an entire nation. About the textbooks that carried errors across state lines, the way the Labyrinth's inscriptions carried meaning across floors. The passage quoted Glenn Branch, the center's deputy director, who cautioned that state-level science standards were "only one limited benchmark" in a country that decentralized decisions to local school boards. Even if a state had strong standards, that did not mean the material would be taught.

The parallel was precise. Cain's reconstruction committee had established standards for the new arena: rules, schedules, oversight. But the standards were only as good as the people who implemented them, and the people who implemented them were chosen by Cain. The structure was legitimate. The implementation was controlled. The gap between the two was where truth went to die.

She thought about the passage's final warning: unofficial educational materials conveying slanted perspectives were being distributed to teachers, including materials sponsored by energy industry associations. The manipulation of knowledge was not always visible. Sometimes it came in the form of supplementary materials, well-designed, professionally printed, bearing the logos of reputable organizations. You did not need to burn books if you could flood the market with alternatives.

Text 2 was about housing.

The passage described the crisis in New England, where record-high home prices and historically low inventory had created an emergency in the rental market. Communities were attempting to regulate short-term rentals, but the solutions were contested. A housing advocate named Nick Taylor said the supply of rental housing was at "crisis level." A realtor named Ryan Castle argued that short-term rentals served the tourism industry. An expert named Keren Hom distinguished between individual homeowners renting out vacation properties and developers building disguised hotels to bypass taxes.

Feimo read the passage and thought about the underground.

Noir's argument was Castle's argument: the system served its users, and the users needed it. Cain's argument was the regulatory argument: the system needed structure, and structure required oversight. Hom's argument was the most nuanced: the problem was not the individual act but the institutional exploitation, the developer who built a hotel disguised as something else, the administrator who built a prison disguised as a school.

The Massachusetts governor had signed a bill requiring registration, insurance, and taxes. Boston had gone further, requiring renters to register with the city's Inspectional Services Department. The regulatory response was comprehensive, layered, and, according to Hom, insufficient. "If we want to make a change," she said, "the main one is we have to build a lot more."

Build more. Not regulate more. Not control more. Build. The answer to scarcity was not restriction but creation.

Feimo circled the word **remove** in her margin. The 2023 reading passage about Waterstones had used it: branch managers were told to **remove** PRH books from prominent areas. The act of removal. The politics of visibility. What you chose to display and what you chose to hide, and who made the choice, and why. The housing passage was about a different kind of removal: the removal of affordable housing from the market by developers who converted it into short-term rentals. In both cases, the removal was an act of power. The books were removed to punish a publisher. The housing was removed to generate profit. The mechanism was the same: take something that belongs to the commons and put it where only those with resources can reach it.

Text 3 was the Waterstones passage.

She read it again, even though she had already read it in the preliminary materials. The dispute between Penguin Random House and Waterstones over credit limits. The branch managers told to remove PRH books from tables and display spaces. The spokesperson's careful statement: "We are not boycotting PRH titles but we are doing our utmost to ensure that availability for customers remains good." The mid-list authors who relied on staff passion. The literary agent Andrew Lownie's warning about concentration of power: "The publishing industry talks about diversity in terms of authors and staff but it also needs a plurality of ways of delivering intellectual contact, choice and different voices."

Feimo read Lownie's sentence three times. *A plurality of ways.* The word struck her with the force of a diagnosis. The Labyrinth had once offered a plurality of ways: multiple corridors, multiple traditions, multiple languages and registers and frameworks for understanding. The war had reduced that plurality. The reconstruction was reducing it further. Cain's plan was a consolidation plan, a PRH plan: make the institution big enough to take on all-comers, and in the process, remove the mid-list voices that depended on passion rather than power.

Text 4 was about citation cartels.

The passage described the manipulation of academic metrics: journals and authors conspiring to inflate citation numbers, predatory journals acting as "milk cows" to boost specific papers, support service consultancies advising contributors to add inappropriate references. The problem was rampant in Scopus, the citation database, and a listing in Scopus seemed to be a criterion for being targeted.

Feimo read the passage and felt a cold recognition settle in her chest. The Labyrinth's knowledge system was a citation network. The inscriptions on the walls referenced each other, linked to each other, created a web of cross-references that gave each individual text its meaning and authority. If someone could manipulate that network, could add false references, could inflate the connections between certain texts while suppressing others, the entire system would be compromised.

The passage's final sentence was a command: *When an editor, a reviewer, or a support service asks you to add inappropriate references, do not oblige and do report the request to the journal.* Report. Refuse. Resist the manipulation by making it visible.

Feimo wrote in her margin: "The Labyrinth's walls are its citation network. If the walls are rewritten, the citations change. If the citations change, the meaning changes. Cain's reconstruction is not just physical. It is epistemological."

---

After the exam, Feimo walked to the library.

The third floor had been restored to its original configuration: individual carrels, reading lamps, the long table disassembled and returned to the cafeteria. The coffee carafe was gone. The mugs with the old emblem were gone. Professor Silence's note, the one that had summoned them to group study, had been taken down and replaced by a printed schedule of Cain's reconstruction timeline.

Feimo sat at a carrel and opened her notebook. She wrote:

*The caravanserai passage teaches that shelter exists to serve passage, not to replace it. The buildings were located outside the walls, not inside. They were funded privately or by local governments, not by empires. They were meeting points, not fortifications. The Labyrinth was built as a caravanserai. Cain is building a fortress.*

She turned the page and wrote about the reading passages. About climate education and the Texas board member who said "where your bread is buttered." About the housing crisis and the expert who said "we have to build a lot more." About the Waterstones dispute and the literary agent who called for "a plurality of ways." About the citation cartels and the journals that served as milk cows for inflated metrics.

Then she wrote about knowledge card #074, the one she had studied last week. Camus versus Sartre. The 1952 rupture, the "quarrel of the century." The core disagreement: Sartre believed revolutionary violence was justified under certain historical conditions; Camus opposed all violence. Sartre believed in the progress of history; Camus questioned historical teleology. Sartre's freedom was absolute, a freedom to choose everything; Camus's rebellion had limits, a freedom to resist the absurd without消灭 it.

Feimo thought about the card and about Cain and about Noir. Cain was Sartre. He believed in historical progress, in the necessity of institutional violence, in the freedom of the system to restructure itself according to its own logic. Noir was something else, something the card did not quite capture: she believed in function, in the pragmatic accommodation of structures to needs, in the principle that a wall should hold something up. And Feimo was Camus. She opposed the violence of restructuring. She questioned the teleology of progress. She believed in rebellion with limits, in resistance that did not seek to destroy the system but to expose its absurdity.

But Camus had lost. That was the thing the card did not say but that Feimo could read between the lines. Camus died in a car crash in 1960. Sartre lived until 1980, received the Nobel Prize, refused it, and continued to write and argue and shape the intellectual landscape of the century. The rebellion with limits was a beautiful position. It was also, historically, the position of those who were **removed** from the conversation by the sheer momentum of those who believed in progress at any cost.

She turned the page and wrote about card #075. The Nouveau Roman. The French experimental movement of the 1950s and 1960s that rejected every convention of the traditional novel: plot, character, psychological depth, linear time. Robbe-Grillet's object-focused narration, describing only surfaces, never entering the interior of a character. Sarraute's sub-conversations, the undercurrents of thought beneath the surface of dialogue. Simon's painterly narratives, sentences that moved like brushstrokes across a canvas.

The Nouveau Roman made form the content. How you wrote became what you wrote. The movement was a refusal: a refusal to tell stories the way stories had always been told. And the refusal was productive. It created new possibilities for what a novel could be, new ways of organizing experience on the page.

Feimo thought: the Labyrinth's walls are a Nouveau Roman. The inscriptions do not follow a linear narrative. They are fragmented, multi-voiced, multi-lingual. They **require** a reader who can hold multiple frames of reference simultaneously. The word surfaced naturally: **require**. The passage about caravanserais described buildings that required maintenance, that required travelers to stop, that required governments or private patrons to fund them. The Labyrinth required readers. Not passive consumers but active participants in the construction of meaning.

She closed her notebook and sat in the carrel, listening to the silence of a library that had been rebuilt to serve a purpose it had not chosen.

---

That night, Feimo could not sleep.

She lay in her bed and listened to the building settle. The new plaster contracting in the cold. The pipes adjusting to the restored water pressure. The cameras humming in the corridors, their red lights blinking through the gap beneath her door. She counted the blinks: one, two, three, four. A rhythm. A pulse. The heartbeat of a system that never rested.

At two in the morning, she heard footsteps.

They were soft, barely audible, the footsteps of someone who did not want to be heard but could not prevent the sound of bare feet on stone. Feimo sat up. The footsteps were in the corridor, moving slowly, deliberately, in the direction of the east stairwell. She recognized the rhythm. Mia.

She got out of bed, pulled on her shoes, and opened the door.

The corridor was empty. The cameras blinked their red pulse. The footsteps were receding, moving down the stairwell, and Feimo followed, keeping her distance, keeping her footsteps soft, matching her pace to Mia's. The stairwell was dark; the emergency lighting had not been restored on this floor, and Feimo descended by touch, her hand on the wall, her fingers tracing the plaster that covered the inscriptions that had once guided students through the building's vertical logic.

Mia was on the ground floor.

Feimo caught a glimpse of her at the end of the corridor: a silhouette, moving with the fluid certainty of someone who could see in the dark. Mia's tail was low, her ears flat, her arms at her sides. She was not walking the way a person walks when they are going somewhere. She was walking the way a river flows when it has no choice but to follow the slope of the land.

Sleepwalking. Feimo had read about it. Somnambulism. The body moving through space while the mind was elsewhere, executing learned patterns of navigation without conscious guidance. The Labyrinth's architecture was in Mia's muscle memory; she could walk its corridors in her sleep the way a pianist could play a sonata in hers.

Mia turned a corner. Feimo followed. They descended. Past the ground floor, past the sub-basement, into a section of the building that Feimo had never visited, a corridor that was narrower than the others, its walls unpainted, its floor uneven. The air was colder here, and it carried a smell that Feimo could not identify: old paper, perhaps, or dried ink, or the particular scent of a room that had been sealed for a long time.

Mia stopped.

She stood before a wall that looked like every other wall in the corridor: plaster, stone, the faint trace of inscriptions that had been painted over. But Mia's hand was raised, her fingers pressed against a specific point on the surface, and she was speaking.

The words were in Chinese.

Feimo did not understand them all. Her Mandarin was functional but not fluent, the product of three years of study and a lifetime of fragments: her grandmother's proverbs, her mother's lullabies, the bilingual inscriptions on the Labyrinth's walls. But she understood enough to catch the shape of what Mia was saying. Something about a door. Something about a name. Something about a person who had lived here before, in a room that had been sealed, in a time that had been forgotten.

Mia pressed harder. The wall moved.

It was not a dramatic movement. There was no grinding of stone, no cloud of dust, no cinematic reveal. The wall simply shifted, a section of plaster sliding inward on a hidden track, revealing a gap the width of a person. Behind the gap: a room.

Mia walked in.

Feimo followed.

The room was small, perhaps three meters square, and it was furnished with the austerity of a monk's cell: a wooden desk, a chair, a shelf mounted on the wall. The shelf held books. Not many. Perhaps twenty. Their spines were in Chinese, the characters worn but legible, and Feimo recognized some of the titles: classical poetry, historical essays, a collection of letters. On the desk lay a notebook, open to a page covered in handwriting that was cramped and urgent, the handwriting of someone writing fast, writing against time.

And beside the notebook, a photograph.

Feimo picked it up. The photograph was old, its edges curled, its surface mottled with the brown spots of chemical degradation. It showed a woman. Young. Chinese. Her hair was short, her expression serious, her posture the posture of someone who had been told to stand still and was doing so with the discipline of a soldier.

The face was Mia's face.

Not similar. Not reminiscent. Identical. The same set of the jaw. The same angle of the ears. The same curve of the brow. The same eyes, dark and precise, the eyes of someone who observed everything and revealed nothing.

Feimo's hands were shaking.

She set the photograph down and opened the notebook. The handwriting was in Chinese, and she read it slowly, character by character, sounding out the words in her head, translating as she went. The first page was a date: 1987. The second page was a name: Lin Mianyu. The third page was a sentence:

*I am writing this because the system will not let me speak. I am writing this because the girl will need to know. I am writing this because the door was made for her, and she will not remember that the door exists until someone shows her where it is.*

Feimo turned the page.

Behind her, Mia stood in the doorway, her eyes open but unfocused, her body present but her mind elsewhere, in a place that Feimo could not reach and could not follow. Mia's lips moved, forming words in a language that was not English, and the words were the words carved on the notebook's first page:

*The door was made for her.*

Feimo looked at the photograph. At the face that was Mia's face. At the eyes that were Mia's eyes. At the woman who had lived in this room, in 1987, and had written a message to a girl who would not arrive for thirty-six years.

She thought: Mia is not a product of the system. Mia is a legacy. She was designed by someone who loved her, and the system took her and sealed her memories and made her into something that could serve its purposes. But the original design persists. The door persists. The photograph persists.

She thought: this is what the Nouveau Roman was about. The refusal of linear narrative. The rejection of the idea that a story moves in one direction, from beginning to end. The past is not behind us. It is beneath us, in sealed rooms, in notebooks written in urgency, in photographs that carry faces through decades. The present is not a destination. It is a corridor, and the corridor has doors, and the doors open in both directions.

Mia's eyes focused.

She looked at Feimo. She looked at the room. She looked at the photograph in Feimo's hands. And her face did not change, did not register shock or recognition or fear, but her tail curled slowly around her own ankle, the gesture Feimo had learned to read as the physical manifestation of an emotion that Mia's face would not express.

"What is this?" Mia said.

"I don't know yet," Feimo said. "But I think it's yours."

They stood in the sealed room, in the silence of a building that had been rebuilt around a secret it did not know it held, and the photograph looked up at them with Mia's face, and the notebook lay open on the desk, and the Chinese characters marched across the page in their urgent, cramped handwriting, carrying a message that had waited thirty-six years to be read.

Outside, the cameras blinked their red pulse. Inside, the door was open.

*Questions for Reflection:*

1. The caravanserais passage describes structures that were "strategically placed so that they could be reached in a day's travel time." How does the concept of strategic placement apply to knowledge in the Labyrinth? Is the placement of inscriptions on walls a form of strategy, and if so, whose strategy is it?

2. The Texas education debate presents two positions: Pat Hardy's claim that "you need to remember where your bread is buttered" and the scientific community's insistence on evidence-based curricula. How does this tension between economic interest and intellectual integrity play out in the Academy's reconstruction under Cain?

3. Knowledge Card #074 describes the Camus-Sartre rupture as a disagreement about violence, history, and freedom. Feimo identifies herself as Camus and Cain as Sartre. Is this identification accurate? What are the limits of the analogy?

4. The Nouveau Roman rejected plot, character, and linear time in favor of formal experimentation. How does this literary movement help Feimo understand the sealed room and the notebook? What does it mean to read a life as a Nouveau Roman rather than a traditional narrative?

5. Mia sleepwalks to a sealed room containing a photograph of a woman with her face. The notebook says "the door was made for her." What does this suggest about the relationship between identity and design? Is Mia a copy, a continuation, or something else entirely?
