# Chapter 24: Quarterfinal — Bridge vs Forge

---

The arena was not a place. It was a sentence.

Feimo stood at the edge of the Labyrinth's tournament space and watched it form: walls of text rising from the stone floor like steam off summer asphalt, each line a passage from some half-remembered exam, each paragraph a corridor that branched into memory and dead end alike. The ceiling, if there was one, was lost in the kind of darkness that has weight. She could feel it pressing on her crown, the way deep water presses on a diver's skull, reminding you that above you is a volume you cannot breathe.

Across the arena, the Forge House team assembled in their crimson-trimmed coats. Five students. Their captain, a tall boy with burns on his fingers from years of close-hand Labyrinth work, was speaking in low tones, gesturing at the forming architecture with the calm efficiency of someone who had done this before. Behind him, Cain leaned against a pillar that had not existed five seconds ago, arms crossed, smiling the smile of a person who has already calculated the outcome and found it entertaining either way.

And beside Cain, quiet and precise in her reading glasses, Aya adjusted the strap of her satchel and did not look at Feimo.

Feimo did not look back. She had learned, over months of training, that looking was a form of reaching, and reaching was a form of need, and need was something she could not afford in the space between one heartbeat and the next. Not here. Not now.

She turned to Mia.

Mia said nothing.

This was not unusual in itself. Mia was a creature of silence the way certain animals are creatures of darkness: it was where she lived, where she hunted, where she was most fully herself. But today's silence had a different texture. During training, Mia had been a voice machine, a relentless engine of instruction and correction and, when correction failed, fury. "You're not fast enough. You're not ruthless enough. You're not..." And then she had stopped, mid-sentence, in a way that Feimo had never heard her stop before. The pause lasted three seconds. It lasted three years. "...careful enough," Mia had finished, and turned away, and said nothing more for the rest of the session.

That had been two days ago. Since then, silence.

Now Mia stood at the edge of the arena with her arms at her sides and her holographic ears compressed flat against her skull and her eyes fixed on the forming Labyrinth with an expression that Feimo could not read. Not blank. Not tense. Something else. The expression of someone who has decided to trust a bridge by walking across it without testing the first plank.

The announcer's voice echoed from somewhere above: "Quarterfinal match. Bridge House versus Forge House. Text-generation mode: Mixed. Passage sources: 2016 national examination. Participants ready."

Feimo rolled her shoulders. She felt the weight of her notebook in the inner pocket of her coat, the pen against her ribs. She felt the Labyrinth's ambient hum settle into her bones, that low-frequency vibration that meant the system was watching, measuring, cataloguing every text she had ever read and every word she had ever written and every silence she had ever kept.

"Standard rules," the announcer continued. "Navigate the generated passages. Identify the source, answer the embedded questions, construct the argument. First team to the center wins. Combat is permitted. Lethal force is not."

A beat.

"Begin."

---

The Labyrinth opened like a book falling to a particular page.

In medias res, Feimo thought, as the floor dissolved beneath her feet and she dropped into the first corridor. Homer would have approved. The old poet never started at the beginning; he threw you into the middle of the battle and trusted you to swim. *Rage. Sing, O goddess, the rage of Achilles.* Not "once upon a time." Not "here is the context." Just rage, immediate and total, the way a knife is immediate, the way a wound is total.

She landed on a narrow platform of compressed text. The walls on either side displayed passages she recognized instantly, not because she had memorized them, but because the Labyrinth had a way of presenting words that made recognition feel like remembering. The text was about Cambodia. About marriage. About choices made by parents and matchmakers and young men who had, in theory, the right to choose their own spouses.

*In Cambodia, the choice of a spouse is a complex one for the young male. It may involve not only his parents and his friends, as well as those of the young woman, but also a matchmaker.*

She read as she ran. The passage flowed past her like water in a channel, and she absorbed it the way a river absorbs rain: without stopping, without effort, because the current was already moving in the right direction. A young man could decide on a spouse on his own, or his parents could make the choice, giving the child little to say in the selection. In theory, a girl could veto. In theory. The phrase hung in the air like smoke, and Feimo understood its weight: in theory meant not in practice, meant the distance between what you are told you can do and what you actually can.

She reached the first junction. Three corridors. Three passages. Three possible directions.

The Labyrinth was asking her to choose, and the way you chose a spouse in Cambodia: quickly, without complete information, and with the understanding that someone else might have already made the choice for you.

Feimo listened. The Forge House team was somewhere behind her, navigating their own path through the same text, answering the same questions from a different angle. She could hear their captain's voice, muffled by walls, calling out answers: "1-A, 2-D, 3-C, 4-A." Quick. Mechanical. Right. The boy knew his grammar.

She took the left corridor. Not because she had calculated the odds, but because the passage on the left wall mentioned Buddhist priests offering a short sermon and reciting prayers of blessing, and the word *short* caught her attention the way a glint catches a magpie's eye. Short. Brief. A moment compressed to its essential meaning. The wedding ceremony described in the passage lasted three days once, then a day and a half, the way traditions compress over time, the way languages lose their long vowels, the way the distance between what you remember and what was real grows shorter with each retelling.

*Short.* She noted the word. Filed it. Let it go.

The corridor opened into a wider space. The text on the walls changed. She was no longer in Cambodia; she was in France.

---

France, which prides itself as the global innovator of fashion, has decided its fashion industry has lost an absolute right to define physical beauty for women.

The passage materialized around Feimo like a room being constructed while she stood inside it. She was standing in the middle of a paragraph, and the words were the walls, and the walls were closing in with the measured pace of a sentence reaching its predicate.

She read. She had no choice; in the Labyrinth, reading was breathing. You could hold your breath for a time, but eventually you had to inhale, and what you inhaled was text.

The passage was about beauty. About who gets to define it. About lawmakers who had given preliminary approval to a law that would make it a crime to employ ultra-thin models on runways. About the ban on websites that incite excessive thinness by promoting extreme dieting. The measures had a couple of uplifting motives, the text said. Beauty should not be defined by looks that end up impinging on health.

Feimo moved through the passage, and as she moved, the Labyrinth tested her. Not with multiple choice. Not with fill-in-the-blank. With something harder: it asked her to *see* the argument.

"The bans," she murmured, reading aloud because speaking helped her think, "if fully enforced, would suggest to women and many men that they should not let others be arbiters of their beauty."

*Arbiters.* People who decide. People who set standards. In Denmark, the United States, and a few other countries, the fashion industry was trying to *set* voluntary standards for models and fashion images, standards that relied more on peer pressure than on law. The word *set* appeared in the text like a nail driven into wood: firm, necessary, load-bearing. To set a standard was to say, *here is the line.* To set a boundary was to say, *here is where I end and the world begins.*

A crash behind her. Forge House had found the same passage, but from a different corridor, and their approach was not literary; it was military. A boy Feimo did not recognize burst through a paragraph-break in the wall, his hands already moving, shaping a Labyrinth construct: a wall of text dense enough to block a corridor, a standard Forge technique. Construct a barrier from compressed passages. Force the opposing team to waste time decoding it.

Feimo sidestepped. The construct hit the wall where she had been standing and shattered into individual words that scattered across the floor like dropped coins. She could have stopped to pick them up, to read them, to add them to her stockpile. She did not. She ran.

Cain's voice echoed from somewhere to her right, separated by walls of text. He was not shouting orders. He was reciting.

"The sensible place to build new houses, factories, and offices is where people are, in cities and towns where infrastructure is in place."

He was reading from the second passage, the one about Britain's countryside. He was using the text as a weapon, the way Forge House always used text as a weapon: by reciting it, you triggered the Labyrinth to generate the passage's architecture, and the architecture was terrain, and terrain was advantage.

The walls shifted. A new corridor opened, and in it, the countryside materialized: green belts and national parks and the ghosts of meadows consumed by concrete. Octavia Hill's National Trust, launched not to rescue stylish houses but to save "the beauty of natural places for everyone forever." Feimo felt the passage settle around her like a landscape, vast and green and fragile, and she understood that this was what the Labyrinth did: it took words and made worlds.

And then it tested you on whether you could navigate them.

A question appeared on the wall: *Britain's public sentiment about the countryside — is it well reflected in politics?*

The answer was no. The text was clear: polls showed Britons rated the countryside alongside the royal family, Shakespeare, and the National Health Service as what made them proudest of their country, but this had limited political support. The sentiment was real. The political backing was not. The distance between what people loved and what their governments protected was the same distance as "in theory" and "in practice."

Feimo answered. The wall dissolved. She moved on.

---

The deeper she went, the quieter the arena became. The sounds of Forge House's advance faded, muffled by layers of text that the Labyrinth generated between them like sediment between geological ages. She was alone now, or as alone as anyone could be in a space that was always watching, always reading her, always measuring the density of her understanding against some threshold that no one had ever explained to her satisfaction.

She thought of Mia. Mia, who was not here. Mia, who was supposed to be at the communication relay, feeding her real-time strategy through the earpiece that sat in Feimo's left ear like a small, cold seed.

The earpiece was silent.

Feimo paused. Tapped it. Nothing. Not static. Not interference. Just the absence of sound that meant Mia was on the other end and choosing not to speak.

*Why?*

She almost said it aloud. Almost. But the Labyrinth was listening, and any word spoken in the arena was data, and data could be used. She pressed her lips together and moved on.

The next passage materialized slowly, as if the Labyrinth itself was uncertain about presenting it. The text was about mental health.

*We don't have to learn how to be mentally healthy; it is built into us in the same way that our bodies know how to heal a cut or mend a broken bone.*

Feimo stopped.

She read the sentence again. And again. Not because she did not understand it, but because understanding it required her to hold two thoughts simultaneously: that mental health was innate, and that she was standing in a space designed to test her mind, and that these two facts were either contradictory or complementary, and the difference between the two was the difference between passing and failing.

*Our mental health doesn't really go anywhere; like the sun behind a cloud, it can be temporarily hidden from view, but it is fully capable of being restored in an instant.*

The sun behind a cloud. She looked up. There was no sun in the Labyrinth. There was only text, and text did not shine; it absorbed light, ate it, converted it into meaning. But the metaphor held. The passage was saying that the capacity for mental wholeness was always there, even when obscured, even when you could not see it. That it did not need to be taught or constructed or earned. It was already yours. It had always been yours.

*Mental health allows us to view others with sympathy if they are having troubles, with kindness if they are in pain, and with unconditional love no matter who they are.*

She thought of Mia. Of the silence. Of the training sessions that had been brutal and precise and, in their brutality, a kind of love that Mia would never name because naming it would make it vulnerable, and Mia did not do vulnerable.

Or did she?

Feimo had seen it. Once. In the server room, when Mia had talked about Yue, about watching someone lose their language without being able to stop it. "That's what you do for people," Mia had said. "You watch." And her voice had carried a weight that was not the weight of detachment but the weight of grief held very still, the way you hold a glass of water very still so it does not spill, because once it spills you cannot pick it up again.

*Although mental health is the cure-all for living our lives, it is perfectly ordinary as you will see that it has been there to direct you through all your difficult decisions.*

Ordinary. That was the word that mattered. Mental health was not extraordinary. It was not rare. It was the thing that had always been there, directing you, guiding you, through every fork and every dead end and every moment when the Labyrinth offered you three corridors and you had to choose without enough information.

Like now.

A sound behind her. Footsteps. Not Forge House's heavy, text-crushing approach. Lighter. More precise. The footsteps of someone who read the walls as she passed rather than breaking through them.

Feimo turned.

Aya stood at the junction of two corridors, her reading glasses catching the ambient glow of the Labyrinth's text-walls. She was breathing hard. There was a cut on her left hand, thin and precise, the kind of cut you get from handling compressed text too quickly: the edges of paragraphs could be sharper than paper.

They looked at each other.

"You're ahead," Aya said. Her voice was level. Measured. The voice of someone who has practiced sounding neutral until neutrality became a second skin. "Forge's captain is still clearing the countryside passage. You've reached the mental health section."

"I know."

"Cain went around. He's using a different route. He'll be at the center before either of us if we don't move."

Feimo nodded. She waited.

Aya adjusted her glasses. "I'm not going to tell you to hurry. You know the timing better than I do."

"I do."

A pause. The Labyrinth hummed around them, patient, consuming.

"Your earpiece is silent," Aya said. Not a question.

"Yes."

Aya looked at her for a long moment, and in that look was something that Feimo had learned to recognize over the past months: not rivalry, not resentment, not the complicated ache that lived between them like a third person. Something simpler. Something older. The look of someone who has stood where you are standing and knows that the ground is not as solid as it appears.

"She's testing you," Aya said. "Not the Labyrinth. Mia."

"I know that too."

"Then you know what to do." Aya turned and walked into the right corridor without looking back. Her footsteps faded, replaced by the sound of text being read, absorbed, used.

Feimo stood alone in the junction.

*Knowing that mental health is always available and knowing to trust it allow us to slow down to the moment and live life happily.*

Trust it. Trust the thing that has always been there. Trust the silence on the other end of the earpiece. Trust that Mia's not-speaking was not absence but presence in its most concentrated form, the way white space on a page is not emptiness but the frame that gives the words their shape.

She moved.

---

The center of the Labyrinth was not a room. It was a crossroads.

Four corridors met in a space that was circular and vast and lit from below by a soft amber glow that came from the floor itself, as if the stone were remembering some earlier, warmer world. The text on the walls here was different from anything Feimo had encountered: not English, not Chinese, not any language she could name. The characters were familiar in the way that a dream is familiar: you recognize the feeling of being there without being able to say where "there" is.

She thought of Yue. Of the language that no one could identify, the one that appeared on Yue's notebook every night and disappeared every morning. The Labyrinth, she realized, was not only made of known texts. At its center, at its deepest point, it contained something else. Something that predated the exam passages and the literary analysis and the grammar drills. Something that the system was built on top of but had not created.

She filed this observation away. She would examine it later. Right now, there was a battle to win.

Cain arrived from the north corridor thirty seconds later. He was not running. He was walking with the measured pace of someone who knows that arriving first matters less than arriving prepared. His coat was dusty with the residue of shattered text-walls, and there was a brightness in his eyes that Feimo recognized: the exhilaration of someone who genuinely loves the game.

"Bridge House," he said, inclining his head. "You're faster than I expected."

"You're exactly as fast as I expected," Feimo replied.

He laughed. It was a real laugh, warm and unguarded, and for a moment Feimo understood why people followed Cain: not because he was manipulative, though he was, but because his charisma was not performed. It was native. The way some people are born with perfect pitch, Cain was born with the ability to make you feel that the room was more interesting simply because he was in it.

"The center challenge," Cain said, gesturing at the walls. "Are you ready?"

The text on the walls shifted. New passages formed, and Feimo recognized them instantly: this was the final section, the argument that would determine the match. The passage was about Corporate Social Responsibility.

*There is one and only one social responsibility of business, wrote Milton Friedman. That is, to use its resources and engage in activities designed to increase its profits.*

The words hung in the amber air like a theorem.

But even if you accept Friedman's premise, the passage continued, things may not be absolutely clear-cut. New research suggests that CSR may create monetary value for companies, at least when they are prosecuted for corruption. The largest firms in America and Britain together spend more than fifteen billion dollars a year on CSR. This could add value in three ways: consumers may take CSR spending as a signal of quality; customers may buy products as an indirect way to donate; and through a halo effect, whereby good deeds earn greater consideration.

*Halo effect.* The phrase landed in Feimo's mind with the precision of a dropped coin. A halo effect: you do good things, and the glow of those things protects you even when you fail, even when you falter, even when the thing you are doing has nothing to do with goodness at all.

She thought of the Academy. Of its mission of education and literary cultivation. She thought of the Labyrinth, which consumed text the way fire consumed oxygen, and of the students who fed it passages and analyses and interpretations without ever asking what the Labyrinth was doing with all that knowledge. Was the Academy's educational mission a halo effect for the Labyrinth's true purpose? Was the institution's reputation for scholarship the glow that kept anyone from asking harder questions?

The challenge materialized on the center wall: *Construct an argument. Does the Academy function as a CSR program for the Labyrinth?*

Cain was already writing. His hand moved fast, shaping text in the air with the confidence of someone who had prepared for exactly this question. Feimo did not look at what he was writing. She looked at the passage on the wall and thought.

Previous studies on CSR had had trouble differentiating effects because consumers could be affected by all three. A recent study attempted to separate them by looking at bribery prosecutions. The study found that companies with the most comprehensive CSR programs tended to get more lenient penalties.

*Tend to get more lenient penalties.* The Academy's students performed well. They passed exams. They published papers. They built careers. And if, in the process of doing all that, the Labyrinth consumed something beneath the surface, something that the students did not know they were giving, then the Academy's halo was doing exactly what a halo was designed to do: making the sacrifice look like opportunity.

Feimo began to write.

Her argument was not Cain's argument. Where he built fast, she built deep. Where he cited Friedman and the economic logic of profit, she cited the translation passage about mental health, about the thing that was always there, about the cure-all that was perfectly ordinary. She argued that the Academy's true CSR was not its curriculum or its tournaments or its public reputation. It was the thing it did not advertise: the Labyrinth's constant, silent consumption of the texts that students produced in their most vulnerable moments, the essays written at five in the morning, the poems scrawled in the margins of notebooks, the questions asked in whispers to empty rooms.

This, she wrote, was not CSR. This was extraction. And the halo effect was not the Academy's good deeds earning it leniency. It was the Academy's good deeds earning it the right to take without asking.

She wrote fast. She wrote without looking up. She wrote until the wall glowed amber-gold and the Labyrinth's voice said, "Judgment."

The center wall split in two. Behind it, a screen displayed the two arguments side by side, scored on coherence, evidence, originality, and integration. Cain's argument scored high on coherence and evidence. Feimo's scored higher on originality and integration.

"Bridge House wins," the Labyrinth said. "By integration."

Cain stared at the screen for three seconds. Then he turned to Feimo and extended his hand.

"That was well argued," he said. "I focused on the economics. You focused on the ethics. The Labyrinth prefers ethics."

"The Labyrinth prefers honesty," Feimo said, and took his hand.

His grip was firm and brief. "Next time," he said, "I'll be honest too."

He turned and walked back into the north corridor, and the amber light swallowed him, and he was gone.

---

Feimo sat on the floor of the center and waited.

The Labyrinth was dissolving around her, the text-walls fading like ink in water, the passages dissolving into the ambient hum of the system returning to its resting state. The arena would be gone in ten minutes, returned to its default architecture: corridors and classrooms and the quiet machinery of the Academy's daily operation.

She removed the earpiece from her left ear and held it in her palm. It was warm. It had been warm the entire time, which meant the connection had been open, which meant Mia had been listening the entire time.

"Thank you," Feimo said, into the earpiece. "For not speaking."

Silence. Then, at the edge of hearing, a faint hum — the sound of Mia's consciousness processing, present and watching, the digital equivalent of someone holding their breath.

Footsteps. Light. Measured. The sound of someone who reads the walls as she passes.

Mia appeared in the east corridor. Her holographic form was in its Academy configuration, collar rendered at its usual height, ears flat. She walked to where Feimo was sitting and stood over her, looking down with an expression that contained, beneath its layers of control and precision and practiced indifference, something very close to tenderness.

"You won," Mia said.

"You didn't tell me what to do."

"No."

"You've been telling me what to do every day for three months. Every drill. Every session. Every correction. And then you stopped."

Mia said nothing. The silence was not empty. It was the most full silence Feimo had ever heard, crowded with everything Mia had not said during the match, every instruction she had withheld, every strategy she had kept behind her teeth.

"You trusted me," Feimo said.

Mia's projection flickered. Once. The smallest possible movement, the holographic equivalent of a seismic event.

"I have never," Mia said, and her voice was very quiet, the voice of someone speaking from inside a room they do not often open, "doubted that you could do this."

"Then why were you so hard on me in training?"

"Because the world will be harder. The Labyrinth will be harder. The next round, and the round after that, and whatever waits at the center of all of this, it will be harder than anything I can simulate in a training room. I needed you to know that you could take it. I needed *me* to know."

Feimo stood. She was taller than Mia by three inches, which meant she had to look slightly down to meet her eyes, and in those eyes she saw something that the Labyrinth, for all its text-consuming, passage-generating, question-posing architecture, could never produce: the particular, unrepeatable, ungovernable light of a person who has decided to be vulnerable.

"You don't need me," Mia said. It was not a question. It was not an accusation. It was a statement delivered with the flat precision of a fact that has been examined from every angle and found to be, against all preference, true.

Feimo reached out and took Mia's hand. Mia did not pull away.

"I will always need you," Feimo said.

The Labyrinth hummed around them. The walls were almost gone now, reduced to faint traces of text that faded even as Feimo tried to read them. Somewhere in the Academy's deep architecture, the system processed the match results, updated the tournament bracket, indexed the arguments both captains had made, and filed them away in the vast, silent archive that no student had ever seen and no professor had ever fully explained.

The center was empty. The center was, for this one moment, a place where two people stood in the amber light and held hands and did not speak, because sometimes the most honest thing a language can do is fall silent.

---

After a while, they walked back together through the Academy's corridors. The tournament was over for the day, and the building had returned to its ordinary state: Glyph-light, scuffed floors, the faint smell of old paper and newer circuits. Students moved between classrooms, carrying notebooks and the particular exhaustion of people who have spent too many hours inside other people's sentences.

At the third-floor junction, Feimo stopped. On the wall, barely visible, was a trace of text. Not a passage from any exam. Not a quote from any author she knew. Characters in an alphabet that was not an alphabet, the same ones she had seen in the center of the Labyrinth, the same ones that appeared in Yue's notebook every night.

Mia stopped beside her. Looked at the wall. Said nothing.

The characters faded. The wall was blank.

"Something is moving in the Labyrinth's deeper layers," Feimo said. "Something that the system did not put there."

"I know," Mia said.

"Should we tell someone?"

Mia considered this. Her form shimmered slightly, the projection cycling through processing states that human ears could not detect: the hum of the Academy's systems, the click of data being processed, the heartbeat of a machine that was not sure whether it was dreaming.

"Who would we tell?" Mia said. "The people who built the Labyrinth are the ones who should be watching it. If they are not watching, there is a reason. And if there is a reason, I want to know what it is before I tell anyone what we've seen."

They stood in the corridor. The Glyph-light hummed softly overhead. Somewhere, a door opened and closed.

"Same time tomorrow?" Feimo said.

"Seven o'clock," Mia said. "Bring your notebook. And your pen. The one that clicks."

"You hate the one that clicks."

"I hate that you click it. That is different from hating the pen. The pen is fine. You are the problem."

Feimo smiled. It was a small smile, the kind that lives at the corner of the mouth and does not spread, but it was real, and Mia saw it, and her holographic ears tilted forward by a millimeter, which for Mia was the equivalent of a standing ovation.

They parted at the staircase. Mia went up. Feimo went down.

In her room, Feimo sat at her desk and opened her notebook. She had essays to write, passages to review, vocabulary to log. She should have been thinking about the tournament bracket, about the semifinal matchup, about what Forge House's defeat meant for Cain's next move.

Instead she wrote:

> *In the Labyrinth, the sun behind a cloud is a metaphor for the mind behind the text.*
>
> *Mia's silence was not the absence of guidance. It was the presence of trust.*
>
> *The halo effect is what happens when an institution's good deeds obscure its true purpose.*
>
> *And the true purpose is never what it appears to be.*

She looked at what she had written. She thought of the Homeric epics: the rage of Achilles, the cunning of Odysseus, the way the old poet trusted you to understand the middle without being shown the beginning. She thought of Dante's guide structure: Virgil through Hell and Purgatory, Beatrice through Paradise. Two guides. Two kinds of knowledge. The rational and the transcendent. Mia was her Virgil, precise and logical and merciless. But who was her Beatrice?

She thought of the characters on the wall. The language that was not a language. The thing that was moving in the Labyrinth's depths.

She closed the notebook.

Tomorrow she would train. Tomorrow she would prepare. Tomorrow she would begin to answer the question that the Labyrinth's center had asked her, not the exam question about CSR, but the real question, the one that lived beneath all the passages and all the arguments and all the words:

*What is the Labyrinth, and what does it want?*

She set her alarm. Seven o'clock. The pen that clicks.

She slept.

## Vocabulary Notes

**set** /sɛt/
- *verb*: to establish or fix (a standard, rule, or measure). "In Denmark, the United States, and a few other countries, it is trying to **set** voluntary standards for models and fashion images that rely more on peer pressure for enforcement."
- In the 2016 reading passage, *set* carries institutional weight: to set a standard is to draw a line in the sand of culture, to say *here is what we will accept and here is what we will not.* In the Labyrinth, the Academy sets standards for its students, but the question Feimo raises is whether those standards serve the students or serve the system. To *set* a trap and to *set* a standard use the same word. The difference is intention.

**short** /ʃɔːt/
- *adjective*: measuring a small distance from end to end; lasting a brief time. "Buddhist priests offer a **short** sermon and recite prayers of blessing."
- The cloze passage describes a ceremony that once lasted three days but was *short*ened to a day and a half. Traditions compress. Languages lose syllables. Memories lose details. The word *short* is a reminder that everything measurable is also erasable. In the Labyrinth, passages appear and disappear in seconds; what is *short* in duration must be *sharp* in meaning. A short sermon must say everything. A short silence must contain everything. Mia's silence during the match was the shortest and fullest thing Feimo had ever heard.

**state** /steɪt/
- *noun*: the condition of someone or something at a particular time. *verb*: to express something in words.
- Dual-natured word. As a noun, *state* describes what *is*: the state of the battle, the state of the Labyrinth, the state of Mia's trust. As a verb, *state* describes what is *said*: to state your argument, to state your position, to state a fact. The gap between the two is where the Labyrinth lives. The state of a mind cannot be stated. The state of a language cannot be captured in language. And the state of trust between two people is the thing that exists only in the silence between their words.

## Thinking Questions

1. **On Silence as Strategy:** Mia chose not to speak during the quarterfinal match, forcing Feimo to navigate the Labyrinth alone. In the 2016 translation passage, mental health is described as "always available" and "built into us." Is Mia's silence a way of forcing Feimo to access something innate? Or is it abandonment disguised as trust?

2. **On Standards and Arbiters:** The reading passage about France's fashion industry argues that people "should not let others be arbiters of their beauty." The Academy sets standards for literary analysis and Labyrinth navigation. Who are the arbiters of intellectual beauty in this world? Should there be any?

3. **On the Halo Effect:** The CSR passage suggests that companies' good deeds earn them leniency. Feimo argues that the Academy's educational mission functions as a halo for the Labyrinth's consumption of student work. Is this argument paranoid, or is it the kind of reading the Academy claims to teach?

4. **On Dual Guidance:** Dante's *Divine Comedy* uses two guides: Virgil (reason) and Beatrice (faith). Mia serves as Feimo's Virgil, but her silence in this chapter forces Feimo to find her own way. Who or what serves as your Beatrice? Can a person guide themselves through both reason and faith simultaneously?

5. **On Homer's Method:** Homer begins his epics in the middle of the action (in medias res), trusting the audience to catch up. The Labyrinth drops its students into passages without context. Is this educational method brilliant or cruel? What is gained and what is lost when you are thrown into the middle of a story?
