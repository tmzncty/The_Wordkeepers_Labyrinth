## Executive Summary

Ch.23–27 is the **Tournament Arc** — five chapters covering buildup (Ch.23), quarterfinal (Ch.24), semifinal + post-mortem (Ch.25), finals (Ch.26), and the Deep Labyrinth door discovery (Ch.27). The arc's structure is sound: escalation from training through competition to revelation. The emotional peaks land — Mia's "You did well" (Ch.25), the hand-holding after the quarterfinal (Ch.24), and the Chinese door reveal (Ch.27) are the strongest scenes in the project to date.

However, the batch has **two P0 worldbuilding contradictions** that constitute hard blockers:

1. **Feimo's House** — The worldbuilding assigns her to **Forge House** (§4.1). All five chapters place her in **Bridge House**. This is not a minor detail; it changes her combat style, her dormitory, her peer group, and her rival dynamics.
2. **Mia's nature** — The worldbuilding defines Mia as a **holographic AI / digitized consciousness** (§4.2). The chapters write her as a **physical human with cat ears** — she has a body, heartbeats, a jaw that clenches, ears that turn pink, legs that go unsteady. There is no trace of her AI nature in any of these five chapters.

Both issues suggest the chapters were drafted against an **earlier version of the worldbuilding** that predated the v2 restructure. The writing itself is strong, but it is strong writing attached to the wrong chassis.

Secondary concerns: the hugging cloze passage recurs near-identically across Ch.25 and Ch.26 (flagged as a concern since Batch 3, now escalating), the Mauna Kea/GDP reading passages are previewed in Ch.25 and then deployed in Ch.26 without enough differentiation, and the Labyrinth's physical description contradicts the worldbuilding in several sensory details.

**Counts**: 2 × P0, 8 × P1, 10 × P2

**Verdict**: Ch.24 and Ch.7 are the strongest chapters. Ch.23 and Ch.25 need revision for passage recycling. Ch.26 needs revision for the cloze duplication. All five chapters need the P0 House and Mia-nature contradictions resolved before the batch can pass.

## P0 — Critical (Hard Blockers)

### P0-1: Feimo's House — Forge vs. Bridge (All Chapters)

**Worldbuilding §4.1**: *"Identity: First-year student, House of the Forge."*

**Chapters**: Feimo is consistently placed in **Bridge House** throughout all five chapters.

Evidence:
- Ch.24 title: "Quarterfinal — Bridge vs Forge" — Feimo fights *for* Bridge House *against* Forge House
- Ch.25 L19: "Noir stood in the corridor… Noir's ears were flat" — Noir brings food to Feimo's room in Bridge House dormitory
- Ch.25 L31: "You're the first Bridge House student in six years to reach the finals"
- Ch.25 L217–219: The reading passage is assigned as "comparative analysis" and connects to Bridge House's cross-cultural specialty
- Ch.26 L9: "The final round of the Inter-House Academic Tournament" — Feimo represents Bridge House against Aya (Forge House)
- Ch.26 L188: "Forge House students filled the left semicircle" — the opposing side

The worldbuilding §2.4 describes Bridge House's specialty as **translation, cross-cultural literacy, bilingual comprehension** — which aligns well with Feimo's bilingual (English/Chinese) identity and her role as a translator between languages and cultures. Meanwhile, §2.5 describes Forge House's specialty as **writing, composition, creative expression** — which does not align with what these chapters show Feimo doing (close reading, analytical extraction, textual navigation).

**Diagnosis**: The chapters appear to have been drafted against an earlier version of the worldbuilding in which Feimo was sorted into Bridge House. The v2 restructure (§4.1) moved her to Forge House, but these chapters were not updated.

**Resolution required**: Either (a) update §4.1 to restore Feimo to Bridge House, which would also require updating her catgirl trait table (§4.1) to remove "natural Bridge talent for cross-linguistic instinct" as a secondary trait and make it primary, and re-examine her relationship with Noir (who is Forge House); or (b) rewrite the tournament arc to place Feimo in Forge House. Option (a) seems more consistent with these chapters and with Feimo's thematic arc as a translator between English and Chinese.

**Cascade check**: If Feimo is moved to Bridge House in the worldbuilding, verify:
- Noir (§4.4) is Forge House — their mentorship crosses Houses, which is fine but should be explicitly noted as unusual
- Ling (§4.3) is Mirror House — rivalry dynamics unchanged
- Cain (§4.8) is Weave House — rivalry dynamics unchanged
- The Forge House gymnasium training scene in Ch.23 would need to be recontextualized (Feimo training in another House's facility)

### P0-2: Mia's Nature — AI Hologram vs. Physical Human (All Chapters)

**Worldbuilding §4.2**: *"Identity: The Academy's central intelligence — an AI that manages the Library… Appearance: Mia manifests as a holographic projection within the Academy… Her form flickers slightly at the edges when she's processing complex data."*

**Chapters**: Mia is written as a **physical human with cat ears and a body** throughout all five chapters.

Evidence (partial list — the issue is pervasive):

| Location | Description | Conflict |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Ch.23 L9 | "Mia stepped into the light… wearing black compression top, black training pants, no shoes. Her feet on the rubberized floor were silent." | A hologram has no feet, no shoes, no weight on a floor |
| Ch.23 L25 | "her movements, her breathing, the way she materialized behind Feimo" | Breathing implies lungs; "materialized" is ambiguous |
| Ch.23 L43 | "Feimo's heart rate was elevated… She could feel it in her throat" | Mia can also be physically felt — she adjusts Feimo's guard position |
| Ch.23 L49 | "Mia allowed breaks… the human body required glycogen replenishment" | Mia herself eats an energy bar (L49: "Mia bit into her energy bar") |
| Ch.24 L23 | "her cat ears flat against her skull" | Mia has physical cat ears |
| Ch.25 L147–148 | "Feimo leaned forward and pressed her palm against Mia's forehead… Mia flinched" | Physical contact — Feimo touches Mia's forehead, Mia flinches |
| Ch.25 L173 | "Mia's heartbeat, slightly elevated, slightly uneven" | Mia has a heartbeat |
| Ch.26 L252–254 | "Mia's ears moved… a slight downward tilt" | Physical ears with emotional expressiveness |
| Ch.27 L13 | "five months of sitting in a sixteen-degree server room, watching her friend's language disintegrate" | Mia sits in a room — she has a body to sit with |

The worldbuilding's Mia is a **digitized consciousness** who believes she is an AI, manifests as a hologram, and has sealed human memories. The chapters' Mia is a **physical catgirl** who trains in gyms, eats energy bars, has a heartbeat, and flinches when touched. These are not minor characterization differences — they are fundamentally different character architectures.

**Diagnosis**: Same as P0-1. The chapters were drafted against an earlier worldbuilding in which Mia was a physical character (possibly a fellow student or senior). The v2 restructure made her an AI / digitized consciousness, but the chapters were not updated.

**Resolution required**: This is the more complex of the two P0s. Options:
- **(a) Update worldbuilding**: Revert Mia to a physical character. This would sacrifice one of the v2 worldbuilding's most interesting design decisions (the sealed-memory mystery, the "am I real" romance tension). The author should decide whether the chapters' physical Mia or the worldbuilding's digital Mia is the intended version.
- **(b) Rewrite chapters**: Convert all physical-Mia references to holographic-Mia. This would be extensive but would preserve the richer character architecture. Key adjustments: Mia cannot eat, cannot be touched (or touching her produces a "cool, not-quite-there" sensation), her emotional states manifest as form flickering rather than heartbeat/ear movement, and her "cat ears" would be a holographic affectation rather than biological.
- **(c) Hybrid**: Mia's holographic form becomes increasingly physical as the story progresses, explaining why she seems "more real" by Ch.27. This would require seeding the progression in earlier chapters and establishing the rules.

**Strong recommendation**: The author should decide this *before* drafting Ch.28+. Every additional chapter written with physical-Mia makes the eventual correction more expensive.

## P1 — Major Issues

### P1-1: Hugging Cloze Passage — Near-Identical Recurrence in Ch.25 and Ch.26

The **Carnegie Mellon hugging/cold study** cloze passage appears in both Ch.25 (L44–61) and Ch.26 (L109–121). The content is nearly word-for-word identical:

**Ch.25 L47**: *"Over four hundred healthy adults were examined for the effects of perceived social support and the receipt of hugs on their susceptibility to developing the common cold after being **exposed** to the virus."*

**Ch.26 L115**: *"a study at Carnegie Mellon University involving over four hundred healthy adults, examining the effects of perceived social support and the receipt of hugs on susceptibility to the common cold."*

Both chapters use the same answers (*exposed*, *besides*, *connected*, *host*, *calculated*, *even*, *symptoms*, *attribute*, *because*, *remained*, *influenced*). Both chapters draw the same emotional parallel (Feimo thinking about Mia while filling in oxytocin-related blanks). Both chapters end with Feimo reflecting on the "measurable effects of the immeasurable."

This is the **third consecutive batch** where passage recycling has been flagged. In Batch 3, the Simonsohn passage appeared in three consecutive chapters. The problem has not improved — it has migrated to new passages.

**Fix**: The Ch.25 version (standalone homework exercise, integrated with the post-mortem) is the stronger deployment. The Ch.26 version (supplementary cloze during the finals duel) should use a **different passage entirely** — ideally one that connects to the Mauna Kea / GDP theme rather than repeating the hugging study. The finals cloze could use a passage about, for example, the science of measurement, the limits of quantification, or the relationship between observation and the observed — themes that would reinforce the close-reading duel's passage content.

### P1-2: Mauna Kea Passage — Preview in Ch.25, Deployment in Ch.26

The Mauna Kea / Thirty Meter Telescope reading passage is **previewed** in Ch.25 (L217–227, assigned as "comparative analysis") and then **deployed** as the finals passage in Ch.26 (L33–63). The GDP/Kennedy passage follows the same pattern (Ch.25 L229–234 → Ch.26 L75–82).

**The problem**: Feimo has already analyzed these passages in Ch.25. When they appear again in Ch.26, the reader has the sense of watching someone retake an exam they've already studied the answers for. The finals should feel like a *new* challenge, not a replay.

**Fix**: Either (a) remove the Ch.25 preview entirely and let the finals passage be the reader's first encounter, or (b) keep the Ch.25 preview but make the Ch.26 finals passage a **different text** on the same theme — same topic (sacred land vs. science, limits of measurement) but different specific passage. This would test whether Feimo can apply her analytical framework to unfamiliar material, which is a more interesting dramatic challenge.

### P1-3: Labyrinth Physical Description — Fluorescent Lights

**Ch.27 L329**: *"In the sub-basement corridor, under the fluorescent lights that buzzed at 50 Hz, the exact frequency of institutional normalcy…"*

**Ch.26 L310–313**: *"She passed the spot on the third floor… She pressed her hand against the wall. The plaster was cool."* (Implies ordinary building construction, not Labyrinth architecture)

**Worldbuilding §1.4**: *"Light: No sun, no electric lights. Illumination comes from the **Glyphs** embedded in the walls."*

The worldbuilding explicitly states the Academy has **no electric lights** — illumination comes from Glyphs. The chapters describe fluorescent lighting, ordinary plaster walls, and the physical sensations of a conventional school building rather than a structure carved into the Labyrinth's text-substrate.

**Fix**: Replace fluorescent lights with Glyph-based ambient glow (e.g., "under the Glyph-light that hummed at a frequency calibrated for archival work"). If the sub-basement is meant to be closer to the real-world Academy building rather than the Labyrinth itself, this distinction needs to be established earlier — the worldbuilding says the Academy is **inside** the Labyrinth (§1.2), so there is no "real building" to fall back on.

### P1-4: Labyrinth Hum — Inconsistent Frequency

**Ch.27 L55**: *"The Academy's constant frequency was 50 Hz, a clean, stable tone."*

**Ch.26 L161**: *"The Labyrinth hummed at forty-seven hertz."*

**Ch.26 L372**: *"the Academy hummed at forty-seven hertz"*

**Ch.26 L254**: *"the Labyrinth's hum at forty-seven hertz"*

**Worldbuilding §1.4**: *"A low hum — the aggregate of millions of people reading — permeates everything. In the Academy, the hum is steady and calm, like distant surf."*

The worldbuilding describes the hum as an organic, variable phenomenon (the aggregate of reading activity), not a fixed frequency. The chapters assign it specific Hz values — and those values are inconsistent between chapters (50 Hz in Ch.27 vs. 47 Hz in Ch.26).

**Fix**: Either (a) remove specific Hz values and describe the hum qualitatively ("the Academy's constant hum, that low vibration that meant the system was processing"), or (b) establish a single consistent frequency and use it throughout. Option (a) is more consistent with the worldbuilding's organic description.

### P1-5: Noir's House Placement — Ambiguous in Ch.25

**Worldbuilding §4.4**: *"Identity: Nekomimi, third-year student, House of the Forge."*

In Ch.25, Noir appears in Feimo's Bridge House dormitory (L19–29), brings her breakfast, sits in her room, and acts as if she lives nearby. If Noir is Forge House and Feimo is Bridge House (as the chapters state), this cross-House intimacy would be notable — the worldbuilding establishes House dormitories as separate spaces (§1.2). No explanation is given for why a Forge House senior has easy access to a Bridge House dormitory at breakfast time.

**Fix**: Add a line establishing that cross-House visitation is common, or that Noir has some legitimate reason to be in Bridge House (shared class, club membership, etc.). Alternatively, if Feimo's House is resolved (P0-1) and she becomes Forge House, this issue resolves automatically.

### P1-6: Tournament Format — Contradicts Worldbuilding

**Worldbuilding §3.3**: Glyph Battles are **two-person duels** using Glyph activation (speaking words aloud with intent). Victory by HP depletion, forfeit, or instructor scoring.

**Chapters**: The tournament is described as a **team-based extraction exercise** — teams of two navigate Labyrinth corridors, answer reading comprehension questions, and reach the center. Combat is permitted but secondary. The final (Ch.26) is a **close-reading duel** — two contestants write analytical essays and are scored by a professor.

The worldbuilding's Glyph Battle system (activation of crystalline Glyphs by speaking them aloud, HP-based combat, semantic shockwaves) does not appear anywhere in these five chapters. The tournament is entirely about reading comprehension, analytical writing, and textual navigation — closer to an academic decathlon than the magical dueling system described in §3.3.

**Fix**: This may be intentional (the tournament tests different skills than Glyph Battles). If so, the distinction should be made explicit — perhaps a character notes that the Championship is not a Glyph Battle tournament but an extraction/analysis competition, and that Glyph Battles are a separate inter-House activity. If unintentional, the tournament needs to incorporate the Glyph Battle system described in §3.3.

### P1-7: "Tower House" Reference in Ch.25

**Ch.25 L7**: *"Her right shoulder throbbed where the Tower House student, Voss, had landed a Resonance strike."*

The worldbuilding establishes **four Houses**: Weave, Mirror, Bridge, Forge. There is no "Tower House." This may be a naming error for one of the existing Houses, or it may be a remnant of an earlier draft with a different House system.

**Fix**: Replace "Tower House" with the correct House name. Based on Voss's described combat style (aggressive, right-flank attacks, "system optimized to counter attackers"), he is most likely **Forge House** or possibly **Weave House**.

### P1-8: Ch.26 Final Score — Feimo's House Victory vs. Individual Victory

**Ch.26 L188**: *"Forge House students filled the left semicircle, their expressions ranging from anxious to aggressively supportive."*

But earlier in the same chapter, Aya is described as Feimo's opponent. If Feimo is Bridge House (as the chapters state), then Forge House students should be rooting **against** her, not for her. The text says they are "aggressively supportive," which would make sense only if Feimo were Forge House.

After the victory (L188): *"Forge House students were on their feet, producing the specific volume of noise that twenty-eight teenagers could generate when their House had won a competition."*

This confirms the confusion — Forge House students celebrate because "their House" won. If Feimo is Bridge House, this makes no sense. If Feimo is Forge House (as the worldbuilding states), it makes perfect sense.

**Fix**: Resolved by P0-1. If Feimo's House is corrected to align across all sources, this scene is internally consistent.

## P2 — Minor Issues

### P2-1: Ch.23 — Dense Pacing, Seminar-Heavy Structure

Ch.23 is 376 lines / ~6,800 words and contains:
1. Training hall scene with Mia (L1–73)
2. Tournament format explanation (L75–83)
3. Professor Jianmo's Aristotle/Poetics seminar (L87–121)
4. Cambodia passage analysis (L125–137)
5. Cain's Library conversation about Danish fashion charter (L141–173)
6. CSR/halo effect seminar (L177–191)
7. Saturday training session with Collapse simulations (L195–275)
8. Library reflection and structure essay (L279–317)

That's **eight distinct scenes** in one chapter. The Aristotle seminar alone runs 35 lines and delivers three distinct theoretical concepts (mimesis, catharsis, plot structure). The pacing is more like a syllabus than a narrative.

**Fix**: Consider splitting into two chapters: training arc (scenes 1, 2, 7) and intellectual preparation (scenes 3–6, 8). Or cut the CSR seminar (scene 6) — its content is recycled in Ch.24's center challenge.

### P2-2: Ch.23 — Aristotle Seminar Is Expository

Professor Jianmo's lecture (L91–121) reads like a textbook summary of the *Poetics*. The concepts (mimesis, catharsis, plot/character/thought/diction/spectacle/song) are listed rather than dramatized. Feimo takes notes but doesn't interact with the material until Cain's question about partners-as-audience (L113).

**Fix**: Compress the lecture to 15 lines and let the students' reactions carry the exposition. Cain's question is the interesting part — get there faster.

### P2-3: Ch.24 — Opening Paragraph Is Overwritten

**Ch.24 L5–7**: *"The arena was not a place. It was a sentence. Feimo stood at the edge of the Labyrinth's tournament space and watched it form: walls of text rising from the stone floor like steam off summer asphalt, each line a passage from some half-remembered exam, each paragraph a corridor that branched into memory and dead end alike."*

This is beautiful but structurally overpacked: "not a place, it was a sentence" is a strong opening metaphor, but the sentence that follows loads three separate images (steam, passages, corridors) in a single clause. The reader has to hold all three simultaneously before the narrative moves on.

**Fix**: Split into two sentences. Let "The arena was not a place. It was a sentence." stand alone as its own beat. Then describe the formation.

### P2-4: Ch.25 — "You Did Well" Beat Has Redundant Follow-Up

Mia's "You did well" (L137) is the emotional peak of the chapter — three words, no qualifier, devastating. The scene then extends for 45 more lines (L138–182) through Feimo's disbelief, the fever-check comedy, Mia's defensive retreat, and the pink-ears coda. The comedy is effective, but the initial impact of "You did well" dissipates as the scene continues to elaborate.

**Fix**: Consider ending the scene at L161 ("Mia left") and moving the pink-ears detail to a later, quieter moment. The comedy between L138 and L159 is good but could be slightly compressed.

### P2-5: Ch.25 — Email Exercise Feels Detached

The Professor Silence email-writing exercise (L239–261) and the essay exercise (L264–274) are charming but don't advance the plot or deepen the tournament arc. They function as character texture — Feimo writing a love letter to the Academy disguised as a tourist guide. This is lovely but could be a paragraph rather than two pages.

**Fix**: Compress to a single paragraph summary, or move the email to a bonus/appendix section.

### P2-6: Ch.26 — "Approach" / "Argue" / "Able" Tier 1 Vocabulary Tracking

The finals duel tracks Feimo's use of three Tier 1 vocabulary words (*approach*, *argue*, *able*) across the 40-minute writing period. The tracking is effective pedagogically but slightly mechanical narratively — it reads like a progress bar ("Two Tier 1 words used. One remaining." — L83).

**Fix**: Integrate more naturally. Instead of "She would find it. She always did," have Feimo use *able* in her analysis without the reader being told it's the target word. Let the Study Break section at the chapter's end identify the Tier 1 words retroactively.

### P2-7: Ch.27 — "Pilgrim" Terminology Introduced Without Prior Establishment

**Ch.27 L267–279**: Mia introduces the concept of "Pilgrims" — people who carry language into the Labyrinth and are processed by it. *"The Labyrinth processes Pilgrims. That's what we are. Inputs."*

This term does not appear anywhere in the worldbuilding document. It is introduced for the first time in Ch.27 and immediately becomes the basis for Mia's climactic line: *"You're not a Pilgrim. You're a conversation."* The line is excellent, but the term "Pilgrim" needs earlier seeding — perhaps in a lecture scene, or in the tournament format description, or in the worldbuilding itself.

**Fix**: Seed "Pilgrim" in Ch.23's seminar or Ch.25's post-mortem. One line is enough: "Students in the Labyrinth are sometimes called Pilgrims — people who walk through text and are changed by it."

### P2-8: Ch.27 — Enlightenment Room Content Density

The bilingual room (L229–299) contains:
- 37 Chinese passages spanning Shijing to Bei Dao
- English passages from the curriculum
- Enlightenment analysis by Mia
- Shakespeare / Three Unities discussion
- Bei Dao "我不相信" connection to Noir
- Automation text alteration
- A synthesis about two languages coexisting

This is a lot of intellectual content for a single room discovery scene. The scene's emotional power (the wonder of finding something impossible) competes with the analytical load (Mia explaining what each passage means).

**Fix**: Let Feimo absorb the room in silence. Have Mia explain 2–3 passages, not all of them. Save the full inventory for a later research session. The wonder is the point — don't explain it away.

### P2-9: Ch.25 and Ch.26 — Gu Cheng Poem Echo

**Ch.25 L318**: *"Darkness给了我黑色的眼睛. I use them to search for light."*

**Ch.26 L318**: *"Darkness给了我黑色的眼睛. I use them to search for light."*

The same Gu Cheng poem is quoted in both chapters, in nearly identical contexts (Feimo touching a wall where the poem had appeared). The echo may be intentional (showing the poem's persistence in Feimo's memory) but reads as accidental repetition.

**Fix**: Keep it in one chapter. Ch.25 (the post-mortem, reflective chapter) is the better placement. In Ch.26, Feimo can touch the wall without quoting the poem — the gesture alone is sufficient.
