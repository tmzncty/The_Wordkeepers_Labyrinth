# Chapter 39: Theatre of the Absurd & Magical Realism

The stage was empty.

Feimo had not expected to find a theatre on the Labyrinth's eighth level. The descent from the seventh had taken her through a passage that narrowed to shoulder-width, then widened without warning into a space so large that her first step into it produced a sensation of vertigo, as if she had walked to the edge of a cliff in the dark and discovered, by the absence of wind, that the cliff was indoors.

The theatre was carved from stone. Rows of seats rose in a semicircle around a raised platform, and the seats were not empty by accident but by design: each one was occupied by a book, open to a different page, arranged with the precision of an audience that had arrived early and was reading while it waited. The books were in multiple languages: French, German, English, Chinese, Russian, Spanish. Some she recognized. *En attendant Godot. La Cantatrice chauve. Die Nashörner.* Others were unfamiliar, their spines cracked and their pages yellowed, the kind of books that had been read so many times their physical structure had begun to express the content: warped by moisture, softened by handling, the paper thin as skin.

On the stage, someone had painted a tree.

It was not a real tree. It was a single trunk, drawn in white paint on the black backdrop, with two branches that extended to either side and ended in clusters of leaves that were also painted, also white, also stylized. Beneath the tree, two chairs had been placed. On one chair sat a pair of boots. On the other, a hat. Nothing else. No text on the walls, no inscriptions, no bilingual commentary. Just a painted tree, two chairs, and the objects of two people who were not there.

Feimo stood at the edge of the seating area and **reflected** on what she was seeing.

She had studied the knowledge card two days ago. Card #070: Theatre of the Absurd. The card had been precise: a movement of the 1950s, born from the collision of existentialist philosophy and the practical devastation of two world wars. Its defining features were fourfold: anti-plot, anti-character, anti-language, and the theme of absurdity itself. Beckett's *Waiting for Godot* was the canonical example: two men wait for someone who never arrives, and the waiting is the point. Not the arrival. Not the meaning of the wait. The waiting itself, as a condition of human existence.

Ionesco's *The Bald Soprano* was the other pole: a play in which language **reduce**s itself to nonsense, where conversations loop and collapse, where the characters begin as recognizable social types and end as phonemes, as sounds stripped of semantic content. The card had quoted Ionesco: "A man's work is nothing but this slow trek to rediscover, through the detours of art, those two or three great and simple images in whose presence his heart first opened."

Feimo looked at the painted tree and the two empty chairs.

She understood why the Labyrinth had placed this here. The eighth level was deeper than the seventh, and the seventh had been the waste land, the corridor of fragments. This was something else. Not fragmentation but presentation: the theatre presented its emptiness as content. The absence of performers was not a lack. It was the performance.

She walked down the aisle between the rows of book-audiences and stepped onto the stage.

The boots were large. Men's boots, well-worn, the leather cracked at the flex points. The hat was a bowler, slightly too small, as if it had been purchased for a different head. She picked up the hat and turned it over. Inside, someone had written a single line in Chinese:

*"等待的人并不知道自己在等什么。"*

She translated: *The person waiting does not know what they are waiting for.*

She set the hat down and sat in the empty chair.

From this position, the theatre looked different. The books in the seats were not facing the stage. They were facing each other, across the aisles, as if the performance they had come to see was not on the platform but between them, in the spaces where their pages overlapped and their languages met. A conversation in which no one spoke. A dialogue conducted entirely in the act of reading.

This was what Beckett had understood. The structure of *Godot* was not a failure of plot but a refusal of the assumption that plot was necessary. Two men wait. They talk. They consider leaving. They do not leave. They talk again. Nothing happens, twice. And in that nothing, something enormous is present: the texture of time itself, the way a Tuesday afternoon can feel identical to the Tuesday before it, the way repetition is not the absence of change but its own kind of experience, thick and slow and resistant to summary.

The 2022 exam was administered in December.

The room had changed again. Cain's cameras were still there, but additional security measures had been added: identification checks at the door, bag searches, a second set of cameras in the corridors outside. The desks were spaced further apart than regulations required, as if the institution had decided that distance itself was a form of protection. Feimo noted the changes with the attention she gave to everything Cain did: not with anger but with analysis. Each restriction was a data point. Each data point was a thread in a pattern she was still learning to read.

She opened the booklet.

The cloze passage was about plants.

More precisely, it was about the claim that plants possess consciousness: the idea, which took root in the early 2000s, that some aspects of plant behavior could be **compare**d to intelligence in animals. The term "plant neurobiology" had been **coin**ed around this notion. **Though** plants lacked brains, the firing of electrical signals in their stems and leaves triggered responses that **hint**ed at consciousness, researchers had reported.

But the passage argued against this. Plant biology **differ**s so greatly from that of animals that the **evidence** for plant intelligence was inconclusive. Beginning in 2006, some scientists had **argue**d that plants possess neuron-like cells that interact with hormones and neurotransmitters, **form**ing a plant nervous system **analogous** to that in animals. They **even** claimed that plants had "brain-like command centers" at their root tips.

This **perspective** made sense if you simplified the workings of a complex brain, **reduc**ing it to an array of electrical pulses; cells in plants also communicated through electrical signals. **However**, the signaling in a plant was only **superficially** similar to the firing in a complex animal brain, which was more than "a mass of cells that communicate by electricity."

"For consciousness to evolve, a brain with a threshold **level** of complexity and capacity is required," the researcher **added**. "Since plants don't have nervous systems, the **chances** that they have consciousness are effectively zero."

And what was so great about consciousness, anyway? Plants couldn't run away from **danger**, so investing energy in a body system which **recognize**s a threat and can feel pain would be a very **poor** evolutionary strategy.

Feimo filled in the blanks. The logic was clean, the argument structure familiar: claim, counter-claim, evidence, conclusion. She recognized the pattern the way she recognized Eliot's fragments and Kafka's doors: not as isolated facts but as instances of a larger grammar, the grammar of how knowledge was made and unmade in the Labyrinth.

But as she worked through the blanks, something else surfaced. The passage was not only about plants. It was about the question of what consciousness required: a threshold level of complexity and capacity. It was about the cost of awareness: investing energy in a system that recognizes threats and feels pain. And it was about the evolutionary logic of ignorance: that not knowing, not feeling, not being aware could be, in certain circumstances, a better strategy than the alternative.

She thought of Mia.

Mia, whose memories were sealed behind a door that the Labyrinth had closed. Mia, who spoke Chinese in her sleep without knowing she spoke it. Mia, whose consciousness was not absent but fragmented, scattered across levels and languages, present in pieces that could not yet be assembled into a coherent whole.

Was consciousness always an advantage? The passage said no. For plants, the cost of awareness outweighed its benefits. They could not run from danger. Feeling pain would not help them escape it. Consciousness, for a plant, would be a burden without utility.

Feimo wrote her answers and moved on.

Text 1 was about plastics.

The passage described a problem she recognized from the Labyrinth's own material history: things that were built to last but did not. Plastics, the passage explained, were supposed to be durable. Water bottles, shopping bags, artifacts from Mount Everest to the Mariana Trench. But some plastic materials changed over time. They cracked. They "wept" out additives. They melted into sludge. And this created enormous problems for institutions trying to preserve culturally important objects.

Feimo read about Thea van Oosten, a polymer chemist who had worked for decades at the Cultural Heritage Agency of the Netherlands. Van Oosten's analogy was precise: making plastic artifacts was like baking a cake. If you didn't have exact amounts, it went wrong. The object you made was already a time bomb.

She thought of the Labyrinth's walls. The inscriptions were not plastic, but they shared a property: they were made of materials that degraded. The plaster cracked. The ink faded. The stone itself, given enough time and enough water, returned to powder. Preservation was not a matter of locking things away. It was a matter of understanding the chemistry of decay and intervening at the right moment with the right substance.

The passage described Gilardi's foam sculptures: bright, colorful pieces that were meant to be walked on, meant to be durable, but that split and crumbled within decades because the polyurethane foam was inherently unstable. Museums locked them in the dark.

Feimo thought: that is what Cain is doing. Locking things in the dark and calling it preservation.

Van Oosten's team had worked to preserve Gilardi's sculptures. They infused them with stabilizing chemicals, "sunscreens" that prevented further light damage and rebuilt worn polymer fibers. Several sculptures had gone on display again, beneath protective cases. The preservation was not perfect, but it was active: it involved intervention, not withdrawal.

The passage's final paragraph widened the lens. Joana Lia Ferreira, a professor of conservation, noted that archaeologists had defined the great material ages of human history by examining artifacts in museums. We now lived in an age of plastic. "And what we decide to collect today, what we decide to preserve... will have a strong impact on how in the future we'll be seen."

Feimo circled the question and wrote her answer. The preservation of plastics was *challenging*. Not costly, not unworthy, not unpopular. Challenging. The word carried a specific weight in academic English: it acknowledged difficulty without dismissing the effort. It was the word you used when the task was real and the outcome uncertain and the work still worth doing.

She thought: the Labyrinth is a museum. The knowledge on its walls is the exhibit. And what we decide to preserve will determine how this place is understood by whoever comes after us.

Text 2 was about degrees.

The passage asked a question that Feimo found unexpectedly personal: what was the point, purpose, and value of a degree? The millennials had been told that a decent degree would set them up for life. But that promise had been found wanting. As degrees became universal, they became devalued. In the UK, twenty-eight per cent of graduates were in non-graduate roles, double the OECD average.

The author's argument was not anti-education. It was anti-assumption. The assumption that a degree was the inevitable next step, that the switch from classroom to lecture hall was the only viable path. Generation Z, the passage suggested, was already learning from its predecessors' experience. Employers were seeing the advantages of hiring school leavers. Some were scrapping degree requirements for certain roles.

For those who did pursue a degree, the advice was specific: consider that this may be the first of many. In an age of generalists, it paid to have specific knowledge or skills. Postgraduates earned forty per cent more than graduates. When more and more people had one degree, it made sense to have two.

Feimo read the passage and thought about the Labyrinth's own credentialing system. The levels were degrees. Each one you passed certified a certain competence, a certain mastery of texts and languages and interpretive frameworks. The students who moved through the system were accumulating credentials, layering expertise, building a curriculum vitae of intellectual achievement that the institution recognized and rewarded.

But the passage's final paragraphs unsettled this. Older generations talked about their degree in the present tense: "I am a geographer." Their children would never say such a thing. It was as if they already knew that their degree would not define them in the same way.

Feimo thought: the Labyrinth defines us. The levels we have passed, the exams we have taken, the languages we have learned: these are the credentials by which the institution knows us. But the institution's knowledge of us is not the same as our knowledge of ourselves. The degree describes what we have done. It does not describe who we are becoming.

She finished the reading section and turned to Text 3.

Text 3 was about art and science.

The passage described collaborations between artists and researchers: partnerships that produced work described as "enlightening, challenging, stimulating, fun." Nearly forty per cent of respondents to a *Nature* poll said they had collaborated with artists. Almost all said they would consider doing so in the future.

Feimo read about the Sydney Symphony Orchestra's performance of Vivaldi's *The Four Seasons*, reimagined with climate prediction data injected into the three-hundred-year-old score. The performance was a call to action ahead of the UN Climate Change Conference. Science had given art new content. Art had given science new reach.

But the passage was not celebratory. It was critical. A genuine partnership must be a two-way street. Artists did not simply assist scientists with their communication requirements. Their work should not be considered only as an object of study. The alliances were most valuable when both sides had a shared stake in a project, when they could jointly design it and critique each other's work.

Feimo thought about the Labyrinth's own art-science divide. The literary societies were artists. The exam system was science. And the two did not collaborate; they coexisted, in separate corridors, on separate levels, speaking separate languages. The knowledge on the walls was the product of a scientific tradition: systematic, verifiable, replicable. The experience of reading the walls was an artistic one: subjective, emotional, transformative.

The passage described MIT's Center for Advanced Visual Studies, founded to explore the role of technology in culture. Its projects centered on light: something both artists and scientists had an interest in, and therefore a basis for collaboration. As science divided into more sub-disciplines, the center looked to a time when leading researchers could also be artists, writers, and poets, and vice versa.

Feimo thought: that is what the Labyrinth is supposed to be. A place where the boundaries between disciplines dissolve, where the person who studies Eliot is the same person who understands thermodynamics, where the reader of Kafka is also the reader of quantum mechanics. A place where the walls carry knowledge in multiple languages and multiple registers, and the act of reading them is simultaneously an act of art and an act of science.

But the passage's conclusion was cautionary. To make a collaboration work, both sides needed to invest time and embrace surprise and challenge. The reach of art-science partnerships needed to go beyond research communication. Participants must not fall into the trap of stereotyping each other. Artists and scientists alike were immersed in discovery and invention. Challenge and critique were core to both.

Feimo wrote her answers. The author held that art-science collaborations should do more than communicating science. The reworked *Four Seasons* showed that art could offer audiences easy access to science. CAVS exemplified valuable art-science alliances. Artists worried that their role might be underestimated.

She set down her pen and looked at the ceiling of the exam room. Cain's cameras watched her from their black hemispheres, red lights blinking in their steady, patient rhythm. She thought: the Labyrinth's cameras are not artists and not scientists. They are observers who do not participate. They record without interpreting. They preserve without understanding.

That, she thought, is the difference between a camera and a reader.

Text 4 was about employment law.

The passage described New Zealand's Employment Relations Act: a set of provisions designed to protect ordinary workers from unjustified dismissal. The premise was that common law lacked sufficient safeguards against arbitrary management. Employers must show cause and act in a procedurally fair way.

But the provisions created difficulties when applied to highly paid managers. Constraining firms from firing poorly performing managers was, the passage argued, a handbrake on productivity. The difference between C-grade and A-grade managers might be the difference between business success or failure. Between preserving the jobs of ordinary workers or losing them.

The paradox was stated clearly: laws introduced to protect ordinary workers might be placing those jobs at risk. If employers couldn't fire underperforming managers, productivity declined. If productivity declined, wages fell. And because employment protection laws made it costlier to fire an employee, employers became more cautious about hiring, which made it harder for marginal managers to gain employment in the first place.

Feimo read the passage and thought about Cain's security provisions.

The structural parallel was exact. Cain's security measures were designed to protect students from Fragmenter attacks. The premise was that the previous system lacked sufficient safeguards. But the provisions, applied indiscriminately, created difficulties. The cameras, the access restrictions, the identification checks: these were handbrakes on the Labyrinth's intellectual productivity. The cost of security was borne by the people the security was meant to protect.

And the paradox held. Laws introduced to protect students were placing their education at risk. The restrictions reduced the time and energy available for study. The surveillance created a climate of suspicion that inhibited the free exchange of ideas. The identification checks added friction to the simple act of moving between levels, between libraries, between the corridors where knowledge was stored and the rooms where it was discussed.

Australia's solution was to exclude high-income employees from unfair dismissal protections. New Zealand had tried to permit firms and high-income employees to contract out of the system, but the mechanisms were unwieldy and the bill was voted down.

Feimo thought: there is no threshold for Cain's restrictions. They apply to everyone equally. A first-year student and a doctoral candidate face the same cameras, the same checks, the same constraints. The system does not distinguish between those who need protection and those who are capable of protecting themselves.

She wrote her answers and closed the booklet.

After the exam, Feimo walked to the maintenance shaft where she and Mia sometimes met.

The shaft was on the sixth level, a narrow passage between two walls that the building's original architects had left unfinished. The pipes were exposed, wrapped in insulation that had yellowed with age, and the floor was concrete, unpolished, the kind of surface that institutional neglect had made honest. There were no cameras here. Cain's security network had blind spots, and the maintenance shaft was one of them: too narrow for the standard mounting brackets, too unimportant for custom installations.

Mia was already there, sitting on an overturned crate, reading a book that Feimo could not see the title of.

"How was it?" Mia asked.

"Fine. The cloze was about plant consciousness."

"Plants don't have consciousness."

"That's what the passage argued."

"Then the passage is correct."

Feimo sat on the floor opposite her. The concrete was cold through her clothes, but she had learned not to mind. Comfort was a variable, not a constant: in the Labyrinth, you adjusted to the temperature of the space you occupied rather than demanding that the space adjust to you.

"The passage said that for plants, consciousness would be a poor evolutionary strategy," Feimo said. "They can't run away from danger. Feeling pain wouldn't help them escape it. Awareness would be a cost without a benefit."

Mia looked up from her book. "And?"

"And it made me think about things that aren't conscious. Things that exist without awareness. The walls, for instance. The inscriptions. They carry meaning, but they don't know they carry meaning. They preserve knowledge, but they don't know they're preserving it. The Fragmenters attacked them, and the walls didn't resist. They just broke."

"Because they're walls."

"Because they're walls," Feimo agreed. "But the breaking was not random. The fragments still carried meaning. The knowledge was not destroyed; it was redistributed. And the redistribution made it harder to access but also harder to destroy. A centralized system is efficient but fragile. A distributed system is chaotic but resilient."

Mia was quiet for a moment.

"You're not talking about walls," she said.

"I'm talking about the **reduce**d form of the Labyrinth's knowledge after the attack. The fragments. The pieces. The waste land."

"You're also talking about something else."

Feimo looked at her. Mia's face was in its default setting: analytical, neutral, the face of a person who had learned to present the minimum of emotional information and the maximum of intellectual precision. But there was something beneath the surface, the way there was always something beneath the surface with Mia: a question that she had not asked, a observation that she had not made, a recognition that she was holding in reserve.

"I found something on the eighth level," Feimo said. "A theatre."

"A theatre."

"An absurd one. Empty stage, painted tree, two chairs. Books arranged in the seats like an audience. No performers."

"Beckett."

"Beckett and Ionesco. The whole space is a reference to the Theatre of the Absurd."

Mia closed her book. Feimo could see the title now: *The Collected Plays of Samuel Beckett*, in a bilingual edition, French and English.

"The Theatre of the Absurd," Mia said, "is not about meaninglessness. That's the common misunderstanding. People think absurdism says: nothing means anything. But that's not what Beckett was doing. Beckett was saying: meaning is not given. It is made. And the making is the point."

"I know."

"Then you know why the Labyrinth put a theatre on the eighth level."

Feimo thought about it. The seventh level had been the waste land, the corridor of fragments. The eighth level was the theatre, the space of emptiness and waiting. The progression was not random. It was a curriculum: first you learned that knowledge could be broken. Then you learned that the breaking was not the end.

"The Labyrinth is teaching me," Feimo said slowly, "how to wait."

"Not how to wait," Mia said. "How to understand that waiting is not a failure. That the absence of an answer is not the same as the absence of a question. That Godot never arrives, and the play is not about his absence."

"What is it about?"

"It's about the two men who wait."

They sat in the maintenance shaft, and the pipes dripped, and the building breathed its slow, structural breath around them. Feimo thought about the Theatre of the Absurd. About the empty stage and the painted tree and the books arranged as an audience. About the hat with the Chinese inscription inside: *The person waiting does not know what they are waiting for.*

"Even the Fragmenters don't eat it," Mia said.

Feimo looked at her. "Don't eat what?"

"The Theatre of the Absurd. The corridor on the eighth level. I've checked the damage reports. The Fragmenters attacked every other section of that floor. Every library, every archive, every storage room. But the theatre is untouched. Not a scratch."

"Why?"

"That's the question, isn't it? The Fragmenters consume knowledge. They break walls, scatter inscriptions, destroy the connections between pieces of meaning. But they left the theatre alone. A space full of books, full of texts, full of knowledge in seven languages. And they walked past it."

"Because there was nothing to break?"

"Because there was nothing to connect. The theatre's knowledge is already fragmented. Beckett's play is already absurd. There's no coherent structure to dismantle, no logical chain to sever, no narrative to interrupt. The Fragmenters can only destroy what has been built. They can't destroy what was never assembled."

Feimo sat with that for a long time.

"Some things," she said finally, "are not meant to be understood."

Mia looked at her. The look lasted longer than Mia's looks usually did: three seconds, four, five. In the maintenance shaft's inadequate light, Mia's eyes were dark and still, the eyes of a person who had spent a long time in the Labyrinth and had learned to see in its particular frequencies of light and shadow.

"That's the most absurd thing you've ever said," Mia replied.

"I know."

"It's also correct."

That evening, Feimo went to find the Magical Realism corridor.

Card #071 had described the movement with precision: 20th-century Latin American literature, the fusion of myth and reality, the supernatural presented as ordinary and the ordinary presented as miraculous. Márquez's *One Hundred Years of Solitude*: the Buendía family's seven generations, the town of Macondo, the rain that lasted four years and the insomnia plague that erased memory and the ascension of Remedios the Beauty into the sky while folding sheets. The card had emphasized the technique: "turning reality into fantasy without losing its truth."

Borges was the other pole: labyrinths, mirrors, infinite libraries, the fiction of fiction, the narrative as a philosophical instrument. The card had quoted him: "I have always imagined that Paradise will be a kind of library."

Feimo found the corridor on the eighth level, adjacent to the theatre. It was narrower than she expected, and the walls were covered in a different kind of text: not the dense bilingual inscriptions of the upper levels but something more like a story, a continuous narrative that flowed from one wall to the next in a single language, Spanish, with occasional passages in English and Chinese, as if the narrative itself was bilingual, speaking in tongues.

The text began: *"Muchos años después, frente al pelotón de fusilamiento, el coronel Aureliano Buendía había de recordar aquella tarde remota en que su padre lo llevó a conocer el hielo."*

She knew the sentence. Many years later, as he faced the firing squad, Colonel Aureliano Buendía was to remember that distant afternoon when his father took him to discover ice.

She walked along the wall, reading. The narrative was not Márquez's novel; it was something else, something the Labyrinth had made. It described the Academy as if the Academy were Macondo: a place where the impossible was routine, where the walls remembered what the inhabitants forgot, where knowledge arrived not through study but through inheritance, passed down from one generation of readers to the next like a family recipe or a recurring dream.

In this version of the Academy, the inscriptions on the walls were not written but grown: they appeared overnight, like mushrooms after rain, and if you read them too quickly they dissolved into the air, and if you read them too slowly they changed before you finished. The only way to read them was at the exact speed of understanding: not faster, not slower, but at the pace at which meaning arrives in a mind that is ready for it.

Feimo stopped at a passage that described the Labyrinth's library.

*"The library was circular and had four billion books. The books were not arranged by subject or author but by the order in which they had been read. The first book on the first shelf was the first book that had ever been read in the Academy. The last book on the last shelf had not yet been read; it was waiting for someone to open it, and when that person did, the library would rearrange itself to accommodate the new entry, and every other book would shift one position to the right."*

She thought of Borges. The Library of Babel: infinite hexagonal galleries, every possible combination of characters, the certainty that somewhere in the infinite shelves was the book that contained the meaning of all the others. Borges had written about labyrinths that were libraries and libraries that were labyrinths. The Labyrinth was both. Its corridors were shelves. Its levels were catalog numbers. Its inscriptions were the spines of books that had not yet been opened because the reader had not yet arrived.

The Magical Realism corridor led to a door.

The door was wooden, unpainted, and on it someone had nailed a piece of paper with a single sentence in Chinese:

*"这本书还没有被写出来，但它已经被读过了。"*

*This book has not yet been written, but it has already been read.*

Feimo stood before the door and thought about the relationship between reading and writing. In the conventional understanding, writing came first: an author wrote a book, and then a reader read it. The causal arrow pointed from creator to consumer. But magical realism reversed this. In Márquez's world, the reader's memory was the author's source: the stories existed before they were told because they had always been known, always been remembered, always been part of the collective experience of a place and its people. The author did not invent. The author recorded.

The Labyrinth operated on the same logic. The knowledge on its walls was not written by the institution. It was inherited. The inscriptions were records of a conversation that had been going on for centuries, in dozens of languages, across levels and corridors and hidden rooms. The Labyrinth did not create knowledge. It preserved the memory of knowledge that had always existed.

She opened the door.

Behind it was a small room, circular, with a single window that looked out onto nothing: a wall of white, featureless, the kind of blank that suggests not emptiness but the space before creation, the page before the first word, the silence before the first note. In the center of the room, a desk. On the desk, a notebook, open to a blank page.

Beside the notebook, a pen.

Feimo sat at the desk and picked up the pen. She understood what the room was asking. The Magical Realism corridor had led her to a place where the distinction between reading and writing did not exist. The blank page was not an invitation to create. It was an invitation to remember.

She wrote:

*"Today I took an exam about plants. The passage argued that plants do not have consciousness, that awareness would be a poor strategy for a creature that cannot run away from danger. I thought about Mia. I thought about the walls of the Labyrinth, which carry meaning without knowing it, and about the Fragmenters, who break meaning without understanding it. I thought about the theatre on the eighth level, where Beckett's empty stage waits for performers who will never come, and about the books in the audience, facing each other across the aisles, reading in silence."*

She paused. Then she wrote:

*"Mia said: even the Fragmenters don't eat it. The theatre is untouched because its knowledge is already fragmented. The Fragmenters can only destroy what has been assembled. They cannot destroy what was never built."*

She paused again. Then:

*"I said: some things are not meant to be understood. I was thinking about the Theatre of the Absurd. About Godot, who never arrives. About the waiting that is not a failure but a condition. But I was also thinking about Mia. About the door that the Labyrinth sealed, the memories that are locked behind it, the Chinese sentences she speaks without knowing she speaks them. I was thinking about the fact that I love her, and that this love is not a problem to be solved or a question to be answered. It is a condition. Like waiting. Like the Theatre of the Absurd. Like the empty stage and the painted tree and the hat with the inscription inside."*

She wrote:

*"Some things are not meant to be understood. They are meant to be inhabited."*

She closed the notebook and left it on the desk. The room was silent. The white wall beyond the window was featureless and still. She thought of Márquez's Remedios the Beauty, who ascended into the sky while folding sheets: an event that was impossible and therefore true, because its truth was not in its possibility but in its presentation, in the matter-of-fact voice that reported it as if it were no more remarkable than a change in the weather.

She thought of Borges, who imagined Paradise as a library: a place where every book that had ever been written and every book that would ever be written existed simultaneously, on shelves that rearranged themselves to accommodate each new arrival.

She thought of the Labyrinth, which was both: a library and a labyrinth, a waste land and a garden, a theatre of the absurd and a magical realist novel that had not yet been written but had already been read.

She left the room and walked back through the Magical Realism corridor. The Spanish text on the walls continued its narrative, flowing from surface to surface, describing the Academy as a place where time moved in circles and knowledge arrived by inheritance and the dead were not gone but merely in another room, reading another book, waiting for someone to open the door and discover that the book they were reading was the same book the living were reading, just in a different edition.

On the wall near the corridor's entrance, she found a passage she had missed on the way in:

*"No one noticed that the inscriptions had changed. The words were the same. The languages were the same. The order was the same. But the meaning had shifted, the way a river shifts its course: slowly, imperceptibly, over years, so that when the change was finally visible, everyone had already adjusted to it and no one could remember what the river had looked like before."*

Feimo stood in the corridor and **reflect**ed.

The Labyrinth was changing. Not just damaged, not just fragmented, but transforming. The knowledge on its walls was the same knowledge it had always carried, but the meaning was shifting, the way the meaning of a text shifts when it is read by a different reader, or in a different century, or in a different state of mind. The Theatre of the Absurd had taught her that waiting was not a failure. The Magical Realism corridor had taught her that reading was not passive. And the exam, the cloze passage about plants, had taught her that consciousness was not always an advantage: that there were forms of existence that did not require awareness, that carried meaning without knowing it, that preserved truth without understanding it.

She thought of the words on the door: *This book has not yet been written, but it has already been read.*

She thought: the Labyrinth is that book. It has not yet been written. But it has already been read. And the reading is happening now, in this corridor, in this moment, in the mind of a student who is standing in front of a wall covered in Spanish, holding a notebook full of English, thinking in Chinese, and understanding, without being able to explain how, that the three languages are not separate systems but different surfaces of the same structure, the way the three levels of the Labyrinth are not separate floors but different depths of the same knowledge.

She walked back to her room. The corridors were quiet. The cameras blinked their red pulse. The walls carried their inscriptions. The building breathed.

In her room, she sat at her desk and opened her notebook to a fresh page. She wrote:

*"The 2022 exam asked me about plant consciousness, plastic preservation, the value of degrees, art-science collaboration, and employment law. But what it really asked me about was the cost of awareness. Is it better to know or not to know? Is it better to feel or not to feel? Is it better to be a wall that carries meaning without understanding it, or a reader who understands meaning without being able to carry it?"*

She paused. Then she wrote:

*"The answer, I think, is that the question is wrong. It assumes that knowing and carrying are different things. But in the Labyrinth, they are the same. To read is to carry. To understand is to preserve. And to love someone who is locked behind a door is not to wait for the door to open. It is to stand in front of the door and know that the door is the point."*

She closed the notebook and looked at the window. The courtyard was dark. The cameras blinked. Somewhere in the building, Mia was sleeping, and in her sleep, perhaps, she was speaking Chinese, and the Chinese sentences were surfacing like inscriptions on a wall that had always been there, waiting for someone to read them.

Feimo turned off the light.

In the darkness, she thought of Beckett's words. *I can't go on. I'll go on.* The two sentences that contained, between them, the entire Theatre of the Absurd: the impossibility of continuing and the necessity of continuing, held together not by logic but by the simple fact that time does not stop, that the body does not stop, that the mind, even when it has decided that there is no reason to go on, goes on anyway.

She thought of Márquez's opening sentence. *Many years later, as he faced the firing squad, Colonel Aureliano Buendía was to remember that distant afternoon when his father took him to discover ice.* The entire novel contained in a single sentence: the future and the past, the firing squad and the discovery, the violence and the wonder, compressed into a line that began with "many years later" and ended with "ice."

She thought of Mia's words. *Even the Fragmenters don't eat it.*

She thought: some things are not meant to be understood. They are meant to be inhabited.

She closed her eyes and let the darkness hold her. In the darkness, the Labyrinth was a library and a labyrinth and a theatre and a garden and a waste land and a magical realist novel, all at once, all in the same space, overlapping and interpenetrating, the way languages overlap on the walls and meanings overlap in the mind and two people sitting in a maintenance shaft, talking about nothing, can understand each other more completely than two people who have said everything there is to say.

She slept.
