# Chapter 15: Mirror House Tour

The invitation arrived at 06:00, inscribed on a sliver of mirror-glass that materialized on Feimo's desk while she slept. She found it when her ears swiveled toward a high-pitched hum at the edge of hearing: the distinctive frequency of Mirror House correspondence, clean and sharp as a tuning fork struck against crystal.

*Bridge House First-Year Feimo. You are invited to observe Mirror House analytical procedures. Cross-House Exchange, Session 4. Arrival: 10:00. South Atrium. Attendance recorded.*

No signature. Mirror House invitations never carried signatures. The text was the authority, the way a reflection was its own proof.

Feimo held the sliver up to the light. Her face stared back at her from its surface, distorted slightly at the edges, her cat ears elongated into strange antler-like shapes by the curvature. She set it down and reached for her uniform jacket, her tail already curling with the particular anxiety that came from entering someone else's house.

"You look like you swallowed a dictionary," said Mia from the doorway.

The Spirit AI materialized in her morning configuration: softer circuit patterns, slower pulse rate, obsidian eyes carrying the faint residual glow of whatever she processed while Feimo slept. She leaned against the doorframe with the practiced nonchalance of someone who had been standing there for exactly long enough to observe Feimo's reaction to the invitation without being caught doing so.

"Mirror House," Feimo said.

"I can read." Mia drifted into the room, her gaze passing over the mirror-glass invitation with the professional detachment of a doctor reviewing an X-ray. "Cross-House Exchange. You've been selected because Professor Silence recommended you after the wall-reading exercise last week." A pause. "Also because Aya Vasquez specifically requested your presence."

Feimo's ears flattened. "She what?"

"Requested. Your. Presence." Mia enunciated each word with the surgical precision of someone who enjoyed delivering uncomfortable information. "It seems your performance in the underground arena caught her attention. Or perhaps it was the way the wall refused to show you anything during Silence's demonstration. Either way, Mirror House is interested in you, which means I am interested in Mirror House being interested in you, which means we are going." She checked the time by some internal mechanism Feimo could never detect. "In approximately three hours and forty-seven minutes. Shall I prepare a briefing on Mirror House protocols, or would you prefer to arrive ignorant and hope for the best?"

"Can I pick a third option?"

"There is no third option. There is never a third option."

---

Mirror House occupied the western face of the Academy, and everything about it was designed to remind you of this fact.

The corridors were silver. Not painted silver, not lit to appear silver: the walls themselves were made of a material that existed in the permanent state of being about to reflect. Walking through them felt like walking through a sentence that had been edited so many times it had lost all its original meaning and was now purely structure, purely form. Feimo could feel the Glyph currents here: cool, analytical, operating at a frequency that prioritized precision over resonance.

"Mirror House was founded in the Academy's second century," Mia narrated at her shoulder, Tour Guide voice engaged. "Its governing principle is reflective analysis: the idea that the most powerful reading is reading that turns the reader's tools back upon themselves. Where Bridge House builds connections, Mirror House disassembles them."

"You sound like a brochure."

"I sound like an AI who has read the brochure and is summarizing it for a first-year who did not."

The corridor opened into a wide atrium: the South Atrium, the meeting point specified in the invitation. The ceiling here was glass, or something that behaved like glass: transparent from below, reflective from above, so that anyone standing in the atrium could see the sky but the sky could not see them. Six students waited in a loose cluster, all from Bridge House, all wearing the same expression of cautious curiosity that Feimo recognized as the face people made when they were trying very hard not to look impressed by their surroundings.

Noah was not among them. Her sister had declined the exchange with a characteristically blunt "I've seen Mirror House. It's a hall of mirrors. I prefer rooms with fewer opinions about me."

Aya was.

She stood at the far edge of the atrium, dressed in Mirror House silver-gray, her platinum hair pulled back in its invariable tail, her posture the architectural equivalent of a well-constructed argument: balanced, deliberate, without a single wasted element. She did not greet them. She observed their arrival with the same focused attention Feimo had seen her bring to the wall-reading exercise, cataloguing details the way a scholar catalogues footnotes: necessary, precise, and invisible until you needed them.

"Welcome to Mirror House," Aya said. Her voice was quiet in the way that Mirror House was quiet: not soft, not gentle, but close. "I will be conducting today's observation. You will not be asked to perform. You will be asked to see."

She turned and walked into the corridor beyond the atrium without waiting for a response. The six Bridge House students exchanged glances, then followed. Feimo fell into step near the back, her ears tracking Aya's footsteps: a metronome rhythm, each footfall identical in pressure and timing, the stride of someone who had measured the distance between her starting point and her destination before she began walking.

"This should be educational," Mia murmured.

"Your definition of educational and mine are very different."

"Yes. Mine is correct."

---

The Mirror House reading room was not what Feimo expected.

She had imagined something cold. What she found instead was a space that resembled a conservatory: tall, arched windows admitting light that had been filtered through layers of mirror-glass until it arrived soft and directionless. The walls were lined with texts arranged not by author, not by date, but by argumentative structure. Texts that used deductive reasoning occupied one wall. Texts that relied on inductive reasoning occupied another.

"This is the area where Mirror House students conduct their primary analytical work," Aya said. "The organization is structural, not thematic. We do not sort texts by what they say. We sort them by how they say it."

She gestured toward the deductive wall. "A text that begins with a general principle and moves to a specific conclusion is fundamentally different from a text that accumulates specific observations and builds toward a general theory. The structure of an argument reveals what the argument is trying to hide."

Feimo's ears rotated forward.

"For today's demonstration," Aya continued, "we will revisit a passage you encountered in Professor Silence's group exercise. The Simonsohn cloze. You analyzed it collaboratively with four Houses. Now you will read it with one."

She raised her hand, and a section of the wall rippled. Text appeared, glowing in Mirror House silver:

*People are, on the whole, poor at considering background information when making individual decisions. At first glance this might seem like a strength that grants the ability to make judgments which are unbiased by external factors. But Dr Uri Simonsohn speculated that an inability to consider the big picture was leading decision-makers to be biased by the daily samples of information they were working with.*

"You have already answered the blanks," Aya said. "Today I am asking different questions. The first sentence makes a claim. The second sentence offers a charitable interpretation: this might be a strength. The third sentence, introduced by 'But,' reverses that interpretation. In three sentences, the passage has established a thesis, offered a counter-argument, and demolished it." She looked at Feimo. "What is the rhetorical technique?"

Feimo blinked. "That ignoring context makes you biased."

"Correct. But notice the rhetorical structure. The author does not begin with the thesis. The author begins with a concession: 'At first glance this might seem like a strength.' Why?"

Silence from the Bridge House students. Feimo's tail curled. She recognized the question, the kind that Professor Silence liked to ask: the kind that had no single correct answer but whose wrong answers were very wrong indeed.

"Because if you start with the strong claim, the reader resists," Feimo said, slowly. "But if you start by acknowledging the opposing view, the reader lowers their guard. Then when you introduce the real argument, they're already partially persuaded."

Aya's expression did not change, but something in her posture shifted: a fractional relaxation of the shoulders, a slight rotation toward Feimo that in anyone else would have been a nod. In Aya, it was practically a standing ovation.

"That is the technique of concession and reversal," Aya said. "It appears throughout academic writing, legal argument, and political speech. The author grants the opponent's strongest point, then pivots. The pivot carries more force because it has absorbed the opponent's momentum."

She advanced the text. The passage continued:

*For example, he theorised that a judge fearful of appearing too soft on crime might be more likely to send someone to prison if he had already sentenced five or six other defendants only to forced community service on that day.*

"Here the author provides a concrete example," Aya said. "The word 'For example' signals a shift from abstract to specific. The judge is afraid of appearing lenient. After several lenient sentences, the judge overcorrects by imposing a harsh one. The decision is not based on the merits of the case. It is based on the sequence of prior decisions."

She paused. The light in the room seemed to lean toward her.

"This is sequential bias. The inability to consider the big picture forces the decision-maker to rely on the most recent samples of information." Her eyes found Feimo's. "Sound familiar?"

Feimo thought of the underground arena. Her first three losses: she had attacked aggressively after winning a round, her confidence inflated by the most recent success. She had not considered the big picture.

"Yes," she said.

"Good." Aya dismissed the text. "The passage continues with a controlled study: 9,323 MBA interviews, 31 admissions officers, a scale of one to five. The finding: when the previous candidate scored 0.75 points or more higher, the next applicant's score dropped by 0.075 points. Small in magnitude, enormous in consequence. To undo that drop, a candidate would need 30 additional GMAT points." She let the number settle. "The surface content is about MBA admissions. The structural content is about how sequence shapes judgment."

Feimo felt the word "consider" resonate in her mind, not as a vocabulary item but as a lived requirement. To consider: to examine carefully, to resist the gravitational pull of the most recent, the loudest, the nearest.

"This type of reasoning appears in cloze examinations," Mia added from behind Feimo's shoulder, having materialized with the timing of a footnote. "Twenty blanks, each requiring the student to reconstruct the logical connective tissue of an argument. Most students approach cloze as vocabulary testing. It is not. It is structural testing. The correct answer is rarely the most impressive word. It is the word that preserves the argument's architecture."

Aya glanced at Mia. The two of them existed in the same room the way a scalpel existed in the same room as a surgical lamp: aware of each other, serving the same patient, neither acknowledging the other's indispensability.

"That is correct," Aya said, which was the closest thing to agreement she had ever offered a Spirit AI in Feimo's presence.

---

They moved to a second reading room. This one was smaller, circular, the walls covered in texts organized by what Aya called "rhetorical mode": persuasion, analysis, critique, synthesis. The air here carried a faint ozone scent, the residue of heavy Glyph processing, and Feimo's ears picked up a sub-audible hum that her body interpreted as the Labyrinth's way of saying *pay attention*.

Aya stopped before the critique wall. "The next passage concerns an industry under scrutiny. You heard it quoted during the Mixer debate. A Forge student cited it from memory to argue that moral instruction hijacks literature's rhythm. Today I want you to read it not for its argument but for its language."

The wall rippled. New text appeared:

*In the 2006 film version of The Devil Wears Prada, Miranda Priestly, played by Meryl Streep, scolds her unattractive assistant for imagining that high fashion doesn't affect her. Priestly explains how the deep blue color of the assistant's sweater descended over the years from fashion shows to department stores and to the bargain bin in which the poor girl doubtless found her garment.*

"This opening does something subtle," Aya said. "It uses a fictional scene to introduce a factual argument. The film is a cultural reference point: it establishes that the reader already knows fashion trickles down from high to low. The passage then pivots."

The text advanced:

*This top-down conception of the fashion business couldn't be more out of date or at odds with the feverish world described in Overdressed, Elizabeth Cline's three-year indictment of "fast fashion." In the last decade or so, advances in technology have allowed mass-market labels such as Zara, H&M, and Uniqlo to react to trends more quickly and anticipate demand more precisely.*

"The word 'indictment' is doing significant work," Aya continued. "An indictment is a formal accusation. The author is not saying Cline wrote a book about fast fashion. The author is saying Cline prosecuted fast fashion. The word choice reveals the author's alignment: sympathetic to Cline's critique."

She looked at the Bridge House students. "Critical reading requires attention to word choice at this level. The difference between 'a book about' and 'an indictment of' is the difference between neutrality and advocacy. Most readers process this unconsciously. Mirror House teaches you to process it deliberately."

The passage continued:

*These labels encourage style-conscious consumers to see clothes as disposable, meant to last only a wash or two, although they don't advertise that, and to renew their wardrobe every few weeks. By offering on-trend items at dirt-cheap prices, Cline argues, these brands have hijacked fashion cycles, shaking an industry long accustomed to a seasonal pace.*

"Consider the word 'hijacked,'" Aya said. "It implies theft, force, illegitimate seizure. If the author had written 'shortened fashion cycles' or 'accelerated trends,' the sentence would carry the same factual content but a fraction of the moral weight. The author chose 'hijacked' because the author agrees with Cline. This is persuasion disguised as reporting."

Feimo leaned forward. She could feel it: the way Aya peeled back the surface of the text to reveal the machinery beneath.

"For H&M to offer a $5.95 knit miniskirt in all its 2,300-plus stores around the world," Aya read, "it must rely on low-wage overseas labor, order in volumes that strain natural resources, and use massive amounts of harmful chemicals."

"Three consequences. Three costs. The structure is parallel: subject, verb, escalating harm. Low-wage labor. Strained resources. Harmful chemicals. A rhetorical crescendo: each item carries more weight because each is harder to dismiss."

The passage continued to its conclusion:

*Though several fast-fashion companies have made efforts to curb their impact on labor and the environment, Cline believes lasting change can only be effected by the customer.*

"Lasting change," Aya repeated. "Not temporary change. Not incremental change. Lasting. The word choice implies that corporate efforts are insufficient because they are impermanent. Only customer behavior, sustained over time, can produce the kind of change that matters." She paused. "This is the argument's thesis, and it arrives in the final paragraph. Everything before this sentence has been preparation."

Feimo heard the word "change" differently now. Not as a vocabulary item on her study card, not as a Glyph she might one day acquire, but as the central problem of every system she had encountered: the Labyrinth, the Academy, the Houses, the underground arena. Change was the thing that structures feared and readers created. Every reading changed the text. Every fighter changed the arena. Every student changed the Academy, whether the Academy admitted it or not.

"This text's argument structure," Aya said, "follows a pattern you will encounter in dozens of examination passages: concession, counter-argument, evidence, escalation, thesis. The author does not state the thesis first. The author builds toward it, brick by brick, so that by the time the thesis arrives, the reader has already been convinced by the evidence. This is how critical reading works: you do not accept the conclusion. You trace the construction."

"She's good."

Feimo said nothing. She was watching Aya's hands, fingers slightly spread, the posture of someone holding a complex idea in the air. It was the most expressive Feimo had ever seen Aya's body language become, and it struck her that this was what Aya looked like when she cared about something.

---

After the critique exercise, Aya led them to a small alcove off the main corridor. The alcove contained a single table, two chairs, and a window that looked out onto an interior courtyard where Mirror House students practiced reflective reading exercises in pairs, their hands moving in the precise gestures of scholars deconstructing texts in real time.

"This area is reserved for advanced analysis," Aya said. "Sit."

Feimo sat. The other Bridge House students had drifted to different parts of the building, guided by other Mirror House students, their voices receding into the silver labyrinth of corridors. It was just Feimo and Aya now, with Mia hovering at the periphery like a watchful parenthesis.

Aya placed a single sheet of paper on the table. Handwritten, in the angular script that Feimo had come to recognize as Aya's private notation system.

"Do you know the difference between Tang poetry and Song poetry?" Aya asked.

The question arrived without preamble, the way Mirror House questions always arrived: already in progress, as if the conversation had been going on for some time before Feimo was invited to join it.

"Tang poetry emphasizes feeling," Feimo said, drawing on Card 023 from her mental study deck. "Image, mood, the emotional resonance of a moment. Song poetry emphasizes intellect: argument, reasoning, the display of learning. Tang poets capture a feeling. Song poets construct an insight."

"Correct. Now extend the principle." Aya's eyes were steady. "Tang poetry is what happens when a reader brings emotion to a text and the text responds with resonance. Song poetry is what happens when a reader brings intellect to a text and the text responds with structure. They are not different kinds of poetry. They are different kinds of reading."

Feimo's ears swiveled forward. The connection clicked: Tang and Song as two modes of engagement, not two eras of Chinese literature. Feeling versus analysis. The same text, read two ways, producing two completely different experiences.

"Yan Luang wrote in Canglang Shihua that Tang poets 'take feeling as their victory' and Song poets 'take reason as their victory,'" Aya continued. "But the distinction is not absolute. Du Fu reasoned. Lu You felt. The best poets, like the best readers, move between both modes without choosing one."

She slid the handwritten sheet toward Feimo. It contained a short passage, copied in Aya's precise hand:

*A sacred place of peace, however crude it may be, is a distinctly human need, as opposed to shelter, which is a distinctly animal need.*

"This is from the translation section of the same examination," Aya said. "Translate it."

Feimo stared at the sentence. The structure was layered: a concessive clause embedded in the main assertion, followed by a contrastive clause that drew a boundary between human and animal need.

"A place of peace, no matter how rough it is, is a human need," Feimo said slowly. "As opposed to shelter, which is an animal need. The distinction is between spiritual necessity and physical necessity. A sacred place serves the spirit. Shelter serves the body."

"Good. Now read the next one."

*The gardens of the homeless, which are in effect homeless gardens, introduce form into an urban environment where it either didn't exist or was not discernible as such.*

Feimo worked through it. "The homeless make gardens. Those gardens are themselves homeless, because they belong to no fixed place. But they bring form, order, beauty into a city space where those things were absent or invisible."

"And the final line."

*It is this implicit or explicit reference to nature that fully justifies the use of the word garden, though in a "liberated" sense, to describe these synthetic constructions.*

"It's an emphasis sentence: 'It is...that...' The author is arguing that calling these constructions 'gardens' is justified because they reference nature, even if the word 'garden' is being used loosely." Feimo paused. "The homeless build gardens. The gardens are not conventional. But they are gardens because they point toward nature, toward the human need for a sacred place. The argument comes full circle."

Aya was quiet for a moment. The light from the courtyard window fell across the table between them, and in that light Feimo could see the fine Glyph-marks on Aya's fingertips: the residue of years of analytical reading, inscribed into her skin like a scholar's tattoos.

"You read well," Aya said. It was not a compliment. It was an observation, delivered with the same precision she brought to everything. But the fact that she said it at all, without prompting, without being asked, without any institutional obligation to do so, meant something that Feimo could not yet name.

"The two schools of classical Chinese lyric poetry," Aya added, "the graceful and restrained school of Liu Yong and Li Qingzhao, and the bold and unconstrained school of Su Shi and Xin Qiji, represent the same dichotomy. Tang feeling versus Song reason. Emotion versus structure. They are not opposed. They are complementary. Like the Houses." She stood. "Remember that."

---

They walked back through the corridors in silence. The silver walls reflected their images in elongated strips, so that Feimo saw herself repeated dozens of times across the surface: a cat-eared girl in Bridge House amber, walking beside a Mirror House scholar in silver-gray, their reflections overlapping where the wall curved, merging and separating like two arguments converging on a shared conclusion.

Mia materialized at Feimo's shoulder as they re-entered the South Atrium.

"Well," the Spirit AI said. Her voice carried the particular frequency that Feimo had learned to identify as Mia's post-evaluation mode: stripped of sarcasm, carrying only the raw signal of assessment. "I watched. I listened. I analyzed."

"And?"

Mia's obsidian eyes fixed on Feimo with the intensity of a searchlight finding its target in fog.

"You improvised. You guessed. You relied on intuition and whatever fragments of your study deck happened to surface at the right moment. Your translation was adequate but imprecise. Your response to the Tang-Song distinction was correct but shallow."

Feimo's ears flattened.

"However." Mia paused. "You engaged. You did not retreat into passivity. You allowed Aya to challenge you and you responded with genuine intellectual effort. Your instinct for the 'consider the big picture' question was genuinely insightful."

Another pause.

"Your reading instincts are not the problem. Your reading discipline is. You perceive things other students miss, but you cannot reproduce your perception on demand. That is the difference between a talent and a skill. A talent is what you have. A skill is what you can control."

"Aya Vasquez reads four hours a day. Every day. Without exception. She has maintained this schedule since her first week at the Academy. She is not smarter than you. She is more disciplined." A beat. "Consider that."

---

The atrium was emptying. The other Bridge House students had filtered out in groups of two and three, their conversations carrying the slightly dazed quality of people who had been shown something they did not yet have the vocabulary to describe. Feimo lingered. She told herself she was waiting for the corridor to clear. She was not ready to return to Bridge House yet, to the amber warmth and the familiar hum and the walls that read her with the gentle attentiveness of a parent monitoring a child's fever.

She was standing at the atrium's edge when Noah appeared.

Her sister emerged from a side corridor that Feimo had not noticed before: a narrow passage, unlabeled, its walls unpolished and its lighting the dim, functional amber of service tunnels rather than the reflective silver of Mirror House's public spaces. Noah wore her Bridge House jacket unbuttoned, her reading glasses pushed up into her hair, and her expression was the one Feimo had learned to associate with the underground: alert, guarded, carrying the residual adrenaline of a space that existed outside the Academy's official cartography.

"You're still here," Noah said. Not a question.

"I'm leaving."

"Not yet you're not." Noah glanced at the corridor behind her, then at Feimo, then at the empty atrium. Something in her calculation changed. "Walk with me."

They walked. The service corridor was narrow, its text sparse: old, faded, the Labyrinth's equivalent of a footnote that no one checked anymore.

"The Mirror House tour," Noah said. "What did they show you?"

"Reading techniques. Structural analysis. How to trace an author's argument through their word choices."

"And Aya?"

"She's precise. She reads like a scalpel."

Noah made a sound that might have been a laugh. "She was trained by the best. Mirror House's methods come from the same tradition as the underground reading chambers. The old methods. The ones that taught readers to dissect a text layer by layer."

They stopped in a junction where three corridors met. The text on the walls here was layered, palimpsest-like, with newer inscriptions written over older ones, the words bleeding into each other.

"The underground fights are not just about combat," Noah said. "They never were. The arena is a reading exercise. Every Glyph you throw, every pattern you read: it is all reading. The Academy teaches reading as an intellectual practice. The underground teaches reading as a survival skill."

She turned to face Feimo.

"I fought in the underground for two years before you arrived. Seventeen matches. Twelve wins. Not because I was the strongest fighter. Because I was the best reader. I could feel the Glyph currents shift before my opponent committed. I could read their intention in the micro-adjustments of their stance."

Feimo felt the words settle into her, heavy and warm, the way a Glyph settled into a wall after activation: not disappearing, not fading, becoming part of the structure.

"Why are you telling me this?" Feimo asked.

Noah was quiet for a long time. Long enough for the text on the walls to cycle through a full rotation of whatever slow, geological process the Labyrinth used to update its inscriptions.

"Because you are going to fight in the underground," Noah said. "And because what you will find there is not what you expect."

She began walking again, and Feimo followed, her ears tracking the sounds of the service corridor: the hum of distant Glyph generators, the drip of condensation from the ceiling, the barely perceptible vibration of the Labyrinth's deeper levels turning in their sleep.

"There was someone," Noah said. "In the underground. During my second year. She was not from the Academy. She came from outside the Labyrinth. A researcher, studying the Glyph system from the outside. She was brilliant. She was fearless. She was the first person I met who understood that reading is not a metaphor."

The corridor turned. The text on the walls changed: older, stranger, written in scripts Feimo did not recognize.

"We worked together for six months. In the underground. Sharing techniques, sharing discoveries. It was the most productive collaboration I have ever had."

"What happened?"

"She left." The word was simple. The weight it carried was not. "The Labyrinth's boundaries are not what they appear. There are doors in the deeper levels that open onto places the Academy does not control. She found one. She walked through."

"Did she come back?"

"Not yet," Noah said.

They stood in the chamber for a moment that stretched like a sentence that had lost its verb. Feimo could feel the weight of what Noah had told her: not just the facts but the shape of the silence around them, the architecture of a story that had a beginning and a middle but whose ending was still being written somewhere in the Labyrinth's unmapped depths.

"You fought alongside her," Feimo said. "In the underground. Not just Glyph duels. Real fights."

"The underground is real, Feimo. Everything that happens there is real. The fights, the alliances, the borders between territories the Academy does not acknowledge." Noah's voice carried a new register: not warning, not instruction. Something closer to confession. "When you enter the underground, you enter a space where reading is power, and power has consequences."

She looked at Feimo.

"Be careful. But do not be afraid. Fear makes you read poorly. And in the underground, reading poorly is the one thing the Labyrinth does not forgive."

---

The walk back to Bridge House took eleven minutes. Feimo counted them: each minute a footstep, each footstep a thought.

Mia reappeared at the South Atrium entrance, falling into step beside her.

"You have been with Noah," Mia said.

"How can you tell?"

"Your posture changes when you speak with her. You stand straighter. Your ears track her voice the way a compass tracks north."

"She told me about the underground."

"She told you about someone." Mia's voice was carefully neutral. "I processed the acoustic data from the service corridor. The resonance pattern of her voice shifted when she mentioned 'a researcher from outside the Labyrinth.' That shift correlates with emotional significance in eighty-seven percent of cases."

Feimo stopped walking. They were at the border between Mirror House and Bridge House, the point where silver-gray walls gave way to amber. The boundary was visible: a line in the stone where one color ended and another began.

"What you said earlier. About systematic training. About Aya reading four hours a day."

"I said that."

"Were you telling me to be more like her?"

Mia's obsidian eyes held steady.

"I was telling you to be more like yourself, with discipline." The pause was deliberate. "You have something Aya Vasquez does not. You read the walls. You feel the Glyph currents. That is your gift. But a gift without training is a weapon without aim. Systematic training does not replace your instincts. It gives your instincts a framework."

"Consider the big picture," Feimo said quietly.

Mia's circuit patterns flared: a brief, bright pulse, a signal that Feimo had learned to read as surprise.

"Yes," Mia said. "Exactly that."

They crossed the boundary together: stepping from silver into amber, from analysis into synthesis, from Mirror House's cool precision into Bridge House's warm, humming chaos.
