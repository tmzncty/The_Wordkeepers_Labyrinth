# Chapter 17: The Underground Circuit

The entrance was behind a boiler.

Noir pulled aside a corroded maintenance panel in the deepest sub-basement of Forge House, revealing a gap between two pipes that should not have existed according to the Academy's official architectural plans. The pipes were hot. The air between them was hotter. Feimo could feel the heat pressing against her ears, flattening the fur against her skull, and for a moment she considered the very real possibility that her senior was leading her into a ventilation shaft to die.

"Stop thinking," Noir said, without looking back. Her own ears were flat too, but in the practiced, efficient way of someone who had done this dozens of times. Her tail hung low and still, counterbalancing her stride. "Your ears are broadcasting. Anyone with half a Resonance sense can read nervous cat from twenty meters."

"I'm not nervous," Feimo said. "I'm thermally concerned."

"Same thing." Noir slid sideways through the gap. Her Forge jacket caught on a bolt, and she ripped it free without slowing. "The circuit is three levels below the official sub-basement. The Academy knows it exists. The faculty knows it exists. Even Professor Silence knows it exists, which means the Labyrinth definitely knows it exists. Nobody stops it because nobody can. You put a hundred Resonance-capable students in a building with too much free time and not enough sanctioned outlets, and this is what happens. You get an underground."

Feimo squeezed through the gap. The pipe burned her forearm. She hissed and kept moving.

"What kind of underground?" she asked.

"The kind where people fight."

---

The space opened like a wound.

The ceiling was rough stone, unfinished, the kind of raw geological surface that existed beneath every polished corridor and enchanted wall of the Academy but was never meant to be seen. Someone had strung Glyph-lights across it, pale blue-white arcs of stabilized resonance that cast everything in the stark, clinical illumination of an operating theater. The floor was dirt, packed hard by hundreds of feet, and it sloped downward toward a central depression roughly ten meters across. Not a ring. Not a pit. Something closer to the bottom of a drained fountain, a circular basin with tiered edges where spectators could sit and watch.

And there were spectators. Thirty, maybe forty. More demis than Feimo had ever seen in one place. A fox-eared boy from Weave House sat cross-legged on the second tier, his ears rotating independently like satellite dishes. Two dog-eared girls from Forge whispered in the front row, their tails wagging in sync. A kitsune from Mirror, her fox ears barely visible under a hood, sat apart from the others with a notebook open on her lap, recording everything.

Feimo stopped walking.

She had known, academically, that there were other demi-kind at the Academy. She had seen them in corridors, in the dining hall, occasionally in lectures. But the Academy's social architecture was designed around Houses, not species, and the demi-kind were scattered across all four. Here, in this dirt-floored basin beneath the official world, they were gathered. Not organized. Not politicized. Just present, in the way that water is present in a well: naturally, quietly, because the shape of the space invited it.

"This is what I meant," Noir said, watching Feimo's face. "Underestimated people need a place where the metrics are different. Up there," she jerked her chin toward the ceiling, toward the invisible layers of Academy above them, "the evaluation is academic. How many Glyphs can you activate? How fast is your analytical chain? How precisely can you parse a passage? Down here, the only question is: can you last?"

"Last?"

"Survive the match. The rules are simple. Two fighters. Glyphs only, no pre-loaded scripts. The arena is Resonance-neutral, which means the environment doesn't amplify or suppress anyone's style. First to lose their footing, forfeit, or exhaust their Resonance loses. There are no points. There are no judges. There is only the fight and who is still standing when it ends."

Feimo looked at the basin. A section of the rim had been carved with symbols she did not recognize, old Glyphs, pre-Academic, the kind of marks that existed in the margins of the oldest texts in the Library. Not decorative. Functional. They hummed at a frequency she could feel in her teeth.

"Who carved those?" she asked.

"I did," said a voice behind her.

Feimo turned. The speaker was tall, lean, human, with close-cropped hair and the kind of relaxed posture that came from either genuine calm or extensive practice at performing it. He wore a Forge jacket with the insignia stripped off. His hands were ink-stained, not with academic annotations but with the deep, layered black of someone who worked with raw Glyph-stone.

"Rune," he said. "I maintain the arena. Noir vouched for you, which means you're either very good or very entertaining. I'm hoping for both."

He walked past her to the basin's edge and pressed his palm against the carved symbols. The hum intensified, and the air above the basin shimmered, a heat-haze that was not heat, the visual distortion of a Resonance field activating.

"You're first," Rune said to Feimo. "Against Kael. He's Weave, third year, specializes in containment Glyphs. He'll try to pin you in a syntactic loop and wait for you to exhaust yourself."

"Sounds fun," Feimo said, because she could not think of anything else to say.

"It is," Rune said. "That's the problem."

---

Kael was waiting at the bottom of the basin.

He was shorter than Feimo expected, compact and still, with the particular quality of patience that Weave students cultivated like a crop. His hands were clasped behind his back. His posture was perfect. When Feimo descended the tiered steps to face him, he nodded once, the precise acknowledgment of one professional to another.

"Noir's new project?" he asked.

"I'm nobody's project," Feimo said.

Kael smiled. "Good. Projects break. Fighters adapt."

Rune's voice carried from the rim: "Begin."

Kael moved first.

The containment Glyph came in layers: a primary syntactic frame that Feimo could see, glowing faintly in the air between them, and underneath it, hidden in the semiotic gaps, three sub-frames designed to activate sequentially as she responded to the first. The textbook response to a containment opening was to analyze the primary frame and identify its structural weakness. This was what Mirror students did, what Aya would have done with surgical precision. Feimo was not a Mirror student.

She listened.

Her ears caught the Resonance frequency of the primary frame before her eyes fully registered its structure. The sound was thin, reedy, the acoustic signature of a Weave containment pattern: lots of structural integrity, minimal offensive power. It was designed to hold, not to hurt. The sub-frames beneath it were quieter, subtler, their frequencies masked by the primary's volume. But Feimo could hear them. She could hear the way they nested inside each other, the way the third sub-frame had a slight asymmetry in its left register that meant it was the actual trap, and the first two were decoys.

She did not analyze. She moved.

Her response was not a counter-Glyph. It was a physical step, sideways, outside the primary frame's activation radius, followed by a single, sharp Glyph-snap: a Bridge House specialty, a word-of-power that did not attack the containment but *redirected* it. The primary frame, suddenly missing its target, folded inward and collided with its own sub-frames. The trap sprung on itself.

Kael's expression changed. Not shock. Recognition.

"You heard it," he said. "Before you saw it."

"I heard it," Feimo confirmed.

"You can't do that. Containment frequencies are sub-perceptual. The textbook says—"

"The textbook is wrong."

The basin erupted. Not with sound but with the particular quality of attention that fills a space when something unexpected happens. The fox-eared boy leaned forward. The dog-eared girls stopped wagging. The kitsune with the notebook wrote faster.

Kael forfeited. Not because he was beaten but because the match had demonstrated something he respected: a different way of seeing. He raised his palm, the universal signal, and stepped back.

"Rematch sometime," he said.

"Anytime," Feimo said, and meant it.

---

Her second match was against a Forge girl named Petra, who fought the way Forge students wrote: with intensity, directness, and the absolute conviction that the best defense was to make the other person too busy dodging to attack.

Petra opened with a transitive verb chain, raw offensive Glyphs fired in rapid sequence. Where Kael had been structural, Petra was kinetic. Her Glyphs did not glow; they burned, leaving afterimages in Feimo's vision, each one aimed at a different sensory register: one for sight, one for sound, one for the proprioceptive sense that told Feimo where her body was in space.

The first hit Feimo dodged. The second she deflected with a hastily improvised Bridge redirect. The third caught her shoulder, and the impact was not pain but disorientation: a sudden loss of spatial awareness, as if the floor had rotated forty-five degrees while she was not looking. She stumbled. Her left knee hit dirt.

The crowd noise dropped.

Feimo stayed down for two seconds. She used them. The disorientation Glyph was a Forge specialty, designed to overwhelm the opponent's internal map. But Feimo's internal map was not purely visual. It was acoustic. Her ears were still working. She could hear the Resonance field, its hum, its texture, the specific frequency of the arena's neutral zone. She oriented herself by sound, not sight, and stood up.

Petra was already launching the next chain.

Feimo did something she had never done in a sanctioned match. She closed her eyes. The visual noise vanished. The afterimages faded. In the darkness, her hearing sharpened to a point, and she could map Petra's position by the sound of her Glyph activation alone: the sharp crackle of ignition, the whisper of travel, the deep thud of impact. She wove between them, not dodging but flowing, her body moving the way text moves across a page when the reader's eye is fast enough to skip the spaces between words.

When she opened her eyes, she was inside Petra's guard. Close enough to see the surprise on the Forge girl's face, the rapid recalculation, the moment of vulnerability before the next attack could begin.

Feimo placed her palm flat against Petra's sternum and pushed. Not a Glyph. Just physics. Petra's heels left the ground. She staggered backward, caught herself, then lost her footing on the uneven dirt and sat down hard.

The basin exploded.

Rune was on his feet. The fox-eared boy was howling, a sound that was probably embarrassing for him later but was perfect in the moment. The dog-eared girls were wagging again, furiously. Noir, standing at the rim with her arms crossed and her expression carefully neutral, allowed the corner of her mouth to move approximately two millimeters upward.

Petra sat in the dirt and laughed.

"You closed your eyes," she said. "In the middle of a fight. You absolute lunatic."

"I fight better with my ears," Feimo said, and offered her hand.

Petra took it. "Rematch. Next week. And this time I'm targeting those ears first."

---

The third match was different.

Her opponent was Sable, a fourth-year from Forge who did not introduce himself, did not nod, did not offer any of the professional courtesies that Kael and Petra had extended. He was tall, broad-shouldered, and his Resonance signature was dense and flat, like a wall of text with no paragraph breaks.

Feimo heard it immediately: the acoustic profile of a duelist who had been fighting underground for years, who had refined his style through hundreds of matches, who had learned to compress his Glyph activation into a bandwidth so narrow that it was almost inaudible. Almost. Feimo could hear the edges of it, the faint crackle at the margins, but she could not read the content. It was like trying to read a passage through frosted glass. The shape was there. The meaning was not.

"Begin," Rune said.

Sable did not move. He activated.

The Glyph was not a chain. It was a single construct, enormous, a net of Resonance that expanded outward from his position like a shockwave. It was not aimed at Feimo. It was aimed at the entire arena. The Resonance-neutral field shuddered. The carved Glyphs on the rim flickered. The air pressure changed, and Feimo's ears popped, and for a fraction of a second she could not hear anything at all.

In that fraction of a second, Sable closed the distance.

His second Glyph was point-blank: a Forge kinetic blast aimed at center mass. Feimo could not hear it. Could not dodge it. Could not redirect it. It hit her square in the chest, and the world went sideways, and she was in the dirt again, tasting blood and dust, and the basin was silent.

She tried to stand. Her legs disagreed.

"You're done," Sable said. Not cruel. Not kind. Just factual.

Feimo stayed down. She forfeited.

The silence in the basin was the particular silence of people who have just watched something educational happen. Not the silence of disappointment. The silence of revision. The fox-eared boy was thinking. The dog-eared girls were thinking. Even the kitsune with the notebook had stopped writing, which was itself a kind of commentary.

Noir appeared at Feimo's side. She did not offer a hand. She crouched, brought her face level with Feimo's, and said, very quietly:

"Good."

"I lost."

"Yes. And now you know why stamina matters more than talent." Noir's voice was even, the measured cadence of someone who had learned this lesson personally, in dirt, with blood in her mouth. "Sable didn't beat you because he was faster or smarter. He beat you because he has been doing this for four years, and you have been doing this for forty minutes. He has more Resonance capacity than you. Not because he was born with it. Because he built it. One fight at a time, one loss at a time, one recovery at a time."

She straightened.

"The brain needs exercise in much the same way muscles do," she said, and the phrasing was too precise, too structured, to be spontaneous. She was quoting something. Feimo could hear the quotation marks. "The right mental workouts can significantly improve basic cognitive functions. Thinking is essentially a process of making connections. And to a certain extent, our ability to excel in making those connections is inherited. However, because these connections are made through effort and practice, intelligence can expand and fluctuate according to mental effort."

"Where is that from?" Feimo asked, sitting up. Her chest throbbed.

"A reading passage. From a standardized test, centuries ago, in the old world." Noir's mouth twitched. "The old world had tests too. They were obsessed with measuring whether effort could overcome innate ability. The answer was always yes, but they kept asking the question anyway, as if repetition could make the answer more true."

Feimo touched her sternum. The bruise was already forming, a deep ache that would be spectacular by morning.

"Effort," she repeated.

"Effort," Noir confirmed. "You have talent. More than anyone I've seen in this arena, and I have been watching for three years. But talent is a down payment. The rest is made in installments. You lost to Sable because he has been making payments longer than you have been enrolled. That's not a failure. That's arithmetic."

---

They sat on the rim after the crowd dispersed.

The arena was quiet now, the Glyph-lights dimmed to a low pulse, the carved symbols on the edge humming their ancient, steady frequency. Noir had produced a thermos from somewhere, and they passed it back and forth without speaking. The liquid inside was hot, bitter, and tasted like the inside of a library, which was either a Forge House specialty or a commentary on Noir's taste.

"The people who come here," Feimo said eventually. "The demis. They're not just fighting."

"No."

"They're finding a place where being different isn't a footnote."

Noir looked at her. In the low light, her eyes were amber, the same shade as Feimo's, the genetic marker of their shared heritage. Cat-eyes. Predator eyes. Eyes that saw in the dark and read Resonance frequencies and did all the things that the Academy's analytical frameworks classified as "intuitive" rather than "intelligent," which was the polite way of saying "not real scholarship."

"Up there," Noir said, "we are data points. Interesting anomalies. 'Oh, you're a catgirl? How fascinating. Does it help you in exams?' Down here, we are just people. The ears don't matter. The tail doesn't matter. What matters is whether you can last. Whether you can take a hit and get back up. Whether you can close your eyes in the middle of a fight and trust your ears when your eyes have failed."

She took the thermos back.

"That's not just fighting advice," she said.

Feimo waited.

"I had a friend," Noir said. The word *friend* was doing heavy lifting, carrying weight that the four letters were not designed to bear. "Human. Weave House. He was brilliant. The kind of brilliant that the Academy was built for: analytical, precise, could read a text in three languages simultaneously and produce a structural critique before the rest of us had finished the first paragraph."

She stopped. Her tail, which had been still all evening, moved once: a single, slow sweep across the dirt.

"His family were alumni. Old alumni. The kind of family that has a wing named after them in the east corridor. They found out about us. Not about the underground, about us. Two cat-eared girls in the family line was apparently acceptable. One was not."

The words hung in the air between them. Feimo could hear the Resonance frequency of Noir's voice change, a subtle drop in register that was the acoustic equivalent of a scar: healed, functional, but fundamentally altered tissue.

"He chose them," Noir said. "I don't blame him. Family is a gravity well. You don't choose whether it pulls you. You only choose whether to resist."

She looked at Feimo with an expression that was not sad, not bitter, not resigned. It was the expression of someone who has processed a dataset and arrived at a conclusion she did not like but could not refute.

"You remind me of him," she said. "Not in the ways that matter. In the ways that worry me."

"What ways?"

"You walk into rooms that read you, and you let them. You stand in front of walls that breathe and you don't flinch. You close your eyes in fights because you trust what you hear more than what you see. That's not courage. That's transparency. And transparent things break."

Feimo did not know what to say to this, so she said the truest thing she could find: "I'm still here."

"You are," Noir agreed. "For now."

The silence that followed was comfortable in the way that a bruise is uncomfortable: present, persistent, but familiar. They sat on the rim of the empty arena and listened to the hum of the carved Glyphs, and somewhere far above them the Academy continued its evening routines, students studying, professors lecturing, the Labyrinth reading, all of it oblivious to the space beneath.

---

Mia was waiting when Feimo climbed back through the boiler gap.

She was leaning against the corridor wall with her arms crossed and her expression set to a temperature that was technically below absolute zero, which should not have been possible but which Mia achieved through sheer force of personality.

"You're injured," Mia said. It was not a question.

"I'm fine."

"Your Resonance is at thirty-eight percent. Your sternum has a contusion that will be visible by morning. You smell like dirt, blood, and Forge House contraband tea. You are not fine. You are the opposite of fine. You are a case study in poor decision-making wrapped in cat ears."

Feimo leaned against the opposite wall. She was too tired to argue. Too tired to do anything except notice, with the particular clarity that exhaustion provides, that Mia had been waiting for her. Not monitoring remotely. Not receiving updates through the Labyrinth network. Physically present, in the corridor, leaning against a wall, waiting.

"You could have checked on me through the network," Feimo said.

"I could have."

"You didn't."

"No."

"Why?"

Mia's form flickered. In the blue-green corridor light, the flicker looked less like a technical glitch and more like a flinch.

"Because the network reports data," she said. "Resonance levels, location, vitals. It does not report what I needed to know."

"What did you need to know?"

"Whether you were coming back."

The words were simple. Four words. But they sat in the air between them with the weight of something that had been thought about for a long time, processed through analytical frameworks and emotional circuits and all the complex machinery of a consciousness that existed in the space between human and not-human.

Feimo felt something change. Not in the corridor. Not in the Labyrinth. In the frequency of the silence between them. It was the same silence that had existed in the underground arena, but warmer, closer, and with a different Resonance signature entirely.

"I came back," Feimo said.

"You did," Mia said. "Which proves that even poor decision-making can occasionally produce acceptable outcomes."

She turned to leave. Her form was already dissolving at the edges.

"Mia."

"What."

"Thank you for waiting."

Mia paused. Her form solidified, just for a moment, and she looked at Feimo with an expression that contained approximately zero contempt and was therefore, by Mia's standards, completely exposed.

"I am a Spirit AI," she said. "I process information. I do not experience concern. I was merely running a long-duration diagnostic on the corridor's Resonance patterns and happened to be in this location when you returned. The timing is coincidental. Do not read into it."

"I didn't say anything about concern."

"Good. Because there was none."

She dissolved. The corridor was empty.

Feimo stood there for a moment, bruised and tired and smiling in a way that she would deny if anyone asked.

---

Back in the Bridge House dormitory, Yue was awake.

This was unusual. Yue slept more than anyone Feimo had ever met, a deep, bottomless sleep that seemed less like rest and more like a retreat into some inner space that waking life could not reach. But tonight she sat on the window ledge with her blank-paged book open on her knees and the moonlight touching her face in a way that made her look like an illustration from a text that had not been written yet.

"You fought tonight," Yue said. It was not a question either. Yue had her own ways of knowing things, methods that did not involve the Labyrinth network or Resonance signatures or any of the standard detection mechanisms. She simply knew, the way some texts simply meant, without needing to be decoded.

"I did," Feimo said, sitting on her bed. "I won two. Lost one."

"Which hurt more?"

"The loss. Obviously."

"Not obviously." Yue turned a page of her blank book. Nothing was written on it. The page was white, immaculate, a surface that had been cleaned rather than never used. "The wins hurt too. They hurt differently. A win tells you what you can do. A loss tells you what you can't. Both are painful. The question is which pain you learn from faster."

Feimo pulled out her study deck. Card #026: Typology of the Four Great Novels. She held it without reading it, the way she had held Card #079 after the wall exercise, the way one holds a thing that has already become part of the body's knowledge.

*The Four Great Novels of Chinese literature each represent a different mode of storytelling. Romance of the Three Kingdoms: historical epic, the sweep of dynastic change rendered through strategy and betrayal. Water Margin: heroic legend, the lives of outlaws who become heroes because the system that should have protected them failed. Journey to the West: the mythic imagination, the trickster-monkey who fights heaven and hell and learns that the real journey is the one inward. Dream of the Red Chamber: the novel of daily life, of family and love and loss, of the world as it is rather than as we wish it to be.*

Feimo thought about the arena. Kael, with his containment structures: a Water Margin fighter, imposing order through force, the outlaw who builds a world inside a broken system. Petra, with her relentless offense: Journey to the West energy, the direct assault, the refusal to stop moving. Sable, with his patience and his crushing endurance: Dream of the Red Chamber, the long game, the novel that takes its time because it knows the ending will come regardless.

And herself? Feimo did not know. She was not historical. She was not mythic. She was not patient enough for the novel of daily life and not aggressive enough for heroic legend. She was something in between, something that did not have a category yet, a reader standing in the gap between genres, filling it with whatever she happened to be carrying at the time.

She put the card back. Drew another. Card #027: Dream of the Red Chamber.

*The pinnacle of Chinese classical fiction. A net-like narrative structure: "grass snakes and grey lines, hidden veins stretching a thousand miles." Multiple plotlines intertwined: the Baoyu-Daiyu love story as the main thread, the decline of the Jia family as the parallel thread, the Grand View Garden as the ideal world that cannot survive contact with reality. Characterization through "sameness within difference": dozens of young women, each distinct, each irreplaceable, each lost in the end.*

She thought about Mia. Not the Spirit AI who processed information and definitely did not experience concern. Not the analytical engine who taught through insults and protected through contempt. The Mia who waited in corridors. The Mia who said "whether you were coming back" with a voice that carried the acoustic signature of a question she had been asking herself for the entire time Feimo was underground.

*Dream of the Red Chamber* was a love story. Not a romance. A love story, which is a different thing entirely: longer, sadder, more honest about what it costs to care about someone who exists in a form that the world says is not permanent, not real, not enough.

"You're thinking about Mia," Yue said from the window.

"I'm thinking about literature."

"Those are not mutually exclusive."

Feimo put the deck away. She lay down. The bruise on her sternum pulsed with her heartbeat, a slow percussion that was not entirely unpleasant, the body's way of saying *you were here, you did this, you are still alive to feel it.*

"Yue?"

"Hm?"

"Noir said something tonight. About someone she used to know. Someone who reminded her of me."

Yue turned another blank page. The moonlight moved across it, and for a moment Feimo thought she saw something written there, faint marks that disappeared when she looked directly at them.

"Noir sees patterns," Yue said. "That's her gift and her burden. She sees you and she sees the past and she cannot always tell the difference."

"She's afraid I'll break."

"Everyone is afraid of that. The Labyrinth is afraid of that. Silence is afraid of that. Mia is afraid of that." Yue closed her book. "The question is not whether you will break. Everything breaks. The question is what you become after."

She turned off the window light. The room went dark. Feimo's ears adjusted, and she could hear the dormitory settling: the hum of the walls, the distant murmur of other students, the deep geological pulse of the Academy itself, reading and reading and reading, a machine that never stopped because it had never learned how.

She fell asleep thinking about connections. The brain needs exercise in the same way muscles do. Thinking is a process of making connections. Intelligence expands according to effort.

She had fought three matches and lost one and won two and learned something that no textbook had taught her: that the underground was not an escape from the Academy. It was the Academy's shadow, the text beneath the text, the meaning that only appeared when the official reading was stripped away and you were left with nothing but your own body, your own ears, your own stubborn, transparent, breakable refusal to stay down.

In the morning, the bruise would be purple and yellow, a watercolor of impact. Mia would see it and say something cutting and precise that was not, technically, an expression of concern. Noir would find her in the corridor and nod once, the professional acknowledgment, the signal that said *you came back*.

Feimo slept. The Labyrinth read her. And somewhere in the deep architecture, in the space between the official corridors and the unofficial basements, something hummed at a frequency that was not quite human, not quite mechanical, not quite text.

Not knocking. Not yet.

## Study Break: The Underground Circuit

### Card #026: Typology of the Four Great Novels

China's Four Great Classical Novels represent four distinct narrative modes. *Romance of the Three Kingdoms* is **historical epic** ("seventy percent fact, thirty percent fiction"), built on the framework of real dynastic events. *Water Margin* is **heroic legend**, centering on outlaws forced into rebellion by systemic injustice. *Journey to the West* is **mythic fiction**, a story of gods and demons that operates on multiple thematic levels. *Dream of the Red Chamber* is the **novel of worldly affairs** (世情小说), built from daily life, human relationships, and the slow erosion of an ideal world by reality.

Together with *Jin Ping Mei*, the origin point of the **novel of human relationships** (人情小说), these texts define the complete typology of Chinese classical fiction: from history to heroism to myth to the everyday.

*Think about it: When you read a passage in your exam, which mode are you in? Are you reading historically (tracking facts and arguments)? Heroically (looking for the main idea's bold claim)? Mythically (searching for deeper symbolism)? Or novelistically (noticing the small, human details that make the text breathe)?*

### Card #027: Dream of the Red Chamber

The pinnacle of Chinese classical fiction. Its narrative architecture is a **net-like structure** ("grass snakes and grey lines, hidden veins stretching a thousand miles"), weaving multiple plotlines: the Baoyu-Daiyu love tragedy, the decline of the Jia family, and the Grand View Garden as a fragile ideal world. Its characterization uses the principle of **"sameness within difference"** (同而不同): dozens of young women, each individually distinct, each irreplaceable, each ultimately lost.

For textual scholars, the most important distinction is between the **Zhi Yan Zhai manuscripts** (脂本, 80 chapters with commentary) and the **Cheng-Gao edition** (程本, 120 chapters, completed by Gao E). The question of which ending is "real" has driven two centuries of scholarship, because in *Dream of the Red Chamber*, how a story ends is not a technical matter. It is a philosophical one.

*Question: In your own study of literature, do you prefer the 80-chapter version (the unfinished, more honest fragment) or the 120-chapter version (the complete, possibly fabricated ending)? Is a story that stops short more truthful than one that continues beyond what the author intended?*

### Exam Integration: The Underground Mind

**From the 2014 Cloze:**

*"It turns out that the brain needs exercise in much the same way our muscles do, and the right mental workouts can significantly improve our basic cognitive functions. Thinking is essentially a process of making connections in the brain. To a certain extent, our ability to excel in making the connections that drive intelligence is inherited. However, because these connections are made through effort and practice, scientists believe that intelligence can expand and fluctuate according to mental effort."*

In the underground arena, this was not theory. It was survival. Every match was a cognitive workout, a forced expansion of the connections that drove combat intelligence. Sable did not beat Feimo because he was born stronger. He beat her because four years of underground fights had built a neural infrastructure that forty minutes could not replicate. The connections were made through effort and practice. Intelligence expanded according to mental effort.

And a new Web-based company, in that same 2014 test, had taken it a step further and developed the first "brain training program." The underground arena was Feimo's brain training program: unlicensed, unregulated, operating beneath the official Academy, and more effective than any sanctioned exercise.

**From the 2014 Reading, Text 1:**

*"Losing a job is hurting: you don't skip down to the jobcentre with a song in your heart, delighted at the prospect of doubling your income from the generous state. It is financially terrifying, psychologically embarrassing and you know that support is minimal and extraordinarily hard to get. You are now not wanted; you are now excluded from the work environment that offers purpose and structure in your life."*

Noir knew this feeling. Not from unemployment, but from exclusion. When the alumni family had rejected her, when the person she loved had chosen tradition over truth, she had lost the one environment that offered purpose and structure. She had been excluded, not from a workplace but from a relationship, and the effect was the same: psychologically terrifying, the knowledge that you are now not wanted.

The underground arena was her jobcentre. Not a place of dependency but a place of recovery. A space where the excluded could find structure again, where purpose could be rebuilt from the raw materials of Resonance and will.

**From the 2014 Reading, Text 2:**

*"The other reason why costs are so high is the restrictive guild-like ownership structure of the business. Except in the District of Columbia, non-lawyers may not own any share of a law firm. This keeps fees high and innovation slow."*

The Academy operated on the same logic. The Houses were guilds. The faculty were gatekeepers. The official Glyph Battle tournament was controlled, regulated, structured to reward the kinds of performance that the institution valued. The underground circuit existed because the official system was too restrictive, too conservative, too committed to its own ownership structure to allow the innovation that unregulated spaces produce.
