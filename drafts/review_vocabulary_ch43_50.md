## 7. Structural Assessment & Recommendations

### 🔴 Critical Issues

#### 1. TTR Uniformly Below 30% — Vocabulary Poverty

All eight chapters fall between 22–30% TTR, well below the 40% floor for genre fiction. The narrative is trapped in a narrow semantic field: *labyrinth, walls, passage, text, knowledge, reading, meaning, silence, language.*

**Root cause**: Limited action verbs (mostly *thought, felt, looked, seemed, knew*) and almost no sensory variety (smell, taste, texture, temperature are absent).

**Action**: (a) Replace 30% of abstract nouns with concrete sensory details. (b) Diversify verbs: *scratched, sagged, snagged, guttered, leaned.* (c) Map each "passage" occurrence — is it *the exercise*? *the excerpt*? *the assignment*?

#### 2. "the passage" × 113 — Default Noun Syndrome

Average ~14 per chapter, peaking at 22 in ch45. "Passage" has become a catch-all for any text the labyrinth presents. A reader encountering it 113 times in 8 chapters will experience semantic satiation.

**Action**: Substitute with *the exercise, the excerpt, the text, the printed block, the assignment.* Target: ≤5 per chapter.

#### 3. ch50 "she had" × 20 — Terminal Tense Fatigue

Density 10.4/1000 tokens — every 96 words. This is a chapter-length past-perfect narration that reads like a case report, not a novel's final page.

**Action**: Rewrite recall passages in simple past. Use short declarative sentences. Drop "she had" in favor of direct action: "She remembered the night—" → "The night came back in fragments."

#### 4. ch46 Copula × 37 + "that had" × 11 — Static Description Trap

"It was… / There was… / The X was…" constitute ~29% of narrative sentences. Combined with 11 "that had" back-references, ch46 reads as a museum catalogue rather than a story.

**Action**: Convert at least 20 copula sentences to active constructions. Replace "that had" clauses with participial phrases or appositives.

### 🟡 Elevated Issues

#### 5. "the door" × 89 — Inevitable but Excessive

Ch.46–49 are the "seven doors" arc, so some repetition is structurally necessary. But 89 is too many. After first mention per scene, use *it, the threshold, the entrance, the frame.*

#### 6. "feimo thought" openers × 44 — Monotone Interiority

Peaks in ch45 (7) and ch46 (10). The reader doesn't need "she thought" to know whose POV they're in. Use free indirect discourse: "This wasn't right. The glyphs had shifted overnight." (No tag needed.)

#### 7. "the silence" × 36, concentrated in ch43–46

Ch46 alone has 18. Replace at least 12 with specific auditory details: *the absence of footfalls, the drip of condensation, the faint hum behind the walls.*

#### 8. "resonance" × 16 in ch46 — Single-Draft Artifact

Almost certainly a drafting artifact. Replace 12 of 16 with *vibrated, thrummed, shivered, rippled through, settled into.*

#### 9. Hedging Adverbs: "simply" (16), "slightly" (12), "slowly" (10)

Collectively 38 occurrences. Each weakens its verb. Delete 70% of "simply" and "slightly." Keep "slowly" only when it genuinely describes pace.

#### 10. "discourse" × 18 in ch47

Conceptually justified (Foucauldian trap) but executes like an incantation. Replace at least 4 with *the text, this system of signs, what the walls spoke.*

### ✅ Clean Areas (No Action Needed)

- **Over-modification**: Only 1 sentence across 8 chapters has 3+ adverbs. Excellent restraint.
- **Body-language clichés**: "Swallowed," "chest tightened," "breath caught," "eyes widened" — all zero. The manuscript avoids these entirely.
- **"a beat of silence"**: Zero occurrences. No filler-pause clichés.
- **"merely"**: Only 2 total. No pseudo-Victorian affectation.
- **Empty time markers**: "a moment later," "after a while," "a few minutes later" — all zero.
