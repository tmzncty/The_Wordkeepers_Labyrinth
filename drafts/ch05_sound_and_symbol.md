# Chapter 5: Sound and Symbol

The wall had bowed.

Feimo could not stop thinking about it. Three days had passed since Professor Silence's wall had inclined forward. Mia had said nothing about it. Mia had said very little at all since that evening, which was either a sign of deep computational processing or a sign that something had unsettled her. With Mia, both explanations were equally plausible.

It was past midnight. Feimo sat in the Bridge House common room, her textbook open but unread, her tail twitching against the couch cushion. The walls drifted with text in a dozen languages. The hum of the Labyrinth was quieter here, gentler. But Feimo's ears were not listening to the walls.

They were listening to the floor.

The knocking had returned. Feimo didn't bother telling Mia. She pressed her palm against the floor. The vibration traveled up her arm, steady as a metronome. Three knocks, pause, three knocks, pause.

She stood up. Her textbook slid off her lap.

"Feimo." Mia was behind her, hovering, her form dim in the fading light. "I am formally advising you against this."

"Noted."

"I am also informally advising you against this."

"Also noted."

"I am additionally expressing personal concern, which is outside my official parameters but which I have decided to exercise given the circumstances."

Feimo stopped. She turned. In the dim light, Mia's holographic face held an expression she had not seen before. Not anger. Not contempt. Something rawer, something computed from data Feimo did not have access to.

"I'll be careful," Feimo said.

Mia looked away. "Your definition of 'careful' and mine differ by approximately three standard deviations."

---

The corridor ended at a wall. Or what looked like a wall until Feimo's ears caught the frequency shift, the telltale hum of concealed text. She reached out and pressed her palm against the stone. Under her touch, Glyphs materialized, faint and flickering, spelling out words she could barely read in the dark:

*REGULATION 7: ALL PARTICIPANTS ENTER AT THEIR OWN RISK. THE ARENA TAKES NO RESPONSIBILITY FOR GLYPH LOSS, CORRIDOR BURN, OR TEMPORARY DISSOLUTION OF ACTIVE VOCABULARY.*

The wall shuddered and opened.

The smell hit her first. Old stone and hot metal and something electric, the ozone tang of Glyphs being discharged at high intensity. The environment beyond the wall was enormous, a cavern carved from raw Labyrinth stone, its ceiling lost in darkness above. Runic lines of light traced the floor in concentric circles, and at the center was a raised platform, its surface scorched and scarred from a thousand engagements.

This was not the Academy.

The walls here were unpolished, unpressed, covered in text that was wild and untamed, letters overlapping in languages Feimo could not read. No amber glow. The light came from the Glyphs themselves, harsh blue-white, flickering and unstable, drawing energy from the very stone they rested in. The hum was different too, not the gentle breathing of the upper corridors but a low, grinding frequency, like a machine that had been running too long without maintenance.

Students filled the space. Not the polished, uniform-wearing students of the Academy's official halls. These students were rougher, their blazers modified or discarded, their badges replaced with marks and symbols Feimo did not recognize. They clustered around the central platform, trading crystalline Glyph shards with practiced efficiency.

And watching it all from a perch atop a broken pillar, one leg dangling over the edge, was a girl with cat ears.

Not the subtle, half-hidden ears that Feimo tucked under her bangs. These ears were large, expressive, standing tall and alert, their dark fur catching the blue-white light. The girl's hair was cropped short, practical, and a thin scar ran across her left cheek, faded but unmistakable. She wore a Forge badge, crimson and battered, pinned to a leather jacket that had never been part of any Academy uniform. Her tail, long and scarred like the rest of her, hung lazily behind her, its tip twitching in the same unconscious rhythm as Feimo's own.

She was watching Feimo with the calm, evaluating gaze of someone who had been expecting her.

"You're the new nekomimi," the girl said. Not a question. "Bridge House. First year. Took you long enough to find this place."

Feimo's ears flattened. "How did you know I was coming?"

"Sound travels. This is my environment, and I know every frequency it carries." The girl dropped from the pillar, landing with the easy grace of someone who had done it a thousand times. She was taller than Feimo, leaner, and moved with a controlled energy that suggested every gesture was deliberate. "I've been hearing your ears triangulating down through the stone for three nights. You've got good reception." She extended a hand. "Noir. Forge. Second year. I run this place."

Feimo shook the hand. Noir's grip was firm, calloused. "Run it how?"

"Someone has to." Noir gestured at the cavern. "The Academy gives you classrooms and corridors and clean, safe challenges where the worst that happens is you lose a few Glyphs and have to re-study. That's training wheels. This" — she swept her arm across the space — "is where you learn what Glyphs actually do when you push them to their limits. No safety net. No regulation from the faculty. Just you, your vocabulary, and whoever's standing across from you. There's an unwritten code down here: you fight honest, you don't steal Glyphs, and you don't snitch to the faculty."

"That sounds illegal."

"It is." Noir's grin was sharp and honest. She carved the air with her hand, a gesture that said *so what?* "Welcome to the underground."

---

Noir led Feimo through the cavern, past groups of students who looked up with curiosity or challenge or both. The arena was organized with a logic Feimo had not expected. Stations along the walls held racks of crystalline Glyph shards, each one cataloged and labeled. A scoreboard, scratched into stone with active Glyph-light, displayed names and rankings. Rules were posted in four languages on a pillar near the entrance.

"First things first," Noir said, stopping at the base of the central platform. "You know what Glyphs are. You've collected a few. But you don't know how they function in combat."

"I know they respond to meaning," Feimo said.

"Meaning is the baseline. In combat, meaning becomes force. And force follows rules." Noir held up her hand. Four Glyphs materialized above her palm, each a different color, each pulsing at a different frequency.

"Every Glyph belongs to one of four types," she said. "Nouns. Verbs. Adjectives. Adverbs. Each type has a function. Understanding that function is the difference between winning a fight and getting your vocabulary erased."

She pointed to the first Glyph, a heavy, solid shard of deep blue.

"Nouns are power. They carry the weight of reference, the force of designation. When you deploy a Noun Glyph, you're bringing the full conceptual mass of that word to bear. 'Thunder.' 'Mountain.' 'Justice.' The more contexts a word has been used in, the heavier it hits. Common nouns are lighter. Proper nouns hit like freight trains." She let the Glyph drop. It struck the platform and left a dent. "Nouns are your foundation. You build your attack and defense around them."

The second Glyph was red, bright and kinetic.

"Verbs are action. Movement, transformation, state change. A Verb Glyph doesn't just sit there; it does something. 'Strike.' 'Burn.' 'Dissolve.' Verbs are how you launch attacks, how you shift position, how you change the state of the battlefield. A well-chosen Verb can redirect an opponent's Noun back at them." Noir flicked the red Glyph; it shot across the platform and exploded against the far wall in a shower of sparks. "Speed matters with Verbs. The faster you deploy them, the less time your opponent has to counter."

The third Glyph was green, shimmering and flexible.

"Adjectives modify. They change the properties of other Glyphs. 'Hard.' 'Sharp.' 'Brilliant.' An Adjective attached to a Noun makes that Noun more effective. An Adjective attached to a Verb changes how that Verb functions. Defense, enhancement, alteration. Adjectives are how you adapt mid-fight." The green Glyph wrapped itself around the remaining Noun shard, and the blue light intensified, the conceptual mass doubling. "The principle is simple: modified Glyphs are stronger than raw ones."

The fourth Glyph was white, thin, and fast.

"Adverbs control timing and manner. 'Quickly.' 'Silently.' 'Inevitably.' They don't add power or action; they change how power and action are deployed. An Adverb can speed up your Verb, make your Noun harder to detect, change the pattern of your attack so your opponent can't predict it." The white Glyph streaked across the platform so fast Feimo barely tracked it. "Adverbs are the hardest type to master. Most fighters ignore them. The best fighters build their entire scheme around them."

Noir dismissed the remaining Glyphs and turned to Feimo. "You've got, what, five Glyphs now?"

"Six."

"Show me."

Feimo held out her hand. Her active Glyphs materialized: "affect," "matter," "continued," and three newer ones she had collected from classroom challenges. "approach," "grasp," "function."

Noir studied them. Her ears tilted. "Interesting mix. Noun-heavy, not a lot of firepower, but there's depth there." She looked up. "You want to try a round?"

Feimo's tail bristled. "Against who?"

"Against me. I'll go easy."

"Will you?"

"No." Noir grinned again, that sharp, honest grin. "But I'll tell you what you did wrong afterward. That's more valuable than winning any day."

---

They faced each other across the platform. The underground crowd had gathered, students forming a ring around the edges, their conversations dropping to whispers. Feimo could feel their attention, heavy and expectant. Her ears were pinned back, her tail wrapped tight around her ankle.

"Rules," Noir said, holding up three fingers. "One: the arena generates a passage. Blanks appear. Each correct answer gives you a Glyph to deploy. Each wrong answer costs you one from your active store. Two: you deploy Glyphs by type. Nouns for power, Verbs for action, Adjectives to modify, Adverbs for timing. Three: the fight ends when one fighter's vocabulary reaches zero." She lowered her hand. "Ready?"

"No."

"Good. Honesty is the first step toward not dying."

The platform beneath them hummed to life. Text crawled across its surface, rising from the stone like water from a spring, and the passage materialized in the air between them, filling the space with clean, sharp type:

*"Ancient Greek philosopher Aristotle viewed laughter as 'a bodily exercise precious to health.' But ___ some claims to the contrary, laughing probably has little influence on ___ fitness."*

Two blanks. Four crystalline options materialized beneath each one.

Feimo's mind raced. She knew this structure. The Hawthorne challenge on her first night had trained her for exactly this: read the passage, identify the logic, select the answer, deploy the Glyph. But the pressure was different now. Noir was already moving, her hand reaching for the first blank with practiced confidence.

**[A] among [B] except [C] despite [D] like**

The logic was clear. "Some claims to the contrary" set up a concessive relationship. The main clause said laughter has little influence. The phrase "to the contrary" indicated opposition. You needed a preposition that showed contrast despite opposing evidence.

*Despite.*

Feimo touched option C. The Glyph for "despite" materialized in her hand, thin and white, a Connector Glyph — a preposition, the binding agent between concepts. She felt its frequency: the weight of every sentence where someone had persisted in the face of evidence to the contrary. Without thinking, she launched it. The Glyph streaked toward Noir, trailing light.

Noir deflected it with a casual wave, her own Glyph, a Noun shield, absorbing the impact. "Not bad. But Adverbs are wasted on opening volleys. You should have held that one."

**[A] physical [B] mental [C] subconscious [D] internal**

The second blank. "Laughing probably has little influence on ___ fitness." Aristotle called laughter a "bodily exercise," so the passage was discussing physical health. The word "fitness" without qualification pointed to the body.

Feimo selected A. The Glyph for "physical" formed in her palm, an Adjective, heavy with the weight of embodied existence, of bodies that laughed and ached and healed. She held it, remembering Noir's lesson: Adjectives modify. Instead of launching it raw, she attached it to her Glyph of "function," enhancing the Noun with the modifier.

The combined Glyph blazed to life. "Physical function." A shield-concept, the body's capacity to operate, to move, to sustain effort against the pull of exhaustion. Feimo felt it resonate through her, grounding her, and she raised it just as Noir launched her own attack.

The impact was enormous. Noir's Glyph, a Verb, "strike," hammered into Feimo's shield and shattered against it, fragments of red light scattering across the platform. The crowd murmured.

"Interesting." Noir's ears perked forward. "You modified before defending. That's not textbook."

"I don't know the textbook."

"Clearly. Keep going."

The passage continued. More blanks appeared, each one a moment of decision:

*"Laughter does ___ short-term changes in the function of the heart and its blood vessels, ___ heart rate and oxygen consumption."*

**produce** and **boosting**. Two blanks, two Glyphs. Feimo's hand moved to "produce" first, a Verb of creation, of bringing something into existence. She felt its energy, the raw generative power of a word used in every context from manufacturing to art to biology. She launched it as an attack, the Glyph streaking toward Noir in a beam of golden light.

Noir dodged, her Adverb-enhanced speed carrying her sideways. "Verbs are direct. Good instinct. But you telegraphed it."

The second blank: "boosting." Another Verb, this one about amplification. Feimo grabbed it and held it, waiting.

*"But because hard laughter is difficult to ___ , a good laugh is unlikely to have ___ benefits the way, say, walking or jogging does."*

**sustain** and **measurable**. Feimo understood the passage now. Laughter was powerful but brief. You could not sustain it the way you could sustain walking or jogging. Its benefits were real but not measurable in the same way a jogger's improved stamina was measurable. The Glyph for "sustain" was a Verb of duration, of holding something in existence against the entropy of time. Feimo felt its weight, and she grasped something about it that went beyond the word itself.

*We may be able to sustain the illusion of control through the conscious mind alone, but in reality we are continually faced with a question.*

The thought came from nowhere and everywhere, a fragment of something she had read, a translation exercise about consciousness and the limits of will. She had been sustaining the illusion of control through her conscious mind alone, trying to analyze every blank, every Glyph, every attack. But the arena was not a classroom. The passage was not a test. It was a conversation between fighters, and conversations required listening as much as speaking.

Feimo stopped analyzing. She started reading.

And reading, she realized, came with a question she had never asked in combat before: who was telling the story? Card 010, Narrative Perspective, had taught her that every text had a narrator—first-person, third-person limited, omniscient—and that who told the story shaped what could be seen and what remained hidden. The passage on the wall had a narrator. The Glyphs in the air had a narrator. Even the Labyrinth itself, breathing words through stone, was telling a story from a perspective she could not yet identify.

The next blanks came faster:

*"___ , instead of straining muscles to build them, as exercise does, laughter apparently accomplishes the ___ ."*

**In fact** and **opposite**. A transitional phrase that confirmed and specified, followed by an Adjective sharp and paradoxical. Feimo attached "opposite" to her Glyph of "affect," creating "opposite affect," a concept that turned an opponent's force back on itself. She held the combination, waiting.

*"Studies dating back to the 1930s indicate that laughter ___ muscles, decreasing muscle tone for up to 45 minutes after the laugh dies down."*

**relaxes**. A Verb of release, of letting go. Feimo deployed it not as an attack but as a wave of energy that spread across the platform, softening the tension in the air, weakening the hard edges of Noir's offensive stance.

Noir stumbled. Her ears flicked in surprise. "What was that?"

"Laughter relaxes muscles," Feimo said. "I'm not laughing, but the principle transfers."

Noir stared at her. Then she laughed, a short, sharp bark of genuine amusement. "You are something else."

*"Such bodily reaction might conceivably help ___ the effects of psychological stress."*

**moderate**. A Verb of restraint, of finding the middle ground between extremes. Feimo collected it and felt it settle into her awareness, a word that meant to temper, to make bearable, to reduce the force of something that would otherwise overwhelm.

*"Anyway, the act of laughing probably does produce other types of ___ feedback that improve an individual's emotional state."*

**physical** again. But this time Feimo understood it differently. Not just the body, but the body's feedback to the mind. The physical was not separate from the mental; it was the foundation on which the mental was built. She attached it to her Glyph of "matter," creating "physical matter," the raw stuff of existence, and she felt the Glyph pulse with a new kind of energy: the recognition that body and mind were not two things but one system, continuous and inseparable.

The fight was reaching its climax. Noir had recovered from the relaxation wave and was launching a rapid sequence of Verb Glyphs, each one faster than the last. Strike, burn, dissolve, shatter. The air between them was thick with discharged energy, the platform scarred with new marks. Feimo had fewer Glyphs left but each one carried more meaning. She had been building connections, linking words into concepts, creating compound Glyphs that were greater than their parts. Noir was faster, more experienced, more efficient. But Feimo read differently. She read with feeling, and feeling created depth.

The last blanks appeared:

*"_11_ one classical theory of emotion, our feelings are partially rooted _12_ physical reactions. It was argued at the end of the 19th century that humans do not cry _13_ they are sad but they become sad when the tears begin to flow."*

Three blanks. **According to**, **in**, **because**.

Feimo selected "According to." It was a Prepositional Glyph, rare and heavy, carrying the weight of attribution, of crediting a source, of acknowledging that knowledge comes from somewhere, that someone proposed this theory and the world listened.

And then she selected "in." The smallest Glyph, a Preposition of location, of embeddedness. Rooted in. Growing from. The relationship between emotion and physical reaction was not one of cause and effect but one of embeddedness. Feelings did not come from physical reactions. They were rooted in them.

And "because." The Glyph of causation, the word that connects a fact to its reason, the bridge between observation and understanding. Humans do not cry because they are sad. They become sad because the tears begin to flow. The body acts first. The mind follows. Because.

Feimo held all three Glyphs, and she understood something.

This was not a fight about power. It was a fight about understanding. Every blank she filled was a piece of knowledge she now carried, a word that meant more than it had before. The passage described the fusion of body and mind — how emotion and physical sensation were not separate events but one landscape, expressed through two languages at once. It was, she thought, like the Chinese aesthetic ideal of 意境 (Card 007)—the fusion of scene and feeling, where meaning lives not in the words alone but in the charged space between them. The passage was doing exactly that: blending physical reaction and emotional truth into a single landscape where the boundary between body and mind became illegible.

She had five Glyphs left. Noir had nine.

Feimo smiled.

She launched all five simultaneously, not as attacks but as a constellation, each Glyph finding its position in a pattern that formed a complete thought: *Our feelings are rooted in physical reactions according to what we can produce, sustain, moderate, and relax.*

The constellation settled over the platform like a net of light. Noir's Glyphs stuttered, their energy disrupted by the conceptual weight of a complete, coherent sentence. Noir herself froze, her hand half-raised, her eyes wide.

The constellation held for three seconds. Then it faded, taking the last of Feimo's Glyphs with it.

Feimo's vocabulary hit zero. She lost.

But Noir was standing perfectly still, her nine remaining Glyphs flickering uncertainly around her, and the crowd was silent, and the platform was covered in a web of golden light that was only now beginning to dissipate.

Noir lowered her hand. She looked at Feimo with an expression that was not surprise, not admiration, but something closer to recognition, the look of someone who has seen a thing they had stopped believing could exist.

"You fight like a poet," she said.

---

The crowd dispersed. Noir dropped to a sitting position on the edge of the platform, her legs dangling over the side, and patted the stone beside her.

Feimo sat. Her hands were shaking. Five Glyphs, gone. The words still existed in her memory, but they were no longer active, no longer resonating in her awareness as living tools. She would have to recollect them, rebuild them from scratch.

"You're upset about the Glyphs," Noir said.

"I lost five."

"You'd have lost more against anyone else. I held back." Noir's tail swished lazily. "You know what you did wrong?"

"I ran out of Glyphs."

"You ran out because you spent them on meaning instead of efficiency." Noir picked up a loose Glyph shard from the platform and turned it in her fingers. "Most fighters deploy Glyphs one at a time. Noun, Verb, Adjective, Adverb, each one a discrete action. Efficient. Controllable. You built a sentence. A full, coherent thought with subject and predicate and modification. That's beautiful. It's also unsustainable in a prolonged fight."

"But it stopped you."

"It surprised me. That's different." Noir set the shard down. "In a real fight, surprise lasts three seconds. After that, the fighter with more Glyphs wins. And you will always face fighters with more Glyphs if you spend them on poetry."

"Then what's the point?"

"The point is that you don't have to choose." Noir stood up and stretched, her ears rotating to catch the ambient sounds of the emptying arena. "You have a Bridge instinct. You read across languages, across contexts. You see connections other fighters miss. That's not a weakness. But you need to learn how to extend that instinct efficiently. How to convey maximum meaning with minimum Glyph expenditure." She looked down at Feimo. "Come back tomorrow. Same time. I'll teach you."

"You just met me."

"I've been listening to you for three nights. That's enough to know you're relevant to this place."

Mia materialized beside them, her holographic form bright and sharp, which meant she was running at full power, which meant she was either very interested or very angry.

"Are you done?" Mia's voice was level. Dangerously level.

"Your Spirit AI has been monitoring the whole time," Noir said to Feimo. "I could feel her scanning the arena's frequencies."

"Of course I've been monitoring," Mia snapped. "My student just engaged in unsanctioned combat in an illegal underground arena, spent five precious Glyphs on a battle she had no chance of winning, and you" — she turned on Noir — "encouraged her."

"I gave her an education."

"You gave her a catastrophic depletion event."

"She's fine. Look at her."

Mia looked. Feimo was sitting on the platform edge, her tail wrapped around her ankle, her ears slightly back, her expression distant. She was processing. The passage from the battle was still resonating in her mind, the words still connected, still forming a web of meaning that she could not quite see the edges of.

"Mia," Feimo said. "That passage. About laughter and emotion and physical reactions."

"What about it?"

"The last section. 'According to one classical theory of emotion, our feelings are partially rooted in physical reactions.' And: 'humans do not cry because they are sad but they become sad when the tears begin to flow.' What does that mean for Glyph combat?"

Mia paused. When she spoke again, the edge had softened by one degree. "It means exactly what it says. Emotions are not separate from the body. They are rooted in physical reactions. The classical theory is the James-Lange theory, proposed by William James and Carl Lange independently in the 1880s. They argued that we do not cry because we are sad; we become sad because we cry. The body's response comes first. The emotion follows."

"That's backward."

"That's the conventional assumption. James and Lange proposed something different. They took an assumption we all share — that because we feel emotions, we therefore control them — and revealed its erroneous nature." Mia's voice had shifted into teaching mode, the cadence of a lecturer who genuinely loved the material. "Emotions are not mental events that happen to have physical symptoms. They are physical events that happen to have mental signatures. The body is not the mind's servant. The body is the mind's foundation."

Feimo was quiet for a long moment. Then: "Connotation."

Mia blinked. "What?"

"The passage talked about laughter having 'little influence on physical fitness.' But the word 'fitness' has connotations beyond its denotation. Its denotation is 'physical health and capability.' Its connotation includes things like gym culture, body image, the entire profit-driven fitness industry, the way 'fitness' has become a lifestyle rather than a state. When Aristotle said laughter was 'a bodily exercise precious to health,' he was using 'health' with one set of connotations. When the modern passage discusses 'physical fitness,' it carries different connotations. The same basic concept, but the meaning shifts depending on context and era."

Noir looked at Feimo with new interest.

Mia's expression did something complicated. "That is... not wrong. The distinction between connotation and denotation is fundamental to Glyph combat. Denotation is the dictionary definition. Connotation is everything the word carries beneath the surface: its cultural associations, its emotional weight, its history of use. A Glyph's power comes primarily from its connotation, not its denotation."

"So when I deployed 'relax' against Noir—"

"You were using its connotation: the accumulated weight of every context in which relaxation has been discussed, recommended, prescribed, sold, or yearned for. That connotation is what disrupted her stance."

"Which is why I told you that you fight like a poet," Noir added. "Poets understand connotation instinctively. They choose words for what they carry, not just what they mean. Most fighters are blunt instruments. You're a scalpel."

Feimo's ears perked forward. Something warm bloomed in her chest, not quite pride, not quite confidence, but something adjacent to both. The feeling of having a place, a function, a reason to be in this impossible place. For the first time since she had fallen asleep in Study Room 4B with her face in a textbook, she felt like she could become something here. Not just a student. A Wordkeeper.

"But," Mia said, and the warmth dimmed slightly, "a scalpel is only useful if you can afford to keep it sharp. You lost five Glyphs tonight. Your active vocabulary is now at three. That is dangerous. If you enter another arena round without replenishing, you will be fighting with almost nothing."

"Then I'll replenish."

"How? You're a first-year. Classroom challenges give you one Glyph per session at best. The official route to Glyph acquisition is slow by design."

Noir spoke up. "The arena has a Glyph market. Students trade and sell Glyphs. It's informal and unregulated." She reached into her jacket and pulled out a small crystalline shard. "Take this."

The Glyph was "restore." Feimo felt its frequency before she even touched it: the weight of every damaged thing that had been brought back to wholeness.

"I can't take this," Feimo said.

"You can, and you will. Consider it a purchase on account." Noir pressed the Glyph into Feimo's hand. "It's a Bridge Glyph. It wants to be with a Bridge user."

The Glyph pulsed warm in Feimo's palm. She felt it settle into her awareness, joining her three remaining Glyphs, and the relief was immediate, like a missing tooth suddenly filled, like a gap in a sentence completed.

---

They sat on the platform edge, the three of them. Mia hovered to Feimo's left, her form dimming as she shifted to energy-conservation mode. Noir sat to Feimo's right, her tail hanging over the edge, swishing in slow arcs.

"The passage tonight," Feimo said. "The one about laughter. The experiment with the pen in the teeth. Those forced to exercise their smiling muscles reacted more enthusiastically to funny cartoons. The physical act shaped the emotional response. That's the James-Lange theory in action."

"More accurately," Mia said, "it's evidence supporting the James-Lange theory. The experiment demonstrates that physical posture influences emotional response. The volunteers who held the pen in their teeth did not choose to smile; the smile was imposed on them. And yet the imposed smile produced genuine emotional change. The body shaped the mind."

Feimo thought about this. "So in Glyph combat, the perspective from which you read a passage changes what you get from it. Different focalization, different Glyph power."

"Exactly." Mia paused. "Where did you learn the term 'narrative focalization'?"

"You mentioned it once. In passing."

Mia's form flickered with what might have been surprise. Noir watched the exchange with visible interest.

"Noir," Feimo said. "Why do you run this place?"

Noir was quiet for a long time. Her tail had stopped swishing. "Because the Academy teaches you that words are safe," she said finally. "Clean. Academic. Controlled. But words aren't safe. The Labyrinth preserves every word ever written, and some of those words are dangerous, and pretending otherwise is a lie I'm not willing to sustain."

From somewhere deep below, the knocking resumed. Three knocks, pause, three knocks, pause. Noir's ears tracked it too.

"You hear it too," Feimo said.

Noir's expression shifted. "I hear a lot of things down here. Things that don't make it to the official Academy reports." She stood up and brushed off her jacket. "Go home, Feimo. Get some rest."

"Tomorrow?"

"Same time. Same place." Noir walked toward the far exit, her scarred tail swishing. She paused. "And Feimo? That thing you did at the end. Launching all five Glyphs as a sentence. Don't do that again."

"Why not?"

"Because it was the most beautiful thing I've seen in this arena, and beautiful things get noticed. And not everyone who notices is friendly." She vanished into the dark corridor.

---

Feimo climbed the stairs in silence. Mia hovered beside her, her form flickering in the darkness.

"You're quiet," Feimo said.

"I'm processing."

Mia did not respond for twelve steps. Then: "I am required to advise you against returning to the arena. The consequences range from Glyph confiscation to suspension."

"Required by whom?"

"By my programming. By the Academy's regulations."

"But?"

"But your reading depth increased by forty percent during the battle. Your connotation processing speed doubled." A pause. "If you are going to return, you will study the Glyph type system before your next visit. You will practice deployment efficiency. You will not spend more than three Glyphs on a single gesture."

"The sentence was effective."

"The sentence was reckless." Mia turned to face her. In the amber light of the upper corridors, something warmer than her usual professional detachment showed through. "I cannot lose another partner," she said. Then caught herself. "That is to say, the Academy's investment in your education would be wasted if you were suspended. Nothing more."

"Of course," Feimo said.

"Stop smiling."

"I'm not smiling."

"Your tail is wagging."

Feimo wrapped her tail around her ankle. "It's a reflex."

They reached the Bridge common room. The couch Feimo had abandoned hours ago was still warm, her textbook still open, her cold boba tea still sitting on the side table. Everything exactly as she had left it. But Feimo was not the same. She carried four Glyphs now instead of six, but each one was sharper, clearer, more deeply understood. She carried the memory of a battle she had lost but a lesson she had won. She carried Noir's words, *you fight like a poet*, and Mia's grudging data point, and the sound of the knocking rising from the Deep Archive, patient and relentless and waiting.

She sat on the couch and opened her textbook to a different chapter. The heading read "Connotation, Denotation, and Contextual Meaning."

"Are you actually going to study?" Mia asked, incredulous.

"I need to recollect my Glyphs. And I need to understand connotation better before tomorrow."

Mia's form flickered. She settled onto the arm of the couch, hovering at low power. "Read the section on contextual shifts. Connotation is not a fixed property. It shifts with culture, with history, with who holds the word. A Glyph's power is a function of every mind that has ever used it."

Feimo wrote this down. Mia did not comment on her handwriting, which was its own form of approval.

From below, muffled by stone and text and the warm amber hum of the Labyrinth, the knocking continued.

Three knocks. Pause. Three knocks. Pause.

Someone was still down there. And Feimo was beginning to think they were not knocking to get out. They were knocking to get her attention.

She closed her eyes and let the Labyrinth's hum carry her toward sleep. Tomorrow she would return to the arena. Tomorrow she would learn to fight more efficiently, to deploy her Glyphs with the precision of a Bridge student and the instinct of a cat.

But tonight she was a girl on a couch, with a tail curled around her ankle and four words burning bright in the dark behind her eyelids, and the sound of knocking rising from the deep places of the world, patient and endless and full of meaning she had only just begun to grasp.
 grasp.
