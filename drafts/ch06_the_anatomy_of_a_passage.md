# Chapter 6: The Anatomy of a Passage

The classroom smelled like chalk dust and quiet disappointment.

Not Feimo's disappointment. She had nothing to be disappointed about, having survived the underground arena with her dignity mostly intact and her Glyph count at a respectable seven. The disappointment belonged to the room itself: a Mirror House lecture hall, borrowed for the afternoon, its silver-gray walls lined with analytical texts that radiated the particular energy of a space that had witnessed ten thousand students not doing the reading.

Mia hovered at the front of the hall, her holographic form sharpened to unusual clarity. She had configured herself for what she called "classroom mode," which meant her circuit-pattern cuffs glowed brighter, her voice projected without shouting, and her expression of professional contempt had been dialed from its usual eight to a restrained six.

"Welcome to Study Break Session Two," she said. "Today's topic: how to read a passage without embarrassing yourself."

There were twelve students in the hall. Most were Bridge House, but a few cross-House stragglers had wandered in, including a Weave boy who kept checking his notes and a Forge girl with ink-stained fingers who looked like she had been dragged here against her will. In the back row, half-hidden behind a book, sat Yue. Silent Yue, who attended every class but spoke in none of them, whose presence was so unobtrusive that people sometimes forgot she was there until they noticed the extraordinarily precise annotations she left in the margins of every text she touched.

Feimo sat in the middle row, her tail draped over the back of her chair, her ears tracking Mia's movements with the involuntary precision of a cat watching a laser pointer.

"Most of you," Mia continued, "read the way you eat: fast, thoughtlessly, and without any awareness of what you're actually consuming. You look at words. You register their surface meaning. You move on. This is not reading. This is skimming with extra steps." She paused. "Today I will teach you the Three-Layer Reading Method. It will change how you process every text you encounter in the Labyrinth. It will also, not incidentally, determine whether you survive your next corridor challenge."

She projected a passage onto the hall's front wall. Feimo recognized it at once—the Aristotle laughter cloze, the same one she had already dissected in her earlier study sessions. The familiarity was almost disorienting, like seeing a familiar face in a dream. The text materialized in clean, sharp Glyph-light, each word pulsing faintly as it appeared:

*"Ancient Greek philosopher Aristotle viewed laughter as 'a bodily exercise precious to health.' But despite some claims to the contrary, laughing probably has little influence on physical fitness."*

"Layer One," Mia said. "Surface Reading. What does the text say?" She looked around the room. "Anyone? Use your words. Assuming you have any."

The Weave boy raised his hand. "Aristotle thought laughter was healthy. The author disagrees."

"Correct and reductive. Like saying the Mona Lisa is 'a painting of a lady.' You are not wrong. You are merely inadequate." Mia's lips curved. "Surface Reading means understanding the literal content: who said what, what happened, what claim is being made. It is the foundation. Without it, the other layers are meaningless. But alone, it is insufficient."

She expanded the passage. More text appeared, flowing across the wall like ink:

*"Laughter does produce short-term changes in the function of the heart and its blood vessels, boosting heart rate and oxygen consumption. But because hard laughter is difficult to sustain, a good laugh is unlikely to have measurable benefits the way, say, walking or jogging does."*

"Question: What is the logical relationship between the first sentence and the second?"

Silence.

"Come on. This is not a trick question. The first sentence admits something. The second sentence limits it. What word signals the relationship?"

Feimo's ears twitched. She knew this. The Strack passage from her exam prep, the one about the experiment in Würzburg. She had studied it so many times the words had worn grooves in her memory. "But," she said. "The first sentence concedes that laughter does something. The second 'but' restricts the concession. It's a 'yes, but not enough' pattern."

Mia's eyes flickered. Not surprise, exactly. More like the recalibration Feimo had learned to expect when she got something right: the brief, involuntary adjustment of an AI who had been prepared for failure and got competence instead.

"Adequate," Mia said. "This is the beginning of Layer Two: Structural Analysis. How is the argument built? What are the logical connectors? Where does the author concede, where do they restrict, where do they conclude?" She gestured at the wall. "Every passage has an architecture. Layer Two is learning to see the blueprint."

She walked them through the rest of the cloze passage. The Aristotle text unfolded on the wall like a cadaver on a table, and Mia dissected it with the precision of a surgeon and the bedside manner of a tax auditor.

"In fact," she said, pointing to the transition marker, "this phrase signals that the author is about to *deepen* the previous point, not contradict it. When you see 'in fact' on an exam, your brain should immediately register: 'The author is about to give me more evidence for what they just said.' This is not optional knowledge. This is survival."

She turned to the section about laughter relaxing muscles, and the Forge girl in the back actually leaned forward, forgetting to look bored.

"Here the author uses a contrast structure. Exercise strains muscles to build them. Laughter does the opposite. What's the key word?"

"Instead," Feimo said.

"And what does 'instead of' signal?"

"A substitution. One thing in place of another."

"Precisely. Instead of straining, laughter *relaxes*. The author is building a comparison, and the comparison is the structure. Layer Two is about seeing these joints, these connectors, the places where one idea bolts onto another." Mia's voice had warmed by exactly one degree, the way it always did when she was genuinely teaching and not merely criticizing. "If Layer One is reading with your eyes, Layer Two is reading with your brain. You are no longer just consuming content. You are analyzing how the content is assembled."

Feimo found herself leaning forward too. Not because the material was new, but because Mia's way of explaining it made the familiar feel structural, as if someone had turned on a light inside a room she had been stumbling through in the dark.

The passage continued into the Strack experiment: a psychologist at the University of Würzburg asked volunteers to hold a pen with their teeth, creating an artificial smile, or with their lips, producing a disappointed expression. Those who smiled reacted more enthusiastically to funny cartoons.

"This experiment," Mia said, "is a perfect example of why Layer Two matters. The author could have simply stated: 'Facial expressions influence emotions.' That would be Layer One: the claim. Instead, the author *shows you the evidence*. They describe the experiment, the method, the result. The structure is: claim, then evidence, then conclusion."

She paused. Her voice dropped half a register.

"But Layer Three is where it gets interesting."

The projection shifted. New text appeared alongside the laughter passage: a different excerpt, this one from a passage about the New York Philharmonic's decision to hire a relatively unknown conductor named Alan Gilbert.

*"For the most part, the response has been favorable, to say the least."*

"What is the author *really* saying?" Mia asked. "Not the surface meaning. Not the structure. The *subtext*."

Aya spoke from the doorway.

She had arrived without anyone noticing, which was typical of her. Aya did not enter rooms; she materialized in them, silver-gray and immaculate, her Mirror House uniform pressed to a standard that made everyone else look like they had dressed in the dark. Her hair, that startling platinum, was pulled back in a precise tail. She carried a single notebook, and her eyes, pale as winter water, were fixed on the projected text with the absolute, terrifying focus that Feimo had seen on their first day.

"The author is being sarcastic," Aya said. "'To say the least' is a hedge. They're saying the response has been favorable, but only if you're being generous about what 'favorable' means."

"Interesting," Mia said. "Wrong, but interestingly wrong."

Aya's expression did not change, but something in the air around her cooled by several degrees.

"The phrase 'to say the least' is not sarcasm," Mia continued. "It is understatement. The author is saying: 'The response has been *extremely* favorable, and I am choosing to express this with deliberate restraint.' The subtext is not doubt. The subtext is emphasis through restraint." She turned to the room. "Layer Three: Subtextual Resonance. What the author means beyond the words. Tone, attitude, implied meaning, rhetorical strategy. This is the layer where most students fail, because it requires you to read not just the text, but the *person behind the text*."

Feimo caught herself staring at Mia with an expression she would later describe as "purely academic interest" and that her ears, swiveled forward and slightly tilted, would describe as something else entirely.

"The parenting passage," Mia continued, projecting a new excerpt, "makes a similar move. The author Jennifer Senior argues that happiness in child-rearing is not a present-tense experience but a *past-tense* condition. The day-to-day grind is miserable. But looking back, it becomes gratifying." She looked at Feimo. "What does that tell you about the author's *attitude*?"

Feimo thought about it. "She's not saying children make you happy. She's saying the *memory* of raising children makes you happy. The distinction matters."

"Correct. And rare. Most students read that passage and conclude 'children = good.' Layer One thinking. Surface consumption." Mia's voice carried a faint edge now, the kind that meant she was about to say something personal. "The ability to read what an author *means* rather than what they *say* is the difference between a student and a reader. Between someone who consumes language and someone who understands it."

The classroom fell quiet for a moment. The hum of the Labyrinth's walls filled the silence, that constant, warm frequency that Feimo had learned to associate with the feeling of words being alive.

"For your exercise," Mia said, snapping back to brisk efficiency, "work through the remaining blanks in the laughter passage in pairs. I want Layer Two annotations: identify every logical connector and explain its function. You have fifteen minutes. If you finish early, I have supplementary material from a passage about marketing media, and I *will* test you on it."

She paused, looking directly at Feimo.

"And for the record, the word 'experiment' comes from the Latin *experientia*, meaning 'a trial.' Every experiment is a trial: of a hypothesis, of a method, of the experimenters themselves. The Strack experiment tested whether physical expressions could *predict* emotional responses. The answer, after a decade of replication studies, is: sort of. Which is science's way of saying 'it's complicated, but we're working on it.'"

Feimo smiled into her notebook. Cat puns were coming. She could feel it.

"Now get to work before I lose my patience. Which, I should remind you, I was not designed with."

---

The exercise lasted twelve minutes. Feimo and her partner, a Bridge boy named Kael who had a gift for translation but struggled with analysis, worked through the laughter passage methodically. Feimo identified the connectors: "despite" signaled a concession. "In fact" deepened the previous point. "Consequently" introduced a result. "Similarly" drew a parallel. Layer Two, once you could see it, was like a skeleton visible beneath skin: you could not unsee it.

Mia circled the room, hovering over each pair, offering corrections in a voice that managed to be simultaneously helpful and devastating.

"No. 'Therefore' and 'consequently' are not the same. 'Therefore' is a logical deduction. 'Consequently' is a causal result. One follows from reason. The other follows from events. If you confuse them on the exam, I will haunt your dreams."

She paused at Feimo's desk. Looked at her annotations. Said nothing for three seconds.

"Adequate," she finally said, and moved on. From Mia, "adequate" was a standing ovation.

After the exercise, Mia assigned supplementary reading: excerpts from a passage about the decision of executives to leave secure positions and pursue new career goals. She said it was for "structural analysis practice," but Feimo noticed that the passage's theme, people leaping without a safety net, made Mia's voice tighten in a way that had nothing to do with grammar.

"Suppose I told you," Mia said, "that every reading passage on an exam is a contest: the author versus the reader's assumptions. Your job is not to agree. Your job is to see the argument clearly enough to *contest* it, if the evidence warrants."

The Forge girl muttered something about there being no revenue in reading comprehension.

Mia turned to her with the slow, deliberate patience of a predator that had already decided when to strike.

"Every passage is a *contested* space," Mia said. "The author has a thesis. The evidence supports or undermines it. The reader must *navigate* the author's argument, not just follow it. Whether the subject is CEOs, laughter, marketing, or the decline of the humanities, the reading strategy is the same. You are not reading about CEOs. You are reading about *how an author builds a case*. The subject matter is incidental. The architecture is everything." She smiled. "Also, the passage about the humanities mentions *reform* of higher education, which is relevant to anyone hoping to survive this academy without dropping out, which, statistically, half of you will."

The Forge girl did not raise her hand again.

---

After the session ended and the students filed out, Feimo lingered. She was packing her notes when she noticed Yue still sitting in the back row, not moving, staring at the book in her hands.

Feimo walked over.

Yue's book was small and leather-bound, the kind of personal copy that had been read so many times the spine was soft. It was open to the middle. The pages were blank.

Not torn. Not damaged. The paper was still there, smooth and whole, but the text was gone. As if the ink had decided to leave.

"When did this start?" Feimo asked.

Yue looked up. Her eyes were calm, the way a lake is calm when the ice has just formed and you cannot see the water beneath.

"Three days ago," she said. "A word here. A word there. I thought I was imagining it." She turned a page. Blank. Another page. Blank. The text resumed further in, but thinner, as if the words were fading from the center outward. "Now whole pages."

Feimo touched one of the blank pages. Her cat instincts prickled: a faint hum rose from the paper, distorted and wrong, like a radio signal being jammed. The Labyrinth's walls hummed with the aggregate of billions of minds reading. This hum was the opposite: the sound of reading stopping.

Mia materialized beside them. She scanned the book, her eyes scrolling with data. Her expression shifted.

"This is not normal text degradation," she said. "Words that nobody reads grow thin. That is natural. This book is being read. Actively. Daily." She paused. "Something is erasing it."

Yue closed the book gently, the way you might close the eyes of someone who had died.

"It will come back," Feimo said, because she did not know what else to say.

Yue looked at her. Something passed across her face, not quite a smile, not quite a flinch. "You are *bound* to think that," she said quietly. "Because you are new. Because you still believe that words, once lost, return." She tucked the book under her arm. "I used to believe that too."

She walked out of the lecture hall without another word. The door closed behind her with a sound like a page turning.

---

Feimo could not sleep.

This was not unusual. Her Labyrinth body did not require much rest, and her real body, still sitting in Study Room 4B with her face in a textbook, was being sustained by whatever mysterious mechanism kept Pilgrims connected to the physical world while their consciousness wandered the Academy. But tonight the restlessness was different. It had a direction.

She found the observatory on the Academy's upper level, past the Weave dormitories and through a corridor she had never explored. The room was a dome, its ceiling open to a vast dark space that was neither sky nor void but something in between: a field of stars, each one a small, pulsing point of light arranged in patterns that shifted as she watched.

She stepped inside. The door closed behind her. The hum of the walls grew softer, almost reverent, as if the Labyrinth itself was holding its breath.

The stars were not decorative. She realized this as she moved closer to the center of the room, where a circular platform held a pedestal engraved with fine Glyph-script. The script read, in a language she could parse instinctively:

*Every Pilgrim carries a star. While the Pilgrim walks, the star burns. When the Pilgrim stops, the star goes dark.*

Feimo looked up. Hundreds of stars. Maybe thousands. Most were bright, scattered across the dome like a map of journeys still in progress. But among them, dark spaces gaped like missing teeth. Stars that had once burned and now were cold, their positions marked only by the faint depression in the light-field where they had been.

There were more dark stars than bright ones.

The knowledge settled into her by degrees, the way cold settles into bones. Each dark star was a student. Someone who had entered the Labyrinth with a star of their own, who had walked its corridors and studied its words and carried its Glyphs, and then had stopped. Failed. Left. Or worse.

Feimo's hand rose without her permission. She reached toward the cluster of dark stars nearest to her, a dense knot of extinguished light near the dome's center, as if whatever had happened to them had happened together, in the same era, in the same wave of loss.

Her fingers touched the first dark star.

It lit up.

Not brightly. Not the fierce, full glow of the active stars overhead. A faint amber pulse, warm and tentative, like a candle flame in a drafty room. It spread from her fingertips through the star's surface, filling the depression with light that should not have been possible, because the star was dead, because the Pilgrim was gone, because that was how the Labyrinth worked.

Feimo pulled her hand back. The star kept glowing. Faint, fragile, but alive.

"Don't."

Mia's voice came from the doorway. She was hovering there, her form dimmer than usual, the circuit-pattern on her cuffs barely visible. She did not move closer.

"What just happened?" Feimo asked.

"I don't know." Three words that, from Mia, were an admission of something vast and terrifying. She floated into the room with deliberation, each inch a negotiation with whatever she was feeling. "Pilgrims don't relight dead stars. It has never been recorded."

"Maybe the recording is wrong."

"The recording is *extensive*. I have been monitoring this observatory for longer than you have been alive." Mia stopped at the edge of the platform, looking up at the dome. In the starlight, her face was all angles and shadows, stripped of its usual sharpness. She looked, for the first time, not like a Spirit AI performing a function but like someone standing in a graveyard.

"That cluster," she said, pointing to the dark stars Feimo had touched. "Six Pilgrims. Same cohort. All Bridge House. All enrolled in the same *era*, six years apart from yours." She paused. "They made it to the third year. All of them. They were talented."

"Were they your students?"

A silence that lasted long enough to have its own geography.

"One of them was."

Mia did not elaborate. She did not need to. The way her gaze lingered on those dark stars, the way her form flickered when the amber glow of Feimo's relit star pulsed brighter, the way her voice had shed every trace of sharpness and become something raw and unguarded, all of it told the story that she would not.

Feimo thought about what she had learned in class that day. Layer One: the surface. Mia had a student before. Layer Two: the structure. The student was talented. The student failed. The star went dark. Layer Three: the subtext. The subtext was that Mia was afraid. Afraid of watching another star go dark. Afraid of caring about someone whose light she could not keep burning.

It struck her that the knowledge cards Mia had referenced during the lesson, projected briefly on the wall as supplementary material. Card 008: Typicality. The idea that a character could be both individual and universal, that one person's story could contain the pattern of everyone's story. Each dark star in the dome was typical: a specific Pilgrim's specific failure, and also the universal fear that every student carried, the question they did not ask aloud: *What if my star goes out too?*

Card 009: Image and Symbol. An image was a concrete thing: a star, a hand, a touch. A symbol was what it meant: hope, fragility, the desperate wish to keep something alive against the certainty of darkness. The star chart was both. Every star was an image, a specific point of light belonging to a specific person. And the chart as a whole was a symbol of the Labyrinth's relationship with mortality: words could be preserved forever, but the people who read them could not.

"Can I touch another one?" Feimo asked.

"You should not."

"Why?"

Mia's answer came after a delay that was definitely computation and not hesitation. "Because every star you relight gives you a connection to that Pilgrim's residual Glyph signature. You carry their echoes. That is not a small thing."

"Is it dangerous?"

"Everything about you is dangerous, Feimo. You are a nekomimi with above-average Glyph reflexes who walks into restricted areas and touches dead stars because your instincts tell you to. Danger is not the question. The question is what you are *becoming*."

Feimo touched another dark star. It lit up, amber and warm. Then another. And another. Each one pulsed in rhythm with her heartbeat, a constellation of impossible light spreading across the dome like dawn breaking over a battlefield.

Mia watched. Her form had stopped flickering. She was still, perfectly still, the way only a hologram could be still: no breathing, no micro-movements, no involuntary shifts of weight. Just presence. Watching Feimo bring light back to the places where light had died.

"You asked me once," Mia said, "what my designation means. Spirit AI. It means I am the Academy's attempt to give guidance to students who would otherwise wander the corridors alone and unprotected." Her voice was quiet, stripped to its lowest register, the voice she used when she was telling the truth without any protective layers of irony. "It also means I have done this before. Many times. For many students. Across many *decades*." The word hung in the air. "Some of them are bright stars over your head. Most of them are dark."

She looked at Feimo. "The Labyrinth's ruling principle is that words survive by being read. Stars survive by being kept lit. And Pilgrims survive by persisting."

Feimo lowered her hand. The relit stars pulsed softly around her, fragile and warm. She could feel them in her awareness, not like her own Glyphs, but like distant radio signals: faint impressions of people she had never met, their residual energy still humming in the Labyrinth's walls.

"I'm not going to go dark," she said.

"You cannot *predict* that."

"No. But I can *persist*." She looked at Mia. "That's Layer One, right? The surface meaning. I'm still here."

Mia's expression did something complicated, something that involved approximately seven micro-movements in sequence, each one cancelled by the next, a war between contempt and gratitude and fear and something else that Feimo could not name but could feel, warm and sharp, like the moment before a Glyph flares to life.

"Layer One is insufficient," Mia finally said. "As I explained in class. Today. At length. While you were paying attention for once." She turned away. "Come on. You have a dawn class tomorrow, and if you show up with dark circles under your eyes, people will think I'm not taking care of you."

"Are you taking care of me?"

"I am managing an asset. There is a difference."

They left the observatory together, the door closing on a dome full of stars, some dark, some bright, and a handful that glowed amber where no amber should have been, warm and stubborn and alive.

---

The Bridge House dormitory was quiet. Most students were asleep, their consciousness returned to their real-world bodies for the night. The common room's multilingual walls moved unhurriedly, sentences drifting in a dozen languages, the Labyrinth's constant murmur reduced to a whisper.

Yue was sitting on the window ledge.

She was not reading. This was notable because Feimo had never seen Yue not reading. Her book, the one with the blank pages, sat beside her, closed. Yue was looking out the window at the Labyrinth's nightscape: corridors stretching into darkness, the distant glow of other Houses, the faint movement of text on far-off walls.

Feimo sat down beside her. They did not speak for a while. The hum of the walls filled the silence, warm and low.

Then Yue said, without turning from the window:

"I used to dream about the words."

Her voice was soft, almost inaudible, the voice of someone who had held a sentence inside for so long that letting it out felt like surgery.

"Every night, the Labyrinth would show me passages in my sleep. New ones. Old ones. Texts I had never read, in languages I did not know, and somehow I understood them all. The words would move behind my eyes like fish in deep water. It was beautiful." She paused. "I thought of it as a gift. The Labyrinth saying: *I see you. I know what you are. Here is language, in all its forms, given freely.*"

Feimo waited.

"Three months ago, the dreams stopped."

Yue's hands were still in her lap. Perfectly composed. Not trembling, not clenched. Just resting, the way hands rest when the body has accepted something that the mind has not.

"I tried to bring them back. I read more. I slept less. I switched books, switched corridors, switched languages. Nothing worked." She turned to Feimo, and her eyes were calm in the way that deep water is calm: clear on the surface, with no visible bottom. "The blank pages started two weeks after the dreams stopped. The book is not the only thing losing text. I have three others at home. All fading. All from the center outward."

"Yue..."

"I can never dream again."

The sentence landed in the space between them with the weight of a book closing for the last time. Not dramatic. Not theatrical. Just final, the way a locked door is final: you can stand in front of it for as long as you want, but it will not open.

Feimo did not say "It will come back." She did not say "I'm sure it's temporary." She did not say anything at all, because some silences are not meant to be filled, and some words are not meant to be answered, only heard.

Behind them, hovering in the doorway where neither Feimo nor Yue could see her, Mia's form dimmed until she was almost invisible. Her obsidian eyes were fixed on Yue. Something moved behind them, a calculation she was running and rerunning, a variable she could not solve for, a pattern she had seen before and could not bear to see again.

She said nothing. But her hands, translucent and precise, curled slowly into fists.

---

Feimo lay on her dormitory bed and stared at the ceiling. The text drifting overhead was in a language she did not recognize tonight, sentences flowing in slow, unfamiliar script, the Labyrinth cycling through its vast archive of human expression. Her own Glyphs pulsed faintly in her awareness: "affect," "matter," "continued," and the newer ones she had collected in the underground arena, each one a small bright point in the dark behind her eyelids.

She considered the three layers. Surface: a student who could not dream. Structure: the dreams stopping, then the text fading, a sequence, cause and effect or coincidence. Subtext: the subtext was that something in the Labyrinth was dying, quietly, in a way that nobody was talking about, and the people closest to it were the ones least able to ask for help.

Her mind drifted to Mia's student. The talented one. The one whose star went dark. She thought about what it meant to be a digital consciousness that could not sleep, could not dream, could not forget, and had to carry the memory of every student it had lost across every era it had served.

The realization came—the stars she had relit. Six of them, amber and faint, pulsing in a dome she was not supposed to have entered. Six echoes of six people who had walked these corridors before her. Their residual Glyphs were faint impressions in her awareness now, not words but feelings: determination, exhaustion, wonder, fear, and something she could only describe as the stubborn refusal to stop reading, even when the text was fading, even when the dreams were gone, even when the stars went dark.

The hum of the Labyrinth felt different tonight. Heavier. Not threatening, but burdened, the way a voice is burdened when it is trying to tell you something important and cannot find the words.

Feimo closed her eyes. Her tail curled around her ankle, warm and comfortable. The Bridge House badge on her lapel pulsed softly.

Somewhere in the dormitory, she heard Yue turn a page. Then turn another. Looking for words that were no longer there, in a book that was slowly becoming blank, in a Labyrinth that preserved every word ever written except, it seemed, the ones that mattered most.

And from somewhere far below, so far that she might have imagined it, the hum of the walls carried a faint, familiar rhythm.

Not knocking this time.

Something else.

Something that sounded, if she held her breath and listened with more than her ears, like someone reading aloud in the dark, very quietly, to no one at all.
