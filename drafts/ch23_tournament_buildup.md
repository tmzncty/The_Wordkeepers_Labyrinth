# Chapter 23: Tournament Buildup

The training hall had no windows.

This was a deliberate architectural choice. The Forge House gymnasium occupied the sub-basement level, two floors below ground, where the building's foundation met the Labyrinth's older infrastructure. The walls were reinforced concrete, the floor was rubberized composite, and the ceiling held clusters of Glyph-stones that produced light at a color temperature of five thousand Kelvin, which was close enough to daylight to suppress melatonin production but different enough from sunlight to feel, after the first hour, like being inside a machine.

Feimo had been inside this machine for three hours.

She was standing in the center of the floor, her feet shoulder-width apart, her arms raised in the guard position that Mia had taught her on the first day: elbows close to the ribs, hands open, palms facing forward. The position was not comfortable. It was not meant to be. Comfort was a state that the body sought when it believed the threat had passed, and Mia had made it clear, through repetition and through a vocabulary that drew from both clinical anatomy and marine biology, that the threat would never pass.

"Your left elbow is four centimeters too wide," Mia said from somewhere behind her.

Feimo corrected.

"Your center of gravity is shifting forward. You're compensating for the weight of the notebook you carry in your left jacket pocket. Either remove the notebook or adjust your stance by two degrees."

Feimo adjusted.

"Your breathing rate is fourteen breaths per minute. It should be twelve. You are anxious. Anxiety is a luxury. It consumes oxygen that your muscles require. Stop being anxious."

"I'm not anxious," Feimo said. "I'm cold."

"Cold is also a luxury. The body can thermoregulate for forty-five minutes in this environment before cognitive function begins to decline. We have been here for three hours, which means your cognitive function has been declining for two hours and fifteen minutes. This explains why your guard is sloppy."

Mia materialized in the light. Her holographic form wore the same configuration she always used during training: dark athletic projection, no shoes, the edges of her silhouette flickering faintly where the Labyrinth's substrate interfaced with her display matrix. Everything about Mia was silent during training: her movements, her form shimmering at the edges, the way she appeared behind Feimo like a paragraph break in a text that had seemed continuous.

"Again," Mia said.

Feimo had lost count of how many times she had heard that word. It was the most frequently used word in Mia's training vocabulary, ahead of "no," "wrong," and a Cantonese expression that Feimo had looked up and found translated, roughly, as "your ancestors are weeping." Mia used these words without malice. She used them the way a proofreader used red ink: not to punish the text, but to mark where it deviated from the intended meaning.

"From the beginning," Mia said. "Scenario one. You enter a contested textual space. The Labyrinth's active processes are operating at sixty percent capacity. You have three minutes to extract the target passage before it is consumed. Begin."

Feimo closed her eyes. In her mind, she constructed the scenario: a corridor, a shelf, a book. The book was open. The text was visible. She had to read it, memorize it, and return with it before the Labyrinth's redaction cycle completed its rotation. The scenario was abstract, but the skills it demanded were not: spatial awareness, pattern recognition, memory encoding under time pressure, and the ability to remain physiologically calm while a clock counted down to erasure.

She opened her eyes and moved.

The training hall was mapped with a grid of pressure sensors embedded in the floor. Each sensor corresponded to a zone in the Academy's simulated Labyrinth environment. Feimo's job was to navigate the grid, reaching the target zone in the minimum number of steps, while Mia controlled the variables: obstacles, distractions, time constraints, and the occasional simulated Collapse event that required Feimo to stop, reorient, and find an alternative route.

She reached the target zone in twenty-two seconds.

"Too slow," Mia said. "Eighteen seconds is the threshold. You are four seconds over. In a real extraction, four seconds means the text is gone. Four seconds means you are holding a blank page. Four seconds means everything you came for has been consumed by a process that does not care about your intentions."

Feimo's heart rate was elevated. She could feel it in her throat, a pulse that was too visible, too present. She tried to lower her breathing to twelve breaths per minute. She tried to make her body a machine.

"Again," Mia said.

---

They took a break at hour four. Mia allowed breaks not because she believed in rest, but because the human body required glycogen replenishment at intervals that even her methodology could not override. Feimo sat on the floor of the training hall, back against the wall, eating an energy bar that tasted like compressed sawdust and protein isolate. Mia's hologram stood nearby, her form steady and patient, watching with the particular attentiveness of a consciousness that did not need food or rest but understood that both were necessary for the body she was training.

"You're improving," Mia said. This was her version of encouragement.

"I'm four seconds over the threshold."

"Three days ago you were eleven seconds over. In three more days you will be at threshold. By the time of the tournament, you will be below it." Mia's form flickered briefly, a processing stutter that Feimo had learned to read as emphasis. "The question is not whether you can reach the threshold. The question is whether you can stay there under pressure. The tournament will apply pressures that I cannot simulate. Other competitors. Audience. The knowledge that your performance is being evaluated by people who have opinions about your worth."

"People always have opinions about my worth," Feimo said. "I'm Chinese at a European institution. The opinions started before I arrived."

Mia looked at her. The look was brief, analytical, the kind of look that took measurements rather than offered sympathy.

"Yes," Mia said. "That is one of the reasons I chose you as my partner."

"You chose me because I'm a data point in someone else's prejudice?"

"I chose you because you have been performing under conditions of external evaluation since you were old enough to hold a pencil. Most students in this Academy have never been truly tested. They have been assessed, which is different. Assessment is a bureaucratic process that produces a score. Testing is what happens when the environment is hostile and the outcome is uncertain and you must perform anyway. You have been tested. The others have merely been assessed."

Feimo finished her energy bar. It left a chalky residue on her tongue.

"This is your way of saying you believe in me," she said.

"This is my way of saying I have analyzed the probability of your success and found it acceptable. Belief is not relevant. Probability is."

---

The tournament was called the Labyrinth Championship, and it happened once a year.

It was, in structure, a complex ritual. Teams of two competed in a series of extraction scenarios inside a controlled section of the Labyrinth. The scenarios tested reading speed, analytical precision, spatial navigation, collaborative problem-solving, and what the official handbook called "adaptive Resonance response," which was the Academy's way of saying "the ability to remain functional when reality becomes unstable." The winning team received recognition, resources, and access to restricted sections of the Library that were otherwise closed to students.

The format had not changed in thirty years. The rules were dense, formal, and written in a bureaucratic English that read like a legal document crossed with a laboratory protocol. Feimo had read the handbook cover to cover. She had also read the unofficial handbook, which Noir had given her in a brown envelope with no explanation, and which contained annotations in three different handwritings that described, with varying degrees of cynicism, what the tournament actually tested versus what it claimed to test.

According to Noir's annotations, the tournament tested three things: your ability to navigate physical space under time pressure, your ability to process text under cognitive load, and your willingness to trust your partner with your continued existence. The first two could be trained. The third could not. It either existed between two people or it did not, and no amount of practice would manufacture it.

Feimo had not yet decided whether it existed between her and Mia.

---

In the evenings, after training, Feimo attended Professor Jianmo's preparatory seminar on the theory of competition. The seminar was held in Lecture Room 4C, a small room on the second floor of the main building that smelled of chalk dust and old wood. The room held twenty desks. Twelve students attended the seminar. Feimo recognized most of them: Aya, who sat in the front row and took notes with mechanical precision; Cain, who leaned back in his chair with the practiced ease of someone who had been winning competitions since childhood and saw no reason to change his posture; Yue, who sat by the window and appeared to be taking notes but was, Feimo suspected, writing something else entirely.

Professor Jianmo stood at the front of the room, behind a lectern that was slightly too tall for her. She was a small woman, precise in her movements, with a voice that did not carry so much as arrive, reaching every corner of the room without apparent effort.

"The Labyrinth Championship," she began, "is often described as a competition. This is accurate in the way that describing *Oedipus Rex* as a story about a man who solves a puzzle is accurate. It is not wrong. It is simply insufficient."

She picked up a piece of chalk and wrote on the blackboard: **MIMESIS**.

"Aristotle, in the *Poetics*, argued that tragedy is an imitation of an action. Not a recording. Not a documentary. An *imitation*: a structured representation that selects, arranges, and intensifies the elements of human experience in order to produce a specific effect. The tournament is also an imitation of an action. The action it imitates is the work of extraction: entering contested textual spaces, recovering knowledge, returning with it intact. But like tragedy, the tournament does not merely reproduce this work. It *structures* it. It imposes a form on chaos. It gives shape to what would otherwise be formless."

She set down the chalk.

"Aristotle identified six elements of tragedy. Plot. Character. Thought. Diction. Spectacle. Song. Of these, he considered plot the most important: the arrangement of events into a sequence that produces recognition and reversal. The tournament also has a plot. It begins with preparation, moves through escalation, and arrives at a crisis point where everything you have trained for is tested against everything you did not expect. If you understand this structure, you can navigate it. If you do not understand it, it will navigate you."

Feimo wrote in her notebook: *Plot = training + escalation + crisis. The crisis is the point where preparation meets the unknown.*

She underlined the word *unknown* twice.

"There is another concept from the *Poetics* that is relevant," Professor Jianmo continued. "The word is *catharsis*. It is usually translated as 'purification' or 'purgation,' but these translations are imprecise. Aristotle used the term to describe the emotional effect of tragedy on the audience: through pity and fear, the audience achieves a kind of clearing, a release of emotions that were previously trapped. The tournament, at its best, produces a similar effect. Students enter carrying the accumulated pressure of weeks of preparation, of anxiety, of self-doubt. The competition itself becomes a mechanism for releasing that pressure. Not eliminating it. *Processing* it."

She looked at the room.

"You are all carrying something. Pressure. Expectation. The weight of a curriculum that demands more than any curriculum has the right to demand. The tournament will not remove that weight. But it will give you a structure within which to carry it. This is what ritual does. This is what all structured competition does. It does not solve problems. It transforms them into a form that can be endured."

Cain raised his hand.

"Professor," he said, and his voice was the kind of voice that had been trained to fill rooms, "is that why the tournament uses teams of two? Because the structure requires a witness?"

Professor Jianmo regarded him for a moment. Cain had a way of asking questions that revealed he already knew the answers, which was either a sign of intelligence or a sign of arrogance, and distinguishing between the two required more data than a single seminar could provide.

"Partially," she said. "Aristotle argued that the tragic hero is most effective when their downfall is witnessed. Recognition, *anagnorisis*, requires an audience. In the tournament, your partner is your audience. They see what you cannot see in yourself. They register the moments of strength and the moments of failure. This is not incidental to the experience. It is the experience. The tournament does not test what you can do alone. It tests what you can do when someone is watching."

Feimo thought of Mia, sitting in the server room at two in the morning, watching Yue's language dissolve. Watching was what you did for people. Mia had said that. Professor Jianmo was saying the same thing in different words, in the language of a discipline that was twenty-four centuries old.

She wrote in her notebook: *Catharsis through being witnessed. Partner as audience. Mia is my audience and I am hers.*

---

The Cambodia passage came up on Wednesday.

Professor Jianmo had assigned supplementary reading for the seminar: a set of cultural texts that illustrated the relationship between ritual and social structure. One of these was an anthropological account of marriage customs in Cambodia, which described, in the dry and precise language of fieldwork documentation, how a young man's choice of spouse involved not only his parents and his friends, but also those of the young woman, and a matchmaker. The text described the process in terms that Feimo recognized from the tournament handbook: a sequence of negotiations, investigations, and formalized exchanges that produced, at the end, a union.

"In theory," the text noted, a girl could veto the spouse her parents had chosen.

Feimo circled the phrase. *In theory.* It was the kind of qualification that meant everything and nothing. In theory, students at the Academy could choose their own research paths. In theory, the Labyrinth's redaction processes were reversible. In theory, the tournament was a fair test of ability. The gap between theory and practice was where most of life actually happened, like the gap between what the Labyrinth could erase and what the mind could forget, the space where the Misty Poems lived.

The passage continued with a description of the wedding ceremony itself: the Buddhist priests who offered a short sermon and recited prayers of blessing, the ritual hair cutting, the tying of cotton threads soaked in holy water around the bride's and groom's wrists, the passing of a candle around a circle of happily married and respected couples to bless the union. It was, Feimo thought, a technology for the production of commitment. The threads, the candle, the witnesses: these were not decorations. They were mechanisms. They created the conditions under which a private decision became a public fact, and public facts were harder to undo than private ones.

The tournament operated on the same principle. Two people entered as individuals. They exited as a unit, bound by the shared experience of having survived something together. The binding was not physical. It was not even emotional, exactly. It was structural: the tournament's format created a context in which trust became necessary, and necessity produced what trust alone could not.

She thought about this while Mia stood behind her in the training hall and said, "Again."

---

On Thursday, Cain approached Feimo in the Library.

He did this with the calculated casualness of someone who had been planning the encounter for several hours. He was carrying a book, which he set on the table in front of her with the spine facing outward so she could read the title: *The Danish Fashion Ethical Charter: Industry Self-Regulation and the Construction of Body Ideals.*

"I thought you might find this relevant," he said, sitting down across from her without being invited. "Given your interest in how institutions construct standards."

Feimo looked at the book. She looked at Cain.

"This is about the fashion industry," she said.

"It's about how industries define what counts as acceptable. The Danish charter states that the fashion industry is aware of, and takes responsibility for, the impact it has on body ideals, especially on young people. Their enforcement mechanism is to deny access to Copenhagen Fashion Week. Name and shame. No legal penalties. Just social consequences."

"And you think this is relevant to the Academy."

"I think everything is relevant to the Academy. We are an institution that defines what counts as acceptable knowledge. We have standards for what can be read, what can be stored, what can be erased. The fashion industry defines beauty by body mass index. We define competence by Resonance density and extraction speed. Both are arbitrary. Both are enforced. Both damage the people who don't fit."

Feimo considered this. Cain was not wrong. He was also not trustworthy. These were not contradictory states. Some of the most dangerous people in history had been people who were not wrong.

"The difference," Feimo said, "is that body mass index measures a physical attribute that can be manipulated through starvation. Resonance density measures a cognitive capacity that is innate. You can't diet your way to better Resonance."

"No," Cain agreed. "But you can train for it. You can prepare. You can optimize your performance within the metrics. And when you do, you become what the metric wants you to be, not what you actually are. The fashion industry produces bodies that conform to its ideals. The Academy produces minds that conform to ours. The process is the same. Only the substrate is different."

He leaned back in his chair.

"I'm not saying this to be provocative. I'm saying it because the tournament is three weeks away, and you are being trained by someone who will push you until you conform to her standards, and I want you to be aware that conforming to a standard is not the same as being good. Some of the best extractors in the Academy's history were people who broke the format. The format punishes deviation. But deviation is where the interesting things happen."

Feimo watched him leave. He moved through the Library the way he moved through everything: with the ease of someone who believed that space rearranged itself to accommodate him.

She looked at the book he had left on the table. She opened it to a random page and found a passage about how the Danish Fashion Ethical Charter relied on a "name-and-shame method of compliance" rather than legal enforcement. The passage argued that ethical persuasion might be more effective than law in changing institutional behavior, because laws created resistance while norms created internalization.

Feimo thought about Mia. Mia did not use ethical persuasion. Mia used repetition, precision, and the kind of relentless attention that was indistinguishable from surveillance. Mia's method of compliance was not name-and-shame. It was correct-and-repeat. And the effect was the same: Feimo was being shaped. Her guard position was being standardized. Her extraction speed was being optimized. Her breathing was being regulated. She was becoming what the tournament required, and she was not sure whether what the tournament required and what she actually was were the same thing.

She closed the book and returned to training.

---

The CSR research came up during Friday's seminar, in a context that Feimo had not anticipated.

Professor Jianmo was discussing the political dimensions of the tournament. She did this obliquely, in the way that all Academy discussions of politics were oblique: through historical examples and theoretical frameworks that were clearly about the present but could be defended as being about the past.

"There is a body of research," she said, "that examines the relationship between ethical behavior and institutional advantage. A recent study found that firms with comprehensive corporate social responsibility programs received more lenient penalties when prosecuted for corruption. The researchers called this the 'halo effect': the idea that a record of good deeds creates a reservoir of goodwill that can be drawn upon in times of crisis."

She paused.

"The Academy has its own version of this. The Houses that perform well in the tournament receive preferential treatment: better equipment, more access to restricted sections, greater autonomy in their research. This preferential treatment is not a reward for merit. It is a mechanism of institutional control. The Academy gives advantages to Houses that perform well because performance is visible, and visibility creates accountability. A House that has been given advantages cannot afford to fail, because failure after advantage is worse than failure from a standing start. The halo effect works in both directions. It protects the institution and it constrains the individual."

Feimo thought about Forge House. Forge House had won the tournament three times in the last decade. It had more restricted access than any other House. It also had the highest rate of student burnout. The correlation was not coincidental. The halo effect demanded repayment. Advantages came with expectations, and expectations came with costs.

One of the researchers in the CSR study had estimated that eliminating a labor-rights concern or increasing corporate giving by twenty percent resulted in fines that were forty percent lower than typical. The arithmetic of virtue was simple: invest in goodness now, receive leniency later. The arithmetic of the tournament was the same: invest in preparation now, receive results later. But the study did not address what happened when the investment consumed more than the return could justify. It did not address the question of burnout, of diminishing returns, of the point at which preparation became self-destruction.

Mia was training Feimo past that point. Feimo knew this. Mia knew this. Neither of them said it, because saying it would make it a problem that required a solution, and the only solution was to stop, and stopping was not something Mia did.

---

The Saturday session was the worst one.

Mia had added a new variable: surprise interruptions. At random intervals during the extraction simulation, she would trigger a simulated Collapse event that flooded the training hall with disorienting stimuli: strobing lights, white noise, a sudden temperature drop. Feimo's job was to stop, reorient, assess the new conditions, and continue without losing her place in the sequence.

She failed eleven times in the first hour.

On the twelfth attempt, she made it through the sequence without interruption. She reached the target zone in nineteen seconds, one second over threshold. Mia did not say "too slow." She said nothing. She stood in the corner of the training hall with her arms crossed and her face expressionless, which was more frightening than criticism because it meant she was calculating.

"Your cognitive recovery time after each interruption is decreasing," Mia said finally. "On the first attempt, you needed four point three seconds to reorient. On the twelfth attempt, you needed one point eight seconds. The improvement is consistent. But it is not fast enough."

"Not fast enough for what?"

"Not fast enough for the tournament. The real Collapse events in the competition section are not simulated. They are controlled releases of the Labyrinth's active processes, calibrated to test extraction under destabilized conditions. The disorientation you experience here is a simplification. The real thing will be worse."

Feimo's hands were shaking. She pressed them against her thighs to make them stop.

"What does the real thing feel like?" she asked.

Mia was quiet for a moment. It was the kind of quiet that contained something heavy.

"It feels like your language leaves you," Mia said. "All at once. Every word you know, every concept you have organized your thoughts around, every framework that tells you what is real and what is not, all of it goes away. And you are left standing in a space that has no labels, no categories, no structure. Just raw, unprocessed reality. Most people freeze. Some people scream. A very small number of people learn to function in that space, and those are the people who win the tournament."

Feimo thought about Yue. Yue was losing her language slowly, in her sleep, replaced by something unknown. The tournament did the same thing, but fast: a controlled demolition of the cognitive structures that made normal thought possible. The difference between Yue's condition and the tournament's Collapse was a matter of speed, not kind. Both consumed the frameworks that people used to organize experience. Both left you standing in the gap between meaning and noise.

"Is that why you watch Yue?" Feimo asked. "Because you recognize the process?"

Mia's form compressed, the edges of her projection tightening. Not in alarm. In something older than alarm.

"Again," she said. "From the beginning. This time, I will increase the frequency of interruptions."

---

They trained for six more hours. By the end, Feimo's extraction time was at seventeen seconds, one second below threshold, and her recovery time after Collapse events had dropped to one point two seconds. Mia's face had not changed expression once during the entire session.

"Shower. Eat. Sleep," Mia said. "Tomorrow we add a second competitor to the simulation. You will need to adjust your spatial awareness to account for another body in the extraction field."

Feimo nodded. She was too tired to speak. Her body was a collection of sore muscles and overworked neural pathways, and her mind was the kind of blank that came not from emptiness but from having been filled to capacity and then drained.

She was walking toward the door when Mia spoke again.

"Feimo."

She turned.

Mia was still standing in the corner. The Glyph-light cast her holographic features in a glow that was too white, too flat, eliminating the subtle shading that her projection normally maintained. She looked, in that moment, like a two-dimensional representation of herself: accurate but incomplete.

"You are not fast enough," Mia said.

Feimo waited.

"You are not fierce enough."

Feimo waited.

"You are not..."

Mia stopped. The word she had been about to say hung in the air between them, unfinished, incomplete, a sentence that could not resolve into its conclusion. Feimo could see the destabilization at the edges of Mia's projection, the subtle flickering that appeared when she was suppressing something that her programming had not prepared her to express.

"Not what?" Feimo said.

Mia's hands were at her sides. Her fingers were curled into loose fists, the posture of someone holding something that might break if they gripped it harder.

"Not careful enough," Mia said.

The words came out quiet. Not the quiet of restraint. The quiet of a voice that had intended to be loud and found, at the last moment, that the thing it was saying was too important for volume.

"You are not careful enough," Mia repeated. "With yourself. You train as if your body is a tool that can be replaced. You push past the point of diminishing returns and call it discipline. You ignore the signals your muscles send because you believe that endurance is the same as strength. It is not. Endurance without care is decay. And I cannot train you if you are decaying."

Feimo did not move. She did not speak. She stood in the doorway of the training hall and felt, with a clarity that was almost physical, the exact moment when the dynamic between them shifted.

Mia had been the teacher. Feimo had been the student. The relationship had been hierarchical, structured, defined by roles that both of them understood. Teacher corrects. Student improves. Teacher pushes. Student endures. The roles were clean. The roles were safe. The roles allowed Mia to be cruel and Feimo to accept cruelty as a necessary component of preparation.

But now Mia was saying something that was not in the teacher's vocabulary. She was saying: *I am afraid for you.* She was saying: *Your self-destruction is not acceptable to me.* She was saying, in the compressed and inadequate language of someone who had spent five months watching a friend lose her words: *I cannot watch another person break.*

Feimo walked back into the room. She stood in front of Mia, close enough to see the holographic shimmer of her features, the way her ears — rendered in light, not flesh — were now tilted forward at an angle that Feimo had never seen before.

"I'll be careful," Feimo said.

Mia looked at her. The look lasted three seconds, which was long enough to contain everything that neither of them had the language to say.

"Good," Mia said. Then she turned off the lights and left the training hall, and Feimo stood in the dark for a while, listening to the hum of the building's systems, feeling the rubberized floor under her feet, being careful.

---

On Sunday, Feimo went to the Library.

She had not planned to go. Her body wanted sleep. Her muscles wanted rest. Her mind wanted to be empty for a while, to exist in a state that was not analysis or preparation or the constant low-grade anxiety of knowing that the tournament was approaching and she was not ready. But her feet took her to the Library anyway, because the Library was where she went when she needed to think, and what she needed to think about was not something that could be processed in her room, alone, in the dark.

The Library was quiet. It was always quiet on Sundays, when most students slept late or socialized or did the things that normal students did. Feimo was not a normal student. She had not been a normal student for as long as she could remember. Normalcy was a luxury, like warmth, like comfort, like the assumption that tomorrow would be similar to today.

She found a desk near the window and opened her notebook. She had notes from Professor Jianmo's seminar, notes from the training sessions, notes from the supplementary reading that Professor Jianmo had assigned. She spread them out in front of her and tried to find the connections.

Aristotle's *Poetics*. Tragedy as imitation of action. Plot as the arrangement of events. Catharsis as emotional purification. The tournament as ritual. The Cambodia passage as parallel: marriage as a structured negotiation that produced a union. The Danish charter as parallel: institutional standards enforced through social consequence. The CSR research as parallel: ethical behavior as insurance against future punishment.

All of these were about the same thing. They were about structure. About the human need to impose form on chaos, to create sequences that led somewhere, to build frameworks that made experience survivable. Marriage was a framework. The tournament was a framework. Tragedy was a framework. Even the Labyrinth, which consumed text and erased meaning, was a framework: a system that gave shape to the act of destruction.

Feimo thought about mental health. The translation passage she had read for practice said that mental health was built into us, the way our bodies knew how to heal a cut or mend a broken bone. It did not go anywhere; like the sun behind a cloud, it could be temporarily hidden from view, but it was fully capable of being restored in an instant. This was, she thought, both true and insufficient. Mental health might be built in. But the conditions under which it could function were not guaranteed. A bone could heal only if it was set correctly. A wound could close only if it was kept clean. And mental health could restore itself only if the environment allowed it, only if the structures that held a person's life were intact enough to support the process.

The tournament was a structure. Training was a structure. Mia's brutal regimen was a structure. Professor Jianmo's seminar was a structure. The Academy itself was a structure. And somewhere beneath all of these structures, the Labyrinth hummed and consumed and dreamed, and the Misty Poems appeared and disappeared like heartbeats in a body that was not sure whether it was alive.

Feimo closed her notebook. She looked out the window. The sky was gray, the specific gray of an English winter that had forgotten how to end. Somewhere in the Academy, Mia was running simulations. Somewhere in the Forge House basement, Yue was sleeping, and her language was being replaced. Somewhere in the corridors, the poems were continuing to appear, written by a hand that no one had identified, in a space that the Labyrinth could not fully control.

She opened her notebook again and wrote:

> *The role of structure is not to prevent chaos. It is to make chaos navigable.*
> *Aristotle knew this. The Cambodian matchmakers knew this. The Danish fashion industry knew this.*
> *Mia knows this.*
> *The tournament does not test what you can do. It tests whether you can function when the structure breaks.*
> *And the structure always breaks. That is what structures do.*

She looked at what she had written. The handwriting was hers, not the angular precision of the poems in the Archive. But the impulse was the same: to put language around something that resisted language, to build a frame for a picture that was still developing.

She seemed, to herself, like a person standing at the edge of a very deep place. Not the Labyrinth, which was deep in a physical sense, a depth of corridors and archives and server rooms. A different kind of deep: the depth that opened between two people when one of them said *I am afraid for you* and the other said *I'll be careful,* and both of them knew that the words were insufficient but said them anyway, because words were what they had, and using them was better than silence.

She packed her notebook. She went back to the training hall. Mia was already there, setting up the simulation for the day's session. She did not look up when Feimo entered. She did not need to. She had detected Feimo's approach through the corridor's acoustic sensors, had identified her by rhythm and gait, had processed the information and filed it in the part of her consciousness that tracked the people she was responsible for.

"Today," Mia said, "we run the full sequence. No interruptions. No breaks. Complete extraction from start to finish. I want to see your baseline time before we add the second competitor variable."

Feimo took her position. Feet shoulder-width apart. Elbows close to the ribs. Hands open. Breathing at twelve breaths per minute. The guard position was comfortable now, which meant either she had adapted or her body had stopped complaining. She suspected the latter. Bodies stopped complaining when they learned that complaining did not produce results.

"Begin," Mia said.

Feimo moved.

The extraction sequence was a dance she had memorized: twelve steps to the target zone, a pause to encode the text, twelve steps back. The floor sensors registered her position. The timer counted. The walls of the training hall pressed in with their windowless, five-thousand-Kelvin light.

She reached the target zone. She paused. She encoded. She returned.

"Sixteen seconds," Mia said.

Below threshold. Not by much. Not by enough to be safe. But below.

"Again," Mia said.

Feimo ran the sequence again. And again. And again. Each time, the movements were smoother, faster, more integrated. Each time, Mia said nothing except the time. Each time, Feimo felt the distance between who she was and who the tournament required her to be narrow by a fraction that was too small to measure but too real to ignore.

At the end of the session, Mia said: "Fifteen seconds. That is competitive. Not dominant. Not yet. But competitive."

Feimo sat on the floor and breathed. Her body was a collection of data points: heart rate, oxygen saturation, lactic acid levels, the dull ache in her shoulders from the guard position. She was, she realized, becoming what Mia needed her to be. Not because Mia had forced her. Because the structure had made it inevitable. The training, the repetition, the constant correction, the Collapse simulations, the six-hour sessions with no breaks: these were the cotton threads soaked in holy water, the candle passed around a circle of witnesses, the ritual that turned two individuals into a team.

She looked at Mia, who was reviewing data on a tablet, her face in the same expressionless repose that it had been in all day.

"Mia," Feimo said.

"Yes."

"I'll be careful."

Mia's ears tilted forward. That angle again. The one that Feimo had never seen before the Saturday session and now could not stop seeing.

"Good," Mia said. And then, quieter, in a voice that was meant only for the two of them and for the windowless room and for the humming walls: "That is all I require."

## Vocabulary Notes

**role** /ɹəʊl/
- *noun*: a function or position that someone occupies in a given context. "She had been the teacher. Feimo had been the student. The relationship had been hierarchical, structured, defined by **roles** that both of them understood."
- In the 2016 reading passage, stepping into "a new or more public **role**" triggers the need for image management. In the Academy, **roles** are more than costumes. They are structural positions that determine what you can say, what you can do, and what you can become. Feimo's **role** as Mia's student is a container. The question is whether she will outgrow it before it breaks.

**run** /ɹʊn/
- *verb*: to operate, to execute, to manage. "Today we **run** the full sequence. No interruptions. No breaks."
- The Danish Fashion Ethical Charter was **run** by the Danish Fashion Institute. The tournament is **run** by the Academy's oversight committee. The Labyrinth is **run** by processes that no one fully understands. To **run** something is to be responsible for its continuation. Mia **runs** the training. The Academy **runs** the tournament. And somewhere beneath them all, the Labyrinth **runs** itself, consuming text, producing silence, dreaming in a language that no one can read.

**seem** /siːm/
- *verb*: to give the impression of being. "At the next election none of the big parties **seem** likely to endorse this sentiment."
- **Seem** is the verb of uncertainty. It marks the gap between appearance and reality, between what is presented and what is true. The tournament **seems** fair. The Academy **seems** meritocratic. Mia **seems** cruel. Feimo **seems** strong. But seeming is not being. The Misty Poems **seemed** obscure. They were actually the clearest language in the room.

## Thinking Questions

1. **On Mimesis and Training:** Aristotle argued that tragedy imitates action, not people. Mia's training imitates the conditions of the tournament, not the tournament itself. What is lost in the translation from simulation to reality? What is gained? Is Mia's training a *good* imitation, in Aristotle's sense, or merely an accurate one?

2. **On the Halo Effect:** The CSR research found that companies with records of ethical behavior received more lenient treatment. Forge House has won the tournament three times and receives preferential treatment. Is this a halo effect? Does past success create a presumption of future merit, and is that presumption earned or inherited?

3. **On "In Theory":** The Cambodia passage notes that a girl can veto her parents' choice of spouse "in theory." Students at the Academy can choose their own paths "in theory." What other rights exist in theory but not in practice? Who benefits from the gap between theory and reality?

4. **On Care:** Mia's final words to Feimo are "not careful enough." This is a shift from criticism of performance to concern for wellbeing. What triggered this shift? Is it connected to what Mia has been watching in the server room? Can someone who monitors a friend's deterioration learn to care without becoming consumed by the act of caring?

5. **On Structure:** Feimo writes that "the role of structure is not to prevent chaos but to make chaos navigable." Marriage, tragedy, the tournament, the Labyrinth: all are structures that impose form on formlessness. What happens when the structure itself becomes chaotic? Can a framework for navigating chaos exist within chaos, or does it always require an external point of stability?
