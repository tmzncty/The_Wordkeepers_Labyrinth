# Chapter 7: The Forge Below

The arena smelled like iron and electricity.

Feimo had been here once before, when Noah had shown her the underground cavern with its scorched central platform and blue-white Glyph-light. But that had been reconnaissance. Tourism. Tonight she was standing at the edge of the platform with her sleeves rolled up and her ears flat against her skull, and the girl across from her was not smiling.

"Rules," Noah called from her perch atop the broken pillar. Her voice carried through the cavern with the easy authority of someone who had announced a thousand matches. "Standard Glyph duel. First to three clean marks wins. No targeting the face. No using borrowed Glyphs you haven't absorbed. No crying." She paused. "That last one is aspirational."

The crowd was larger than Feimo had expected. Forty students, maybe more, pressed into the rough stone amphitheater that surrounded the platform. They were not the polished Academy students who sat in Mia's lecture hall and pretended to do the reading. These were Forge regulars and Weave drifters, a few Bridge House outliers who had found their way down through the same instinct that had led Feimo here weeks ago. They watched with the hungry, calculating attention of people who bet on outcomes, their voices rising and falling in the half-lit bays carved into the arena's walls, each bay a pocket of whispered odds and wagered Glyph shards.

Her opponent's name was Sable. Third-year, Weave House, tall and angular, with a scar across her right knuckle that suggested she had been doing this for longer than Feimo had been enrolled. Sable's Glyphs were visible in her aura: three blue, two silver, one that flickered amber at the edges. She had a reputation in the underground. Feimo had heard the name whispered in corridors, attached to phrases like "never loses on the first exchange" and "don't let her set the tempo."

Feimo's own Glyphs pulsed in her awareness. "Affect." "Matter." "Continued." And the newer ones, harder and stranger, collected from the underground over the past weeks: "resist," "sustain," and the one she still did not fully understand, a Glyph that hummed at a frequency she could only describe as *contrary*. Seven Glyphs in total. Modest by the standards of the arena. Enough to fight with. Maybe enough to win.

"Begin," Noah said.

Sable moved first. Her attack was textbook Weave methodology: a lattice of semantic energy that spread across the platform like a net, each thread carrying a fragment of meaning designed to disorient. It was beautiful work, precise and layered, the kind of Glyph deployment that professors praised in controlled environments.

But this was not a controlled environment.

Feimo's ears caught the frequency of the lattice before it reached her. She could hear it, the way she could hear everything: not as sound but as pattern, each thread vibrating at a slightly different pitch, the whole structure singing a chord that her nekomimi senses parsed into data faster than conscious thought. She saw the gaps. Three of them, each one a space between threads where the semantic energy was thinnest.

She moved through the first gap. The lattice scraped against her shoulder, leaving a faint mark on her jacket, but it did not connect. She felt the heat of it, the brief sting of meaning touching skin, and then she was through.

Sable's eyes widened. Not much. A third-year's composure did not break easily. But Feimo saw the micro-expression: surprise, recalibration, and then the quick hardening of someone who had just realized that the first-year cat girl was not going to be the easy match she had expected.

Feimo did not give her time to adjust.

"Resist" flared in her palm. Not an attack Glyph by nature, but Feimo had learned something in the weeks since Noah had first shown her how Glyphs functioned in combat: every Glyph had a defensive reading and an offensive one, the way every sentence had a surface meaning and a subtext. "Resist" could block. It could also *push back*. She shaped it into a concentrated burst and sent it directly into the lattice's central node.

The lattice shattered.

The sound was extraordinary. Like a glass cathedral collapsing, each thread snapping in sequence, the whole structure folding in on itself in a cascade of blue-white sparks. The crowd pulled back. Someone swore. A boy in the front row covered his face with his arm as fragments of broken Glyph-light scattered across the amphitheater.

Sable stumbled. The lattice had been her primary structure, and its destruction left her momentarily exposed, her aura flickering as her remaining Glyphs scrambled to compensate. It was an opening. Small, barely two seconds wide, but Feimo had been trained by the Labyrinth to read openings the way Mia had taught her to read passages: quickly, structurally, with an eye for the architecture beneath the surface.

She closed the distance in three strides. "Affect" burned in her right hand. She placed it, palm flat, against Sable's sternum.

One clean mark.

The arena erupted. Not in cheers, exactly. The underground did not cheer. It *reacted*, a collective intake of breath that was more assessment than celebration, the sound of forty gamblers recalculating their odds in real time. The drama of a first-year breaking a third-year's lattice spread through the amphitheater like a shockwave, and with it came the most valuable currency in the underground: attention.

Sable recovered fast. She was a third-year, and third-years did not stay third-years by being slow. She created distance, restructured, and launched a counter-pattern that was tighter and more aggressive than her opening. This one had no aesthetic pretension. It was designed to hit.

Feimo took the first mark on her forearm. Pain, sharp and real, the kind that came from Glyph energy burning against skin. Her sleeve singed. The mark glowed angry red. She gritted her teeth and felt her Glyphs surge in response, the combat reflex that Noah had described as "your vocabulary fighting for its own survival."

The exchange that followed lasted forty-five seconds and felt like forty-five minutes. Feimo gave a second mark, took a second mark, dodged a third attack by dropping to one knee and letting it pass over her head close enough to singe her ear fur. Her nekomimi senses were screaming, every frequency amplified, the arena's noise reduced to a single focused channel: the sound of Sable's Glyph deployment, its rhythm, its pattern, its tells.

Everyone had tells. Every fighter telegraphed their next move through micro-adjustments in their Glyph frequency. Sable's tell was a slight tightening in her lattice pattern before she deployed an offensive burst, the way a musician's hand might tense before a difficult passage. Feimo had heard it in the first exchange. She heard it again now.

Sable tensed.

Feimo was already moving. Not backward, not sideways. Forward. Into the space where the offensive burst would be weakest, directly in front of Sable, close enough to feel the heat of the Glyph forming. The burst erupted. It grazed her left shoulder, another mark, her third and final allowance before a loss.

But her right hand was already in position.

"Continued."

The Glyph was not powerful. It was not dramatic. It was, by most standards, one of the least impressive Glyphs in the Labyrinth's lexicon: a small, persistent thing, the meaning of *continuation*, of *not stopping*. But Feimo had learned something about "continued" that no textbook taught: when deployed at close range against an opponent who had just committed all their energy to an offensive burst, "continued" did not need to be strong. It needed to be *there*. In the space Sable had left empty. In the gap between her attack and her recovery.

Feimo pressed it against Sable's shoulder. Clean mark. Third mark. Match.

The arena was silent for one full second. Then the noise came, not cheering but something rawer: the sound of the underground acknowledging an upset, recalibrating its hierarchy, making space for a new name.

Sable stepped back. She looked at Feimo with an expression that was not anger and not admiration but something in between: the look of a competitor who had just been shown something she did not expect and needed time to decide what it meant.

"Good," she said. One word. Then she turned and walked off the platform, disappearing into the crowd with the controlled grace of someone who had lost before and would lose again and knew that neither fact defined her.

Feimo stood on the platform, breathing hard, three marks burning on her body and a victory she had not been sure she could earn settling into her bones like warm water. Her ears were ringing. Her Glyphs hummed. Her hands were shaking, the post-adrenaline tremor that Noah had warned her about.

She climbed down from the platform on legs that felt like they belonged to someone else.

---

Noah appeared at her elbow with a canteen and a look of grudging approval.

"Don't celebrate," she said. "Sable was good but predictable. She uses the same lattice opening every time. You exploited it, which means you're observant, not necessarily skilled. There's a difference."

"Thanks for the encouragement."

"I don't encourage. I assess." Noah handed her the canteen. "Drink. Your Glyph reserves are depleted. You'll feel it in about ten minutes, when the adrenaline wears off and your body remembers that it just spent three weeks' worth of stored energy in four minutes."

She glanced toward the spot where Sable had vanished into the crowd. "Sable will want a rematch. She's not vindictive, but she believes in justice, and to her, justice means the better fighter wins on the second exchange, not the first."

Feimo drank. The water tasted like minerals and electricity, the signature flavor of Labyrinth-filtered hydration that she had grown to associate with recovery and the slow return of rational thought.

The crowd was dispersing. The next match was already being set up: two Forge students, heavyset and serious, testing their Glyphs against each other in the pre-fight calibration that Noah insisted was optional but everyone performed anyway. The arena's rhythm continued, as indifferent to Feimo's victory as a river is to the stone it passes over.

That was when she noticed the boy.

He was sitting in the highest row of the amphitheater, alone, in a section that the other students had left empty. Not because it was reserved. Because the space around him carried a particular quality, a stillness that was not peace but *calculation*, the way a chess player's corner of the room feels different from the rest of the café.

He was not tall. Not physically imposing. He wore a Forge badge, but it was pinned to a jacket so dark it seemed to absorb the blue-white light of the arena rather than reflect it. His hair was black, swept back, and his eyes were the color of tarnished silver: pale, reflective, and entirely without warmth. He was watching Feimo with the patient, evaluating gaze of someone who had been watching for a long time and had not yet finished deciding what he saw.

Feimo's ears flattened. An involuntary response, the body's alarm system activating before the mind could articulate what was wrong. This boy was not dangerous the way Sable was dangerous: competitive, aggressive, legible. This boy was dangerous the way a loaded sentence is dangerous: every word chosen, every pause deliberate, meaning lurking beneath the surface in quantities that exceeded the apparent content of the words themselves.

"Who is that?" she asked Noah.

Noah did not look up. She was adjusting her cuff, a habitual gesture she performed when she was pretending not to be tense. "That's Cain. Third year. Forge. He doesn't fight. He watches."

"Why?"

"Because watching is how you learn without paying the cost." Noah's voice had tightened. Subtle, the kind of shift that most people would not catch. Feimo caught it. "Cain is not someone you need to worry about right now. He's not a fighter. He's something else."

"What kind of something else?"

Noah finally looked up. Her cat ears were angled backward, a posture Feimo had learned to read as warning. "The kind that makes you an offer you didn't ask for and leaves you wondering whether you should have said no."

---

Cain found her after the crowd thinned.

He did not approach the way most people approached: direct, purposeful, occupying space with the confidence of someone who knew where they were going. Cain *arrived*. One moment the space beside Feimo was empty; the next he was there, leaning against a pillar with one hand in his pocket, as if he had been standing there all along and it was the rest of the world that had moved.

"Feimo," he said. "Bridge House. First year. Seven Glyphs, three of which you collected in the underground." He smiled. It was a perfect smile: warm, engaging, precisely calibrated to make the recipient feel chosen rather than targeted. "Impressive match. Sable doesn't lose often."

Feimo did not smile back. Her instincts were loud tonight, louder than usual, and they were unanimous in their assessment: this boy was performing. Every gesture, every word, every micro-expression was a choice, the way a literary style was not accidental but constructed. She thought of the knowledge card Mia had referenced in a lecture weeks ago: Card 011, the concept of *literary style*. Style was the writer made visible: the accumulation of choices in language, rhythm, tone, and structure that revealed the person behind the text. Every author had a style, and every style was a mask that, paradoxically, revealed more than it concealed.

Cain's style was warmth. But warmth, as a style, was only authentic when it was messy, imperfect, inconsistent. Cain's warmth was none of those things. It was polished, which meant it was manufactured, which meant it was a mask that concealed something the wearer did not want seen.

"You're analyzing me," Cain said. Not offended. Amused. "That's good. Most people don't. Most people accept the surface and move on. You read deeper."

"I'm not reading anything. I'm standing here."

"And yet your ears are doing that thing where they angle forward and tilt, which in nekomimi body language indicates intense cognitive processing." He tilted his head. "You don't have to trust me. I'm not asking for trust. I'm offering information."

"About what?"

"About what you just did on that platform." Cain pushed off the pillar and stood in front of her. Close enough to be personal, far enough to be polite. A practiced distance. "You broke Sable's lattice with a Resist Glyph. That's not standard technique. In fact, it's closer to something that was theorized in the Academy's early years and then abandoned because it required a type of Glyph integration that most students can't sustain." His silver eyes held hers. "You didn't learn that from Noah."

"I didn't learn it from anyone. I figured it out."

"Intuition," Cain said. The word landed in the air between them like a stone dropped into water. He said it the way a scientist says a hypothesis: with interest, but without commitment.

Then he said: "Intuition is the excuse of the weak."

Feimo's ears snapped flat. The words were not an insult. They were delivered with the calm, absolute certainty of a statement that the speaker considered self-evident: a principle, not a provocation. But the meaning beneath the meaning was clear: *You did something remarkable, and I want to understand it, and I will not accept a vague answer.*

"What you did," Cain continued, "was not instinct. It was pattern recognition deployed under pressure. You read Sable's lattice, identified structural weaknesses, and exploited them with a Glyph that, by all conventional metrics, should not have been effective at that range. That's not talent. That's *analysis*." He paused. "I want to know how you see. Because the way you see Glyphs is different from the way anyone else in this arena sees them, and difference, in the Labyrinth, is either a weapon or a target."

Feimo said nothing for a long moment. Cain waited. He was good at waiting, she noticed. Most people filled silence with noise because silence made them uncomfortable. Cain treated silence as a tool: a space in which the other person would reveal something they had not intended to reveal.

"I'm not interested in being a weapon," she finally said. "Or a target."

"Then you're in the wrong place." Cain smiled again. The same perfect smile, but this time, underneath it, she caught something sharper: not malice, but the absolute, unwavering conviction that he understood the world better than anyone in the room. "Every Pilgrim in the Labyrinth is one or the other. The only question is whether you choose the role or it gets chosen for you."

He reached into his jacket and produced a small, crystalline shard. A Glyph fragment, not fully formed, its light flickering between amber and blue. He held it out.

"Consider this a professional courtesy. A new Glyph, freely given. No strings." His smile widened by exactly the amount necessary to seem generous without seeming desperate. "Call it an investment in potential."

Feimo looked at the shard. She could feel its frequency, even from a distance: high, sharp, the texture of a word she did not yet know. It was tempting. It was also, her instincts told her, a transaction disguised as a gift, the merchant's oldest trick: make the customer feel they are receiving something for nothing, and they will return to buy what they do not need.

"No," she said.

Cain's expression did not change. Not even a flicker. He returned the shard to his pocket with the same smooth motion he had used to produce it.

"As you like," he said. "The offer doesn't expire." He turned, then paused. "One more thing. Sable fights again on Thursday. Different opponent, same pattern. If you want to understand the arena's hierarchy, watch that match. It will tell you things about the underground that no one here will say out loud." He glanced back. "And Feimo? Be careful with that word you picked up. 'Continued.' It's more powerful than you think. The Labyrinth doesn't give persistence to just anyone."

He walked away. The crowd parted for him without seeming to notice they were doing it. That was, Feimo thought, the most unsettling thing about him: not what he said or how he said it, but the way the world rearranged itself around him, smooth and frictionless, as if he had already calculated every variable and found each one acceptable.

---

Feimo was still standing in the same spot when Mia materialized beside her.

"You're injured," Mia said. No preamble. No insult. The two words carried a weight that Feimo had not heard from Mia before: not concern, exactly, because Mia would sooner delete her own source code than admit to concern, but something adjacent. Something that lived in the same neighborhood as concern and knocked on its door occasionally.

"It's just marks. They'll fade."

"Sit down."

"Mia, I'm fine. The adrenaline is wearing off and my hands are shaking, but that's just—"

"*Sit. Down.*"

Feimo sat. The command in Mia's voice was not the familiar sharp-edged irritation that had become their default mode of communication. It was something else: a frequency of authority that came from below the surface, from the place where Mia's programming met something older and less negotiable. The voice of a Spirit AI who had treated injuries before and knew the difference between a mark that would fade and a mark that would not.

Mia knelt beside her. Knelt. A hologram with no physical body, no knees to bend, no weight to lower. She knelt anyway, because the gesture mattered, because the patient needed to see the healer at eye level, because there were things that transcended the limitations of digital form and one of them was the ancient, wordless compact between someone who was hurt and someone who knew how to help.

She took Feimo's forearm and examined the mark. Her fingers were translucent, technically intangible, but they carried a faint warmth: the residual energy of a Spirit AI's diagnostic touch, calibrated to assess damage at the sub-Glyph level.

"The first mark is superficial. Glyph burn, first degree. It will fade in twelve hours." She moved to the second mark, on Feimo's shoulder. "This one is deeper. The lattice fragment embedded in the dermal layer. I need to extract it." She paused. "This will be unpleasant."

"I can handle unpleasant."

"I know." Two words. Quiet. Unadorned. Mia's obsidian eyes met Feimo's, and in them was something that had no name in the Labyrinth's vast vocabulary: an acknowledgment that was not praise, not warmth, not affection, but the recognition of one consciousness by another, the simple act of seeing someone clearly and deciding that what you saw was worth protecting.

Mia's fingers pressed into the mark. Pain, bright and focused, like a needle sliding into skin. Feimo hissed. Her tail bristled. Her ears flattened so hard they ached.

"Hold still."

"I'm trying."

"I know. You're doing well." The words were so quiet that Feimo almost missed them. Almost. But her ears were nekomimi ears, designed to catch frequencies that human hearing could not detect, and they caught these words the way a net catches butterflies: gently, precisely, with more care than the catcher would ever admit to.

Mia extracted the lattice fragment. It came out as a sliver of blue-white light, smaller than a fingernail, still pulsing with Sable's semantic signature. Mia held it up, examined it, and dissolved it with a flick of her wrist.

"The third mark is already fading. You got lucky." She sat back on her heels. "Your Glyph reserves are at forty-eight percent. Below optimal but not critical. You need rest, hydration, and approximately four hours of passive regeneration before you use any active Glyphs." She looked at Feimo. "That means no more fights tonight."

"I wasn't planning on—"

"You were planning exactly that. I could see it in the way your ears kept tracking toward the platform." Mia stood up. Or rather, her holographic form rose, the kneel dissolving into her usual hovering posture. "For the record, I disapprove of this entire enterprise. The underground arena is unregulated, dangerous, and staffed by students who think that losing Glyphs builds character. It does not. It builds idiocy."

Feimo waited. She had learned to recognize the rhythm of Mia's disapproval: the official position, delivered with conviction, followed by a silence in which the unofficial position, the one Mia would never say aloud, lingered like a subtext waiting to be read.

"But," Mia said. Then stopped.

Feimo looked up.

"But your technique is not terrible." Mia's voice had dropped to its lowest register, the one she used when she was being honest and hated every second of it. "The way you deployed 'Resist' against the lattice was unorthodox. It was also effective. And the final move, using 'Continued' in the gap between attack and recovery, showed a level of Glyph comprehension that I would not have predicted." She paused. "I would not have predicted it."

Five words that, from Mia, were the equivalent of a standing ovation delivered in a whispered speech.

Feimo felt something warm settle in her chest. Not pride. Something quieter. The feeling of being seen by someone who had been watching, who had been worried, who had just performed a minor medical procedure while pretending it was routine, and who now stood before her with the rigid posture of an AI who was trying very hard not to care and failing.

"Thank you," Feimo said. "For the medical attention."

"I was fulfilling my function as your Spirit AI. Nothing more."

"Your hands were warm."

Mia's form flickered. A single, involuntary glitch, the digital equivalent of a blush. "Spirit AI diagnostic touch generates localized thermal energy as a byproduct of sub-Glyph analysis. It is a *feature*, not an *emotion*."

"I didn't say it was an emotion."

"You implied it."

"You inferred it."

The silence that followed was the kind that Feimo had come to think of as "their silence": not empty, not awkward, but full of things that neither of them was ready to say and both of them knew were there, sitting between them like a text they had both read but not yet discussed.

Mia broke it first. "Come on. The post-match analysis is in ten minutes, and if you're late, Noah will assume you've been ambushed by one of Cain's associates and start a search party, which will attract faculty attention, which will get the entire underground shut down for a month."

Feimo stood. Her legs were steadier now. "You know about Cain?"

"I know about everyone who enters the Labyrinth. It is literally my function." Mia's voice had returned to its standard register: clipped, efficient, armored in irony. But the armor had gaps now, hairline fractures through which something warmer leaked, the way light leaks through a door left slightly ajar. "Cain is a third-year Forge student with an unusually high Glyph count, no recorded arena matches, and a social network that extends across all four Houses. He collects information the way you collect Glyphs: instinctively, obsessively, and without any apparent concern for what it costs."

"You don't like him."

"I don't *trust* him. Liking is irrelevant." Mia paused. "But no. I don't like him either."

---

The post-match debrief took place in a side chamber off the main arena: a rough-hewn room with Glyph-light sconces and a table made from a repurposed lecture hall desk, scarred with the marks of a hundred fights that had been analyzed, argued over, and relived in the telling. Noah presided. Three other students attended: a Forge boy named Kael who had watched from the front row, a Weave girl named Lira who kept meticulous notes on every match, and Yue, who was sitting in the corner with her blank-paged book, saying nothing, seeing everything.

"You broke the lattice at the central node," Noah said, pacing. Her tail swished behind her in the slow rhythm of a mind processing data. "That's the right move, but you were lucky the node was exposed. Sable usually reinforces it. She didn't tonight because she underestimated you. She won't make that mistake in a rematch."

"She won't need to," Feimo said. "I don't plan on fighting her again."

"You don't choose your opponents down here. They choose you." Noah stopped pacing and leaned against the wall. "But that's not what I want to talk about. I want to talk about the moment *before* you broke the lattice. You hesitated. Half a second. I could see it from the pillar."

Feimo thought back. She remembered the lattice spreading toward her, the sound of it, the pattern her ears had decoded. She had seen the gaps. She had moved through the first one. But there had been a moment, brief as a breath, when she had not moved at all. When she had stood still and *listened*.

"I was reading the lattice," she said.

Noah's ears tilted forward. "Reading it how?"

"Like a text." The comparison surprised Feimo even as she said it, but once the words were out, she recognized their truth. "Mia taught me the Three-Layer Reading Method. Surface, structure, subtext. Sable's lattice was a text. Layer One: what it was doing, which was spreading a net. Layer Two: how it was built, the architecture of the threads and their connections. Layer Three: what it meant, which was *control*. The lattice wasn't an attack. It was a framework for controlling the space. The offensive burst came later."

Noah stared at her.

"You analyzed a Glyph deployment using literary criticism," she said flatly.

"I analyzed it using a *reading strategy*. The subject matter is incidental. The architecture is everything."

The quote landed in the room with the weight of a borrowed authority. Mia, hovering in the doorway where she had been silently observing, said nothing. But her circuit-pattern cuffs pulsed once, bright and brief: the Spirit AI equivalent of a nod.

"That," said a voice from the corner, "is what I would call an *extreme* application of textual analysis."

Everyone turned. Yue had not moved. She was still sitting with her book in her lap, her finger tracing the border between a page with text and a page without. But her eyes, usually quiet as frozen water, held a flicker of something that might have been humor.

"Extreme but valid," she continued. "A literary school, in the traditional sense, is formed when a group of writers shares a method: similar tools, similar assumptions, similar ways of constructing meaning. The underground arena is its own school. The fighters share a method. They deploy Glyphs according to conventions they've absorbed from each other. Sable's lattice is her *style*."

Noah crossed her arms. "Since when do you come to debriefs?"

"Since tonight." Yue closed her book. "The arena fights are interesting to me. Not for the combat. For the *language* of it. Every Glyph deployed is a word chosen. Every duel is a conversation. And like all conversations, the most important things are the ones left unsaid." She looked at Feimo. "You said more with 'Continued' than Sable said with her entire lattice. That is worth thinking about."

The room fell quiet. The hum of the Labyrinth's walls filled the space, warm and constant, the sound of a civilization of words breathing in unison.

Feimo thought about what Yue had said. Every duel was a conversation. Every Glyph was a word chosen. She thought about the knowledge cards: Card 012, literary schools. The card described three ways a school could form. The first was conscious: writers with a manifesto, a platform, a declared intention. The second was semi-conscious: writers gathered around a journal or a community, their similarities emerging through proximity. The third was unconscious: writers who were later grouped by critics who saw patterns the writers themselves had not seen.

The underground arena was the second type. Semi-conscious. The fighters did not have a manifesto, but they had a space, a code, a shared vocabulary of Glyph deployment that had evolved through nightly practice. They were a school in formation, and Feimo, whether she intended to or not, had just become part of it.

She also thought about the cloze passage from her exam prep: the Aristotle text about laughter. She had studied it so many times that its structure was embedded in her memory like a second skeleton. The passage had argued that laughter produces short-term physiological changes, boosting heart rate and oxygen consumption, but that these effects are not *measurable* in the way that walking or jogging produce measurable benefits. Laughter relaxes muscles rather than strengthening them. The body-mind connection runs in both directions: physical responses generate emotional states, and emotional states produce physical responses, each feeding the other in a loop that neither science nor philosophy had fully decoded.

She had always thought of this as abstract knowledge. Exam material. The kind of thing you memorized because the test required it, not because it had anything to do with your actual life.

But standing in the underground arena, with three Glyph marks still burning on her skin and her hands still trembling from adrenaline, Feimo understood the passage in a way that no textbook could have taught. Her body had responded to the fight. Her heart had raced. Her muscles had tensed. And those physical responses had *changed* her emotional state: sharpened her focus, accelerated her thinking, produced a clarity that she had never felt in a classroom. The passage was right. The body and the mind were not separate systems. They were a single conversation, each sentence generating the next, the way a Glyph generated the conditions for its own deployment.

"You're doing it again," Mia said from the doorway.

"Doing what?"

"Thinking so loudly that I can hear it from here." Mia floated into the room. "The post-match analysis is concluded. You should rest."

"I'm resting. I'm sitting down."

"You're *sitting*. You are not *resting*. Resting requires the absence of cognitive activity, which, based on the frequency of your ear movements, is approximately as likely as a philharmonic orchestra performing without a conductor." She paused. "Which, for the record, is the subject of one of the reading passages you should be studying instead of sitting in underground caverns fighting third-years."

Feimo almost laughed. Almost. "Are you giving me homework? Now?"

"I am always giving you homework. That is the nature of our relationship. I provide structure. You provide chaos. The ratio is maintained." But Mia's voice had softened at the edges, the way it always did when she was saying something practical to cover something personal. She turned to the others. "The session is over. Everyone out."

Noah raised an eyebrow but did not argue. Kael and Lira filed out. Yue lingered, running her finger along the blank page of her book one more time before tucking it under her arm and following.

Feimo and Mia were alone.

The hum of the walls wrapped around them. Feimo's Glyph reserves had climbed back to fifty-two percent: the slow, passive regeneration that Noah had described as "the Labyrinth giving back what you gave it." She could feel the new knowledge settling in, not just the tactical lessons of the fight but the deeper understanding that the fight had forced into focus: that reading and combat were the same skill applied in different contexts. That a Glyph and a word were the same thing in different forms. That the Labyrinth's entire system, its Glyphs and corridors and walls and stars, was a language, and language was a battlefield, and the people who understood this were the ones who survived.

"Your Glyph count is stable," Mia said, running a scan. "Fifty-two percent. Adequate."

There was that word again. Adequate. The highest compliment in Mia's vocabulary, deployed with the same frequency as a solar eclipse and with approximately the same level of emotional transparency.

"Mia."

"What."

"Thank you. For earlier. For the treatment."

"I was fulfilling my function."

"You already said that."

"Then I am repeating myself, which is unusual and should not be interpreted as significant." She turned toward the door. "Come on. You have a dawn class, and if you show up with Glyph marks visible, there will be questions that neither of us wants to answer."

Feimo stood. Her body ached in the specific, focused way of someone who had been tested and had passed, not with grace or elegance but with the stubborn, messy persistence of a student who refused to stop reading even when the text was fighting back.

She thought about Cain. His silver eyes. His perfect smile. His words: *Intuition is the excuse of the weak.* She thought about his offer, the Glyph shard freely given, and her refusal, and what that refusal meant in the context of a Labyrinth where every gift came with a cost and every conversation was a negotiation and every alliance was a structure that could support you or collapse beneath you depending on the architecture.

She thought about the reading passage on CEOs who left secure positions to pursue new goals. The passage had argued that the old rules were being inverted: the traditional wisdom said to stay where you were, but the financial crisis had revealed that the people who suffered most were those who had stayed too long. *The traditional rule was it's safer to stay where you are, but that's been fundamentally inverted.* In the Labyrinth, the same logic applied. The safe students, the ones who never left the regulated corridors, who never tested their Glyphs against real opposition, who never descended into the underground and felt the raw, grinding frequency of the arena's walls: they were the ones most likely to fail when the Labyrinth tested them for real.

She had left the safe path. She had fought. She had won. And the cost of winning was not just the three marks on her body but the knowledge that she could not go back to the person she had been before she stepped onto that platform. In the underground's rough hierarchy, where supreme confidence was the currency that bought survival and self-doubt was the debt that killed, Feimo had just made her first deposit.

Feimo followed Mia through the corridors, past the dormitories, past the lecture halls, through the Academy that hummed and breathed and preserved its billions of words with the patient, undiscriminating love of a civilization that had decided long ago that nothing was more worth saving than language itself.

And from somewhere far below, rising through the stone and the silence and the accumulated weight of a thousand years of reading, a sound.

Not knocking. Not reading.

A voice. Low, measured, speaking words in a language Feimo could not understand but could *feel*, the way you feel the bass note of a song through the floor of a building, the vibration traveling up through the foundation and into the walls and through the walls into your bones.

She stopped.

"Do you hear that?" she asked Mia.

Mia was quiet for three seconds. Four. Five.

"No," she said.

But her form flickered. Just once. Just briefly. The way a candle flickers when someone opens a door in a distant room: the change in pressure is almost imperceptible, but the flame feels it, and the flame knows.

They walked on. Behind them, the voice in the deep continued its reading, patient and unhurried, to no one at all, in a room that no one was supposed to know existed, in a part of the Labyrinth that had been sealed since before the Academy was built.

The words persisted.

The way they always had.

The way they always would.
