# Chapter 2: Forget to Remember

The problem with Glyphs was that they forgot you.

Not right away. That would be too clean, too surgical, a loss you could name and file away in a drawer and retrieve only on rainy days when you needed something to be sad about. No. Glyphs forgot you the way a coastline forgets a shipwreck: grain by grain, wave by wave, until one morning you look out and the thing that was so sharp and solid and *present* has become smooth, has become sand, has become nothing at all.

Feimo was sitting at her desk in Bridge House dormitory at half past midnight, trying to remember the Glyph for "affect."

She knew she had acquired it. Four days ago, in the Hawthorne Corridor, when she'd reached out and touched the crystalline shard and felt the word settle into her bones like warm water. The Glyph had been vivid then: every use of "affect" she'd ever encountered, every sentence, every context, compressed into a single point of light that pulsed in her chest. She could feel the factory workers, the bewildered researchers, the way a single word could carry the weight of an entire phenomenon.

Now she could barely feel it at all. The Glyph had dimmed to a faint ember at the edge of her awareness, like a star seen through fog. She reached for it and her mind slid off, finding nothing to grip. A slow, quiet panic was settling into her chest, the kind that didn't announce itself but simply moved in and started rearranging the furniture. She thought about Yue's blank book, its empty pages, its total absence of resonance. Was this how it started? A Glyph dimming here, a memory slipping there, and then one morning you wake up holding a book with nothing written in it?

"You're losing it," said a voice behind her.

Feimo did not jump. She had learned, over four days, that Mia appeared without warning and considered surprise to be a personal failing of the surprised. "I know."

"You don't know. If you knew, you'd have reviewed it." Mia materialized at the edge of the desk, sitting on nothing, her holographic legs crossed at the ankle and her obsidian eyes fixed on Feimo with the kind of focus usually reserved for lab specimens. "Your Resonance is at 61%. Three days ago it was 74%. At this rate, you'll be Glyph-bare inside two weeks. Do you understand the issue here?"

"I'd have to start over?"

"You'd have to start over. Every Glyph you've earned, gone. Every corridor you've stabilized, destabilized. The Bridge House badge on your lapel would go dark, and you'd be a first-year applicant with no Resonance and no prospects." Mia's form flickered, the edges sharpening as her emotional processing kicked in. "I've seen it happen. Too many times."

There it was. The thing Mia did sometimes, where her voice lost its edge and became something else entirely, something older and more tired than a Spirit AI should sound. It lasted about half a second before the snark came back, like a reflex.

"So," Mia said, all business again. "We're going to fix this tonight. Sit up. Pay attention. And for the love of all that is literate, stop hunching. Your posture is an insult to desks everywhere."

Feimo sat up. Her tail, which had been curled around the chair leg, unwound and swished once in acknowledgment.

"Good. Now." Mia held up one hand, and a small glowing display materialized above her palm: a timeline, segmented into blocks of increasing length. "This is SM-2. Spaced Repetition. The single most important study technique you will ever learn, and the one that every student thinks they understand until their Glyphs start fading and they come running to me in a panic. The purpose of this method is simple: don't let your memories die."

"I didn't run to you. You showed up in my room uninvited. We've been over this."

"Semantics." Mia tapped the timeline, and it expanded. "Here's how memory works, Feimo. Think of it like a cat patrolling its territory."

Feimo's ears perked. She could not help it. Cat metaphors landed differently when you had actual cat ears.

"A cat doesn't visit every corner of its territory every day," Mia continued. "That would be exhausting and pointless. Instead, it rotates. Different corners on different days. Monday the north wall. Tuesday the garden fence. Wednesday the spot under the old oak tree. But it never lets any corner go unvisited for too long, because if it does, the scent marks fade, and another cat moves in, and suddenly that corner isn't yours anymore." She paused, letting the image settle. "Your Glyphs are scent marks. Your memory is the territory. And right now, you've let three perfectly good corners go feral."

"So I need to patrol more often?"

"You need to patrol *smart*. That's what SM-2 does. The algorithm spaces your reviews at increasing intervals: one day, three days, seven days, fourteen days. Each time you successfully recall a Glyph, the interval doubles. Each time you fail, it resets to one. The trick is to review each Glyph just before it fades, because that's when the review does the most work. Too early and you're wasting time on something you already know. Too late and you're rebuilding from scratch."

Mia's display showed the intervals stacking up, a staircase of expanding gaps. "Most students review everything every day. That works, but it's brutal. You burn out. You have a hundred Glyphs and you're spending four hours a night just keeping them alive. It's like a shopkeeper who tries to serve every customer at once: technically possible, practically a disaster. SM-2 lets you prioritize. Review what's about to fade. Leave what's still bright. Trust the algorithm. Over time, you'll develop the instinct to feel when a Glyph needs attention, the same way a cat knows which corner of its territory smells wrong."

"Have you always been this intense about study methods?"

"Have you always been this slow to recognize when someone is trying to save your academic career?" Mia's lips twitched. Not a smile. The ghost of a smile's second cousin. "The goal is simple: keep your Glyphs alive long enough for them to become permanent. Right now, they're temporary. Fragile. Likely to fade within days if you don't intervene." "Now. Close your eyes. Reach for 'affect.'"

Feimo closed her eyes. She reached for the Glyph.

It was there. Dim, thin, like trying to read a sentence written in pencil over erased pencil. She could feel the shape of it: the soft 'a,' the hard consonants, the way it sat in a sentence like a hinge, connecting cause to consequence. She pressed into the feeling and the Glyph brightened, just slightly, a candle catching after being blown out.

"Good," Mia said, and the word sounded like it cost her something. "Hold it. Feel the contexts. The Hawthorne workers. Their behavior was *affected* by observation. The meaning: to produce a change in something. To alter. To influence. Not 'effect,' which is the noun. This is the verb. The doing word. The one that says: something happened because something else made it happen."

The Glyph pulsed. Brighter now. Feimo could feel the weight of it returning, the accumulated resonance of every sentence that had ever used the word "affect," stacking up like sediment in a riverbed.

"Now release it gently. Don't clutch. If you grip too hard, you distort the resonance." Mia's voice had shifted into its teaching register, which was softer and more patient than its default setting, the vocal equivalent of a library lowering its lights. "The next review is in one day. Set the interval."

"How do I set an interval in my brain?"

"You just did. By recalling the Glyph at the right moment, you've strengthened the memory trace and extended its active lifespan. The SM-2 principle is biological, Feimo. It's how brains work. The algorithm just makes the process conscious, the same way knowing about gravity doesn't stop you from falling but does help you choose better cliffs." Mia's display shifted, showing Feimo's three Glyphs with decay curves. " 'Affect' is at 40% now, up from 15%. Good. Next: 'matter.'"

Feimo reached. "Matter" was in better shape, probably because she used the word in everyday speech. The Glyph flared warm on the second try, and she felt the Hawthorne workers again, the researchers' bewilderment, and something else, something personal: the word "matter" carried an emotional charge that was hard to separate from its meaning. This matters. You matter. The way a word could be both ordinary and extraordinary, depending on who was saying it and why.

"Interval: three days," Mia said. " 'Continued' next."

"Continued" was the strongest of the three. Feimo touched it and it lit up immediately, bright and steady, because she used the word constantly, because persistence was something she understood on a level that went deeper than vocabulary. The Glyph felt like a heartbeat: regular, insistent, refusing to stop. The effect of using it every day had kept its resonance high without any conscious effort.

"That one's holding at 72%," Mia said, and there was something in her voice that might have been approval, or at least the grudging acknowledgment that one data point out of three was not a complete disaster. "You use it instinctively. That helps. The best Glyphs are the ones you live, not just the ones you study." She closed the display. "We'll do this again tomorrow night. And the night after. And the night after that. SM-2 is not a one-time fix. It's a practice. A daily practice. You share your time with it or you lose what you've earned."

"A practice," Feimo repeated, and the word felt right in her mouth, the way some words do when they name something you've always done but never had a word for. A daily practice. Like breathing.

---

"Now," Mia said, shifting gears with the abruptness of someone who had no patience for transitions. "Your Resonance is recovering. Let's review what you learned today."

She walked Feimo through the day's classes. Inscription Theory had covered the basics of how Glyphs were formed: not through rote memorization but through genuine encounters with language in context. Glyph Ecology had introduced the idea that words existed in ecosystems, that the health of a word depended on how many minds were actively using it, that rare words needed more maintenance than common ones. Feimo had found the second class more interesting than the first, though she suspected that was because Glyph Ecology's professor, a tall woman with ink-stained fingers, had set fire to a diagram of word-frequency distribution and laughed when the flames turned blue.

"She's Forge House," Mia said. "They're all like that."

At one point Feimo mentioned the reading strategy Professor Wren had hinted at — something about layers of interpretation.

"Three-layer reading," Mia said. "Surface, structural, resonant. We'll cover it properly when you're ready. Right now you'd misapply it, and I don't have the patience to watch that happen."

"You could just teach me."

"I could. But some things need the right moment. The right passage. The right crisis." Mia's expression suggested she was enjoying the withholding more than she should. "Patience. You've been here four days."

She paused, then added: "One more exercise. Close your eyes. Reach for 'matter.'"

Feimo closed her eyes. "Matter" was warm and solid in her chest, the easiest of her three Glyphs to find. The word pulsed with quiet insistence: *this counts. This is important.*

"Good. Now reach for 'continued.'"

"Continued" was steadier. A heartbeat word. Regular, insistent, refusing to stop.

"Now reach for 'affect.'"

Feimo reached. The Glyph was dimmer, harder to grip. She had reviewed it earlier, but the decay was already setting in, the edges softening like water wearing down stone. She pressed into the feeling and felt it brighten, just slightly.

"That one needs daily attention," Mia said. "Set your interval. One day. Review it tomorrow at the same time. If you miss the window, the decay accelerates."

"What happens if I miss by an hour?"

"An hour is fine. A day is not. The algorithm is forgiving at small scales and merciless at large ones. Like gravity. Or me." Mia closed the display. "We'll do this every night. Every single night. The practice is the point, Feimo. Not the theory. The doing."

"I understand."

"You say that now. We'll see if you're still saying it in a week."

---

A knock at the door.

Not the Labyrinth's hum, not the subtle vibration of text shifting in the walls. A knock. Three deliberate raps, the kind that said *I am here and I am choosing to be here, and I need you to know that.*

Feimo's ears swiveled toward the sound. Her tail went still.

"Expecting company?" Mia asked.

"No."

Feimo crossed the room and opened the door.

The girl in the corridor was small. Not short, exactly, but compact, folded in on herself, as if she had been carrying something heavy for so long that her body had simply adjusted its architecture around the weight. She wore a Mirror House badge, silver-gray, but it was dim, its glow barely visible against the dark fabric of her blazer. The condition of her uniform was immaculate, pressed and precise, but it hung on her like a costume on a mannequin, as if the person inside had nothing to do with the person the clothes were meant to present.

Her hair was dark and straight, cut bluntly at her shoulders, and it did not move. Not because of product or style but because she did not move enough to make it.

Her eyes were the problem.

They were open. They were looking at Feimo. And they were empty. Not hostile, not sad, not anything that had a name Feimo could find in any language she knew. They were the eyes of someone who had looked at something so far away for so long that everything close had gone out of focus. They were the color of fog over a field where nothing grows.

She was holding a book. It was bound in dark leather, the same size and shape as the Glyph books Feimo had seen other students carry. But this one was blank. No title on the spine. No text on the cover. No glow, no hum, no resonance at all. It was the book equivalent of an empty room. She held it against her chest with both arms, not like a student carrying study materials, but like a patient carrying a diagnosis.

"I'm sorry," the girl said. Her voice was soft and completely flat, like a page with no writing on it. "I heard the resonance from the corridor. I wanted to know what it felt like. It's been a while."

Feimo opened her mouth. Closed it. She did not know what to say to someone whose eyes looked like that.

"I'm Yue," the girl said. "I'm in Mirror House. I've been here for two years." She paused, and the pause had a weight to it, like a stone dropped into a well and no splash ever reaching the surface. "I can't dream anymore."

"I..." Feimo started.

"Don't." Yue's voice was gentle, the way silence is gentle when it asks you to stop filling it with words that don't help. "It's not a question. It's not a thing that needs an answer. It's just something I said. Some things are like that. They don't want to be fixed. They just want to be spoken." She looked down at her blank book, then back at Feimo, and for a moment her eyes focused, and what was behind the fog was not emptiness but something much worse: a clarity that had nowhere left to go.

"Your Glyphs are pretty," Yue said. "I remember when mine were pretty."

She turned and walked away down the corridor, her footsteps making no sound, her dark hair not moving, her blank book held against her chest like a shield against a weapon that had already struck. The silver-gray badge on her lapel flickered once and went dark, and the corridor swallowed her shape the way water swallows a stone: completely, without effort, without malice.

Feimo stood in the doorway for a long time after the corridor turned empty.

"Close the door," Mia said, and her voice was very quiet.

---

"Who was that?" Feimo asked, still staring at the empty corridor through the narrowing gap as the door swung shut.

Mia was silent for three full seconds, which was, for her, an eternity. "Her name is Yue. She's a Pilgrim. Or she was."

"What happened to her?"

"She stopped dreaming." Mia's voice was carefully neutral, the way a voice gets when it is trying very hard not to convey the thing it is conveying. "Dreams are the source of new Glyphs, Feimo. When you dream in the Labyrinth, your subconscious reaches into the text and draws connections your waking mind can't see. That's where Glyphs come from. Not from study. Not from challenge. From the act of imagining, freely, without social constraint or academic pressure. From the willingness to let language surprise you. The mind makes leaps in sleep that it would never dare make while awake. New words connect to old words. Meanings shift. Resonances form. The dreaming mind is the Labyrinth's most productive student."

"And Yue can't do that anymore?"

"She hasn't been able to for over a year. Her Glyph count is zero. Her Resonance is zero. She carries the book because she can't bring herself to leave it behind. The book is a sign, in the old sense of the word: a mark of something that used to exist." Mia turned away, her translucent form drifting toward the window, which in the Labyrinth was not glass but a thin membrane of compressed text through which you could see the amber glow of the campus at night. "I shouldn't have told you that. It's not relevant to your study plan. Focus on what you can develop, not on what you can't fix."

But Feimo was still standing in the doorway, staring at the empty corridor. She could see the faint trail of Yue's footsteps in the dust — or rather, the absence of footsteps, the places where the text on the floor had not been disturbed, marking a path of deliberate care, of someone who had learned to move through the world without leaving a mark on it.

"What does it feel like?" Feimo asked. "To stop dreaming?"

Mia was quiet for a long time. "I wouldn't know. I'm an AI. I don't dream." A pause. "But the students who've described it say it's like losing a sense. Not going blind or deaf. Something more specific. Like losing your sense of smell. The world is still there. You can still see it, touch it, navigate it. But a dimension of experience has vanished, and the world is flatter for its absence, and you can't explain the missing dimension to anyone who still has it."

Feimo closed the door. The room felt smaller than it had a minute ago, the walls closer, the drifting text overhead more urgent, as if the Labyrinth itself was trying to fill the silence that Yue had left behind.

"It's relevant to me," Feimo said, and the words came out with a certainty that surprised them both.

Another pause. Longer this time. Mia's form flickered at the edges, the way it did when her emotional processing was working harder than her systems wanted to admit.

Feimo sat on the edge of her bed, her tail curling around the bedpost. Through the closed door, she could hear the distant murmur of Bridge House students in the common room, trading Glyphs, comparing notes, living their lives at a volume that Yue no longer seemed to occupy. She thought about the SM-2 intervals: one day, three days, seven days. What was the interval for a dream that had stopped coming? What algorithm could you use to schedule the review of something that no longer existed?

"Come with me," Mia said. "I want to show you something."

"Where?"

"The roof. There's something you need to see." Mia's form was already drifting toward the door, her edges sharp with an urgency she was not bothering to conceal. "Bring your badge. You'll need it for the access corridor."

Feimo grabbed her blazer and followed.

---

She led Feimo out of Bridge House, through corridors that grew narrower and less maintained the farther they went from the dormitory. The text on the walls here was older, denser, the letters smaller and more tightly packed, as if the words were trying to fit as much meaning as possible into the available space before the walls closed in. The hum of the Labyrinth changed register, dropping into a lower frequency that Feimo felt in her chest more than heard in her ears. The condition of these passages told a story of their own: cracks in the prose, faded sentences, places where the text had worn so thin you could see the raw stone beneath. Feimo's cat instincts prickled. These corridors were not dangerous, exactly, but they were neglected, and neglect had a smell — old paper, stale air, the particular staleness of words that had not been spoken aloud in a very long time.

They climbed. A spiral staircase that should not have existed in a building whose architecture was determined by the logic of text rather than physics. The steps were made of compacted prose, each one a different passage, and Feimo's feet left faint impressions in the letters as she walked. She could read fragments beneath her soles: *the act of being experimented upon changed subjects' behavior*, *birds should continue as a matter of intrinsic right*, *literariness exists in the language itself.* Each step was a sentence. Each sentence was a world.

The staircase opened onto the roof.

Feimo stopped breathing.

The sky above the Labyrinth was not a sky. It was a map.

Stars. Thousands of them, tens of thousands, scattered across a dome of absolute darkness in patterns that were not random, not astronomical, not the constellations she had learned in science class. These stars were arranged in clusters and chains and spirals, connected by faint lines of amber light, and each one was a distinct color: warm gold, cool silver, pale blue, deep crimson. They covered the entire dome, from horizon to horizon, and the hum of the Labyrinth was loudest here, each star contributing its own frequency to the aggregate, a choir of light singing in a key that had no name. Some stars pulsed slowly, like breathing. Others flickered with the rapid heartbeat of a student pulling an all-nighter. A few, in the outermost ring, burned with the steady, unhurried warmth of something that had been shining for a very long time and intended to keep shining indefinitely.

"Welcome to the star map," Mia said. "Every star is a Pilgrim. A student of the Labyrinth. When a Pilgrim is active, when their Resonance is strong, their star burns bright. When a Pilgrim graduates, their star turns gold and moves to the outer ring." She pointed. Sure enough, a band of golden stars circled the dome's edge, steady and warm, each one a word that had been spoken and remembered and passed on.

"And when a Pilgrim fails?"

Mia did not answer immediately. She drifted to the center of the roof and hovered there, her translucent form catching the starlight, and for an instant she looked less like a hologram and more like a constellation herself, a pattern of light that someone had given a name and a purpose and then asked to keep watch over a sky full of other people's stories.

"When a Pilgrim fails," Mia said, "their star goes dark."

Feimo looked up. And she saw them.

For every bright star, there were three dark ones. Maybe four. They were easy to miss at first, because the bright stars drew the eye, but once you saw the dark ones, you could not unsee them. They were everywhere: gaps in the constellations, holes in the chains, absences in the spirals. They did not glow. They did not hum. They were the color of nothing, the color of a word that has been erased so thoroughly that even the impression it left in the page has been smoothed over. Thousands of them. More dark stars than bright, like a night sky that had forgotten its own light.

"There are more dark stars than bright ones," Feimo whispered.

"Many more," Mia said. "Seven or eight out of every ten students who enter the Labyrinth lose their Glyphs and leave. Their stars go dark. The Labyrinth preserves them, because the Labyrinth preserves everything, but they don't shine. They don't sing. They're just there."

Feimo stared at the dark stars. She thought about the corridors in the Labyrinth that were dim and eroding, the ones nobody maintained. The dark stars were the same thing, written in light: conversations that had stopped mid-sentence, voices that had gone quiet and never come back.

"Can they come back?" she asked.

"In theory. If a former Pilgrim returns and regains their Resonance, their star brightens. But it's never the same. The intervals reset. The accumulated resonance is gone. You'd be starting from the very beginning, and the Labyrinth remembers that you failed. The corridors you once stabilized will test you harder. The Glyphs you once earned will fade faster." Mia paused. "Most people don't come back. They make the decision once and it stays made."

Feimo noticed that Mia was staring at a particular section of the sky, a cluster near the western horizon where the dark stars were densest, packed so tightly together that the gaps between the bright ones looked like a wound.

"That cluster," Feimo said. "Whose are those?"

Mia did not answer immediately. Her obsidian eyes were fixed on the dark stars, and her form had gone very still, the blue glow steady, the way a candle stops flickering when someone closes a door.

"Those are mine," Mia said. "Students I was assigned to guide. They didn't make it."

The words were flat. Clinical. The voice of someone who had said them so many times that the edges had worn smooth. But beneath the flatness, Feimo heard something catch — a frequency, a hesitation, the ghost of a wound that had healed over without closing.

Mia turned away from the star map. "We should go back. You have a review session tomorrow, and if your Resonance drops below fifty percent, I'm filing a formal intervention."

"Wait." Feimo reached out, not quite touching Mia's arm. "Those students. The ones who didn't make it. Did you—" She stopped, because the question she was asking was too large for the four days they had known each other.

"Did I what?"

"Did you try to save them?"

Mia's form flickered. The edges went sharp with static, then smoothed. When she spoke, her voice was carefully neutral, the way a voice gets when it is trying very hard not to convey the thing it is conveying.

"I am a Spirit AI. My function is to guide, to track Resonance, to manage Glyph inventory, and to provide support. I do my function." A pause. "But functions don't always produce outcomes. And some outcomes resist every input."

The silence between them was not empty. It was full of the weight of stars that had gone dark and the stubborn persistence of the ones that hadn't.

Feimo thought about the SM-2 intervals Mia had taught her that evening. One day, three days, seven days, fourteen days. Each successful recall doubled the interval. Each failure reset it to one. The algorithm was simple. The practice was not. Because the thing about forgetting was that it did not happen all at once. It happened the way stars go dark: one by one, so gradually that you don't notice until the sky is mostly darkness and you can't remember when you last saw it full of light.

"I won't forget," Feimo said. "That's a promise. And I'll keep it the way I keep my Glyphs. Daily. Deliberately. One review at a time."

"A promise is a word," Mia said, very quietly. "Words can be broken. They can be misused. They can fade."

"Then I'll use SM-2 on it. I'll review the promise at increasing intervals. One day, three days, seven days, fourteen days. And each time I recall it, it'll get stronger."

Mia looked at her. The ghost of a smile crossed her features, there and gone, faster than a Glyph fading, but real. Real enough to notice. Real enough to store in the part of memory that doesn't need SM-2 because some things are not stored in intervals but in the steady, permanent hum of having been said by someone who meant it.

"You're applying spaced repetition to emotional commitments," Mia said. "That's either very clever or very sad."

"Can't it be both?"

"Go to bed, Feimo."

Feimo stood and looked up at the star map one last time. The dark stars outnumbered the bright ones. But the bright ones burned with a steady, defiant warmth, each one a word that someone had chosen to keep alive, each one a memory that had survived the slow, patient erosion of time and forgetting.

She would be one of those stars. She would choose to be.

They walked back down the spiral staircase in silence, each step a passage, each passage a choice. Behind them, the star map hummed and flickered and sang, and somewhere in its vast and ancient darkness a Spirit AI hovered in the space between memory and duty and did not look back.

The hum continued. The words remained.

Some things, if you chose to remember them, refused to be forgotten. Tonight, Feimo would remember all of it.
