# Chapter 27: The Door That Shouldn't Exist

The corridor that was not on any map began thirty-seven steps past the point where the floor changed from marble to raw stone.

Feimo counted the steps because counting was what you did when the walls stopped behaving like walls. In the upper levels, the Academy's architecture followed rules: right angles, consistent ceiling heights, doors at predictable intervals. Down here, the stone breathed. Not literally, not yet, but the surfaces expanded and contracted in micro-shifts that Feimo's cat ears could register as changes in air pressure, and the effect was of walking through the interior of something alive.

"Thirty-seven," she said.

"Thirty-eight," Mia corrected, without turning around. "You started counting from the wrong mark. The transition point was one step back."

"It doesn't matter."

"It always matters. Accuracy is the difference between a map and a guess." Mia's voice was steady, but Feimo could hear the undertone: a frequency slightly above normal, the vocal equivalent of raised hackles. Mia was afraid. She hid it well. She hid everything well. But five months of sitting in a sixteen-degree server room, watching her friend's language disintegrate column by column, had eroded the polish on her composure, and the cracks were audible to anyone with ears designed to hear them.

They had been descending for forty minutes. The entrance to the Deep Labyrinth was technically off-limits: a sealed door in the Library's sub-basement, marked with a sign that read MAINTENANCE ACCESS ONLY in letters that were, themselves, a kind of lie. There was no maintenance down here. There was no access. There was only the slow, patient architecture of a system that had been building itself for longer than either of them had been alive.

Mia had unlocked the door with a key she should not have had. Feimo had not asked where she got it. There were questions that, if answered, would make both of them complicit in something the Academy's rules were designed to prevent. Feimo preferred the ambiguity of ignorance. It was a form of trust.

---

The passage narrowed. The ceiling dropped from two meters to one and a half, forcing them into a half-crouch. The stone was damp, not with water but with something that had the viscosity of oil and the smell of old paper. Feimo's whiskers registered it as a residue: the molecular byproduct of text being consumed. Every wall down here was a surface that had once held writing, and the dampness was what remained when the writing was gone.

"About that cloze exercise," Mia said, her voice bouncing off the close walls. "The one about trust."

"What about it?"

"The passage argues that trust is a necessary condition for many worthwhile things. Child care, friendships, partnerships. But it also argues that placing your faith in the wrong place carries a high price." She paused to duck under a protrusion of stone that looked, in the dim light, like a frozen wave. "The Swiss study. Oxytocin spray. Subjects who inhaled it were willing to lend significantly higher amounts to strangers."

"I remember the exercise."

"Do you remember the conclusion? The passage calls it a 'sixth sense for dishonesty.' Children as young as fourteen months can differentiate between a credible person and an unreliable one. Before they can speak, before they can walk properly, they can tell who to trust. Sixty toddlers, each introduced to an adult tester. Half found a toy in the container. The other half discovered it was empty and realized the tester had fooled them."

"And the ones who weren't tricked were willing to cooperate," Feimo said. "While only five out of thirty paired with the unreliable tester participated in the follow-up."

Mia stopped walking. The corridor was so narrow that stopping meant Feimo nearly walked into her back. In the darkness, Feimo could see the outline of Mia's shoulders, the tension in the way she held herself: rigid, controlled, the posture of someone who had decided to be brave and was executing that decision with the same precision she applied to everything else.

"Why are you bringing this up now?" Feimo asked.

"Because I need to know something." Mia's voice was quiet. The acoustics of the narrow corridor swallowed it, made it intimate, the kind of voice you used in confessionals or operating rooms. "The passage says we have an instinct for detecting dishonesty. A biological mechanism, oxytocin-driven, that lets us know when someone is lying. But it also says the mechanism can be fooled. Or rather, that it works best with children, who haven't yet learned to override it with rationalization. Adults rationalize. Adults explain away the signal. Adults decide that the trustworthy-seeming person must be trustworthy because it would be inconvenient if they weren't."

"I know the passage, Mia."

"Then you know the question I'm about to ask."

Feimo did know. She had known since Mia unlocked the door with the key she should not have had, since they stepped past the MAINTENANCE sign, since the ceiling began to drop and the air began to thicken with the residue of consumed text. Mia was asking: *Can I trust you with what we're about to find?*

"Yes," Feimo said. "You can."

Mia resumed walking.

---

The first anomaly appeared at step one hundred and twelve.

Feimo's ears caught it before her eyes did: a sub-bass vibration that did not match the Labyrinth's background hum. The Academy's constant frequency was 50 Hz, a clean, stable tone that you stopped noticing after the first week. This was different. This was lower, felt more than heard, and it pulsed at irregular intervals, like a heartbeat that had forgotten its rhythm.

She put her hand on Mia's shoulder. Mia stopped.

"Listen," Feimo whispered.

They stood in silence for eleven seconds. The vibration came again: a throb in the stone, traveling up through the soles of their shoes, through their bones, settling in the sternum like the memory of a drumbeat.

"That's not the Labyrinth," Mia said.

"No."

"The Labyrinth's processes are constant. Predictable. This is intermittent. Something is generating it in real time."

Feimo moved past Mia, taking the lead. The corridor widened slightly, the ceiling rising back to a comfortable height, and the stone on the walls changed texture. Where it had been smooth, consumed flat, it was now rough, scored with marks that Feimo recognized as the residue of a process she had never witnessed: text being *written*, not erased. The marks were horizontal, evenly spaced, the ghost-lines of characters that had been inscribed and then partially consumed, leaving only the physical impression of their existence.

She touched the wall. The stone was warm.

"Mia."

"I see it."

At the end of the corridor, where the passage should have continued into darkness, there was a door.

Not a maintenance door. Not a utility hatch. A door carved from a single slab of dark stone, its surface covered in characters that Feimo could read because they were in a language she had spoken since birth.

Chinese.

The characters were not Misty Poetry. They were not Bei Dao or Gu Cheng or Hai Zi. They were ancient, written in a style that predated modern handwriting by centuries: the formal, balanced strokes of classical calligraphy, each character occupying its square with the authority of something that had been placed there deliberately and had no intention of leaving.

Feimo read them aloud, and her voice sounded strange in the narrow space, the tones of her mother tongue rebounding off walls that had only ever held English:

"此门之后，非汝所知之世界。"

*Beyond this door lies a world you do not know.*

Mia came up beside her. In the faint light that seemed to emanate from the characters themselves, Feimo could see Mia's face clearly for the first time since they had entered the Deep Labyrinth. What she saw there was not confusion. It was not academic curiosity. It was fear, raw and undisguised, the kind of fear that bypasses the rational mind entirely and speaks directly to the body.

"This can't be here," Mia said. Her voice had dropped to a register Feimo had never heard from her before: not analytical, not controlled, but stripped down to something human and shaking. "The Labyrinth is an English-language system. Every text it processes, every Glyph it generates, every wall it consumes is indexed to English. The Resonance frequencies are calibrated to English phonemes. The entire architecture is built on the assumption that language means English. If there are Chinese characters here, then either the system has been breached or the system is something other than what we thought it was, and both of those possibilities are very, very bad."

"Or," Feimo said, "there's a third option."

"What?"

"That the system was never English-only. That it just hadn't encountered anything else. Until now."

She reached for the door.

---

The Fragmenter came from the walls.

That was the only way to describe it. One moment the stone was solid, continuous, a surface. The next, it fractured along invisible fault lines and something emerged: not a creature, not a machine, but a *process*, given temporary form by the energy differential between the consumed text in the walls and the unconsumed text on the door. It was made of letters. English letters, broken free from their words, spinning in a loose approximation of a body: A's for shoulders, T's for limbs, O's for the hollow circles of its eyes. It had no mouth. It did not need one. It communicated through the displacement of air, the way a thrown stone communicates with a still pond.

It moved toward the door.

"No," Mia breathed. She pressed herself against the corridor wall, her ears flat, her tail rigid. "A Fragmenter. They're not supposed to exist below level six. The models don't predict them here. The Resonance gradient is all wrong."

But the Fragmenter was here, and it was reaching for the door with hands made of consonants, and Feimo could see, with a clarity that felt like the opposite of fear, what would happen if it touched the characters. It would consume them. It would do what the Labyrinth did to everything: erase, process, index, reduce to nothing. The door would become a wall. The Chinese would become absence. And whatever world the door pointed toward would cease to be accessible.

She did not think. Thinking was too slow for this. The Fragmenter was three meters away and closing, its letter-hands extending, the O's of its eyes fixed on the glowing calligraphy with the mindless hunger of a system encountering input it could not classify.

Feimo pulled the notebook from her jacket pocket. The same notebook she carried everywhere, the one she wrote her exercises in, the one that held her translation practice and her reading notes and, on page forty-seven, a poem she had written at five in the morning after watching Mia watch Yue. She tore out a blank page and, with the pen she kept behind her ear, she wrote.

Not English. Chinese.

She wrote the character for *door*. 门. Two vertical strokes, connected at the top by a horizontal stroke that bridged them like a lintel. The simplest character in the language. A child's first lesson. The shape of an opening in a wall.

Then she held the paper against the corridor wall, and the wall accepted it.

The stone did not consume the paper. It *absorbed* it, the way soil absorbs water: slowly, completely, with a faint sound that was less a sound than a vibration, a hum at a frequency Feimo had never heard the Labyrinth produce. The character 门 spread from the paper into the stone, ink migrating through mineral matrix, each stroke branching into a network of lines that pulsed once, twice, three times, and then went dark.

The Fragmenter stopped.

It turned its O-eyes toward Feimo. The letters that composed its body began to vibrate, each one oscillating at a different frequency, the collective effect producing a sound like a thousand typewriters being struck at random. It was confusion. It was the sound of a system encountering input that violated its parameters.

Feimo wrote another character. 墨. Ink. The character for ink, written in ink, on paper, absorbed by stone. She pressed it against the wall and felt the stone take it, felt the vibration intensify, felt the Fragmenter's typewriter-sound rise in pitch and then, suddenly, cut out.

The letter-body disassembled. The A's fell first, clattering to the stone floor like dropped coins. Then the T's, the O's, the consonants and vowels separating and scattering, each one seeking its word, finding nothing, going dark. In four seconds, the Fragmenter was gone. Only scattered letters remained on the floor, already fading, already being re-consumed by the stone.

Feimo stood with her back against the wall and her pen in her hand and her heart beating at a frequency that had nothing to do with the Labyrinth.

---

Mia did not move for a long time.

The light from the door's characters had intensified during the confrontation, as if the Fragmenter's attempt to consume them had strengthened rather than weakened their presence. The calligraphy glowed with the steady, warm luminescence of something that had always been there, waiting, and was now, briefly, fully visible.

"Feimo." Mia's voice was barely audible. "What did you do?"

"I wrote."

"You wrote Chinese into the Labyrinth's architecture. You gave it input in a language it has never processed. The wall accepted it. The Fragmenter couldn't classify it and collapsed. That shouldn't be possible. The Labyrinth's intake system is designed to handle any text, any language, any script. It consumed the Misty Poems when they appeared. It consumed Yue's unknown language in the Redaction Archive. But it didn't consume what you wrote. It *absorbed* it. Integrated it. Used it."

"I don't understand the mechanism."

"I do." Mia pushed herself off the wall. Her legs were unsteady, which was something Feimo had never seen before. Mia's legs were always steady. Mia's everything was always steady. "The Labyrinth isn't a library. Libraries store what they're given. The Labyrinth is a processing system. It takes input, classifies it, and either stores it or destroys it based on whether the input matches its existing architecture. English text matches. The Misty Poems didn't match, but they were close enough to be consumed as anomalies. Yue's language didn't match at all, and the system has been trying to overwrite it, to replace it with something indexable."

She looked at the door.

"But what you just wrote wasn't consumed and it wasn't rejected. It was *integrated*. Which means the Labyrinth's architecture is not fixed. It can expand. It can incorporate new languages. New logics. New systems of meaning. And if it can do that, then everything we thought we knew about how this place works is wrong."

Feimo looked at the door, at the characters that continued to glow with their impossible light.

"I need to tell you something," she said. "About the reading passage. Text 1, from the 2018 exam materials."

"About automation?"

"About what happens when a system encounters something it was not designed to handle. The passage describes the middle class being squeezed by automation. Half of U.S. jobs at high risk. Not because the jobs are unnecessary, but because the system has evolved past them. The passage says optimists point out that technological upheaval has benefited workers in the past. The Industrial Revolution displaced the Luddites, but it eventually raised living standards. In the medium term, though, the passage admits, workers may need a lot of help adjusting."

"You think the Labyrinth is going through an industrial revolution."

"I think the Labyrinth was designed for English. And now it's encountering something else. And instead of displacing the old system, the new input is being integrated. The architecture is expanding. That door was not there before. Those characters were not there before. But they don't feel new. They feel like they've always been there, and we just now gained the ability to see them."

Mia was quiet for a long time. The glow of the door pulsed. The sub-bass vibration continued, steady now, less a heartbeat and more a hum, the sound of something that had found its rhythm.

"The author of that automation text," Mia said eventually, "suggests that tax policies should aim at preventing the income gap from widening. Cutting taxes on low-wage labor. Expanding wage subsidies. The policy is a Band-Aid, the text admits. A response to the symptoms rather than the cause. But the cause is structural: a system that has evolved beyond its original parameters and now processes reality in ways its designers never intended."

"That's what the Labyrinth is doing."

"Yes." Mia's voice was very quiet. "That's what the Labyrinth is doing."

---

They stood before the door for seven minutes. Feimo knew because she counted.

The characters had stopped glowing and settled into a steady state: visible, legible, neither bright nor dim, as if they had always been there and always would be. The stone around them was warm to the touch. The residue of consumed text had retreated from the immediate area, leaving a zone of clean stone that smelled of nothing, which was, as Feimo had learned from the Misty Poems' appearance, worse than smelling of something wrong, because nothing meant the space was curated. Designed. Intended.

"此门之后，非汝所知之世界," Mia read, slowly, her tones approximate but intelligible. She had been studying. Of course she had been studying. Mia studied everything. "Beyond this door lies a world you do not know."

"You read Chinese."

"I read everything. That's what I do." The ghost of her usual sharpness, returning like color to a bruised face. "I started three months ago. When the Misty Poems began appearing. I cross-referenced the characters against every database I had access to. Classical Chinese, modern Chinese, dialectal variants. I couldn't match the handwriting, but I could match the grammar. The poems were modern. The calligraphy on this door is not. This is pre-modern. Formal. The kind of writing that appears on temples, on government buildings, on the gates of places that matter."

"Why didn't you tell me you were studying Chinese?"

"Because I was afraid." The admission cost her something. Feimo could see it in the way her jaw tightened, the way her ears flicked back and then forward again, a cat's involuntary tell. "Not of the language. Of what it meant that the Labyrinth was producing it. The system I have spent three years studying, the system I have built my entire academic identity around understanding, is changing. Expanding. Becoming something I don't recognize. And if I don't recognize it, then I can't predict it. And if I can't predict it, then I am, for the first time in my life, genuinely helpless."

"You're not helpless."

"I was helpless when the Fragmenter came. You saved us. Not me. You."

Feimo did not know what to say to that. The compliment sat between them like a character absorbed into stone: present, integrated, impossible to remove.

"We should go back," Feimo said. "Report what we found."

"Not yet." Mia stepped closer to the door. She raised her hand, not touching it, but hovering, the way you hover your hand over a flame to test whether it will burn. "The passage about trust. The cloze exercise. Do you remember the last paragraph?"

"The children who had not been tricked were willing to cooperate with the tester. But in contrast, only five of the thirty paired with the unreliable tester participated in the follow-up activity."

"In contrast. The passage uses those words deliberately. It's a signal. The paragraph before describes cooperation based on trust. The paragraph after describes refusal based on broken trust. The transition between them is the hinge of the entire argument. Trust enables. Betrayal disables. Not permanently, but functionally. The children who had been fooled didn't refuse to cooperate because they were incapable of learning. They refused because they had learned the wrong thing: that the person offering to teach them could not be trusted."

"You think this door is a test of trust."

"I think everything down here is a test of trust. The sealed entrance. The key I should not have had. The corridor that narrows to force us single-file, to separate us physically even as we move together. The Fragmenter that appeared at the exact moment we found the door, as if the system were measuring our response. And now this door, written in a language that one of us can read and the other cannot, asking us to decide whether to open it together."

Mia lowered her hand.

"I have spent my career assuming that the Labyrinth was a machine," she said. "Machines can be understood. Machines can be predicted. Machines follow rules. But this door is not the output of a machine. It's a question. And questions require answers, not analysis."

She turned to Feimo. The light from the door's characters caught her eyes, and for a moment, Feimo saw something there that was not fear, not analysis, not the controlled precision that Mia applied to every surface of her life. It was the thing underneath all of it: the part of Mia that sat in a cold server room for five months, watching, because watching was what you did for people.

"I'll go through it," Mia said. "If you will."

Feimo thought about trust. She thought about the cloze passage and its Swiss study and its oxytocin and its neat, clinical description of the mechanism by which human beings decided to be vulnerable to one another. She thought about the toddlers who were introduced to an adult tester holding a plastic container, who were asked "What's in here?" before the tester looked inside, smiled, and exclaimed, "Wow!" She thought about the half who found a toy and the half who found nothing and realized they had been fooled.

She thought about Noir, who had stayed when leaving was easier. She thought about Yue, whose language was being overwritten with something no one could read. She thought about the Misty Poets, who had asked *what now* when the world offered them only *why*.

She thought about Mia's hand hovering before the door, not touching, testing.

"Yes," Feimo said. "I'll go."

---

The door opened inward.

Beyond it was a room. Not a corridor, not a passage, not another descent into deeper dark: a room, roughly circular, with walls that were covered floor to ceiling in text. English text and Chinese text, interwoven, each language filling the gaps in the other, creating a bilingual surface that was neither one thing nor the other but something new, something the Labyrinth had not produced and could not have produced on its own.

The English was legible. Passages from the Academy's curriculum, fragments of reading exercises, sentences from cloze tests, questions from examinations. Feimo spotted a line from the trust passage: *"exposure to this hormone puts us in a trusting mood."* She spotted a line from the automation text: *"the best uses of 3D printers and virtual reality haven't been invented yet."* She spotted a line from the writing prompt: *"the creation of new forms under the incentive of classical tragedy and comedy."*

The Chinese was also legible, but it was not from any text Feimo recognized. It was formal, classical, structured in couplets that alternated with the English passages like voices in a duet:

"理性是启蒙的武器，正如语言是记忆的武器。"

*Reason is the weapon of enlightenment, just as language is the weapon of memory.*

Mia read it aloud, her tones still approximate, her pronunciation careful.

"The Enlightenment," she said. "This is about the Enlightenment. The eighteenth-century movement. Reason over authority. Individual thought over collective dogma. Kant's imperative: 'Dare to use your own reason!' Sapere aude."

"I know what the Enlightenment is."

"Do you know what it's doing here?" Mia moved along the wall, reading, her fingers tracing the text without touching it. "The Labyrinth is a system of authority. It decides what is consumed and what is preserved. It enforces its own rules. It does not tolerate deviation. And this room, this door, these characters, are an Enlightenment within the Labyrinth's own walls. A declaration that reason is not the monopoly of any single system. That languages other than English can carry meaning. That the architecture can be questioned."

Feimo found another passage on the wall, this one in English alone, and recognized it from the 2018 translation exercises:

*"By the date of his birth Europe was witnessing the passing of the religious drama, and the creation of new forms under the incentive of classical tragedy and comedy."*

The sentence was about Shakespeare. About a moment in European history when one form of expression died and another was born. The religious drama passed. Classical forms inspired new ones. The old system did not simply end; it was *replaced* by something that incorporated the best of what came before.

Beside it, in Chinese:

"戏剧是文明的镜子。每一面镜子都映出不同的脸。"

*Theater is the mirror of civilization. Every mirror reflects a different face.*

Feimo thought about the Three Unities. She had studied them weeks ago, in Professor Silence's lecture on classical drama: the unities of time, place, and action that governed seventeenth-century French theater. Everything in one day. Everything in one location. Everything on one narrative line. The rules were strict because the rules were meant to create the illusion of order in a chaotic world. Moliere's comedies. Racine's tragedies. Corneille's grand gestures. All of them shaped by the same constraint: that a story must be contained, must be bounded, must be *unified*, or it ceases to be a story and becomes mere noise.

The Labyrinth was a unity. One language. One system. One set of rules.

This room was the violation of that unity. Two languages. Two systems. Two sets of rules, coexisting on the same wall, in the same space, in the same moment. It was not chaos. It was something more frightening than chaos: it was order of a kind the Labyrinth had never been designed to produce.

"You're not just a Pilgrim," Mia said.

Feimo turned. Mia was standing in the center of the room, the bilingual text surrounding her on all sides, and her expression was one Feimo had never seen on her face before. Not fear. Not analysis. Not the practiced neutrality of a researcher confronting data. It was wonder. The specific, disorienting wonder of someone who has spent years studying a system and has just discovered that the system is alive.

"The Labyrinth processes Pilgrims," Mia continued. "That's what we are. Inputs. Text-bearing organisms that move through its corridors and are measured, tested, indexed. Every student in the Academy is a Pilgrim, whether they know it or not. We carry language into the system, and the system takes what it can use and discards the rest."

She looked at the wall. At the English and the Chinese. At the place where two languages met and did not cancel each other out.

"What you did with the Fragmenter, and what this door is doing, and what this room is showing us, none of that is the behavior of a system processing a Pilgrim. The Labyrinth didn't just read your Chinese. It *responded* to it. It changed its architecture. It created new space. It opened a door."

Her voice dropped again, to that register Feimo had heard once before, in the server room, at four in the morning, when Mia admitted that she had been watching Yue for five months because watching was what you did for people.

"You're not a Pilgrim," Mia said. "You're a conversation."

---

They stayed in the room for two hours.

They read every line on the walls, photographing what they could, transcribing what they couldn't. The bilingual text was dense, layered, the English and Chinese occupying the same surface in patterns that shifted as you moved around the room: from a distance, the languages appeared separate, each occupying its own zone; up close, they merged, the characters interleaving at the stroke level, a single mark that was simultaneously an English letter and a Chinese radical.

Feimo transcribed the Chinese passages. There were thirty-seven of them, she counted. They spanned the entire history of Chinese literature: lines from the *Shijing*, the oldest surviving collection of Chinese poetry; fragments of Li Bai's Tang dynasty verse; sentences from Lu Xun's modern prose; and, near the floor, almost hidden by shadow, a single line from Bei Dao:

"我不相信。"

*I do not believe.*

The same line that Noir had recited in the basement kitchen, weeks ago, over jasmine tea, in the voice she used when she was being serious rather than sardonic. The line that defined a generation. The line that said: the only honest response to a world built on lies is to refuse to believe in any of it.

Mia found the English counterpart across the room:

"The challenge of coping with automation underlines the need to revive fading dynamism."

The sentence was from the automation text, but it had been altered. In the original passage, the word was *business*. Here, it was simply *fading dynamism*, unmoored from its economic context, made universal. The challenge of coping with change underlines the need to revive what is being lost.

Feimo sat on the floor of the room and closed her notebook.

"We can't report this," she said.

Mia looked at her.

"If we report it, the Academy will seal this section. They'll send researchers. They'll classify the door, the room, the bilingual text. They'll turn it into data. And the moment it becomes data, it stops being a question and becomes an answer, and answers are things the Labyrinth can consume."

"So what do we do?"

"We come back. We study it ourselves. Quietly. Without telling anyone."

"That's against the rules."

"Everything down here is against the rules. You unlocked a door with a key you should not have had. I wrote Chinese into the Labyrinth's walls. The Fragmenter attacked us and I destroyed it with a character a child could write. We are already so far past the rules that the rules are a memory."

Mia was quiet. The room hummed. The bilingual text pulsed, gently, the way the door had pulsed, the rhythm of a system that had found a new frequency and was still calibrating.

"Okay," Mia said. "We come back."

---

They climbed back in silence.

The corridor widened as they ascended, the ceiling rising, the stone drying, the residue of consumed text returning to the walls like color returning to a winter landscape. By the time they reached the sealed door, the Deep Labyrinth was behind them and the Academy was ahead: familiar, rule-governed, English-only, a system that processed language and memory and did not ask questions it could not answer.

Mia locked the door behind them. The key disappeared into her pocket.

In the sub-basement corridor, under the fluorescent lights that buzzed at 50 Hz, the exact frequency of institutional normalcy, they stopped and looked at each other. The descent had taken forty minutes. The return had taken twenty. They had been gone for three hours. It was four in the morning.

"Feimo," Mia said.

"Yes?"

"What you wrote on the paper. The character for *door*. 门. How did you know the wall would accept it?"

Feimo thought about the question. She thought about the cloze passage and its description of a sixth sense for dishonesty, a biological mechanism that allowed even toddlers to differentiate between credible and unreliable sources. She thought about the Swiss study and the oxytocin and the trusting mood. She thought about the passage's final word: *unreliable*, applied to the tester who had fooled the children, the tester who had smiled and said "Wow!" at an empty container.

She thought about the walls of the Deep Labyrinth, warm to the touch, resonating at a frequency that was not the Labyrinth's own.

"I didn't know," she said. "I attempted it. That's different. An attempt is not a guarantee. It's a decision to act in the absence of certainty. The passage about automation says the best uses of new technology haven't been invented yet. You don't wait for certainty. You write the character. You press it against the wall. You watch what happens."

"And if the wall had consumed it?"

"Then we would have lost a piece of paper. And I would have tried something else."

Mia looked at her for a long time. The fluorescent light was unkind: it showed every line of fatigue on her face, every hour of the five months of watching, every crack in the polish of her composure. But beneath the fatigue, in the set of her mouth and the angle of her chin, there was something Feimo had not seen before. Not respect. Respect was old. This was new. This was the expression of someone who has just discovered that the person standing in front of them is not the person they assumed, and who is not afraid of the difference.

"Your behavior," Mia said carefully, "does not match any model I have of you."

"Is that a problem?"

"No. It's a data point. And I collect data. That's what I do."

She turned and walked toward Mirror House, her footsteps precise, her posture reassembling itself into its usual rigidity, piece by piece, like a machine rebooting after a system update. But at the end of the corridor, just before she turned the corner, she paused. She did not look back. But she paused, and in the pause, her ears angled slightly toward Feimo, a cat's gesture of attention, of awareness, of the acknowledgment that someone is present in the room behind you and their presence matters.

Then she turned the corner and was gone.

Feimo stood in the corridor alone.

She opened her notebook and looked at the page she had not torn out. The poem she had written at five in the morning, weeks ago, after watching Mia watch Yue:

> *Someone is losing their language.*
> *Not the way the Labyrinth erases, which is mechanical and clean.*
> *The way a dream is lost: slowly, incompletely, leaving only the shape of what was there.*

She turned to a fresh page and wrote:

> *Today I found a door that shouldn't exist.*
> *It was written in a language the Labyrinth was never designed to read.*
> *I opened it.*
> *Inside was a room where two languages spoke to each other.*
> *I don't know what they said.*
> *But I know they were talking.*

She closed the notebook.

The fluorescent lights buzzed at 50 Hz. The Academy hummed its constant hum. Somewhere below them, in the Deep Labyrinth, the door stood with its classical calligraphy, patient and warm, waiting for them to return.

Somewhere in Mirror House, in a server room maintained at sixteen degrees Celsius, a column of numbers continued to decrease, the rate at which Yue's readable text was being replaced with something no one could identify.

Somewhere in Forge House, in a basement kitchen that smelled of jasmine tea, Noir read Bei Dao and understood what it cost to stay.

And somewhere in the walls of the Academy, in the space between what the Labyrinth could erase and what the mind could forget, a conversation was beginning that no system had been designed to hear.

## Vocabulary Notes

**attempt** /əˈtɛmpt/
- *noun*: an act of trying to achieve something, typically with difficulty or uncertainty. "I **attempted** it. That's different. An attempt is not a guarantee."
- In the 2018 exam context, the word carries the weight of agency: to *attempt* is to act without certainty, to make a decision in the absence of complete information. The Labyrinth rewards attempts that match its architecture and punishes those that don't. Feimo's attempt to write Chinese into the walls succeeded because the system was ready to expand. A different attempt, at a different time, might have been consumed.

**author** /ˈɔː.θə/
- *noun*: a writer of a book, article, or report. "The **author** of that automation text suggests that tax policies should aim at preventing the income gap from widening."
- In the Academy, the concept of *author* is complicated. Who wrote the Misty Poems on the blank pages? Who wrote the calligraphy on the door? Who is the *author* of a system that produces output beyond its own understanding? The 2018 reading passage asks about the author's view, treating the author as a fixed, knowable intelligence. In the Labyrinth, authorship is distributed, uncertain, and sometimes impossible.

**behavior** /bɪˈheɪvjə/
- *noun*: the way in which one acts or conducts oneself. "Your **behavior** does not match any model I have of you."
- The 2018 cloze passage examines the *behavior* of children in response to trustworthy and unreliable adults: a measurable, predictable pattern driven by biology (oxytocin) and experience (being fooled or not). In the Labyrinth, *behavior* is what the system measures: how students respond to exercises, how Pilgrims navigate corridors, how language moves through architecture. But Feimo's behavior breaks the model, because the model was built for a single-language system, and she carries two.

## Thinking Questions

1. **On Trust and Architecture:** The cloze passage describes trust as a biological mechanism driven by oxytocin, one that can be overridden by adult rationalization. Mia trusts Feimo enough to descend into the Deep Labyrinth, but she admits she was "afraid" of what the Chinese characters meant. Is Mia's trust in Feimo biological, rational, or something else entirely? What happens when trust in a person conflicts with distrust of a system?

2. **On Automation and Expansion:** The reading passage about automation describes a system (the economy) evolving beyond its original parameters, displacing workers who cannot adapt. Feimo argues that the Labyrinth is undergoing a similar transformation: expanding from English-only to bilingual. But the passage also notes that "in the medium term, workers may need a lot of help adjusting." Who helps the Labyrinth adjust to its own expansion?

3. **On Two Languages in One Room:** The bilingual room in the Deep Labyrinth contains English and Chinese texts that interweave at the stroke level. The Enlightenment principle of "dare to use your own reason" is written beside classical Chinese couplets about reason and memory. What does it mean for two languages to coexist not just side by side but *within* each other? Is this integration, translation, or something new?

4. **On Authorship and the Unities:** Classical dramatic theory (Card #046) demands unity of time, place, and action: one story, one setting, one day. The Labyrinth operates under a similar constraint: one language, one system, one set of rules. The Deep Labyrinth violates all three. If the Labyrinth is a drama, what happens when a new author enters the stage?

5. **On Being a Conversation:** Mia tells Feimo, "You're not a Pilgrim. You're a conversation." What does it mean to be a conversation rather than an input? If Feimo is a conversation between the Labyrinth and something the Labyrinth was not designed to hear, who is the other speaker?
