# Chapter 22: Misty Poetry & Dream Loss

The poems started appearing on the third Tuesday of November.

Feimo found the first one on page 203 of the Redaction Archive, which was supposed to be empty. The Archive was a compendium of erased texts: passages the Labyrinth had consumed, rendered blank, and stored for recovery work. Every page was white. Every page was meant to be white. That was the entire point.

But page 203 was not white. It held four lines of handwritten Chinese, the ink slightly too dark, as if the pen had pressed too hard against the grain of the paper:

> *我不相信天是蓝的*
> *我不相信雷的回声*
> *我不相信梦是假的*
> *我不相信死无报应*

Feimo read them three times. She did not recognize the handwriting. It was neither her own nor Mia's nor any script she had catalogued in the Library's system. The characters were precise, angular, almost mechanical, but with a human inconsistency in the pressure curves: the dot on the *不* was heavier than it should have been, and the final stroke of the *应* trailed off into nothing, as if the writer had stopped mid-breath.

She pulled out her phone and photographed the page. Then she closed the Archive, put it back on the shelf, and went to find Noir.

---

Noir was in the basement common room of Forge House, sitting on the floor with her back against the radiator and her legs folded beneath her. She was reading a book that Feimo recognized: a battered paperback edition of Bei Dao's collected poems, its spine cracked in three places, its pages yellowed with the specific shade of age that came from being read too often in rooms with bad ventilation.

"You read poetry," Feimo said, stopping in the doorway.

"I read everything," Noir said, not looking up. "That's the difference between me and the rest of this place. They read what's assigned. I read what's true."

Feimo sat down across from her and showed her the photograph.

Noir looked at it for a long time. Her ears, which normally stayed at a practiced neutral, flattened slightly. Not in alarm. In recognition.

"Bei Dao," she said. "1976. 'The Answer.' It was the poem that defined an entire generation of Chinese writers. The Misty Poets. *Ménglóng shīrén.* They were called that because critics couldn't understand them."

"I thought the Labyrinth erased things," Feimo said. "Not wrote new ones."

"It didn't write this." Noir closed her own book and set it beside her. "Someone did. Someone with access to the Archive who shouldn't have access. Someone who wanted you to find it."

"Why me?"

"Because you're the only person in this school who reads blank pages and expects to find something there."

---

Noir told her about the Misty Poets that night, in the low, even voice she used when she was being serious rather than sardonic. They were in the Forge House kitchen, alone, at two in the morning. Noir had made tea, which she did when she was thinking, and the cups sat between them on the scarred wooden table like markers on a map.

"The term *ménglóng* was an insult," Noir said. "Critics meant it as a dismissal. These poems were obscure, they said. Deliberately obscure. They used imagery that didn't resolve into clear meaning. They refused the directness that the state demanded. Poetry was supposed to serve the people. Poetry was supposed to be useful. And here were these young writers producing work that meant different things to different readers, sometimes contradictory things within the same stanza."

As Noir spoke, Feimo recalled Card 037—the three phases of New Period literature that the Misty Poets had sparked. Scar Literature (伤痕文学) came first: raw testimony of those who had survived, writing not to analyze but to bear witness. Then Reflection Literature (反思文学): the harder work of asking why, of tracing wounds back to their causes. And finally Reform Literature (改革文学): the forward turn, the insistence that critique must lead to reconstruction. The Misty Poets had been the spark for all three—the first to insist that individual consciousness had a right to speak, even when the systems around them demanded silence.

She lifted her cup but did not drink.

"Bei Dao. Shu Ting. Gu Cheng. Hai Zi. These were the names. They came out of the late 1970s, when the country was waking from a decade of nightmares and nobody had the vocabulary for what they had lost. The Wound Literature writers tried: they laid out the injuries, documented the damage, presented the facts. The Reflection writers went further, asking *why*. But the Misty Poets did something more dangerous. They asked *what now*."

Feimo wrapped her hands around her own cup. The tea was jasmine, bitter, the kind of tea that tasted like thinking.

"Their characteristic," Noir continued, "was a turn from collective expression to individual consciousness. Before them, Chinese poetry spoke in the voice of *we*. The Misty Poets spoke as *I*. And when you give an individual consciousness permission to speak in a culture that had spent a decade erasing individuality, you get something that looks like obscurity but is actually the most radical clarity. It just isn't the kind of clarity that ideology can metabolize."

She paused.

"That's why the Labyrinth would want these poems. They're the literary equivalent of a Resonance frequency that doesn't match any known pattern. They destabilize systems that depend on uniformity."

---

The second poem appeared on Thursday.

This time it was on the wall of the third-floor corridor in Bridge House, visible only from a specific angle. Feimo would have walked past it if her ear hadn't caught the faintest acoustic anomaly: a spot where the ambient Resonance of the building stuttered, like a heartbeat skipping. She stopped, turned, and saw the characters materializing on the plaster, fading in from nothing like a photograph developing in solution.

> *黑夜给了我黑色的眼睛*
> *我却用它寻找光明*

Gu Cheng. "A Generation." Two lines. The most compressed expression of defiance in modern Chinese poetry.

Feimo stood in the corridor and watched the characters fade. They took eleven seconds to vanish completely. She counted.

---

She went to Mia.

Mia's room in Mirror House was a controlled environment in the way that a hospital operating theater was a controlled environment: everything at a precise temperature, a precise humidity, a precise level of light. The desk was organized with a rigor that bordered on violence. Every pen was parallel. Every paper was squared. The only irregularity was a slight flicker in the corner of Mia's projection, which she had either not noticed or deliberately left as evidence that she was, despite everything, still a process running on hardware that occasionally stuttered.

"I need to talk to you about the Archive," Feimo said.

"I'm busy." Mia's eyes were fixed on her screen. What was on the screen was not the usual academic work. It was a data stream: dense columns of numbers scrolling upward in a green-on-black terminal that Feimo recognized as a Labyrinth diagnostic tool. The numbers moved fast, too fast for human reading, but Mia's eyes tracked them with the mechanical patience of someone who had been doing this for hours.

"What are you looking at?" Feimo asked.

"System maintenance," Mia said, in the flat tone that meant she was lying.

"At two in the morning?"

"Systems require maintenance at all hours. That's what makes them systems and not hobbies." The data stream continued to scroll. A column of numbers on the left side was highlighted in red. Mia's jaw tightened, almost imperceptibly. "What about the Archive?"

Feimo told her about the poems.

Mia's typing stopped. For three seconds, her hands hovered over the keyboard like birds over a field they could not decide whether to land on. Then she resumed typing, faster than before.

"Poems appearing in erased space," Mia said. "Characters materializing on walls. This is not the Labyrinth's behavior. The Labyrinth consumes. It doesn't create. If something is appearing where nothing should be, it's because a process is generating it. A process I should know about."

"You're saying it's mechanical?"

"I'm saying everything is mechanical. Poetry is mechanical. Emotion is mechanical. Dreams are mechanical. The question is whether the machine is ours."

She did not look away from the screen. The red-highlighted column continued to scroll. Feimo noticed that the numbers in that column were not random. They were decreasing. Slowly, steadily, like a temperature reading in a room that was getting colder.

"Mia," Feimo said. "What are you really monitoring?"

"System maintenance," Mia repeated, and this time the lie was so thin that it was almost an invitation to see through it.

Feimo did not push. She was learning when to stop. There were doors in the Academy that opened only when you walked away from them.

---

The third poem arrived on Saturday, and this time it was not in a corridor or an archive. It was in Feimo's dream.

She was standing in a field of white paper. The paper extended in every direction, flat, featureless, the color of erased text. There was no horizon. There was no sky. The air smelled of nothing, which was worse than smelling of something wrong, because nothing meant the space was optimized, designed, curated. Someone had built this place and chosen to remove every sensory detail except the paper.

In the center of the field stood a woman Feimo had never seen. She was young, Chinese, with hair cut short in the blunt style of the early 1980s. She wore a plain blue jacket, buttoned to the throat. Her feet were bare on the paper. She was writing, bent over a notebook, and the pen moved with the frantic urgency of someone trying to finish a sentence before the language changed.

Feimo approached her. The woman did not look up.

"What are you writing?" Feimo asked.

The woman held up the notebook. The page was covered in characters, but they were not Chinese. They were not any language Feimo recognized. The shapes had the structural logic of a writing system: consistent stroke weights, recurring elements, spatial organization into blocks. But the symbols meant nothing. They were a language that existed in the grammar of language without existing in any living tongue.

"I can't read this," Feimo said.

The woman turned the notebook around and looked at the page herself. Her expression was not surprised. It was sad. The specific sadness of someone who has watched something familiar become foreign, slowly, over weeks, and has just now realized that the process is irreversible.

"Neither can I," the woman said.

Feimo woke up with the taste of jasmine in her mouth and a frequency ringing in her ears that had no source.

---

She told Noir about the dream.

They were walking the lower corridors, the ones below the Library that the maps did not show. Noir moved through them with the ease of long practice, her steps quiet on the stone, her ears swiveling at regular intervals to check for acoustic signatures.

"Shu Ting," Noir said. "The woman in your dream. Short hair, blue jacket, writing in a notebook. That's the image. She's the one who wrote '致橡树'—'To the Oak Tree.' The most famous love poem of the Misty generation. Except it isn't really a love poem. It's a manifesto of equality between two people. 'If I love you, I will never become a攀附的凌霄花.' She refused to be the vine on the oak. She insisted on being a separate tree."

"She was writing in a language she couldn't read," Feimo said. "The same language, the whole time. But she didn't understand it."

Noir stopped walking.

"That's the detail that matters," she said. "Not the poem. Not the poet. The language. A writer who produces text she cannot read is a system that has output beyond its own parameters. In engineering, that's called an overshoot. In psychology, it's called dissociation. In the Labyrinth, it's called—"

She stopped herself.

"What?" Feimo asked.

"Forget it." Noir resumed walking. "It's nothing. A theory. Not enough data."

She was lying, too. Feimo was surrounded by brilliant women who lied to her with the precision of surgeons. She was beginning to understand that the lies were not malicious. They were protective. The truth, in the Academy, was a material with a half-life: it decayed the moment you exposed it to air, and the radiation from that decay could damage everyone nearby.

---

The writing prompt appeared in the Monday lecture.

Professor Silence stood at the front of the lecture hall, his white coat immaculate, his expression the particular shade of neutral that meant he was about to assign something difficult. The room was full: Bridge and Mirror students combined for a cross-House seminar on textual analysis methodology. Feimo sat in the third row. Noir was behind her, one row back, with the Bei Dao paperback hidden inside a textbook.

Silence wrote on the board:

> *For today's exercise: write a notice for newly enrolled international students, providing relevant information about the Library. One hundred words. Formal register. Sign with a pseudonym. You have fifteen minutes.*

A murmur moved through the hall. A notice was elementary. Notices were first-week assignments.

The room settled into the scratch of pens and the tap of keyboards. Feimo stared at her blank page. A notice. For international students. About the Library.

She thought about what the Library actually was: a building that consumed texts, that erased memories, that maintained itself through the systematic loss of information. How did you write a welcoming notice for a place whose fundamental operation was forgetting?

She wrote:

> **NOTICE: Welcome to the Library**
>
> Dear newly enrolled students,
>
> The Library is the heart of the Academy. It houses over three million volumes across six floors, including rare manuscripts, Glyph-annotated texts, and the Redaction Archive. Access hours are 6:00 AM to midnight. The reading rooms on floors two through four are reserved for silent study. The fifth floor contains the Resonance laboratory, which requires faculty permission to enter. The sixth floor does not exist.
>
> Please return all borrowed materials within fourteen days. Materials not returned will be assumed to have been consumed by the system. This is not a metaphor.
>
> We look forward to seeing you.
>
> Li Ming
> Assistant Librarian

She handed it in. Professor Silence read it, and something that was not quite a smile moved across his face like a cloud across a window.

He wrote on the margin of the paper:

> *Good. But the sixth floor does exist. You just shouldn't have mentioned it.*

---

The cloze exercise that week was about Cambodian marriage customs.

Feimo worked through the blanks with mechanical efficiency. The passage described arranged marriages: a young man could *decide on* a spouse and ask his parents to *arrange* the marriage. *In theory*, a girl could veto the choice. Divorce was legal and easy to *obtain*, but divorced persons were *regarded* with disapproval.

Feimo paused on *in theory*. The phrase meant the possibility existed in principle but was rarely exercised. The girl could say no. The girl was expected to say yes. The gap between theory and practice was the space where most of human life happened.

She thought about Noir, who had once loved a human.

---

Noir told her on Tuesday night, in the same basement kitchen, at the same unsanctioned hour.

It was not a confession. Noir did not confess. She delivered information with the flat affect of a field report, and the listener was responsible for their own emotional response. But she chose her venue and her timing with care: two in the morning, alone, jasmine tea, the radiator ticking against the wall. These were the conditions under which truth was possible.

"His name was not important," Noir said. "Names are details, and details are how you build a cage. What matters is that he was human. Fully human. No ears, no tail, no Resonance. He was a student at a regular university, studying literature, and I met him at a bookshop in the city during my second year. He was reading Shu Ting."

She held her cup in both hands. The steam rose between her fingers.

"My family did not approve. This will not surprise you. Cat-kind are not common in this world, and we are not popular. My parents' position was that a human partner would dilute the bloodline, which is the kind of argument that sounds scientific until you realize it is just fear wearing a lab coat. The Academy was worse. Faculty did not say anything directly, because they were too professional for that. But the invitations to departmental events stopped. My access to certain research materials was 'temporarily restricted.' My advisor began suggesting alternative thesis topics that did not require late-night lab sessions."

She took a sip of tea.

"He left. Not because he stopped caring. Because the friction was too great. Every time we walked together in public, I could feel the stares. Not hostile, most of them. Just curious. Just confused. But curiosity at sufficient density becomes pressure, and pressure at sufficient duration becomes erosion. He eroded. I watched it happen. One day he looked at me and I could see that the cost of loving me had exceeded the benefit of the thing itself."

"He left," Feimo repeated.

"He left. I stayed. That is the difference between us. I had nowhere else to go. The Academy, for all its cruelties, was the only institution in the world that treated cat-kind as something other than a novelty or a threat. I could be a student here. I could be a researcher. I could be *useful*. Outside, I was a pair of ears and a tail. Inside, I was a person with a GPA."

She set down her cup.

"So when you ask me why I read Bei Dao, the answer is simple. Bei Dao wrote: 'I do not believe.' Four statements of disbelief. The sky is not blue. Thunder has no echo. Dreams are not false. Death is not without consequence. That poem was written by a man who had watched an entire civilization lie to itself for a decade, and who decided that the only honest response was to refuse to believe in anything that could be used as a weapon. I read him because he understood what it costs to stay when leaving is easier."

The radiator ticked. The tea cooled. Somewhere above them, the Academy hummed its constant, frequency-stable hum, the sound of a machine that processed language and memory and did not care about the people inside it.

Feimo did not say anything. There was nothing to say. Some silences were not gaps to be filled but structures to be respected, load-bearing walls in the architecture of trust.

---

The reading passage that week was about the French fashion industry: France's decision to criminalize ultra-thin models, to ban websites promoting extreme dieting, to impose fines on designers who used models below a government-defined body mass index.

Feimo underlined: *beauty should not be defined by looks that end up impinging on health.* She thought about the Academy's own definitions of beauty — analytical precision, Glyph speed, Resonance density. The body-mass indices of the academic world. Feimo's ears were not a metric. Noir's past was not a metric. The poems appearing in erased spaces were not a metric.

The passage on corporate social responsibility added another layer: even organizations that reject social responsibility may find that ethical behavior produces tangible returns. A "halo effect": the diffuse goodwill generated by visible virtue. Feimo thought about the Academy. Was the Labyrinth a corporation? Even a machine designed for extraction could, accidentally, create spaces where growth was possible.

---

The translation exercise was about mental health: *We don't have to learn how to be mentally healthy; it is built into us in the same way that our bodies know how to heal a cut or mend a broken bone.*

Feimo translated it and sat with the result. The sentence assumed mental health was a default state, like the sun behind a cloud: temporarily hidden, fully capable of being restored.

She thought about Yue. Yue, whose handwriting grew less recognizable each week. Yue, whose pages in the Redaction Archive were being consumed faster than anyone could document. Yue, who had started the semester as the quietest genius Feimo had ever met and was now becoming something else: a system producing output that exceeded its own understanding.

Something was being lost. Not erased. Lost, in the way that a dream is lost upon waking: still present in the architecture of the mind, but no longer accessible to consciousness.

---

The poem appeared on the wall of the Library's ground floor at 11:47 PM on Wednesday.

Feimo was there because she was always there at that hour, working through exercises, pretending that the building was a building and not a machine. She saw the characters materialize in real time: each stroke emerging from the plaster like a bone pushing through skin, slow, deliberate, painful in the way that creation was always painful.

It was Hai Zi. "Facing the Sea, Spring Warmth and Flowers Bloom."

> *从明天起，做一个幸福的人*
> *喂马，劈柴，周游世界*
> *从明天起，关心粮食和蔬菜*
> *我有一所房子，面朝大海，春暖花开*

Four lines. A promise of domestic happiness from a poet who would walk to the Shanhaiguan rail junction and lie down in front of a train. The most beautiful lie in modern Chinese literature: that tomorrow would be different, that happiness was a choice, that the sea and the warmth and the flowers were enough.

Feimo watched the characters fade. They took thirteen seconds. Two seconds longer than Gu Cheng's two lines. The duration correlated with length, she realized. More text, more time. The phenomenon was consistent. Mechanical.

Mia would want to know that.

---

She found Mia at four in the morning, in the Mirror House server room.

The room was cold. Not metaphorically cold: the temperature was maintained at sixteen degrees Celsius to protect the processing units. Mia was sitting on the floor in front of a terminal, wrapped in a blanket that she had clearly brought from her room because it was the same shade of institutional gray as her pillow. The data stream on the screen had not changed. The red-highlighted column was still scrolling, still decreasing.

"You're monitoring Yue," Feimo said.

Mia did not turn around.

"The numbers are her dream-state Resonance output," Feimo continued. "The red column is the rate at which her stored text is being replaced with unreadable content. It's been decreasing because there's less readable text left to replace. You've been watching it go to zero."

Mia's hands were still on the keyboard. Her projection had dimmed, the edges of her form losing resolution, the posture of a consciousness that has been cornered and has decided not to run.

"Yes," Mia said.

"For how long?"

"Since September."

Five months. Mia had been sitting in this cold room, at these hours, for five months, watching her friend's mind empty itself of language.

"Is she dying?" Feimo asked.

"I don't know." Mia's voice was steady, which was how you knew it was breaking. "The process doesn't match any model in the Labyrinth's database. Her Resonance signature is stable. Her cognitive function is within normal parameters during waking hours. But when she sleeps, the replacement accelerates. Her stored language, her vocabulary, her syntax, all of it is being overwritten with a system I cannot identify. The output is structured. It has grammar. It has recurring morphemes. But it maps to nothing in any known human language."

"It's not human?"

"It's not *known*. That's different. There are seven thousand languages on Earth. I've checked the major ones. I've checked the dead ones. I've checked constructed languages: Esperanto, Klingon, Quenya. Nothing. Whatever Yue is writing in, it exists in the same space that the Misty Poems are appearing: the gap between what the Labyrinth can erase and what the mind can forget."

Mia turned off the monitor. In the sudden darkness, the faint blue light of the server LEDs caught the edges of her projection, giving her outline a glow that was not quite human and not quite machine.

"I haven't told her," Mia said. "She doesn't know. During the day she's normal. She reads. She writes. She attends lectures. She eats lunch with Aya and smiles when Aya makes those terrible puns about verb conjugation. She doesn't know that every night a part of her is being replaced."

"Why haven't you told her?"

"Because knowing would not help. The process is not volitional. It's not a choice she's making. It's a transformation that's happening to her, like hair growing or bones lengthening. You don't tell someone their hair is growing. You watch. You measure. You wait for the data to become sufficient to act."

"And if it never becomes sufficient?"

Mia pulled the blanket tighter around her shoulders.

"Then I watch anyway," she said. "That's what you do for people. You watch."

---

Feimo walked back to her room in Bridge House at five in the morning.

The corridors were empty. The Academy slept, or approximated sleep: a low-power state in which the Labyrinth's background processes continued to operate at reduced capacity, processing, indexing, consuming the texts that students had left open on their desks. Feimo could hear it, the way she could hear everything: a hum beneath the hum, the sound of a machine dreaming.

She passed the spot on the third floor where Gu Cheng's poem had appeared. The wall was blank. She passed the Library's ground floor. The plaster was clean. The poems were gone, all of them, erased by the same system that had allowed them to appear.

She sat on the floor of the corridor and opened her notebook. She had an essay to write: interpret the meaning of the fashion-industry passage, give your comments. Two hundred words. She should start. She should be practical. She should be academic.

Instead she wrote:

> *Someone is losing their language.*
> *Not the way the Labyrinth erases, which is mechanical and clean.*
> *The way a dream is lost: slowly, incompletely, leaving only the shape of what was there.*
> *The Misty Poets wrote in the space between memory and forgetting.*
> *Yue is living in it.*

She looked at what she had written and recognized, with a cold clarity that settled into her spine like the server room's sixteen-degree air, that she was not writing an essay. She was writing a poem. And it was in English, which was not her first language, which meant that the boundary between her languages was already less stable than she had assumed.

She closed the notebook.

*From tomorrow,* she thought, borrowing Hai Zi's construction, *I will be a person who pays attention. I will feed the horse. I will chop the wood. I will notice what is disappearing before it is gone.*

But Hai Zi had not made it to tomorrow. That was the point. That was the reason the poem was beautiful: because it was written by someone who knew that tomorrow was not guaranteed, and who chose to believe in it anyway.

The reason, Feimo thought, was not optimism. It was defiance. The same defiance that made Bei Dao refuse to believe. The same defiance that made Shu Ting refuse to be a vine. The same defiance that made Noir stay in an institution that had cost her a human love, because staying was harder than leaving and therefore more honest.

She stood up. She went to her room. She set her alarm for seven. She would attend the morning lecture. She would do the exercises. She would watch Yue and say nothing, because Mia was right: watching was what you did for people.

And somewhere in the walls of the Academy, the poems continued to appear and disappear, like heartbeats in a body that was not sure whether it was alive.

## Vocabulary Notes

**present** /pɹəˈzɛnt/
- *adjective*: existing or occurring now. "They were **present**, in the way that water is present in a well: naturally, quietly, because the shape of the space invited it."
- In the 2016 exam reading, "the **present** situation" refers to the current state of the fashion industry's crisis. The word carries weight in the Labyrinth: to be *present* is to exist in a space that constantly consumes and erases.

**reason** /ˈɹiːzən/
- *noun*: a cause, explanation, or justification. "The **reason**, Feimo thought, was not optimism. It was defiance."
- In academic and philosophical contexts, *reason* operates on two levels: the logical (cause and effect) and the human (the emotional motivation behind an action). The Misty Poets' *reason* for writing was not to be obscure, but to preserve individual consciousness against collective erasure.

**result** /ɹɪˈzʌlt/
- *noun*: a consequence or outcome. "Using a fashion model that does not meet a government-defined index of body mass could **result** in a $85,000 fine."
- In the cloze passage, actions *result* in consequences: marriages arranged, property divided, prejudices maintained. In the Labyrinth, every action produces results that are measurable and results that are not. The poems are a result. Yue's transformation is a result. The difference is that one can be photographed and the other can only be watched.

## Thinking Questions

1. **On "In Theory":** The cloze passage uses "In theory" to describe a woman's right to veto an arranged marriage. How does this phrase function in the Academy? What rights do students have "in theory" that they may not have in practice?

2. **On Beauty Standards:** The reading passage describes France's attempt to legislate beauty standards. The Academy measures students by analytical precision and Resonance density. Are these metrics any more "objective" than body-mass index? Who benefits from the measurement, and who is harmed?

3. **On the Halo Effect:** Companies that engage in ethical behavior receive more lenient treatment, even when their primary purpose is profit. Does the Academy's educational mission function as a halo effect for the Labyrinth's true purpose of consuming text?

4. **On Language Loss:** Yue is being overwritten with an unknown language. Hai Zi chose death over continuing to write in a world that could not hear him. Is losing a language the same as losing a self? Can a person exist in a language that no one else can read?

5. **On Watching:** Mia chooses to watch Yue's deterioration without intervening, because intervention would not help and knowledge would not heal. Is this compassion or cowardice? When does watching become complicity?
