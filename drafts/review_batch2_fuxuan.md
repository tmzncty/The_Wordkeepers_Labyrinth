## Executive Summary

The six chapters are **emotionally ambitious** and **thematically rich**. Ch.8 (Shijing Corridor) is the strongest piece in the batch — a genuinely atmospheric discovery scene. Ch.11's Reception Aesthetics lesson with Professor Silence is pedagogically brilliant. Feimo's growth across the year-skip (Ch.9) and the first public battle (Ch.10) track well against the worldbuilding's "Finding Her Feet" arc.

However, the batch suffers from **three critical problems**:

1. **Naming inconsistency persists** — Batch 1 flagged this as P0. It is not fixed. New cases added: Aya/Ling, Cain/Kain, Noah/Noir.
2. **Cross-chapter exam passage recycling** — The same passages (Aristotle laughter, Strack experiment, Supreme Court ethics, peer pressure) appear 2–3 times across these six chapters, re-explained each time as if the reader has not read previous chapters.
3. **Ch.10 is bloated** — 480 lines, ~180 of which are a continuous teaching lecture. The narrative momentum from the battle is crushed under the weight of exam analysis.

Mia's sealed memory (Ch.12 spoiler) is **properly contained** — hints only, no premature reveal. Knowledge card embedding is dense but generally natural (~10 cards across 6 chapters).

**Verdict**: 2 chapters pass, 2 need revision, 2 need significant cuts.

## Chapter 6: The Anatomy of a Passage

**Lines**: ~102 | **Est. Words**: ~1,800 | **Verdict**: ✅ Pass (1 P1, 1 P2)

### ✅ What Works
- Lean and focused — the shortest chapter in the batch, and it earns every line
- Three-Layer Reading Method introduced cleanly through the Aristotle laughter passage
- Aya's sarcasm-vs-understakes mistake (L79) is a character-efficient beat: it shows her analytical precision AND her blind spot in one exchange
- Yue's silent presence in the back row (L11) — characterization without a word of dialogue
- Mia's voice is pitch-perfect: "Your question is stupid" energy without actually saying it
- Feimo's ears as a barometer — "tracking Mia's movements with the involuntary precision of a cat watching a laser pointer" (L13) is great

### Issues

#### P1 — Ch.6 as Study Break must not duplicate Ch.10's teaching content

The task specification warns that Ch.6 and Ch.10 are both Study Break chapters, and their teaching content must not repeat. **Current status**: Ch.6 teaches the Three-Layer Reading Method using the Aristotle laughter passage. Ch.10 teaches post-combat analysis using entirely different passages (credibility process, peer pressure, Entergy). **The specific passages do not repeat between these two chapters** — this is good. But the Three-Layer Reading Method *framework* is re-explained in Ch.10 (L285-293) as if for the first time. Since Ch.10 occurs after the year-skip, a brief callback is acceptable. A full re-explanation is not.

**Fix**: In Ch.10, reference the Three-Layer framework in one line ("Layer Two, as always — structure") instead of walking through it again.

#### P2 — L15: Mia's teaching monologue is dense

The opening lecture (L15) is a single long paragraph mixing exposition, insult, and instruction. It works, but it's doing a lot of lifting.

**Fix**: Minor. Could break with a Feimo reaction or ear-movement beat.

## Chapter 7: The Forge Below

**Lines**: ~358 | **Est. Words**: ~6,200 | **Verdict**: ⚠️ Conditional Pass (1 P0, 2 P1s, 1 P2)

### ✅ What Works

### Issues

#### P0 — "Cain" vs. "Kain" naming

Ch.7 L99: Noah identifies him as **"Cain"**. Ch.9 L42: The text uses **"Kain"**. The worldbuilding (§4.8) says **"Cain (凯因)"**.

**Fix**: Standardize to **Cain** across all chapters. This is the same naming-class of error flagged in Batch 1 (Moon/Yue).

#### P1 — L283-287: Aristotle passage reflection after the battle

After the underground fight, Feimo has a 5-line reflection on the Aristotle laughter passage:

> *"She had always thought of this as abstract knowledge... Her body had responded to the fight... The passage was right. The body and the mind were not separate systems."*

**Problem**: This passage was fully taught in Ch.6. Re-stating its findings here, in a post-battle reflection, breaks the narrative momentum. Feimo just fought an incredible match and the first thing she thinks about is an exam passage? The body-mind connection she's discovering is valid — but it should emerge from the fight itself, not from recalling a cloze exercise.

**Fix**: Cut the Aristotle reference. Let the body-mind insight emerge organically from the fight experience: *"Her body had responded to the fight — heart racing, muscles tensing, focus sharpening in ways that no classroom had ever produced. The body knew things the mind had not yet been taught."* Same insight, no Chapter 6 recycling.

Yue's commentary about literary schools triggers a 3-line exposition block about Card 012: "The card described three ways a school could form..."

**Problem**: This is knowledge-card exposition dropped into the middle of a character moment. Yue said something profound; Feimo should sit with it, not immediately categorize it into a taxonomy.

**Fix**: Move the Card 012 content to the Study Break notes at the chapter's end. Let the narrative moment breathe.

#### P2 — "Smelled like" opening

Ch.7 L3: "The arena smelled like iron and electricity." This is fine in isolation, but the batch uses sensory-opening ("smelled like," "was underground") as a pattern. Low priority.

## Chapter 8: The Shijing Corridor

**Lines**: ~334 | **Est. Words**: ~5,800 | **Verdict**: ❌ Needs Revision (0 P0s, 3 P1s, 2 P2s)

### ✅ What Works
- **The strongest chapter in the batch.** The discovery scene — walking through the service corridor that transforms into something ancient — is genuinely atmospheric.
- The paintings described as "ink-wash scenes that seemed to grow out of the wall material itself, as if the images had not been applied but had emerged" (L29) — perfect.
- The Six Principles sequence (Fu, Bi, Xing speaking to Feimo, L61-65) is the best knowledge card embedding in these six chapters. The theory becomes experience, not exposition.
- Mia's fear (L87-88, "Mia looked afraid") is a powerful characterization beat — it tells us this corridor is serious without over-explaining why.
- The blank door at the corridor's end (L229-233) is perfectly disturbing. "The wall had been built *away* from it" is an excellent detail.
- The *Tap. Tap. Tap.* callback (L287) connects to Ch.1's mysterious knocking.

### Issues

#### P1 — L119-131: Philharmonic exam passage insertion breaks the spell

Mia suddenly introduces the Alan Gilbert / New York Philharmonic analogy:

> *"The Philharmonic hired a conductor named Alan Gilbert in 2009..."*

**Problem**: They are standing in an undiscovered corridor filled with 3,000-year-old Chinese paintings. Mia is afraid. The atmosphere is thick with mystery. And then Mia delivers a 12-line analysis comparing the corridor to an orchestra management crisis from a reading comprehension passage. This is the equivalent of a character in a horror movie stopping to cite a journal article about why haunted houses are structurally unsound. It kills the atmosphere dead.

**Fix**: Delete the Philharmonic comparison entirely. The earned-media analogy (L151-161) is more than sufficient for connecting the corridor to exam material. Let Mia respond to the corridor with *fear*, not with *analysis*. Her fear is more interesting than her exam knowledge, and we've already established that this place is beyond her framework.

#### P1 — L206-207: Aristotle laughter passage re-reference

In the Shijing Corridor, Mia connects the body-knowledge of the poems to the Aristotle laughter passage:

> *"Laughter works the same way... Aristotle called it a bodily exercise precious to health. The 2011 cloze passage demonstrated that laughter produces short-term physiological changes..."*

**Problem**: Third appearance of this passage (Ch.6 full teaching, Ch.7 reflection, now Ch.8 reference). The reader has gotten the message.

**Fix**: Cut the Aristotle reference. The Strack experiment connection (L187-191) is sufficient for the body-knowledge argument. Two exam callbacks in one corridor scene is one too many.

#### P1 — L297-316: End-of-chapter reflection is pure filler

After the narrative concludes (Feimo in bed, the knocking continues), there are **20 lines** of internal reflection that re-state everything already experienced:

- L297-298: Re-explains the Strack experiment (third mention across the batch)
- L299-303: Re-explains the parenting passage from Ch.9's exam prep
- L305-309: Re-explains the concertgoers/recording passage
- L309: *"No exam question could capture what she had felt tonight."* — This is the narrator telling us what the reader already feels.

**Problem**: The reader just lived this scene. They don't need a 20-line debrief explaining what they should have felt. The chapter's emotional impact is in the corridor scene itself; the reflection dilutes it.

**Fix**: Cut L297-316 entirely. End the chapter at L296 ("She did not sleep for a long time.") or with the dream sequence (L311-313), which is evocative and doesn't explain itself. The knocking continues — that's the ending.

#### P2 — "Not metaphorically" used twice

L7: "Not metaphorically. They breathed." and L83: "Not metaphorically, the way most classrooms at the Academy felt underground." Same rhetorical device, same chapter.

**Fix**: Change one instance. L83 could simply be "Not the way most classrooms felt underground."

#### P2 — "Earned media" analogy is clever but over-extended

L151-161: Feimo's "earned media" / "hijacked media" analogy runs 10 lines. It's a good connection, but it goes one step too far with "hijacked media," which adds a plot implication (the tradition pushing back against the Labyrinth) that should develop gradually, not be stated in a single analogy.

**Fix**: Keep the earned-media connection. Cut the "hijacked media" extension. Let the idea that the Shijing is pushing back against the Labyrinth emerge over chapters, not be stated in one conversation.

The battle ends at L153. The medical ward scene runs L213-265. Then the Study Break lesson begins at L269 and runs **uninterrupted** for 170 lines through L440. This is not a scene. It is a lecture.

**The core issue**: Mia is re-teaching passages the reader has already encountered in Ch.9, using the Three-Layer framework the reader learned in Ch.6. The reader is three steps ahead of the lesson for 170 lines.

**Fix**: **Cut the lesson to 60–80 lines.** Keep:
1. The credibility process connection (it's the chapter's thematic spine)
2. The audience-legibility point (practical and new)
3. The emotional "hugged" beat (the chapter's emotional payoff)

Cut:
1. Supreme Court passage (already in Ch.9)
2. Peer pressure passage (already in Ch.9)
3. Entergy passage (already in Ch.9)

5. Tang Poetry periods (move to a separate chapter or Study Break notes)
