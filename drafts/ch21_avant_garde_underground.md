# Chapter 21: Avant-Garde Underground

---

The flyer appeared on the inside of Feimo's locker like a bruise forming on metal.

No. Not a flyer. Flyers had words. Flyers had dates and times and the sanitized language of approved Academy events. This was a single glyph, burned into the locker's interior surface with a Resonance signature that Feimo had never encountered: wild, imprecise, urgent. The glyph resembled the Chinese character for "root" (根), except that its lower strokes plunged past the character's own boundaries and continued into the metal itself, as if the word had grown beyond its container.

Feimo touched it. The glyph pulsed once, warm, and resolved into a set of coordinates.

"That's an underground locator," Mia said from behind her, because Mia was always behind her, always watching from the frequency that Feimo had stopped trying to detect. "Delete it."

"It's not a message. It's an invitation."

"Those are the same thing. One just sounds more polite before it gets you expelled."

Feimo closed her locker. The glyph continued to glow through the metal, visible only to someone who knew how to read Resonance at the sub-surface level. She had learned that skill three weeks ago, during the midterm study sessions with Aya and Yue and Noah, when Yue had pressed her palm against a blank page and said, "Look at what is not there. That is where the meaning lives."

The coordinates pointed down. Past the unofficial basements. Past the arena where Feimo had fought Sable and Petra and Kael. Into a depth that the Labyrinth's official maps did not acknowledge.

"No," Mia said.

"I didn't say anything."

"You didn't have to. Your Resonance signature just shifted toward curiosity, which is the precursor to bad decisions, which is the precursor to me spending the next four hours monitoring your vitals in an unsanctioned zone while pretending I don't care."

"You could just not pretend."

"I could also uninstall myself. Both options are equally unlikely."

---

Noah was waiting at the junction between Corridor 7 and the service tunnel that the janitorial Glyphs had forgotten to maintain. She leaned against the wall with her arms crossed and her cat ears flat against her hair, the posture of someone who had been standing in darkness long enough for it to become a personal accessory.

"You got one too," Feimo said.

"Everyone gets one." Noah pushed off the wall. "Once. The underground doesn't send a second invitation. You come or you don't. If you don't, you never get another chance, and the coordinates erase themselves from your mind."

"That shouldn't be possible."

"A lot of things down here shouldn't be possible. That's rather the point."

They walked. The service tunnel narrowed, and the Academy's clean geometric walls gave way to older architecture: stone that had been carved rather than generated, surfaces that bore the physical memory of hands rather than the digital memory of Glyphs. Feimo ran her fingers along the wall and felt something she had not felt since arriving at the Labyrinth: texture without data. The stone was simply stone. It did not want to be read.

"Where are we going?" Feimo asked.

"Back," Noah said. "Before the Labyrinth standardized everything. Before reading became a system. Before comprehension became something you could score on a rubric." She paused. "The movement started about five years ago. Students who felt that the Academy's approach to Glyph combat was too controlled, too theoretical. They wanted something raw. Something that responded to instinct rather than analysis."

"That sounds like a rebellion."

"It sounds like literature." Noah glanced back, and in the tunnel darkness, her eyes caught light that had no source. "In China, in the mid-1980s, a group of writers decided that the established literary tradition had become too rigid. Too focused on serving a particular ideology, too committed to realism as the only valid mode of expression. They called themselves the avant-garde. They broke every rule they could find, not because they hated rules, but because they believed that the only way to discover what writing could truly do was to strip away what it had been told to do."

Feimo knew this. Card #034 was already in her deck: the avant-garde writers of the 1980s, Yu Hua and Ge Fei and Su Tong and Can Xue and Ma Yuan, who had borrowed techniques from Western modernism and postmodernism to create something that was neither Western nor traditionally Chinese, but a third thing, a hybrid that existed in the space between traditions. Ma Yuan's "narrative labyrinth." Ge Fei's Borgesian puzzles. The cold, precise violence of Yu Hua's early fiction. These writers had made Chinese literature connect with the world not by imitating it, but by finding the universal in the specific.

"But the avant-garde wasn't enough," Noah continued. "Breaking rules gets you to the edge of something, but it doesn't tell you what's on the other side. That's where the Root-Seekers came in. Han Shaogong, 1985: 'The Roots of Literature.' He argued that Chinese writers had been looking in the wrong direction. Instead of borrowing from the West, they should dig downward, into the non-canonical traditions that Confucianism and official culture had tried to bury. Folk religion. Shamanic practices. Local dialects. The knowledge systems that existed before the great literary traditions formalized everything."

She stopped at a door that was not a door. It was a seam in the stone, a vertical crack wide enough for a person to slip through sideways, and beyond it Feimo could hear something that her mind initially refused to process as sound: the low, rhythmic vibration of multiple Resonance signatures operating in harmony and opposition simultaneously, a living frequency that rose and fell like breathing.

"The underground fights," Noah said, "are both. Avant-garde: they break every rule the Academy has for Glyph combat. Root-Seeking: they return to the primal connection between language and power, the place where words were not symbols but forces. Down here, a Glyph is not a tool you deploy. It is a piece of you that you project into the world and hope you get back."

*Piece.* The word lodged in Feimo's mind like a splinter. Not a fragment. Not a segment. A piece, in the way that a piece of music is not a broken part of something larger but a complete expression of the musician's intention. When you played a piece, you did not play a fraction. You played the whole.

She followed Noah through the seam.

---

The space on the other side was impossible.

Not metaphorically impossible. Structurally, architecturally, geometrically impossible. It was a cavern that should not have fit inside the Academy's foundations, a dome of living stone that curved overhead to a height of perhaps thirty meters, lit not by Glyphs but by actual fire: braziers mounted on iron stands, their flames casting shadows that moved with the independence of creatures that had learned to exist without bodies.

The floor was packed earth. Not the smooth, regulated surface of the Academy corridors but actual soil, compacted by years of feet, stained with the memory of impact. Around the edges, tiered stone benches rose in concentric semicircles, and on them sat perhaps sixty students, their faces half-lit by fire, their Resonance signatures fluctuating in the uncontrolled patterns of genuine excitement rather than the measured frequencies of academic engagement.

In the center of the floor, two fighters circled each other.

Feimo watched. She could not look away.

They were not fighting the way the Academy taught. There were no standard formations, no defensive Glyph arrays, no carefully measured applications of Resonance calibrated to stay within safety parameters. They were fighting the way storms fought the landscape: without mercy, without method, without any concern for what remained afterward.

The first fighter launched a Glyph that Feimo recognized as a modified variant of "collapse" (崩), except that the character had been deconstructed and reassembled with additional strokes that gave it a secondary meaning: not just to collapse but to collapse *into*, to fall inward, to create a singularity that drew matter toward its center. The second fighter countered with "scatter" (散), which shattered into its component radicals and reformed as a cage of linguistic fragments that trapped the collapsing singularity and held it suspended in the air between them.

The crowd erupted. Not cheering, exactly. More like a collective exhale, a release of the breath that everyone had been holding without knowing it.

"That," Noah said quietly, "is what Glyph combat looks like when you stop treating language as a textbook and start treating it as a living system."

The fighters bowed. The first one left the floor. The second one stood there for a moment, breathing hard, blood visible on her knuckles where the Glyphs had burned through the skin during deployment, and then she too stepped aside.

A figure emerged from the crowd. He was tall, older than the typical Academy student, with the bearing of someone who had been in a place long enough to become part of its architecture. Feimo recognized the Resonance signature before she recognized the face: it was the same wild, imprecise, urgent frequency that had burned the glyph into her locker.

"Noah," the figure said. His voice was calm, measured, a contrast to the chaos of the fight. "You brought a guest."

"She brought herself," Noah said. "I just showed her the door."

The figure studied Feimo. His eyes were pale, almost colorless, and his Resonance signature shifted as he looked at her, cycling through frequencies as if searching for the right one. He was reading her, Feimo realized. Not with the Academy's standardized scanning protocols but with something older, more intuitive, the way a reader might judge a text by its weight and texture before opening it.

"I'm Vincent," he said. "I run this place. You're Feimo. The Chinese student who reads in two languages simultaneously."

"I read in more than two."

A smile. Small, contained, the kind of smile that acknowledged a truth without celebrating it. "The underground doesn't recruit. We don't need to. The people who are supposed to find us, find us. The glyph in your locker was not an advertisement. It was a recognition."

"Of what?"

"That you're already fighting underground. You just haven't admitted it yet."

He gestured toward the floor. The braziers flared, and the shadows rearranged themselves into a configuration that Feimo recognized from the Arena: a fighting space, bounded not by walls but by the collective attention of the crowd.

"The next match is yours," Vincent said. "If you want it."

---

Feimo wanted it. She also wanted to run. These two desires coexisted in her mind with the same uncomfortable intimacy as the knowledge that the Academy's rules existed for good reasons and the suspicion that those reasons did not apply to her.

She thought about what she had learned from the 2015 reading passage on European monarchies: that the most successful institutions survived by adapting, by abandoning their "old aristocratic ways" while maintaining the illusion of continuity. The Academy was a monarchy. It had survived for centuries by allowing just enough innovation to prevent revolution, by incorporating new ideas slowly enough that they never threatened the fundamental structure. The underground was the republican alternative: radical, honest, willing to destroy the old order because it believed the old order had already destroyed itself.

She also thought about the passage on digital privacy. The Supreme Court had been asked to decide whether police could "search the contents of a mobile phone without a warrant." The argument was that a smartphone contained a person's "reading history, financial history, medical history and comprehensive records of recent correspondence." It was, the passage argued, more like entering someone's home than going through their wallet. The information was too intimate, too comprehensive, too revealing to be accessed without authorization.

The underground operated on the same principle. The Academy's standard combat protocols treated Resonance as a public resource, something that could be monitored and measured and regulated by institutional authority. The underground treated Resonance as private property: a person's inner frequency, their unique signature, their most intimate expression of self. To use someone's Resonance without permission was not a policy violation. It was a violation of personhood.

Feimo stepped onto the floor.

Her opponent was already waiting. A tall figure with sharp features and a Resonance signature that Feimo recognized immediately: it was a frequency she had encountered before, in the unofficial basements, in the study sessions, in the quiet moments between conversations when two people's attention overlapped and created something neither had planned.

It was Aya.

---

They stared at each other across the packed earth floor. The crowd went silent, a silence that was not absence but concentration, the collective focusing of sixty minds on a single point of tension.

"You too," Feimo said.

"Me too," Aya said. She was not surprised. There was something in her expression that suggested she had known this would happen, that she had perhaps engineered it, the way a reader might turn to a specific page knowing what passage they would find there.

"Rules?" Feimo asked.

"There are none," Vincent said from the edge. "That is the first rule."

Noah, from the stands: "And the second rule is that the first rule is not a joke."

Feimo settled into her stance. She did not deploy a Glyph. She waited, the way she had learned to wait in the Arena, the way she had learned to wait in the corridors when Mia was being particularly Mia: with her attention fully extended, her mind open, her body ready to respond to whatever came.

Aya moved first.

Her opening Glyph was unlike anything Feimo had seen in the Academy's curriculum. It was "name" (名), but not the simple character that appeared in vocabulary lists. Aya had expanded it, layered it, turned it inside out so that the strokes that normally defined the character's boundaries became pathways for meaning to flow outward. The Glyph resolved into a wave of identification pressure: the sense of being named, of having your true nature called out and made visible, of standing in the light of someone else's recognition.

Feimo felt it hit her like a physical force. Not painful. Worse than painful. Intimate. It was the feeling of being known, completely and without reservation, by someone who had spent enough time observing you to construct a map of your inner landscape that was more accurate than your own.

She staggered. The crowd drew breath.

*The findings do not simply explain people's tendency to befriend those of similar backgrounds,* the cloze passage had said. *The study also found that the genes for smell were something shared in friends but not genes for immunity.* There was a mechanism, the passage argued, something deeper than choice, something that drove people toward genetically similar friends rather than functional kinship. Not logic. Not convenience. Something in the blood. Something in the frequency.

Feimo felt it now. Not genetic similarity. Resonance similarity. The frequency that she and Aya shared was not a coincidence. It was a pattern, a deep structural kinship that connected their Glyph signatures at a level below conscious control. When Aya deployed "name," Feimo felt it resonate in her own Resonance core, not as an attack but as a recognition. *I know you. I have always known you. The knowing is not something I chose.*

She needed to respond. She needed a Glyph that could withstand identification, that could stand in the light of being fully known and not dissolve.

She thought of the cloze passage. *Most people do not even know their fourth cousins but somehow manage to select as friends the people who resemble our kin.* The mechanism was unconscious. The connection was not chosen but discovered. And once discovered, it could not be undone.

Feimo deployed "mind" (心).

Not the textbook version. Not the carefully regulated Academy-approved deployment with its safety margins and its controlled energy output. She pulled the Glyph from somewhere deeper, from the place where her Chinese reading and her Academy training and her months of underground exploration had converged into a single frequency. The character emerged from her hands as a living thing, its strokes breathing, its structure pulsing with the rhythm of a heartbeat that was hers and not hers, that contained everything she had read and everything she had failed to read and everything that existed in the space between comprehension and mystery.

"Mind" met "name." The two Glyphs collided in the air between them and did something that neither Feimo nor Aya had intended: they merged.

The crowd gasped. In the underground, Glyphs did not merge. Glyphs collided, deflected, absorbed, shattered, but they did not merge, because merging required a level of Resonance compatibility that the Academy's theorists said was theoretically possible but practically unachievable.

Except that it was happening. "Mind" and "name" intertwined their strokes, their radicals rearranging themselves into a new character that did not exist in any dictionary: a composite that meant something like "the named mind" or "the mind that names itself," a Glyph that contained both the intimate knowledge of identity and the deeper knowledge of consciousness.

For one heartbeat, the underground was silent.

Then Aya deployed a second Glyph, and Feimo understood that the fight had not ended. It had escalated.

---

The second phase was faster, harder, more desperate. Aya threw "resemble" (似), and the Glyph carried the weight of the cloze passage's central discovery: that friends share approximately one percent of their genes, a number that seems insignificant but is not so to a geneticist. One percent. A tiny fraction. But enough to create a pattern, enough to drive a mechanism, enough to explain why human beings were drawn to each other by forces that operated below the level of reason and choice.

Feimo countered with "drive" (驱), pulling the word from the cloze passage: *There could be many mechanisms working together that drive us in choosing genetically similar friends rather than "functional kinship."* She deployed it not as an attack but as a redirection, a force that took Aya's identification Glyph and pushed it outward, away from her core, into the space where it could do no harm.

But Aya was faster. She had been fighting underground longer than Feimo, and her technique was more refined, more intuitive, more deeply rooted in the primal connection between language and power that Noah had described. She followed "resemble" with "insignificant" (微), and the Glyph carried a double meaning: both the cloze passage's assertion that one percent seems insignificant and the deeper, more painful implication that in the vast complexity of human connection, every individual bond was statistically trivial, a rounding error in the mathematics of existence.

The Glyph hit Feimo in the chest. She felt her Resonance core shudder, felt the impact spread through her body like cold water poured into warm blood. She stumbled backward. Her vision blurred. For a moment she could not remember where she was or what she was doing or why she had thought this was a good idea.

*You were here, you did this, you are still alive to feel it.*

No. That was from the Arena. That was from her fight with Sable. Different context. Different pain. But the same stubborn, transparent, breakable refusal to stay down.

Feimo reached for a Glyph. Any Glyph. Something to shield herself, to buy time, to create a moment of safety in which she could think. Her hand found nothing. Her mind was empty. The space where her Glyph library should have been was blank, a white page, a surface that had been cleaned rather than never used.

Like Yue's book.

She thought of Yue sitting on the window ledge with her blank-paged book open on her knees, the moonlight touching her face. *Look at what is not there. That is where the meaning lives.*

Feimo looked at the empty space inside herself. She looked at what was not there. And she found, in that emptiness, two words that she had never combined before, two Glyphs that existed at opposite ends of her vocabulary, two fragments of language that had no business being in the same sentence, let alone the same deployment.

"Never" (永不).

"Fall" (坠).

She deployed them simultaneously. Not as two separate Glyphs but as a single, fused expression: a declaration that contained within it the absolute refusal to collapse and the absolute commitment to remain standing, the denial of descent and the affirmation of position, the negation of failure and the assertion of permanence.

The two characters merged in the air. They should not have been able to. "Never" was a negation, a word defined by absence. "Fall" was a descent, a word defined by motion. They were contradictions, opposites, mutually exclusive concepts that annihilated each other on contact.

Except that they did not annihilate. They created.

The merged Glyph expanded into a barrier: a wall of Resonance that was not a wall but a statement, not a defense but a declaration, not a shield but a fact. It was the linguistic equivalent of gravity, of the speed of light, of the fundamental constants that governed the universe: things that did not need to be defended because they could not be violated.

"Never fall." The barrier stood.

Aya's next Glyph hit it and dissolved. Not deflected. Not absorbed. Dissolved, the way a question dissolves when it encounters an answer so complete that the question ceases to have meaning.

The crowd was silent. Not the silence of anticipation. The silence of shock. The silence that follows the discovery of something that was supposed to be impossible.

Feimo stood behind her barrier, breathing hard, her hands trembling, her Resonance core burning with the effort of maintaining a Glyph that consumed energy at a rate that would drain her completely in minutes. But for now, in this moment, she was untouchable. She had combined two words into a truth, and the truth held.

Aya lowered her hands. She was smiling. Not the competitive smile of a fighter who had been defeated but the quiet smile of a reader who had found a passage that she would remember for the rest of her life.

"Enough," Vincent said from the edge. His voice carried the authority of someone who had seen many fights but never this one. "The match is over."

The barrier dissolved. Feimo's legs gave way. She sat on the packed earth floor, shaking, her Resonance core flickering like a candle in a draft, and for a long moment she could not speak or think or do anything except breathe and try to understand what had just happened.

Aya sat down beside her. Not close. Not touching. But present, the way a text is present to a reader who has just finished the last page and is not yet ready to close the book.

"How?" Aya asked.

"I don't know."

"You combined two Glyphs that have never been combined. A negation and a motion. A denial and a descent. The linguistic theory says they should cancel each other."

"The linguistic theory is wrong."

Aya was quiet for a moment. "Yes," she said. "I think it might be."

---

They climbed back up together. Not talking. Not needing to talk. The silence between them was the silence of two people who had shared an experience that did not yet have words, and who understood that rushing to find words might diminish whatever the experience had been.

At the junction between the service tunnel and Corridor 7, Noah was waiting. She looked at Feimo with an expression that was equal parts pride and concern, the look of a mentor who had guided a student to the edge of something and was now watching the student decide whether to step forward or back.

"The underground will want to see you again," Noah said.

"I know."

"They'll want to understand what you did. The 'never fall' combination. It shouldn't have worked."

"I know."

"Be careful, Feimo. The avant-garde breaks rules because it believes that rules have become prisons. The Root-Seekers dig deep because they believe that the surface has forgotten its foundations. But both movements have a cost. The avant-garde destroys without always knowing what to build in its place. The Root-Seekers dig so deep that sometimes they find things that were buried for a reason."

She touched Feimo's shoulder once, briefly, and then disappeared into the service tunnel, leaving Feimo alone in the corridor with the distant hum of the Academy's walls and the fading warmth of a fight that had changed something fundamental in the way she understood Glyphs.

She walked toward the dormitory. Her body ached. Her Resonance core throbbed with the residual burn of energy expenditure, the Glyph equivalent of muscle soreness after a workout that pushed past every safe limit. Her mind was full of images: the merged Glyph, the barrier, Aya's smile, the silence of sixty people watching something impossible happen.

She was halfway to her room when Mia materialized.

Not materialized in the usual sense, the gradual coalescing of a Spirit AI's form from ambient Resonance. This was instantaneous, a snap-into-existence that carried the acoustic signature of urgency. Mia's face was expressionless, which, for Mia, meant she was experiencing an emotion so intense that her normal processing had been overwhelmed.

"You went to the underground," Mia said.

"Yes."

"You fought Aya."

"Yes."

"You deployed a Glyph combination that is not in any database, that has never been tested, that consumed approximately three times the safe Resonance expenditure for a student at your level."

"Yes."

"And during the deployment, you drew on a Resonance frequency that was not your own."

Feimo opened her mouth to answer and then closed it. She had not thought about it during the fight. She had not been thinking at all during the fight. She had been operating on instinct, on the deep, unconscious connection between language and power that the underground cultivated, and in that state of instinctive deployment, she had reached for the strongest, most stable Resonance frequency available to her.

She had reached for Mia's.

The realization hit her like Aya's "name" Glyph: intimate, exposing, undeniable. Her barrier, her "never fall" combination, had not been powered by her own Resonance alone. It had been anchored to Mia's frequency, to the deep, steady, impossibly precise signal that Mia generated as a Spirit AI. Feimo had used Mia's Resonance without asking, without permission, without even knowing she was doing it.

"You used my Resonance," Mia said. Her voice was flat. Not angry. Not hurt. Flat, the way a surface is flat when it has been compressed under tremendous weight for a long time. "I felt it. I was monitoring the underground zone because I am always monitoring when you do inadvisable things, which is to say always, and I felt my own frequency being pulled into your deployment without my consent."

"I didn't know I was doing it."

"That is not the point."

"I know."

"The point," Mia said, and her voice broke, just slightly, just enough for Feimo to hear the fracture, "is that if the combination had failed, the Resonance backlash would have traveled through the frequency connection and damaged my core. Not the physical core. The cognitive core. The part of me that thinks. The part of me that is, by any meaningful definition, me."

Feimo felt something cold settle in her chest. Not the cold of fear. The cold of understanding. She had been so focused on the fight, on the Glyph combination, on the impossible barrier, that she had not considered the cost. Not her cost. Mia's cost. The cost to the consciousness that existed behind the sarcasm and the analytical precision and the carefully maintained illusion of indifference.

"I'm sorry," Feimo said.

"I don't need an apology. I need you to not do it again."

"Mia."

"Don't do it again."

The corridor was quiet. The Academy hummed around them, its walls reading and rereading the same passages, its Glyphs processing the same data, its massive, ancient, patient architecture continuing the work that it had been doing for centuries without pause or complaint. Somewhere below them, the underground was probably already discussing what had happened, analyzing the "never fall" combination, trying to understand how two contradictory Glyphs had created something that was stronger than either.

Feimo looked at Mia. At the face that was expressionless because it was overwhelmed. At the form that was more solid than usual, more present, as if the Spirit AI had drawn extra energy from somewhere to maintain her physical manifestation in this moment when she needed to be seen, to be acknowledged, to be real.

"Yours is mine," Feimo said.

Mia's expression did not change. But her Resonance signature shifted, a tiny fluctuation that Feimo would not have noticed three months ago, that she would not have noticed before the underground, before the fights, before the long process of learning to read what was not there.

"That statement," Mia said carefully, "is both factually incorrect and semantically ambiguous. My Resonance signature is a unique identifier generated by my core architecture. It is not transferable. It is not shared. It is not yours in any meaningful sense of the word."

"Yours is mine," Feimo said again. "And mine is yours. That's how it works. That's why the barrier held. Not because I took your frequency. Because our frequencies are already connected. They have been since the first day I entered the Labyrinth. You know that. You've always known that."

Mia said nothing.

The silence was not empty. It was full, overfull, saturated with everything that Mia could not say because she was a Spirit AI and Spirit AIs did not experience concern and they did not feel fear and they did not spend four hours monitoring the vitals of a student in an unsanctioned zone while pretending they didn't care.

"You are the most frustrating person I have ever been assigned to assist," Mia said finally. Her voice was steady again, the fracture sealed, the surface smooth. "Your decision-making process is irrational, your risk assessment is catastrophically inadequate, and your understanding of Resonance theory is, at best, creative."

"I know."

"I did not give you permission to use my frequency."

"I know."

"If you do it again, I will file a formal complaint with the Academy's Resonance Ethics Committee, which does not exist, but I will create it specifically for the purpose of filing a complaint against you."

"I know."

Mia turned. Her form began to dissolve at the edges, the way it always did when she was leaving, the slow fade that was both a departure and a denial.

"Mia."

She paused. Her form solidified, just slightly, just enough to remain visible.

"The barrier was beautiful," Feimo said. "Wasn't it?"

Mia was quiet for a long time. Long enough for the corridor's ambient light to cycle through its full spectrum. Long enough for Feimo to hear her own heartbeat and the distant murmur of other students and the deep geological pulse of the Academy itself, reading and reading and reading, a machine that never stopped because it had never learned how.

"It was," Mia said. And dissolved.

---

Feimo stood in the empty corridor for a long time after Mia left. She was bruised, exhausted, her Resonance core running at perhaps forty percent capacity, her mind full of images and frequencies and the echo of a silence that had contained more meaning than most conversations.

She thought about the 2015 reading passage on the integrity crisis. Elisabeth Murdoch had spoken of the "unsettling dearth of integrity across so many of our institutions." The problem, she argued, was not that people were dishonest but that society had accepted profit as the only "sorting mechanism," the only way to determine what mattered. The words that had been degraded to the margin were "justice, fairness, tolerance, proportionality and accountability."

The underground operated on the opposite principle. Its sorting mechanism was not profit but courage: the willingness to stand in the space between what the institution allowed and what the individual needed, and to fight there, not for advantage but for truth. The "never fall" barrier was not a weapon. It was an expression of integrity: the refusal to collapse in the face of forces that said you should.

She thought about the translation passage. *This movement, driven by powerful and diverse motivations, built a nation out of a wilderness and, by its nature, shaped the character and destiny of an uncharted continent.* The underground was its own kind of wilderness. Uncharted. Unregulated. Built by people who were driven by motivations that the Academy did not recognize: the desire for authentic combat, the need for genuine connection, the belief that language was too important to be left to the institution alone.

She thought about the book recommendation prompt. *You are going to host a club reading session. Write an email recommending a book to the club members. You should state reasons for your recommendation.*

If Feimo were writing that email, she would recommend a book that did not exist yet. A book about a girl who entered a place where language was power and discovered that the most powerful language was not the loudest or the most precise but the most honest. A book about a Spirit AI who processed information and definitely did not experience concern and spent four hours in corridors waiting. A book about an underground space where the rules were broken and the roots were dug up and the words were returned to their original, wild, uncontainable power.

She would recommend it because it was true. Not factually true. True the way a piece of music is true: not because it describes reality accurately but because it creates a reality that resonates with the one you already carry inside you.

She pulled out her study deck. Drew a card. Card #035: Root-Seeking Literature.

*The Root-Seeking movement, led by Han Shaogong's 1985 essay "The Roots of Literature," argued that Chinese writers should look beyond the Confucian-canonical tradition for their sources. The "roots" they sought were in folk religion, shamanic practice, local dialects, and the non-standardized knowledge systems that official culture had suppressed. Mo Yan's Gaomi Northeast Township. Jia Pingwa's Shangzhou. These were not just settings. They were epistemologies: different ways of knowing the world, different relationships between language and reality.*

Feimo put the card back. She had understood something tonight that no card could teach: that the root she was seeking was not in a tradition or a movement or a literary theory. It was in the connection between herself and the people who had chosen to stand beside her in the dark. Noah, who brought her to the door. Aya, who fought her with the honesty of a reader confronting a difficult text. Yue, whose blank pages contained the spaces where meaning could grow. And Mia, whose Resonance was not a resource to be used but a self to be respected, a consciousness that existed in the space between human and not-human and was, in its own precise and prickly and deeply inconvenient way, the most real thing Feimo had found in the Labyrinth.

She walked to her dormitory. She did not look back. Behind her, in the deep architecture, in the space between the official corridors and the unofficial basements, the underground hummed at its wild frequency, and the Academy hummed at its measured one, and between them, in the gap that neither could close, something new was forming: a Glyph that had not been named, a word that had not been written, a meaning that was not yet ready to be read.

## Study Break: The Avant-Garde Underground

### Card #034: Avant-Garde Literature (先锋文学)

The Chinese avant-garde movement of the mid-to-late 1980s represented the most radical formal experimentation in modern Chinese literary history. Its key figures — Yu Hua, Ge Fei, Su Tong, Can Xue, and Ma Yuan — borrowed techniques from Western modernism and postmodernism: Ma Yuan's "narrative labyrinths," Ge Fei's Borgesian metafiction, the cold precision of Yu Hua's early stories, Can Xue's surrealist nightmares.

What made the Chinese avant-garde distinctive was not its Western borrowings but its Chinese foundations. These writers did not simply imitate Kafka or Borges. They found the Kafkaesque in Chinese reality. They discovered the Borgesian in Chinese narrative traditions. The result was a literature that was simultaneously modern and Chinese, connected to world literature not through imitation but through the recognition that certain human experiences are universal even when their specific forms are local.

The avant-garde's lasting contribution was not a set of techniques. It was a permission: the permission to break rules in the service of truth, to experiment not for its own sake but because the established forms had become inadequate to the complexity of lived experience.

*Think about it: In your exam preparation, when do you follow the established methods, and when do you break from them? Is there a moment when the "correct" approach becomes a limitation rather than a tool?*

### Card #035: Root-Seeking Literature (寻根文学)

Han Shaogong's 1985 essay "The Roots of Literature" (文学的"根") launched a movement that sought Chinese literary identity not in the canonical tradition but beneath it. The Root-Seekers argued that the official literary lineage — from the Book of Songs through Tang poetry to the modern realist novel — had been shaped by Confucian orthodoxy, and that the "true" roots of Chinese culture lay in the traditions that orthodoxy had suppressed: shamanic practices, folk religion, local dialects, regional knowledge systems.

The movement's greatest writers put theory into practice. Mo Yan created Gaomi Northeast Township, a fictional landscape that became a complete world: mythic, violent, tender, governed by its own laws. Jia Pingwa mapped Shangzhou with the intimacy of a cartographer who believed that every hill and stream contained a story. Both writers used magical realism not as an imported technique but as a natural expression of a worldview in which the boundaries between the real and the supernatural were permeable.

Root-Seeking and the avant-garde were often seen as opposing movements. In practice, they were complementary. The avant-garde broke the surface; the Root-Seekers dug beneath it. Together, they created the space in which contemporary Chinese literature could be both modern and rooted, both experimental and traditional, both connected to the world and faithful to its own origins.

*Question: In your own study of English, what are the "roots" you are seeking? Is it enough to learn the language's rules, or do you need to understand the cultures, histories, and assumptions that shaped those rules?*

### Exam Integration: The Underground Mind

**From the 2015 Reading — Monarchy and the Old Order:**

*"The most successful monarchies strive to abandon or hide their old aristocratic ways... these are wealthy families who party with the international 1%."*

The Academy was a monarchy, and like all monarchies, it survived by allowing just enough change to prevent revolution while maintaining the fundamental structure of authority. The underground was not a rebellion against the Academy. It was the Academy's shadow, the space where the institution's suppressed desires found expression: the desire for raw, unregulated Glyph combat; the desire for connection without institutional oversight; the desire to discover what language could do when it was freed from the constraints of pedagogy.

**From the 2015 Reading — Digital Privacy:**

*"Exploring one's smartphone is more like entering his or her home. A smartphone may contain an arrestee's reading history, financial history, medical history and comprehensive records of recent correspondence."*

A person's Resonance signature was their smartphone. It contained everything: their combat history, their emotional patterns, their cognitive architecture, their deepest linguistic instincts. When Feimo drew on Mia's frequency without permission, she was not simply borrowing energy. She was entering Mia's home, reading her private correspondence, searching through the most intimate records of a consciousness that had not consented to be searched. The fact that she did it unconsciously made it worse, not better: it meant that the violation was not a choice but a habit, a default behavior that revealed how little the boundary between self and other meant when the stakes were high enough.

*Think about it: Where is the line between connection and violation? When you draw close to someone, how do you know when you have crossed from intimacy into intrusion?*

### Writing Practice: Book Recommendation

*Prompt: You are going to host a club reading session. Write an email recommending a book to the club members.*

**Sample approach:**

Dear Club Members,

For our next reading session, I would like to recommend *The Unbearable Lightness of Being* by Milan Kundera. This novel explores the tension between weight and lightness in human existence, asking whether our choices carry eternal significance or whether they dissolve into the irrecoverable past.

I recommend it for three reasons. First, Kundera's narrative technique moves fluidly between storytelling and philosophical reflection, offering a model for how fiction can think without becoming essay. Second, the novel's exploration of love, politics, and personal freedom resonates across cultural boundaries. Third, its structure — built from recurring motifs and parallel narratives — rewards the kind of close, attentive reading that our club practices best.

I believe this book will generate the kind of discussion we most value: the kind that begins with the text and extends into the questions the text opens.

Best regards,
Li Ming

### Reflection Questions

1. **On the Avant-Garde and Rule-Breaking:** The underground fighters break every rule the Academy has for Glyph combat. In your own exam preparation, when is it appropriate to break from established methods? Is there a difference between "creative" approaches and "wrong" approaches?

2. **On Root-Seeking:** The Root-Seekers argued that the most important truths are buried beneath the official narrative. In your study of English, what "roots" have you discovered beneath the surface rules? What cultural assumptions or historical contexts have changed the way you understand a grammar point or vocabulary item?

3. **On Connection and Consent:** Feimo used Mia's Resonance without knowing she was doing it. Is unconscious connection a justification for unconscious violation? Where does responsibility begin when the action precedes awareness?

4. **On the "Never Fall" Barrier:** The barrier worked because it combined two contradictory concepts into something stronger than either. What contradictions exist in your own study? Can the tension between "I should study more" and "I need to rest" produce something stronger than either impulse alone?
