# Chapter 18: Lu Xun's Iron House

The seminar room in Bridge House smelled of old paper and anxiety.

Fourteen students sat in a rough semicircle, their notebooks open, their pens still, their faces arranged in the particular expression of people who had been told that what came next would be important and were determined to look like they already understood why. The window was open. April air drifted in, carrying the distant sound of someone practicing Glyph activation in the courtyard below: a rhythmic crackle, like static electricity arguing with itself.

Feimo sat between Mia and an empty chair. The empty chair belonged to Aya, who was late, which was unusual enough to register as a data point.

Professor Silence stood at the front of the room. She did not sit. She did not lean against the desk. She stood the way a column stands: vertical, load-bearing, necessary. In her left hand she held a single sheet of paper. In her right, nothing.

"The 2014 National Postgraduate Entrance Examination," she said, "is in fourteen days."

The room absorbed this information the way dry soil absorbs rain: immediately, completely, and with a slight cracking sound that might have been someone's knuckles or might have been the collective sphincter of fourteen students who had not studied enough.

"This seminar is not a review session," Professor Silence continued. "I am not here to teach you test-taking strategies. I am not here to drill you on cloze patterns or reading comprehension techniques. If you want those things, the review modules in Forge House are open every evening until midnight."

She placed the sheet of paper on the desk. Feimo could see, from her angle, that it was covered in Chinese characters. Not printed. Handwritten, in the precise, angular script of someone who had learned calligraphy young and never lost the habit.

"What I am going to do," Professor Silence said, "is read you a passage. And then we are going to talk about what it means to wake up inside a room you cannot leave."

---

She read it in Chinese.

The language filled the seminar room like water fills a glass: completely, inevitably, finding every corner and surface. Feimo's ears flattened. Not from discomfort. From recognition. The sounds were familiar, not because she had heard this particular text before, but because Chinese, spoken well, had a texture that English did not: a tonal architecture, a rising and falling that was not melody but meaning, the way a building's shape is not decoration but structure.

Professor Silence's voice was low, steady, and entirely without drama. She read the way a surgeon cuts: precisely, with the minimum force necessary, leaving nothing wasted.

"假如一间铁屋子，是绝无窗户而万难破毁的，里面有许多熟睡的人们，不久都要闷死了，然而是从昏睡入死灭，并不感到就死的悲哀。现在你大嚷起来，惊起了较为清醒的几个人，使这不幸的少数者来受无可挽救的临终的苦楚，你倒以为对得起他们么？"

The room was silent.

Professor Silence translated without being asked. "Suppose an iron room without windows, absolutely indestructible, and full of soundly sleeping people who will soon suffocate. But since they are in a state of deep slumber, they will not feel the sorrow of dying. Now if you make a great outcry, awakening several of the lighter sleepers, so that these few unfortunate people have to suffer the agony of irrevocable death, do you think you are doing them a kindness?"

She let the question hang.

"This," she said, "is Lu Xun. The preface to *Call to Arms*, his first collection of short stories, published in 1922. The man speaking is a friend of the narrator's. The iron room is China. The sleeping people are the Chinese people. The question is whether it is better to let them sleep or to wake them."

She paused.

"The examination will not ask you about Lu Xun. The examination will ask you about cognitive decline, about welfare policy, about legal reform, about Beethoven's dynamics. But every question on that test is asking the same question Lu Xun asked: is it better to know or not to know? Is awareness a gift or a cruelty? And if you wake up inside the iron room and see the bars, what do you do next?"

---

Mia's pen was not moving.

This was notable because Mia's pen was always moving. In fourteen weeks of seminars, lectures, and study sessions, Feimo had observed that Mia processed external information through a continuous stream of written notation: observations, corrections, counter-arguments, diagrams, and the occasional editorial comment in a shorthand that Feimo could not read. The pen was Mia's externalized cognition, the physical trace of her mental activity.

It was still.

Feimo glanced at her. Mia was staring at Professor Silence with an expression Feimo had not seen before. Not the usual analytical detachment. Not the faintly amused superiority. Something older. Something that lived in the same neighborhood as recognition.

"The 2014 cloze passage," Professor Silence said, "describes what happens to the brain as it ages. Memory fades. Mental clarity declines. We forget where we put the keys. We lose the name of an old acquaintance." She paused. "These are small losses. Individually, they are trivial. Collectively, they are the sound of the iron room filling with gas. The passage says: 'While seemingly innocent, this loss of mental focus can potentially have a damaging impact on our professional, social, and personal wellbeing.'"

Feimo wrote this down. Then she stopped writing, because the next thing Professor Silence said was unexpected.

"But the passage also says something hopeful. It says the brain needs exercise in much the same way our muscles do, and the right mental workouts can significantly improve our basic cognitive functions. Thinking is essentially a process of making connections in the brain. To a certain extent, our ability to excel in making the connections that drive intelligence is inherited. However, because these connections are made through effort and practice, scientists believe that intelligence can expand and fluctuate according to mental effort."

She looked at the room.

"This is not just neuroscience. This is Lu Xun's answer. The iron room is real. The suffocation is real. But the brain is not a fixed object. It is a system that can be trained, expanded, reshaped through effort and practice. The sleeping people in the iron room are not doomed by their nature. They are doomed by their inaction. And the person who cries out, who wakes them, is not cruel. They are offering the only thing that matters: the chance to exercise the mind before the air runs out."

"Professor," said a boy from Forge House, his hand shooting up, "is the iron room the exam?"

"The iron room," Professor Silence said, "is whatever you are inside and cannot see outside of. For some of you, it is the exam. For some of you, it is the Academy. For some of you, it is the assumption that your value is determined by a number someone else assigns to your performance." She looked at Feimo when she said this. Or Feimo thought she did. The professor's gaze was quick, surgical, and gone before Feimo could confirm it.

"For Lu Xun," Professor Silence continued, "the iron room was China in 1918: a civilization that had perfected the art of sleeping through its own suffocation. The May Fourth Movement, which Lu Xun's work both anticipated and shaped, was the outcry. The question was whether the outcry was mercy or cruelty."

---

Aya arrived seven minutes late.

She entered the seminar room without apology or explanation, which was her right as the top-ranked student in Bridge House but was also, Feimo suspected, a deliberate choice. Aya did not do things accidentally. Every lateness, every silence, every precisely calibrated word was a move in a game that Feimo could sense but not fully see.

She sat in the empty chair beside Feimo. Her notebook was already open to a blank page. Her pen was uncapped.

"Lu Xun," Aya said, quietly, to no one in particular.

"You know him?" Feimo asked.

"I know of him. My grandmother had his collected works. I was not allowed to read them until I was twelve, and by then I had already decided that Chinese literature was something that happened to other people." She paused. "I was wrong about that."

Professor Silence was still speaking. She was describing the context of Lu Xun's preface: the intellectual climate of 1918, the debate between those who believed in awakening the masses and those who believed that awakening without the power to change was simply cruelty with extra steps.

"The May Fourth Literary Revolution," Professor Silence said, "began in 1917 with Hu Shi's proposal for a literature in the vernacular. Before that, serious writing in China was done in classical Chinese, a language so distant from ordinary speech that it functioned as a barrier: a way of keeping the uneducated out of the conversation. Hu Shi argued that literature should be written in the language people actually spoke. Chen Duxiu went further, calling for a complete overthrow of the old forms. And then Lu Xun wrote 'A Madman's Diary' in 1918, and the argument was over. Not because he won it, but because he demonstrated it. You cannot argue against a text that has already changed the language it is written in."

Feimo thought about this. She thought about the Labyrinth, about the walls that responded to written text, about the difference between words that were locked in a formal system and words that breathed.

"What happened to the madman?" she asked.

The room turned to look at her. Questions in Professor Silence's seminars were rare. Students listened, took notes, and saved their confusion for the review sessions. Feimo had not asked a question in fourteen weeks.

Professor Silence did not seem surprised. "The madman," she said, "realized that the people around him were cannibals. That they had been eating people for thousands of years. That the entire system of Chinese civilization, its ethics, its rituals, its hierarchies, was organized around the consumption of human beings. He tried to tell them. He tried to wake them. The last line of the story is: 'Save the children.'"

"And did they save the children?"

"That," Professor Silence said, "is what the next thirty years of Chinese literature tried to answer."

---

After the seminar, Feimo went to the Library.

Not the main reading room, with its polished tables and its carefully indexed collection of approved texts. She went to the sub-level, the section that was not on any map, the section that Noir had shown her three weeks ago: a narrow corridor behind the periodicals rack, lined with shelves that were not shelved according to the Academy's classification system but according to a logic that Feimo did not yet understand.

The books here were different. Some were old, their spines cracked, their pages yellowed. Some were new, published within the last decade, but they had the look of books that had been read hard: coffee stains, underlined passages, margin notes in multiple hands. These were not display copies. These were working copies, the books that the Academy's faculty actually used when they were not performing the role of faculty.

Feimo found the Lu Xun shelf. It was small: seven volumes, bilingual editions, published by a press she did not recognize. She pulled down *Call to Arms* and opened it to the preface.

She read it in Chinese.

Not aloud. Not yet. She read it silently, her eyes moving across the characters with the particular speed of someone who had learned to read Chinese before she learned to read English, who had processed ten thousand characters before she processed her first alphabet, for whom the shapes were not symbols pointing at meaning but meaning itself, direct, unmediated, the way a face is not a symbol pointing at a person but the person themselves.

The words were old. A hundred years old. The language was not the Chinese she spoke at home with her parents, which was modern, colloquial, full of borrowed English words and half-finished sentences. This was literary Chinese, shaped by the classical tradition but broken open by the vernacular revolution, a language that was simultaneously ancient and new. Lu Xun wrote the way a surgeon operates: each word precise, each sentence necessary, each paragraph a controlled incision into the body of a culture that had been sleeping for centuries.

Feimo read the iron room passage again. And this time, she understood something she had not understood in the seminar.

The iron room was not a metaphor for China. The iron room was a metaphor for any system that was too complete to be questioned from inside. The Academy was an iron room. The examination system was an iron room. The hierarchy of Houses, the ranking of students, the allocation of prestige and resources according to metrics designed by people who had never been measured by them: all iron rooms.

And Feimo was inside one.

She closed the book. She sat on the floor between the shelves, her back against the wall, and she thought about what it meant to wake up. Not metaphorically. Literally. What did it feel like to go from sleep to awareness, from unconsciousness to the knowledge that the room you were in had no windows and no doors and the air was getting thinner?

It felt like the underground arena. It felt like the moment Petra's disorientation Glyph had hit her and the floor had rotated forty-five degrees and her spatial map had dissolved and she had been forced to rebuild it from sound alone. It felt like losing and then standing up and then fighting with her eyes closed because the visual world had become unreliable and the acoustic world was the only one she could trust.

It felt like reading.

She opened the book again. This time, she read aloud.

"假如一间铁屋子，是绝无窗户而万难破毁的——"

Her voice was quiet. The Library was empty. The shelves absorbed sound the way they absorbed light: completely, without reflection. But something happened that Feimo did not expect.

The wall behind her vibrated.

Not much. A tremor, barely perceptible, the kind of thing you would dismiss as a pipe settling or a door closing elsewhere in the building. But Feimo's ears were not average ears. She could hear the frequency: a low hum, just below the threshold of human perception, resonating at the exact tonal center of the Chinese syllable she had just spoken.

She stopped. The vibration stopped.

She spoke again. "——里面有许多熟睡的人们——"

The wall hummed. Not in response. In harmony. The vibration was not a reply to her voice but a continuation of it, as if the stone itself had learned to speak Chinese and was trying to complete the sentence.

Feimo's breath caught.

She kept reading. The wall kept humming. Sentence after sentence, the stone vibrated in sympathy with the Chinese text, its frequency shifting to match the tonal contours of Lu Xun's prose. The effect was not eerie. It was intimate. It was the feeling of being read back, of hearing your own words returned to you by something that was not supposed to be listening.

She read the entire preface. It took twelve minutes. The wall hummed for twelve minutes. When she reached the final line, the last word of Lu Xun's preface, the vibration faded slowly, like a bell losing its ring, and the silence that followed was not empty but full, the silence of a room that had just been listening very carefully and was now processing what it had heard.

Feimo sat still for a long time.

Then she heard footsteps.

---

Mia was standing at the end of the aisle.

Feimo did not know how long she had been there. Mia's face was unreadable, which was unusual, because Mia's face was normally a precisely calibrated instrument of expression: every eyebrow angle, every lip curve, every micro-adjustment of posture was a data point, a signal, a piece of information delivered with the efficiency of a well-formatted paragraph. Right now, all of those instruments were offline. Mia's face was blank. Not neutral. Blank. The difference was the difference between a page that has nothing written on it and a page that has been erased.

"You read Chinese," Mia said.

"Yes."

"You read Chinese like it's breathing."

Feimo did not know what to say to this, so she said nothing.

"Like it's not a translation. Like the words are already in your head and your mouth is just letting them out." Mia walked closer. She was not looking at Feimo. She was looking at the wall. "The wall was vibrating."

"You heard it?"

"I felt it. Through the floor. I was in the next aisle." She paused. "I came because I thought you were having a seizure. Then I heard the voice and I stopped walking and I listened and I realized it was you and it was not English and it was not Glyphs and it was not any of the languages the Academy teaches."

"It was Chinese."

"I know what language it was." Mia's voice had an edge to it now, the familiar sharpness that was her default setting, but underneath it something else was running, a frequency that Feimo could hear but not yet name. "What I don't know is why the wall was responding."

Feimo held up the book. "Lu Xun. *Call to Arms*. The iron room parable."

"I know the iron room parable. I read it in translation last year. The walls did not vibrate when I read it."

"Did you read it in Chinese?"

Mia stopped. Her eyes narrowed. Feimo could see the processing happening, the rapid assembly of hypothesis and counter-hypothesis, the analytical engine that Mia ran on spinning up to full speed.

"The walls respond to text," Mia said slowly. "We know this. The Labyrinth is built on text. Every wall, every corridor, every door is a textual structure. But the response has always been to the *content*, not the *language*. You can activate a wall in English, in French, in the old Glyph-script. The language is a vehicle. The meaning is the key."

"Unless it isn't."

"Unless what isn't?"

"Unless the language is part of the meaning. Unless Chinese is not just a vehicle for Lu Xun's ideas but part of the ideas themselves. Lu Xun wrote in the vernacular because the act of writing in vernacular Chinese was itself a political statement. The language was the message. If the Labyrinth is sensitive to content, and if the content includes the language, then reading Lu Xun in Chinese would activate something that reading him in translation would not."

Mia stared at her.

"That is either brilliant or insane," she said.

"Those are not mutually exclusive."

"They are in the Academy's grading rubric." But the corner of Mia's mouth moved. Not a smile. An acknowledgment. A data point she had not previously considered, now filed and indexed and stored for later analysis.

She sat down on the floor next to Feimo. Not close. Not far. The distance of two people who had been working in adjacent aisles for months and had not yet decided whether the adjacency was coincidence or choice.

"Read it again," Mia said.

"What?"

"The passage. The iron room. Read it again. In Chinese."

Feimo looked at her. Mia was not looking back. She was looking at the wall, her head tilted slightly, her ears (human ears, round, blunt, with no fur and no mobility and no Resonance sensitivity) angled toward the stone as if she could hear what the wall was hearing.

Feimo opened the book. She read the passage again. Her voice was steady. The wall hummed.

Mia listened. Her eyes were closed. Her hands were flat on the floor, palms down, as if she could feel the vibration through the stone. When Feimo finished, Mia opened her eyes and said, very quietly:

"I will never be able to do that."

"Do what?"

"Read like that. In a language that makes walls listen. I process text as data. You process it as sound. I see the structure. You hear the resonance. The difference is not skill. It is architecture. My brain is built for one kind of reading. Yours is built for another."

"That doesn't make mine better."

"I didn't say better. I said different." Mia paused. "The exam doesn't test for different. The exam tests for a specific set of cognitive functions: pattern recognition, logical inference, vocabulary deployment, syntactic parsing. The 2014 cloze passage says intelligence can expand and fluctuate according to mental effort. It says the brain needs exercise in much the same way our muscles do. It says the right mental workouts can significantly improve our basic cognitive functions."

She turned to face Feimo.

"But it doesn't say anything about the kind of intelligence that makes walls vibrate. The exam measures one kind of mind. The Labyrinth responds to another. And you have both, which means you are either the most advantaged person in this Academy or the most confusing."

"Can I be both?"

"You can be whatever you want. Just don't expect the grading rubric to understand it."

---

They stayed in the sub-level for two more hours.

Mia asked questions. This was not unusual; Mia was always asking questions. But these were different questions. Not the sharp, surgical, I-already-know-the-answer-and-I-am-testing-whether-you-do questions that Mia deployed in seminars and study groups. These were genuine questions, the kind that came from a place of not knowing, and Feimo understood, with the particular clarity of someone who could hear the emotional frequency of a voice, that Mia asking genuine questions was an act of trust.

What does Chinese sound like when you think? *Like water moving through a space that was shaped for it.*

Do you dream in Chinese or English? *Both. Sometimes in the same dream.*

When you read Lu Xun, do you hear him or do you see him? *I hear him. I always hear text before I see it. English too. But in Chinese, the hearing comes first and the seeing comes second, and in English, they happen at the same time, and the difference is the difference between a solo instrument and a duet.*

Mia wrote this down. Not in her usual shorthand. In full sentences, with punctuation, as if she were transcribing testimony for a court that had not yet been convened.

"The 2014 reading comprehension," Mia said at one point, her pen not stopping, "includes a passage about George Osborne's welfare reform. The key question is about 'signing on,' which means registering for unemployment benefits. The passage argues that the reform redefines the unemployed person: no longer someone who has earned the right to support through national insurance contributions, but a 'jobseeker' whose allowance is conditional on actively seeking work."

"I remember."

"The principle of British welfare is no longer that you can insure yourself against the risk of unemployment and receive unconditional payments if the disaster happens. The allowance is time-limited, conditional, one of the least generous in the EU." Mia looked up from her notebook. "The Academy does the same thing. Your value here is conditional. Your ranking is time-limited. Your access to resources is not a right you have earned but an allowance you must constantly justify. And the justification is always the same: actively seeking improvement. Actively seeking performance. Actively seeking the metrics that the institution has decided are the ones that matter."

"The iron room."

"The iron room. Except Lu Xun's iron room was at least honest about being a prison. The Academy calls it an opportunity."

Feimo thought about this. She thought about the cloze passage's claim that thinking is essentially a process of making connections in the brain. She thought about the connections she had made in the underground arena: between sound and movement, between loss and learning, between the official Academy and its shadow. She thought about the connections she had made just now, between Lu Xun's Chinese and the wall's vibration, between a language and a structure, between a voice and a stone.

And she thought about the reading passage's question: what motivated the chancellor? The answer was a passion to ensure fairness for taxpayers. But the real answer, the one the passage's author understood, was that fairness was a story the powerful told themselves to justify the systems they had already built. The iron room was fair. The iron room was structured. The iron room had rules that applied equally to everyone inside it.

The only unfair thing about the iron room was that it had no doors.

---

Aya found them at midnight.

The Library sub-level was technically closed after ten, but the locks on the periodicals corridor had been broken for at least three years, and nobody had fixed them because nobody officially acknowledged that the corridor existed. Feimo and Mia were sitting on the floor, surrounded by books, their notebooks open, their pens dry, their conversation having migrated from the exam to the nature of reading to the question of whether a text could be alive in the way that a person was alive.

Aya stood in the entrance to the aisle. She was still in her seminar clothes, which meant she had not gone back to her dorm. Her hair was slightly disarranged, which for Aya was the equivalent of another person showing up in pajamas.

"You were in the seminar," Feimo said.

"I was late. I heard the last twenty minutes." Aya walked closer. She was looking at the Lu Xun volumes on the shelf, not at Feimo. "My grandmother used to read *Wild Grass* aloud. My grandfather would leave the room. He said Lu Xun's prose made the furniture nervous."

"He wasn't wrong," Mia said.

Aya sat down. Not next to Feimo. Across the aisle, with a shelf between them, so that their conversation was mediated by books. This was Aya's spatial grammar: she communicated proximity through attention, not distance. She could be three feet away and more present than someone sitting shoulder to shoulder.

"I came to tell you something," Aya said. "About the seminar. About what I saw."

"Heard," Feimo corrected. "You said you heard the last twenty minutes."

"I heard the last twenty minutes. But I saw you leave the room. I saw the direction you went. And I followed, not because I was curious, but because I had a hypothesis and I wanted to test it." She paused. "The hypothesis was that the Lu Xun passage would affect you differently from the other students. That the text would not just be information for you. That it would be something else."

"What kind of something else?"

"I didn't know. That's why it was a hypothesis." Aya's voice had the particular quality it took on when she was being honest rather than strategic: flatter, less musical, more like the sound of a page being turned. "I've been watching you since the beginning of the term. Not closely. Not obsessively. But with the attention of someone who has been told she is the best reader in Bridge House and has met someone who might read differently enough to make 'best' a meaningless category."

Feimo did not know what to say.

"In the seminar," Aya continued, "Professor Silence asked the question about the iron room. Whether it is better to wake the sleepers or let them sleep. And you asked about the madman. You asked if they saved the children. No one else asked that. Every other student was thinking about the metaphor, about what the iron room represented, about how to deploy the reference in an essay. You were thinking about the children."

"Someone should."

"Yes. Someone should." Aya was quiet for a moment. "I came to tell you that I was wrong about you. Not wrong in my assessment. Wrong in my framing. I thought you were a rival. Someone whose abilities threatened mine. That is the logic of the ranking system: one person's gain is another's loss. Zero-sum. The reading passage from the 2014 exam talks about legal reform. It talks about restrictive guild-like ownership structures that keep outsiders out. The Academy is a guild. The Houses are guilds. And I have been operating as a guild member: protecting my position, guarding my territory, treating every capable person as a threat."

"And now?"

"Now I think you are not a threat. I think you are an example."

"An example of what?"

"Of what reading looks like when it is not optimized for the examination." Aya reached across the shelf and pulled down a volume. Feimo could see the spine: *Wild Grass*, Lu Xun's prose poetry collection, the book Aya's grandmother had read aloud. "The exam asks you to process text as data: extract information, identify arguments, parse syntax, select the correct answer from four options. That is one kind of reading. A useful kind. A measurable kind. But it is not the only kind, and it may not be the most important kind."

She opened the book. Her fingers found a page without looking, the way someone finds a familiar song on a playlist.

"There is a poem in here," Aya said. "About hope. Lu Xun wrote: 'Hope cannot be said to exist, nor can it be said to not exist. It is just like roads across the earth. For actually the earth had no roads to begin with, but when many men pass one way, a road is made.'"

She looked at Feimo.

"You are making a road," she said. "Not the road the examination measures. Not the road the Academy has paved. A different road. And I am watching to see where it goes."

---

They left the Library at one in the morning.

The corridors were empty. The Labyrinth's night mode had activated: the walls glowed faintly, a phosphorescence that was not light but the memory of light, the way a text is not meaning but the memory of meaning. Feimo walked between Mia and Aya, and the arrangement felt significant, though she could not have explained why.

Mia was talking about the translation section of the 2014 exam. She talked about it the way she talked about everything: precisely, with structure, with the unshakable conviction that every sentence was a building and every word was a load-bearing wall.

"There is a sentence about Beethoven," Mia said. "It says his habit of increasing the volume with an extreme intensity and then abruptly following it with a sudden soft passage was only rarely used by composers before him. The point is not the volume. The point is the contrast. The sudden shift from loud to quiet, from intensity to gentleness. That is what makes Beethoven Beethoven: not the power but the transition."

"Lu Xun does the same thing," Aya said. "The iron room passage is the intense volume: the image of suffocation, of sleeping people, of inevitable death. And then the very next paragraph is the soft passage: the narrator's friend saying, 'But you are one of the few who are awake.' The shift is not just stylistic. It is philosophical. It says: the situation is hopeless, and you are the kind of person who sees hopelessness clearly, and that clarity is itself a form of hope."

Feimo thought about this. She thought about the wall humming. She thought about the underground arena, where the transition from noise to silence was the signal that something had changed: a hit landed, a Glyph activated, a fighter fell. She thought about the exam, which would ask her to process text as data, to select answers from options, to perform a kind of reading that was measured and ranked and conditional.

And she thought about Lu Xun's friend, the one who had described the iron room, the one who had asked whether waking the sleepers was a kindness. Because that friend was wrong. Not about the iron room. About the sleepers. The sleepers were not helpless. They were not passive. They were choosing to sleep, the way people always choose the path of least resistance, the way a brain that is not exercised will atrophy, the way a system that is not questioned will persist.

The iron room was not indestructible. It was just comfortable. And comfort was the most effective prison of all, because it did not need bars. It needed only the absence of a reason to stand up.

"Beethoven's other point," Mia was saying, "is about freedom. The translation sentence says: 'Especially significant was his view of freedom, which, for him, was associated with the rights and responsibilities of the individual: he advocated freedom of thought and of personal expression.' Freedom is not the absence of constraints. Freedom is the presence of courage. The courage to think differently. The courage to express what you think. The courage to read in a language that makes walls listen."

"You're extrapolating," Aya said.

"I'm connecting. There's a difference."

"There is. And I think you might be right."

They reached the dormitory corridor. Mia's room was to the left. Aya's was to the right. Feimo's was straight ahead, at the end of the hall, the room she shared with the Labyrinth's persistent, unexplained attention.

Mia stopped walking. She looked at Feimo with the expression that was not neutral but blank, the expression that meant the instruments were recalibrating, that the usual precision was temporarily offline because something had entered the system that the existing parameters could not process.

"Fourteen days until the exam," Mia said. "You need to study."

"I know."

"You need to practice cloze patterns and reading comprehension strategies and translation techniques and essay structures."

"I know."

"You need to do all of those things because the exam is an iron room and the only way to survive it is to know its walls well enough to find the seams." She paused. "But after the exam. After the iron room. You should keep reading Lu Xun. In Chinese. In the Library. With the wall listening."

"Why?"

"Because there are two kinds of intelligence. The kind the exam measures, which is the kind that helps you survive inside systems. And the kind the Labyrinth responds to, which is the kind that helps you see through walls. You need both. Most people only get one."

She turned and walked to her door. She did not look back. This was Mia's version of an emotional statement: deliver the payload, leave the blast radius, do not linger to observe the impact.

Aya was still standing at the other end of the corridor. She raised one hand, a gesture that was not quite a wave and not quite a salute. The gesture of one reader to another, acknowledging the shared weight of a text they had both carried out of the Library and would carry into the exam and beyond.

Then she was gone.

Feimo stood alone in the corridor. The walls were quiet. The Labyrinth was in its night mode, patient, waiting, its vast textual architecture humming at frequencies too low for human ears. But Feimo was not human ears. She could hear it: the faint, continuous vibration of stone processing meaning, of walls reading themselves, of a structure that was not alive in the way people were alive but was not dead in the way buildings were dead.

She thought about the ending of Lu Xun's preface. The part where the narrator says he agreed to write because even though hope cannot be proven to exist, the act of writing is itself a form of hope. You write not because you know the sleepers will wake. You write because the alternative is silence, and silence is the iron room's native language.

The exam would end. The Academy would rank her. The system would process her through its machinery and assign her a number and slot her into a position and tell her, in the precise and impersonal language of institutional assessment, who she was and what she was worth.

But the wall would still hum when she read Chinese. The Labyrinth would still listen. And somewhere in its deep architecture, in the space beneath the official corridors and the unofficial basements, something was waking up.

Not knocking. Not yet.

But listening.

## Study Break: Lu Xun's Iron House

### Card #028: Lu Xun's Fiction

Lu Xun (1881-1936) is the founding father of modern Chinese literature. His significance is not merely literary; it is civilizational. He did not just write stories. He invented a way of seeing.

His two major collections, *Call to Arms* (*Nahan*, 1923) and *Wandering* (*Panghuang*, 1926), established the vocabulary of modern Chinese fiction. "A Madman's Diary" (1918) was the first major work written in the modern vernacular, and its final line, "Save the children," became one of the most quoted sentences in twentieth-century Chinese literature. *The True Story of Ah Q* gave the world the concept of the "spiritual victory method" (精神胜利法): the habit of reframing defeat as triumph, of finding comfort in delusion, of sleeping through one's own suffocation and calling it peace.

But Lu Xun was not only a fiction writer. His prose poetry collection *Wild Grass* (*Yecao*, 1927) is perhaps his most personal work: a series of meditations on hope, despair, and the courage to continue when the rational response would be to stop. The tension between hope and hopelessness runs through everything he wrote. He was never optimistic. He was never despairing. He was something harder than both: honest.

For exam purposes, remember the key texts, the key themes (national character critique, the awakening metaphor, the "spiritual victory method"), and the distinction between his fiction (systematic, observational, almost clinical) and his prose poetry (intimate, symbolic, emotionally raw). Both modes were essential. Both modes were Lu Xun.

*Think about it: Which mode are you in when you study? Are you the clinical observer, dissecting passages and extracting information? Or are you the raw reader, letting the text affect you before you analyze it? The best students, like Lu Xun, can do both. But they have to learn to switch.*

### Card #029: The May Fourth Literary Revolution

In 1917, two articles changed Chinese literature forever. Hu Shi's "Some Modest Proposals for the Reform of Literature" (《文学改良刍议》) argued for writing in the vernacular (白话) instead of classical Chinese (文言). His "eight points" were practical: write with substance, do not imitate the ancients, use proper grammar, do not moan without cause, eliminate clichés, do not use obscure allusions, do not chase parallelism, do not avoid common words.

Chen Duxiu's "On Literary Revolution" (《文学革命论》) was less modest. It called for the destruction of the old literary aristocracy and the creation of a new literature that served the people, not the scholars.

Lu Xun's "A Madman's Diary" (1918) proved that the revolution was not just theoretical. It was possible. It had already happened. The story was written in the language people spoke, about the things people feared, in a form that people who had never read a novel before could understand.

The May Fourth Movement (1919) was the political explosion that the literary revolution had anticipated. Student protests, strikes, the rejection of the Treaty of Versailles, the birth of Chinese nationalism as a modern force: all of it was already present, in embryonic form, in the decision to write in the language of the street instead of the language of the temple.

For exam purposes, the key distinction is between the **content** of the revolution (new themes, new subjects, new ways of seeing) and the **form** of the revolution (vernacular language, free verse, short stories instead of classical genres). Both mattered. The content without the form would have been preaching. The form without the content would have been empty experimentation. Together, they remade a civilization's relationship with its own words.

*Think about it: The 2014 examination is written in a specific formal register: academic English, standardized syntax, controlled vocabulary. It is the modern equivalent of classical Chinese: a language designed to test, not to communicate. When you take the exam, you are translating your thoughts into a formal register. When Feimo reads Lu Xun in Chinese, she is translating her thoughts into a living register. Both are valid. But they test different things. Which one reveals more about who you actually are?*

### Exam Integration: The Iron Room and the Examination Hall

**From the 2014 Cloze:**

*"Neuroscientists, experts who study the nervous system, are increasingly showing that there's actually a lot that can be done. It turns out that the brain needs exercise in much the same way our muscles do, and the right mental workouts can significantly improve our basic cognitive functions. Thinking is essentially a process of making connections in the brain."*

The iron room parable assumes that the sleepers are doomed. That the room is indestructible. That the air will run out. But the cloze passage offers a counter-argument: the brain is not a fixed object. It can be trained. Connections can be built. Intelligence can expand.

If the sleepers in the iron room had been exercising their minds, building connections, expanding their cognitive functions through effort and practice, they might not have been sleeping at all. The iron room's real prison was not the walls. It was the assumption that thinking was optional.

In the Academy, the same logic applies. Students who treat the exam as a fixed obstacle, a wall that cannot be changed, are sleeping. Students who treat it as a cognitive workout, a chance to build new connections, are awake. The difference is not talent. It is effort. And effort, as the cloze passage reminds us, is a choice.

**From the 2014 Reading, Text 1:**

*"The principle of British welfare is no longer that you can insure yourself against the risk of unemployment and receive unconditional payments if the disaster happens. Instead, the claimant receives a time-limited 'allowance,' conditional on actively seeking a job; no entitlement and no insurance."*

Lu Xun's iron room is unconditional. You are inside it whether you consent or not. The Academy's system is conditional: your ranking, your resources, your status are all "allowances," contingent on performance. This makes the Academy, in one reading, more fair than the iron room: at least here, effort is rewarded. But in another reading, it makes the Academy more insidious: because the conditions are set by the institution, and the institution's definition of "actively seeking" may not include the kind of seeking that actually wakes you up.

Feimo reading Lu Xun in Chinese, making the walls vibrate, was not an activity the Academy measured. It was not on the exam. It was not in the rubric. It was, by the institution's standards, worthless. And yet it was the most real thing that had happened all day.

**From the 2014 Reading, Text 2:**

*"Except in the District of Columbia, non-lawyers may not own any share of a law firm. This keeps fees high and innovation slow. There is pressure for change from within the profession, but opponents of change among the regulators insist that keeping outsiders out of a law firm isolates lawyers from the pressure to make money rather than serve clients ethically."*

The guild structure of the legal profession mirrors the guild structure of the Academy. Houses are guilds. Professors are guild masters. The examination is the guild's certification process. And the argument for maintaining the structure is always the same: purity. Keep outsiders out to preserve standards. Keep non-lawyers from owning firms to preserve ethics. Keep unorthodox readers from the curriculum to preserve academic rigor.

But the 2014 passage also argues that "allowing non-lawyers to own shares in law firms would reduce costs and improve services to customers." Innovation requires openness. The iron room was indestructible because it was closed. The Academy's walls, for all their Resonance, might be more fragile than they appeared.

**From the 2014 Translation:**

*"One could interpret much of the work of Beethoven by saying that suffering is inevitable, but the courage to fight it renders life worth living."*

This is Lu Xun's answer to his own question. The iron room is real. The suffocation is real. The sleepers may not wake. But the courage to cry out, to read aloud, to make the walls listen, is what renders the iron room bearable. Not escapable. Bearable. There is a difference. Escape is a physical act. Bearing is a spiritual one. Lu Xun did not escape China. He bore it. He wrote about it. He made it audible.

And in doing so, he proved that the iron room was not as indestructible as it seemed. Because a room that can hear a voice is a room that has ears. And a room with ears is a room that is not entirely sealed.

**From the 2014 Writing:**

The writing prompt asks you to suggest how to improve students' physical condition. The obvious answer is exercise, nutrition, rest. The less obvious answer is this: physical condition is inseparable from mental condition. A student who sleeps through the iron room is not just intellectually inactive. They are physically deteriorating. The body follows the mind. When the mind is awake, the body has a reason to move. When the mind is asleep, the body follows it into the dark.

Lu Xun's friend asked whether waking the sleepers was a kindness. The better question is whether sleeping is a kindness to yourself. The answer is no. Sleep, in the iron room, is not rest. It is surrender. And the body that surrenders does not recover. It declines.

The 2014 cloze passage says the brain needs exercise. The 2014 writing prompt says the body needs care. Lu Xun says the spirit needs courage. All three are saying the same thing: the iron room is not the problem. The sleep is the problem. And the cure for sleep is not comfort. It is effort.

### Vocabulary Glyphs

**end** /ɛnd/ — *n.* The final point of something. The limit. The boundary. In the iron room parable, the end is suffocation: the point where the air runs out and the sleepers die. But Lu Xun's preface does not end with suffocation. It ends with the narrator agreeing to write. The end of the preface is a beginning. This is Lu Xun's deepest insight: that endings are not conclusions. They are transitions. The exam has an end. The Academy has an end. The Labyrinth, presumably, has an end. But none of these ends are the actual end. They are thresholds: points where one kind of experience stops and another starts. The question is not whether things end. The question is what begins when they do.

**example** /ɪɡˈzæmpl̩/ — *n.* A thing characteristic of its kind; a specimen. Aya called Feimo an example: not of excellence, but of a different kind of reading. In the 2014 exam, the reading passage about archaeological sites begins with examples: the Parthenon, the Pyramids, Stonehenge. These are not just illustrations. They are arguments. Each example says: *this is possible; this exists; this has been done before.* When Aya called Feimo an example, she was not giving a compliment. She was making an argument. She was saying: *this kind of reading exists. This kind of mind is possible. Here is the evidence.*

**explain** /ɪkˈspleɪn/ — *v.* To make something clear by giving a detailed account. Professor Silence explained Lu Xun. Mia explained the exam's logic. Aya explained her change of mind. But the wall did not explain itself. It hummed. It vibrated. It responded to Chinese in a way that no one could yet explain. This is the gap between explanation and experience: the exam measures your ability to explain, but the Labyrinth measures your ability to experience. Both are forms of understanding. But they are not the same kind of understanding. The person who can explain the iron room is not the same as the person who can hear the wall humming. And the person who can do both is the one the Labyrinth is listening for.

### Reflection Questions

1. **On waking and sleeping**: Lu Xun's friend argues that waking the sleepers in the iron room is cruel, because they will suffer the knowledge of their impending death. Professor Silence argues that the real cruelty is letting them sleep. Which position do you agree with? In your own preparation for the exam, are you awake or asleep? What would it take to wake you up?

2. **On language and resonance**: Feimo's reading of Lu Xun in Chinese makes the wall vibrate. Mia's reading of the same text in English does not. Is there something inherent in a language that cannot be translated? In your own study, are there concepts that only make sense in Chinese? Are there concepts that only make sense in English? What is lost when you move between them?

3. **On guilds and innovation**: The 2014 reading passage argues that restrictive ownership structures keep fees high and innovation slow. The Academy operates on the same logic. Can you think of ways that the examination system, which is designed to measure ability, might actually be preventing the development of new kinds of ability? Is the exam a window or a wall?

4. **On endings**: Lu Xun's preface ends with an agreement to write, which is a beginning. The exam has a fixed end: three hours, a number of questions, a score. How do you prepare for an ending that is also a beginning? What does it mean to study not just for the exam, but for what comes after it?
