# Chapter 42: Silence's Secret

The professor's study was on the fourth floor, at the end of a corridor that smelled of pipe tobacco and old paper. Feimo had been there only twice before: once to deliver a set of annotated translations, once to return a borrowed volume of Kafka's diaries. Both times, the room had struck her as the private space of a man who had arranged his life around the avoidance of visitors. The desk was clear. The shelves were organized not by author or subject but by some principle that only the professor understood, a taxonomy of silence. The books stood in their rows like patients in a waiting room, each one holding its place with the discipline of something that had been taught to wait.

There was a window. Through it, Feimo could see the courtyard, where the reconstruction crew had stacked damaged stone in neat piles. The rain had stopped, but the sky was low and grey, the kind of sky that made the building feel smaller than it was, as if the clouds were pressing down on the Labyrinth's roof with the weight of unfinished business.

Professor Silence sat behind his desk.

He was writing. This was how he always communicated: in the precise, unhurried hand of a man for whom each character was a small act of construction. His handwriting was unusual for a Chinese speaker trained in the classical tradition. The strokes were deliberate but not calligraphic, the work of someone who knew the language well enough to write it without hesitation but did not regard the writing as art. Feimo had seen this handwriting before. On the door at Level 8. On the carved inscription in the maintenance shaft.

She had not connected them until now.

The professor looked up. His eyes were steady, the eyes of a man who had made a decision and was now executing it with the mechanical calm of someone who has rehearsed this moment many times. He pushed a sheet of paper across the desk.

Feimo sat down and read.

---

*I have asked you here because the time has come to tell you something that I have told no one. I have observed you, Feimo. You listen the way a translator reads: not for what is said but for what is meant. This is why I have chosen you.*

*I am going to tell you what I am. And what Mia is.*

*I am the Labyrinth's voice. Mia is its memory. We were once one.*

*Not one body. One system. The Labyrinth thinks in two languages and stores its thoughts on walls, but it also thinks through people. I am the part that speaks. Mia is the part that remembers. Twenty-three years ago, we entered a room that separated us. It took my voice. It took her memory. It left me able to write but not persuade, and it left her able to read but not recall. The Labyrinth has been incomplete ever since.*

*There are seven Labyrinths. Not seven levels — seven Labyrinths, each one a structure of knowledge in a different language. This one, the bilingual Labyrinth, is the largest. The Russian Labyrinth is the deepest. The Chinese Labyrinth, which this building was built to contain, is the oldest. I found its door twenty-three years ago, and what I found inside changed everything.*

Feimo looked up. The professor was watching her. His right hand trembled — a slight, rhythmic vibration, as if the hand were keeping time with a signal too faint for anyone else to detect. She had never seen this before.

She looked back at the paper.

*The door was on the third floor, behind a shelf. A wooden panel with an inscription in the same handwriting as the Level 8 door: "此门通向词语的根。" This door leads to the root of words. Beyond it: a passage covered in Chinese text — classical, then modern, then English emerging, the two languages interweaving. At the end: a second door with a second inscription. "The one who enters will lose what he values most."*

*I opened it anyway. The room beyond was the Labyrinth's engine: three meters by three, walls covered in moving text, characters shifting and rearranging. A table. A paper. A pen. "Sign to enter. Sign to leave."*

*I signed. The room took my voice — not my speaking voice, but my ability to use language as a tool of persuasion. I could state facts. I could teach. I could translate. But I could no longer argue, no longer make people do what I wanted. The room took the part of my language that served power and left the part that served truth.*

Mia had been his student. Not in the formal sense, not enrolled in a course or assigned to a thesis, but in the older sense: an apprentice, a disciple, a young person who attached herself to an older one because she recognized in them a knowledge that she needed and could not find elsewhere. Mia had come to the Labyrinth at nineteen, already fluent in three languages, already carrying the burden of a memory that she could not fully access. The sealed memories were not new. They had been there since childhood, fragments of a life before the Academy that surfaced sometimes in dreams and then receded, leaving behind a residue of unease that Mia managed with the discipline of someone who had learned early that weakness was a luxury she could not afford.

The professor had recognized Mia's condition because he had seen it before. In himself. The room had taken his voice. It had taken Mia's past. The mechanism was the same: the Labyrinth's engine demanded a price for access, and the price was always the thing you could least afford to lose.

He had not told Mia about the door. He had not planned to take her there. But Mia had found the passage on her own, the same way the professor had: through the routine work of the Labyrinth, through the slow, patient accumulation of knowledge that eventually reveals the structure beneath the surface. She had come to him one evening and said, in her flat, analytical voice: "I found a door. It has writing on it. I think you should see it."

They went together.

The room was the same. The moving text, the table, the paper, the pen. The inscription: *Sign to enter. Sign to leave.* The professor signed first. He felt the change immediately: a settling in his throat, a thickening, as if the air itself had become denser. He tried to speak and found that the words came out wrong. Not garbled, not inaudible, but stripped of their persuasive force. He could say: "The door is open." He could not say: "You should go through it." The distinction was precise and absolute. The room had not taken his language. It had taken his authority.

Mia signed.

What happened to Mia was different. She did not lose her voice. She did not lose her ability to argue or persuade or command. What she lost was more fundamental: she lost the connection between her present self and her past. The sealed memories, those fragments of a life before the Academy that had always been partially accessible, became fully sealed. The door that Kafka had described, the door that was made only for you, closed. The doorkeeper, who had always been there, became absolute.

Mia walked out of the room and could not remember how she had gotten there.

The professor wrote:

*She looked at me and said: "Who are you?"*

*I wrote my name on a piece of paper and showed it to her. She read it, and something in her face shifted, a recognition that was not quite memory but was not quite forgetting either. She knew my name. She did not know why she knew it. The connection between the name and the person had been severed, and what remained was a residue: a feeling, a familiarity, the ghost of a relationship that the room had dissolved.*

*I led her back through the passage. She followed without speaking. When we reached the main Labyrinth, she stopped and looked at the walls, at the bilingual inscriptions, at the text that covered every surface. She read the Chinese characters and the English words and her face was the face of someone seeing a landscape for the first time, a landscape that should have been familiar but was not, that carried the weight of recognition without the substance of memory.*

*She said: "This place knows me."*

*She did not say it again.*

---

Feimo read the pages and thought of the 2023 reading section.

Text 4 had been about citation cartels. About the way journals, authors, and institutions conspire to inflate citation numbers, creating a system where the measure of knowledge becomes more important than the knowledge itself. The passage had described a particular kind of fraud: support service consultancies that provide language and editorial support to authors, sometimes advising contributors to add a number of citations to their articles. The fraud was not in the citations themselves but in the system that made citations valuable. The careers of scientists and the reputation of their institutions depended on the number and prestige of the papers they produced, but even more so on the citations attracted by these papers.

The professor's room was a citation cartel of one. He had signed a document that inflated the value of his knowledge by stripping away the part of his language that could be used to challenge, to argue, to dissent. What remained was citation: the ability to state, to reference, to point to the work of others. He could quote Eliot. He could translate Kafka. He could annotate Borges. But he could not stand in front of a room and say: "This is wrong, and here is why." The room had taken his critical voice and left him with his scholarly one.

And Mia. Mia's sealed memories were a different kind of citation fraud. The system had not inflated her citations. It had erased them. The connections between her present knowledge and its sources, between what she knew and how she knew it, had been severed. She could still read. She could still analyze. But the provenance of her understanding, the chain of experience and learning that connected a fact to the person who had learned it, was gone. She was a paper without a bibliography. A text without a tradition.

The passage's final advice was practical: when an editor, a reviewer, or a support service asks you to add inappropriate references, do not oblige and do report the request to the journal.

The professor had obliged. He had signed. And now he was reporting, twenty-three years too late, to the only person who might understand what the signature had cost.

---

"You said Mia was with you," Feimo said. "Does she remember any of this?"

The professor wrote:

*She remembers nothing of the room. She remembers nothing of the passage, nothing of the door, nothing of the inscription. What she retains is a feeling: a sense that the Labyrinth is familiar, that its walls carry something she once knew, that the Chinese characters are not foreign but ancestral. This feeling is the residue of the sealed memories, the ghost of a connection that the room severed but could not erase entirely.*

*She has been trying to recover the memories since the day she lost them. Every visit to the lower levels, every hour spent reading the walls, every translation she completes is an attempt to rebuild the bridge that the room destroyed. She does not know this. She believes that her inability to access her past is a flaw in herself, a failure of memory, a weakness. She does not know that it is a wound inflicted by a room that demanded a price and took it without asking.*

*This is why I am telling you now. Because the Labyrinth is changing. The Fragmenters have damaged the walls. Cain has sealed the lower levels. The knowledge that was once accessible is now restricted. And Mia's memories, those sealed fragments that have been slowly surfacing for twenty-three years, are at risk. If the Labyrinth's system is further disrupted, the seals may harden. The door may become permanent. The memories may be lost entirely.*

*I cannot stop this. I have tried. I have written letters to the administration. I have submitted proposals for a more open approach to the Labyrinth's architecture. I have argued, in writing, that the security measures are counterproductive, that they treat the symptom rather than the disease, that the real threat to the Labyrinth is not the Fragmenters but the institution's response to them. No one has listened.*

*I cannot **respond** to this crisis in the way that a crisis demands. I cannot stand in front of the administration and make them hear. The room took that from me. All I can do is write, and hope that someone reads what I have written and understands.*

Feimo looked at the word **respond**. The professor had underlined it. She thought of the vocabulary from the 2023 exam: respond, seek, service. Three words, each one a thread in the fabric of the professor's story. He could not respond with his voice. He had spent twenty-three years seeking a way to undo what the room had done. And his service to the Labyrinth, his decades of teaching and cataloguing and translating, had been performed in the silence that the room had imposed on him.

She thought of the reading passage about the publishing industry. About Penguin Random House and Waterstones, about the dispute between a publisher and a retailer, about the way power operates through the control of distribution. The passage had said that mid-list authors, who normally rely on staff's passion for promoting books by lesser-known writers, were the ones who suffered most from the dispute. The big names could survive. The small voices could not.

The professor was a mid-list author. His knowledge was vast, his dedication absolute, but his ability to promote his own work, to advocate for his own understanding of the Labyrinth, had been crippled by the room's intervention. He could write, but he could not publish. He could teach, but he could not persuade. He was a voice in a system that valued volume over precision, and his volume had been turned down to a whisper.

"Who else knows?" Feimo asked.

The professor wrote:

*No one. You are the first. In twenty-three years, I have told no one. This is not because I am ashamed, although I am. It is because the telling requires a listener who can understand that the loss was not a failure but a transaction. I entered the room. I signed the document. I received something in return.*

*What did you receive?* Feimo wanted to ask. But the professor was already writing.

*I received knowledge. The room showed me the Labyrinth's true architecture — the one behind the walls and the bilingual inscriptions. I saw the connections between the texts, the patterns that link Eliot to Dante to Kafka to the anonymous writers of the Chinese classical tradition. I saw the Labyrinth not as a building but as a mind, a vast, distributed intelligence that thinks in two languages and stores its thoughts on walls. I saw the engine that generates the knowledge the walls carry, and I understood how it works and why it sometimes fails.*

*This knowledge has been the foundation of everything I have done since. Every paper I have published, every lecture I have given, every student I have taught has been shaped by what the room showed me. The price was my voice. The compensation was understanding.*

*I do not know if the transaction was fair. But I know this: the room did not take something from me. It revealed what I was. I was the Labyrinth's voice before I entered that room. The room simply made the role permanent. And Mia was the Labyrinth's memory before she entered. The room sealed it. We were once one — one system, one function — and the room separated us into two incomplete people. The Labyrinth has been incomplete ever since.*

*I have spent twenty-three years trying to complete it again. Not by recovering what was lost — the room made that impossible — but by finding someone who could hold both truths at once. Someone who could hear the voice and read the memory and understand that they are the same thing.*

*That someone is you.*

*But I can show you.*

The professor stood. For the first time in their acquaintance, Feimo saw him leave his desk. He walked to the bookshelf on the far wall and pulled a volume from the middle row — not a book but a lever. The shelf shuddered and slid sideways on a hidden track, revealing a wooden panel behind it. The same panel he had described. The same inscription: *此门通向词语的根。*

He had kept it. All these years, behind his books, the door had been there.

He pressed his palm against the panel. It swung inward. Beyond it: amber light, narrow walls, Chinese text flowing floor to ceiling in a continuous current of characters that shifted and rearranged as Feimo watched. Classical poetry, philosophy, passages from the Analects and the Dao De Jing, written in a hand she recognized — the same hand that had carved the inscription on Level 8, the same hand that had written the pages on the desk. The air that came through the opening was cold and smelled of old ink and stone dust and something else, something she could not name, the particular scent of a place where language had been accumulating for centuries without being read.

She stepped closer. The text on the nearest wall was moving — not quickly, not dramatically, but with the slow inevitability of water finding its level. Characters migrated across the stone, joined other characters, formed phrases and then dissolved. The wall was writing itself. The Labyrinth's engine, visible for the first time.

"She went this way," the professor said. His voice was flat, stripped of persuasion, the only kind of speech the room had left him. Statement. Fact. "Twenty-three years ago. Through this door. Into the room. And out again without her memory."

He paused.

"She will go again. When the memories return. She will find this door, and she will go through it, and this time I will not be able to follow. The room will not admit me twice. But you —"

He looked at Feimo.

"You must follow."

He wrote on a fresh sheet: *The seven Labyrinths are connected. Each one is a way-station on a longer road. The road leads down. The Russian Labyrinth is the deepest. The Chinese Labyrinth is the oldest. The English Labyrinth — this one — is the bridge between them. Mia's memories are in the Chinese Labyrinth. The answer to what she is, and what I am, is in the Russian one.*

He pushed the sheet toward Feimo.

*I cannot speak this aloud in a way that would convince you. The room saw to that. But I can show you the door, and I can tell you: she went this way. And you must follow.*

---

The door to the study opened.

Feimo turned. Mia stood in the doorway.

She was wearing the same clothes she always wore: dark trousers, a white shirt, the uniform of a student who had decided early that clothing was a problem to be solved once and then forgotten. Her ears were flat against her skull, not in anger but in a focused, inward-directed attention that Feimo recognized from the corridor on Level 8, from the moment in front of the Kafka door when Mia's face had shown something that was not analysis and not skepticism but longing.

Mia looked at the professor. Then at Feimo. Then at the stack of pages on the desk.

"What is this?" she asked.

Her voice was even. Controlled. The mask was in place, but Feimo could see the strain at the edges, the way the muscles around Mia's eyes were tighter than usual, the way her hands, hanging at her sides, were not quite still.

The professor picked up his pen and wrote a single line on a fresh sheet of paper. He held it up for Mia to read.

*You have been here before.*

Mia's face did not change. But her tail, which had been wrapped around her left thigh in its habitual posture of concentration, unwound and hung straight, motionless, the way it had in front of the Kafka door. A signal. A crack in the architecture of control.

"I know," Mia said. "I've always known."

She walked to the desk and looked at the pages. She did not pick them up. She read the first page, the second, the third, standing, her eyes moving across the professor's handwriting with the speed of someone accustomed to processing large volumes of text quickly. Feimo watched her face. The mask held for four pages. On the fifth, the one that described the room, the moving text, the inscription, the signature, something shifted.

Mia's eyes stopped moving.

She read the page again. Slower this time. Her lips moved slightly, forming words that she did not speak, the habit of a reader encountering a text so dense that the eyes alone cannot carry its weight.

"You signed," Mia said. "And then I signed."

"Yes," the professor said. His voice was flat, stripped of inflection, the voice of a man who could state facts but could not color them with emotion. The room had left him this: the ability to confirm. The ability to say yes.

"And the room took your voice."

"Yes."

"And it took my memory."

"Yes."

Mia was quiet for a long time. The study was silent except for the sound of the professor's breathing, which was slightly labored, and the distant hum of the Labyrinth's ventilation system, which had never been quite silent and had grown louder since the Fragmenter invasion, as if the building itself were compensating for damage by running its systems harder.

Then Mia said: "You should have told me."

The professor wrote:

*I know. I should have told you twenty-three years ago. I should have told you the day it happened. I should have told you every day since. I did not tell you because I was afraid that the telling would make the seal permanent, that the act of revealing the room's existence would trigger a defense mechanism in the Labyrinth's system, a hardening of the barrier between your present self and your past. I did not tell you because I was protecting you. I did not tell you because I was a coward.*

*I do not know which of these reasons is true. I suspect all of them.*

Mia read the page. Her face was very still.

"Twenty-three years," she said.

"Yes."

"You watched me search for something I had lost. You watched me spend years trying to recover memories that you knew had been sealed by a room you had taken me into. You watched me blame myself for a failure that was not mine. And you said nothing."

"Yes."

The word hung in the air. A single syllable, stripped of everything that normally gives language its force: no apology, no justification, no plea for understanding. Just the fact. The professor had said yes, and the yes was the truest word he had spoken, because it was the only word the room had left him the power to use without distortion.

Mia's eyes were dry. Her face was composed. But Feimo, who had spent months learning to read the signals that Mia's body transmitted in defiance of her face's attempts to conceal them, saw the tremor in Mia's left hand, the same tremor she had seen in the professor's right. Not the same cause, perhaps, but the same effect: the body registering what the mind refused to acknowledge, the muscles carrying the weight of an emotion that the mask would not permit to surface.

Then Mia sat down.

She sat in the chair next to Feimo, the one that had been placed there for visitors, the one that Feimo had occupied during her previous visits. Mia sat in it the way a person sits when they have been standing for too long and the body's need for rest has finally overridden the mind's insistence on control. She sat and she put her hands on her knees and she looked at the professor and her face was the face of a woman who had been told something she had always known and had spent twenty-three years hoping was not true.

She cried.

Not loudly. Not with the abandon of grief or the convulsions of despair. She cried the way the professor wrote: precisely, deliberately, each tear a small, controlled emission that she acknowledged and then moved past. Her eyes filled and overflowed and she did not wipe them, did not look away, did not apologize. She sat in the chair and she cried and she looked at the professor and the professor looked back at her and the silence between them was not the silence of avoidance but the silence of a conversation that had been delayed for twenty-three years and was now, finally, happening.

The professor picked up his pen. He wrote on a fresh sheet of paper, in handwriting that was less steady than before, the tremor in his right hand more pronounced:

*I am sorry. I know this is insufficient. I know that words on paper cannot repair what was broken. But they are all I have. The room left me nothing else.*

He pushed the page across the desk.

Mia read it. She looked at the professor for a long time. Then she said, in a voice that was quiet and even and carried no anger:

"I know."

She reached across the desk and took the professor's pen. She wrote on the same page, below his words, in her own handwriting, which was smaller and sharper, the handwriting of a person who had learned to write in multiple languages and had developed a style that was efficient in all of them:

*I am not sorry. I am angry. I will be angry for a long time. But I am also grateful. You showed me the room. You signed first. You paid the price before I did. This does not excuse the silence. But it explains it.*

*We will talk about this again.*

She put down the pen and stood up. She looked at Feimo.

"You heard all of this," Mia said. It was not a question.

"Yes."

"Good." Mia's ears rotated, once, a quick, involuntary movement that she did not suppress. "Someone should have heard it a long time ago."

She walked to the door. She stopped and turned back.

"Professor."

He looked up.

"The room took your voice," Mia said. "But it left you your hand. Use it."

She left.

---

That night, Feimo sat in her room and wrote.

She wrote about the professor's silence: not the silence of someone who has nothing to say but the silence of someone who has been forbidden to say it in the way that would make it heard. She wrote about Mia's tears: not tears of weakness but tears of recognition, the body's response to the confirmation of something it had always known.

She thought about Borges. Card #076. The writer who built labyrinths out of words, who used precise, documentary prose to describe impossible things. The professor's confession was Borgesian: a small room containing the engine of a building's intelligence, a contract that delivered a transformation, a man describing something impossible with the calm authority of a witness.

She thought about Kawabata. Card #077. *Mono no aware* — the bittersweet awareness of impermanence. The professor's confession was an act of *mono no aware*. He was telling her the story of his recognition that the loss was permanent. The beauty of the confession lay in its timing: twenty-three years too late, and yet precisely on time.

Feimo wrote: "Kawabata said that beauty is what remains when hope is gone. The professor's pages are beautiful. Not because they offer hope. Because they do not."

She turned off the light.

In the darkness, she thought about the seven labyrinths. She had now seen six: Language, Memory, Power, Fragmentation, the Door, and Silence. The seventh was still ahead, waiting where the damage was worst and the text on the walls was most fragmentary.

She thought: the seventh labyrinth is the one that thinks about itself.

She closed her eyes and let the darkness hold her.

*Questions for Reflection:*

1. The professor says he is "the Labyrinth's voice" and Mia is "its memory." What does it mean for a building to think through people? If the professor is the voice and Mia is the memory, what is Feimo?

2. Mia says: "You should have told me." The professor offers three reasons for his silence: protection, fear, cowardice. Is it possible to act from multiple, contradictory motivations simultaneously? How does this complicate our understanding of responsibility?

3. Feimo connects the professor's confession to Borges (who built labyrinths out of words) and to Kawabata (who wrote about beauty inseparable from loss). Which framework better describes the professor's story? Is his silence a labyrinth or a loss?
