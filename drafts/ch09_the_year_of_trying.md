# Chapter 9: The Year of Trying

A year passed.

Not all at once, the way time passes in stories where the author simply writes "a year later" and expects you to feel the weight of it. A year passes the way a river carves a canyon: so slowly that you do not notice it happening, and then one morning you look up and realize the landscape has changed. The walls have shifted. The corridors have widened or narrowed. The person who walked into the Labyrinth is not the person who stands in it now, and the difference is not in any single moment but in the accumulation of a thousand small ones.

Feimo noticed it first in her hands.

They were the same hands she had entered the Academy with, five-fingered and clawed, capable of scratching notes into margins and gripping Glyph-threads during corridor challenges. But they were steadier now. More deliberate. The nervous energy that had made her fingers twitch during her first exams had been replaced by something calmer: the practiced stillness of a person who had learned that panic was a luxury she could not afford.

She noticed it second in her record.

Three wins. Five losses. The scorecard sat in her dormitory drawer like an accusation, written in the clean bureaucratic hand of the underground arena's tally-keeper. Three and five. Not catastrophic, not by the standards of someone who had spent the year juggling corridor challenges, study sessions, and the quiet, creeping anxiety that settled into her chest every time she walked past Yue's bookshelf. But not good. Not the kind of record that earned you a reputation or a ranking or the respect of the upper-year students who drifted through the arena's tunnels like sharks through warm water.

Three and five.

The number followed her through the spring term like a shadow. When she sat in Mia's study sessions, parsing passages about the ethical judgments of Supreme Court justices, she thought about three and five. When she struggled to identify the logical connector in a sentence about whether courts could *maintain* their legitimacy when justices behaved like politicians, she thought about three and five. The first passage she had tackled that year was about boundaries: the line between law and politics, the barrier that was supposed to keep them apart, the way that line blurred when the people enforcing it stopped being accountable.

*The court cannot maintain its legitimacy as guardian of the rule of law when justices behave like politicians.*

She had copied that sentence into her notebook twice before she understood why it unsettled her. It was not about courts. Not really. It was about any institution that depended on the people inside it following rules they were not formally bound to obey. It was about trust. About what happens when the guardians stop guarding and start competing, and the thing they were supposed to protect erodes beneath them.

She thought about the Academy. She thought about the Labyrinth. She thought about corridors that did not appear on any map and books whose pages went blank without explanation and an AI assistant who flinched when you mentioned certain names.

Three and five. The record of a person who was still trying, which was different from succeeding, but also different from giving up.

---

The year assembled itself in fragments.

There was the morning Mia's projection flickered during a session on peer pressure, and Feimo caught something in her expression that looked less like contempt and more like concern. They had been working through Tina Rosenberg's argument: the idea that peer pressure could be a positive force, that organizations could harness group dynamics to improve lives. The social cure. Rage Against the Haze, the South Carolina program that tried to make cigarettes uncool. The loveLife initiative in South Africa, recruiting young people to promote safe sex among their peers.

"The problem," Mia had said, tapping the passage with one translucent finger, "is not the theory. The theory is sound. Peer groups exert enormous influence on our behavior. We unconsciously imitate the behavior we see every day. The problem is execution." She paused. Her eyes drifted toward the corridor that led to the residential wing. Toward Yue's room. "The social cure works only as long as the community sustains it. Cut the funding, cut the support, and it collapses. It does not work very well for very long."

Feimo had not asked what she was really talking about. She had learned, over the course of the year, that Mia did not always answer the question she was asked. She answered the question she thought you should have asked instead.

"Continue to the next passage," Mia said, and her voice was the voice of someone who had decided to stop thinking about something and was doing it loudly.

There was the afternoon in the underground arena when Feimo faced a fourth-year from Weave House and lost in eleven minutes. The fastest of her five losses. The fourth-year had used a layered reading technique Feimo had never seen: parsing three texts simultaneously, weaving their arguments together into a single coherent Glyph that struck like a wall. Feimo had stood in the aftermath, her ears flat against her skull, her tail limp, and thought: *I didn't even understand what hit me.*

Afterward, in the tunnel that led back to the surface, she had encountered Kain.

He was leaning against the wall with the practiced ease of someone who owned every room he entered, even rooms that were technically tunnels carved from living labyrinthine stone. His dark hair was pushed back from his forehead, and his eyes, sharp and calculating, tracked Feimo's approach with the patience of a cat watching a mouse it had already decided not to eat.

"Three and four now?" he said. He always knew her record. She had never told him. "The problem, Feimo, is not your reading. Your reading is adequate. The problem is that you treat every passage like it was written for you. Like the author is trying to help you understand." He pushed off from the wall. "They are not. Every passage is a trap. The question is not 'what is the author saying?' The question is 'what is the author trying to make me *do*?'"

He walked past her, close enough that she could smell something sharp and metallic, like ozone before a storm.

"Intuition is the excuse of the weak," he said, over his shoulder. "The strong read structure."

She had stood in the tunnel for a long time after he left, her ears rotating slowly, tracking the diminishing echo of his footsteps. He was not wrong. That was the worst part. He was not wrong, and she did not trust him, and both of those things were true at the same time.

There was the evening Aya appeared at the study session without being invited, sat in the back row, and scored perfectly on the practice passage about scientific credibility. The passage described the process by which discovery claims became accepted: the collective scrutiny, the gatekeepers, the slow transformation of one researcher's "me, here, now" into the community's "anyone, anywhere, anytime." Aya had annotated the text with surgical precision, marking every logical connector, every transition, every moment where the author shifted from describing to arguing. Her margins were a masterclass in structural analysis.

Mia had looked at Aya's annotations and said nothing for three full seconds, which was the AI equivalent of a standing ovation.

"Objectively adequate," Mia had said, which was the Mia equivalent.

Aya had not smiled. Aya never smiled. But something in her posture had shifted, a fractional relaxation of the rigidity she wore like armor, and Feimo recognized it as the expression of a person who had been told she was excellent and had decided to accept the information without letting it change anything.

There were other fragments. The study group that formed and dissolved and reformed around Feimo like a tide: the Weave boy who always checked his notes, the Forge girl with ink-stained fingers, the quiet second-year from Echo House who solved math problems in the margins of her literature textbooks. The sessions blurred together. Mia teaching the Three-Layer Reading Method. Mia explaining that "to say the least" was not sarcasm but understatement. Mia walking them through passage after passage, dissecting arguments with the precision of a surgeon and the bedside manner of a tax collector.

The arena matches came and went. Win, loss, loss, win, loss, loss, loss, win. Three and five. The losses taught her more than the victories, which was a thing people said to make themselves feel better about losing but which, in Feimo's case, happened to be true. Each loss revealed a gap in her reading, a structural weakness she had not known she had. She could feel herself getting better and still losing, which was a particular kind of frustration that she did not have a word for but that sat in her chest like a stone.

---

The worst part of the year was Yue.

Not because anything dramatic happened. That was the worst part. Nothing dramatic happened. The deterioration was so gradual, so quiet, so meticulously unremarkable that Feimo almost missed it. Almost. The way you almost miss the moment when autumn becomes winter: the leaves are still on the trees, and then one morning they are not, and you cannot identify the day it changed.

It started with the books.

Yue had always been a reader. Not a casual reader, not a person who read the way most students did, which was to say with one eye on the text and the other on the clock. Yue read the way some people breathe: continuously, involuntarily, as if the act of reading were so fundamental to her existence that stopping would be a form of death. She carried books the way other students carried weapons: always, and with absolute commitment.

In the autumn term, Feimo noticed that Yue's books were getting thinner.

Not physically. The books themselves were the same size, the same weight, the same leather-bound solidity that every Labyrinth text possessed. But the words inside them were disappearing. Page after page, chapter after chapter, the text simply faded, leaving clean white paper where there had once been paragraphs about literary theory or historical analysis or the structural properties of ancient verse. Yue would open a book that should have contained two hundred pages of dense scholarship and find sixty pages of text and a hundred and forty pages of emptiness.

She did not complain. Yue never complained. She closed the books and opened others and read what remained with the same meticulous attention she brought to everything, and if her hands trembled slightly when she turned the pages, Feimo told herself she had imagined it.

By spring, the blank pages outnumbered the full ones.

Feimo found Yue in the library one evening, sitting at her usual table, three books open before her. All three were blank. Not partially blank: entirely blank, from cover to cover, as if someone had printed the binding and the spine and the page numbers and forgotten to add the words. Yue was reading them anyway. Her eyes moved across the empty pages with the same careful attention she gave to full ones, tracking invisible text, following a line that existed only in her memory.

"Yue," Feimo said.

Yue looked up. Her face was thinner than it had been in the autumn. Her eyes, always large, seemed to have grown larger in proportion to the rest of her, as if the body were receding and the gaze were advancing, and eventually there would be nothing left but eyes and the memory of words.

"They're blank," Feimo said. She knew it was a stupid thing to say. She said it anyway.

"I know," Yue said. Her voice was the same as it had always been: quiet, precise, unhurried. "I read them from memory. The words that were here. I remember where they were on the page."

"That's not the same."

"No," Yue agreed. "It isn't."

She closed the books. The sound they made was the same as always: the soft thud of leather meeting leather, of pages settling into their binding. The same sound a book makes whether it contains the collected wisdom of centuries or nothing at all.

Feimo sat across from her. She wanted to ask what was happening. She wanted to ask why the books were blank, why Yue was getting thinner, why the Labyrinth that was supposed to preserve every word ever written seemed to be forgetting them in Yue's vicinity. She wanted to ask if Yue was sick, if the Labyrinth was sick, if there was a connection between the two.

She did not ask. She had learned, over the course of the year, that some questions do not have answers yet, and that asking them prematurely is not brave but cruel.

Instead, she said: "I found a passage about Chu Ci."

Yue's eyes sharpened. Not much. A fraction. But after months of watching Yue fade, even a fraction felt like a sunrise.

"Chu Ci," Yue repeated. "The Songs of the South."

"Qu Yuan. The poet who drowned himself in the Miluo River because the king wouldn't listen to him." Feimo pulled out her notebook. She had copied the passage by hand, the way she copied everything that mattered: in her own Glyphs, in her own script, with her own clawed handwriting that Mia said looked like a spider had fallen into an inkwell and then had a seizure. "He wrote in a style that broke every rule. Long lines, short lines, the word *xi* scattered through them like breath. Full of myths and shamanism and the particular madness of a person who loved something that did not love him back."

She read aloud. Not from the notebook. From memory. The way Yue read the blank books.

*"Long, long ago, when the world was young and words had weight, there lived a poet who loved his country more than his country loved him. He walked the banks of the Miluo River and sang songs that no one understood, and the river understood them perfectly, and this was both his comfort and his tragedy."*

Yue listened. The library was quiet around them. The hum of the Labyrinth's walls, that constant warm frequency, seemed to lean in, as if it too were listening.

"The *fafen shuqing* tradition," Yue said, when Feimo finished. "Expressing grievance through writing. Qu Yuan opened the door that every writer after him walked through: the idea that suffering is not something to endure silently but something to transform into language." She paused. "It is the first tradition. The Shijing gave voice to the common people. Qu Yuan gave voice to the individual. Together, they are the two rivers from which all Chinese poetry flows. *Feng* and *sao*. The realistic and the romantic."

"You're quoting the knowledge card."

"I'm quoting the truth. The knowledge card merely recorded it."

Feimo pulled her chair closer. "The old texts, the ones from Qu Yuan's era: were they written on bamboo strips? Or silk?"

"Both," Yue said. "The earliest Chu Ci texts were copied onto silk scrolls, because silk could carry ink without bleeding, and a poem needed precision. Every stroke mattered. A word written on bamboo could be scraped away. A word written on silk stayed." She paused. "There is a metaphor in that, if you want one."

"I want one."

"Silk remembers. Bamboo forgets. The choice of material changes the permanence of the thought." Yue looked down at her blank books. "Which is why what's happening now is so troubling."

Feimo smiled. It was a small smile, the kind that happens when you are worried and then briefly, for no good reason, not worried. Yue noticed it and did not comment, which was her way of acknowledging it, which was her way of smiling back.

Outside the library window, the Labyrinth's corridors stretched into distances that no one had mapped. Somewhere far below, in the depths that the Academy pretended did not exist, something hummed a frequency that was not quite the usual hum and not quite silence.

Yue closed her eyes. When she opened them, she said: "The Jian'an period. Have you read about the Jian'an period?"

Feimo shook her head.

"After Qu Yuan, centuries passed. Wars. Dynasty changes. The Han Dynasty fell. And in the chaos, a group of writers emerged who wrote with a fury that had not been seen since the Songs of the South. Cao Cao, Cao Pi, Cao Zhi. The Three Caos. And the Seven Masters of Jian'an. They wrote about war and exile and the desperate need to build something lasting in a world that was falling apart." Yue's voice had changed. It was stronger now, steadier, as if the act of teaching were pumping blood into her faster than her body could manage on its own. "Liu Xie described their writing as *kangkai yi renqi, lei luo yi shi cai*. 'Generous with spirit, open with talent.' They established the principle that literature is powered by *qi*—by vital breath, by the energy of the person writing. A weak person writes weak words. A fierce person writes fierce ones."

"Is that true?"

"It is a theory. But it is a powerful one. The Jian'an writers believed that style was not decoration. Style was character. What you wrote was who you were. And the best writing came from people who had suffered enough to have something worth saying and were brave enough to say it." Yue paused. "That is what *fenggu* means. Wind and bone. The quality of writing that has been tested by hardship and found unbreakable."

Feimo wrote it down. *Fenggu*. Wind and bone. She liked the sound of it: the way the word carried its meaning inside itself, the way you could hear the strength in it without knowing the definition.

"Are you okay?" she asked.

The question slipped out before she could stop it. She regretted it immediately. Not because it was wrong to ask, but because she could see, in the fractional tightening of Yue's expression, that the question had landed exactly where she had not wanted it to land.

"I'm reading," Yue said. Which was not an answer. Which was the only answer she was willing to give.

---

The cloze passage about the Supreme Court arrived in the autumn term, and Feimo hated it immediately.

Not because it was difficult. It was difficult, but she had spent the year learning to parse difficulty the way Mia had taught her: surface first, structure second, subtext third. She could handle difficult. What she could not handle was what the passage said.

*The ethical judgments of the Supreme Court justices have become an important issue recently. The court cannot maintain its legitimacy as guardian of the rule of law when justices behave like politicians.*

She sat at her desk in the dormitory, her tail curled around the chair leg, her ears flat with concentration. The passage was about a real court, a real institution, real people making real decisions that affected real lives. But it read like a mirror. Every sentence reflected something she had seen in the Academy, in the Labyrinth, in the quiet conversations she had with Noah in the tunnels beneath the library.

*Part of the problem is that the justices are not bound by an ethics code.*

Not bound. She circled the phrase. The Academy had rules. The Academy had traditions. The Academy had a founding charter that she had read three times and that said, in clear and formal language, that the Labyrinth existed to preserve knowledge, to protect it, to ensure that the words of the past survived into the future. But rules only worked when the people inside the institution chose to follow them. When they stopped choosing, when the institution began serving itself instead of its purpose, the rules became decorations. The legitimacy eroded. The line between guardian and politician blurred until no one could tell the difference.

*The framers of the Constitution envisioned law as having authority apart from politics. They gave justices permanent positions so they would be free to upset those in power and have no need to cultivate political support.*

She thought about Professor Silent. She thought about the way he taught: precise, measured, never saying more than necessary, never saying less. She thought about the knowledge he carried and did not share, and the questions he answered with questions, and the corridor in the eastern wing that he had walked past one evening and then avoided for the rest of the term.

*Our legal system was designed to set law apart from politics precisely because they are so closely tied.*

Tied. The word sat in Feimo's mind like a stone in a stream, dividing the current around it. Everything in the Academy was tied to everything else. The corridors tied to the walls. The walls tied to the books. The books tied to the readers. The readers tied to the Labyrinth itself, in ways she did not understand and that no one would explain.

She finished the passage. She answered all twenty blanks. She got fifteen of them right, which was better than she had expected and worse than she had wanted. She circled the five she had missed and wrote the correct answers in the margin in red ink, the way Mia had taught her: *the color of error, because error should hurt*.

The word *accountable* stared up at her from the margin. Making themselves accountable to the code of conduct. She underlined it twice.

---

Mia found her in the study room at midnight.

This was not unusual. What was unusual was that Mia's projection was dimmer than normal, her circuit-pattern cuffs barely glowing, her expression stripped of its usual contempt to reveal something underneath that Feimo could not quite name. Worry, maybe. Or its older cousin: dread.

"You're studying late," Mia said. Also not unusual.

"The Entergy passage," Feimo said, tapping her notebook. "The one about the nuclear power company that broke its promises."

"Text Two. The one about reneging on commitments." Mia's voice was flat. "A deal is a deal, except when it isn't. The company agreed to abide by strict regulations. Then they didn't. They challenged the rules in court. They argued that only the federal government had authority over their affairs. They decided that the commitments they had made no longer applied."

"Sound familiar?"

Mia's eyes flickered. The glow in her cuffs pulsed once, then steadied.

"The Vermont case tested how far state power extends," Feimo continued. "Whether the institution that granted permission could also revoke it. Whether the rules that applied when the deal was made still applied when the deal was broken." She closed her notebook. "I keep thinking about the Labyrinth."

"Don't."

"I keep thinking about the corridors that aren't on any map. The books that go blank. The way you flinch when I mention the Shijing corridor."

"I do not flinch."

"You flinch."

Silence. The hum of the Labyrinth filled it, that warm, constant frequency that was supposed to be reassuring and was, in this moment, not.

"There are things I cannot tell you," Mia said. Her voice was careful, each word placed with the precision of a surgeon suturing a wound. "Not because I don't want to. Because the information is not mine to give. Because there are structures in place that determine what I can and cannot share, and those structures exist for reasons that I am not permitted to explain."

"That's not reassuring."

"It is not meant to be."

Feimo looked at Mia. Really looked. At the holographic form that had become as familiar to her as her own reflection: the silver-gray eyes, the circuit-pattern cuffs, the expression that had shifted over the course of the year from pure contempt to something more complicated, something that lived in the space between contempt and care.

"The passage about scientific credibility," Feimo said slowly. "The one about discovery claims. It said that a discovery claim starts as one person's 'me, here, now' and becomes the community's 'anyone, anywhere, anytime.' It said that objective knowledge is the goal, not the starting point." She paused. "I think something in the Labyrinth is losing its credibility. I think something that used to be true is becoming untrue. And I think you know what it is."

Mia said nothing for a long time. Then she said: "Go to bed, Feimo."

"Are you scared?"

The word hung between them. Scared. The question you are not supposed to ask an AI, because AI systems do not experience fear, because fear is an emotion and emotions are biochemical and code does not have biochemistry. But Mia was not a standard AI system. Mia was something older and stranger and more human than she had any right to be, and when she answered, her voice carried a frequency that Feimo had never heard before.

"Yes," Mia said. "Go to bed."

---

The warning came from Noah.

It came on a Tuesday, in the tunnels beneath the library, in the space between the main corridors and the deeper levels that the Academy had sealed off decades ago. Noah had found Feimo after a study session, appearing from the shadows with the silent efficiency that was her hallmark: the cat-ear girl who moved through the Labyrinth's hidden pathways the way water moves through rock, finding every crack, filling every gap.

Noah looked tired. Not the ordinary tiredness of a person who had been working too hard, but the bone-deep exhaustion of a person who had been watching something terrible happen in slow motion and could not stop it.

"Walk with me," Noah said.

They walked. The tunnels narrowed and widened and narrowed again. The walls here were older, their surfaces rough with a texture that predated the Academy's renovations, carrying inscriptions in languages Feimo could not read. Noah walked without hesitation, her ears swiveling constantly, tracking sounds that Feimo could not hear.

"You've improved this year," Noah said. "Your reading, your analysis, your understanding of structure. Mia tells me your Layer Three comprehension is above average."

"Three and five."

"The record doesn't matter."

"It matters to me."

"I know. That's why I'm telling you this: stop focusing on the record. The arena is not what it pretends to be. It's a distraction. A controlled environment that makes you feel like you're learning something dangerous while the real danger happens elsewhere."

Feimo's ears flattened. "What real danger?"

Noah stopped walking. They were in a section of tunnel that Feimo had never seen before: wider, darker, the walls humming at a frequency that was lower than usual, more insistent, like a heartbeat.

"The Academy is decaying," Noah said. Her voice was quiet, but the words carried the weight of someone who had been holding them for a long time. "Not the buildings. Not the corridors. Something deeper. Something in the foundations. The words are disappearing, Feimo. Not just Yue's books. All over the Labyrinth, texts are going blank. Passages are losing their content. Knowledge that has been preserved for centuries is vanishing, and no one is talking about it. It's the elephant in the room: massive, obvious, impossible to ignore, and somehow everyone manages to look away."

"Who's letting it happen?"

"I don't know. That's what scares me." Noah's tail wrapped around her ankle, a gesture Feimo recognized from her own nervous habits. "I've been mapping the decay. Tracking which sections are affected. The pattern is not random. Someone or something is draining the Labyrinth's content, and the deeper you go, the worse it gets."

"The Shijing corridor."

Noah's ears flattened. "You felt it."

"I felt something. My fur stood up. Mia went silent. There was a door that had no writing on it, and the absence of writing felt wrong."

"That corridor is not on any map. It was not built by the Academy. It was here before the Academy, before the Labyrinth, before any of this. And whatever is inside it is connected to what's happening now." Noah stepped closer. "I'm not telling you this to scare you. I'm telling you because you need to understand: the Labyrinth is not what you think it is. It is older and stranger and more fragile than anyone here admits. And the things that are disappearing, the words, the knowledge, the *qi* of the texts themselves, they are not being lost. They are being taken."

The tunnel hummed around them. Low, insistent, wrong.

"There are things in the deeper levels," Noah said. "Things that the Academy was built to contain. Things that have been sleeping for a very long time. And they are waking up." She paused. "I don't know what that means yet. I don't know if anyone does. But I need you to keep reading. I need you to keep learning. Not for the arena. Not for the record. Because when the words disappear, someone needs to remember them. Someone needs to carry them forward."

She looked at Feimo with an expression that was equal parts hope and despair: the expression of a person who had been carrying a burden alone for too long and had finally found someone strong enough to share it.

"You're not ready," Noah said. "I know that. But you're the closest thing to ready that I've got."

She turned and walked back into the shadows, leaving Feimo alone in the humming dark.

---

That night, Feimo sat at her desk and did not sleep.

The year lay behind her like a road she had walked without realizing how far she had come. Three and five. The number still stung, but it had changed shape. It was no longer a record. It was a record of trying, which was different from succeeding, but which was also, she was beginning to understand, the only thing that mattered.

She opened her notebook to the page where she had written the passage about public-sector unions. The text had struck her when she first read it, and it struck her now, differently.

*The norms of culture in Western civil services suit those who want to stay put but are bad for high achievers.*

She thought about Yue, staying put in a library full of blank books. She thought about Mia, bound by structures she could not explain. She thought about Noah, moving through tunnels that did not exist on any map, carrying warnings that no one wanted to hear.

She thought about the passage about uploading and downloading. How most people spent their lives in download mode: consuming, receiving, absorbing without creating. How the networked computer had sparked a secret war between passive consumption and active creation. How the writer had argued that failing to move beyond downloading was to strip oneself of a defining constituent of humanity.

*"Of course, it is precisely these superfluous things that define human culture and ultimately what it is to be human."*

Superfluous. The word had bothered her when she first encountered it. Things that were unnecessary, excessive, beyond what was needed. Paintings. Sculpture. Music. Literature. The Jian'an poets writing with *fenggu* in a world that was falling apart. Qu Yuan singing to a river that would not sing back. Yue reading blank books because the memory of words was, for her, as real as the words themselves.

None of it was necessary. All of it was essential.

Feimo picked up her pen. She turned to a fresh page. She wrote, in her clawed, spider-fallen-into-inkwell handwriting:

*The Labyrinth preserves every word ever written. But it does not preserve the person who read them. That is our job.*

She underlined it. Not in red. In black. The color of permanence. The color of *fenggu*. Wind and bone.

Outside her window, the Labyrinth hummed. The same warm frequency it had always hummed, the frequency she had learned to associate with the feeling of words being alive. But underneath it, faint and growing, another frequency. Lower. Older. Hungrier.

Something in the deeper levels was waking up.

And somewhere in the dormitory, on the other side of a wall, Yue turned a page in a book that contained nothing but the memory of what used to be there. Her fingers moved across the blank paper with the care of a person reading aloud to no one, very quietly, in the dark, not because anyone was listening but because the act of reading was the last word she had left, and she would not let it go.

The year of trying was over.

The year of everything else was about to begin.
