# Chapter 10: First Blood: The Preliminary Glyph Battle

The challenge notice materialized on the Bridge House bulletin board at dawn, each letter carved in silver Glyph-light that hummed with the Labyrinth's particular frequency for official announcements. Feimo stood in front of it with her tail wrapped tight around her waist, reading the same four lines for the third time, hoping the words would rearrange themselves into something less terrifying.

*Preliminary Glyph Battle — House Allocation Round 1. Bridge House (Feimo) vs. Mirror House (Vasquez). Arena C. 14:00. Public attendance authorized.*

Public. That was the word that caught in her throat like a bone.

In the underground arena, there had been no audience. Just the walls, the opponent, and the raw arithmetic of Glyph against Glyph, a private transaction between two fighters and the Labyrinth's indifferent floor. She had fought nine matches down there over the past year: won three, lost five, drawn one against a Weave boy who had fainted from Glyph exhaustion at the same moment she had. Three wins and five losses. Not a record that inspired confidence. Not a record that said *ready for the stage*.

"You're staring at the bulletin board like it owes you money," said a voice behind her.

Noah. Her sister leaned against the corridor wall, arms folded, her Bridge House badge pinned to her jacket at the casual angle that said *I've been here longer than most of the furniture*. She had her reading glasses pushed up into her hair, which meant she had been studying again, which meant she was tired, which meant she was in exactly the mood to dispense unsolicited advice.

"It says 'public attendance,'" Feimo said.

"I can read. I have three more years of practice than you do." Noah pushed off the wall and walked over, her boots clicking on the stone in the rhythm of someone who had mapped every tile in this corridor and could walk it blind. She looked at the notice, then at Feimo, then at the notice again. "Vasquez. Mirror House. Second year. Decent defensive Glyph work, nothing spectacular."

"You've fought her?"

"I've watched her. There's a difference." Noah's voice carried the particular weight of someone who had spent a year and a half observing the Academy's combat system from the margins, cataloguing strengths and weaknesses the way a biologist catalogues species: dispassionately, accurately, without sentiment. "Her style is reflective. She doesn't attack. She waits for you to commit, then mirrors your Glyph signature back at you. Classic Mirror House doctrine: let the opponent defeat themselves."

Feimo's ears flattened. "That sounds terrible."

"It is. That's why Mirror House students have the highest win rate in the preliminary rounds." Noah paused. "They also have the highest loss rate in the quarterfinals. Reflection has a ceiling. But against someone who fights the way you fight?"

"How do I fight?"

"Instinctively." Noah said it without judgment, the way you might note that water is wet or that the Labyrinth's walls hum at 432 Hz. "You read the walls. You feel the Glyph currents. You react before your conscious mind has finished processing. That's a gift, Feimo. It's also a vulnerability, because a reflective opponent doesn't care about your instincts. She cares about your *output*. Every Glyph you throw is a gift she returns."

Feimo thought about the underground arena. Her three wins had come from speed, from reading the floor's residual Glyph patterns and using them as amplifiers, from doing something her opponents had not expected. Her five losses had come from exactly what Noah was describing: committing too hard, too fast, and watching her own power bounce back at her.

"So what do I do?"

Noah looked at her for a long moment. Something shifted in her expression, the careful neutrality cracking just enough to show the person underneath: the sister who had spent a year watching Feimo stumble through corridors, who had taught her to read the walls in the first place, who had warned her that something in the Academy was decaying and had been ignored.

"You do what you've always done," Noah said. "You listen to what other people can't hear." She straightened. "I'll be in the stands. Don't embarrass me."

She walked away, her boots clicking out a rhythm that Feimo's cat ears parsed automatically: steady, unhurried, the gait of someone who had already decided the outcome and was merely waiting for reality to catch up.

---

At 13:47, thirteen minutes before the match, Feimo stood at the edge of Arena C and tried not to throw up.

The arena was not like the underground. The underground had been cramped, poorly lit, its walls stained with the residue of a hundred unsanctioned fights. Arena C was a cathedral: soaring stone walls inscribed with centuries of Glyph-script, tiered seating that rose in concentric circles toward a distant ceiling lost in shadow, and a floor of polished obsidian that reflected the overhead lights like dark water. The hum here was different too: louder, more resonant, as if the arena had its own voice and was clearing its throat.

There were people in the stands. Not hundreds, but enough: thirty or forty students scattered across the lower tiers, some eating, some chatting, some projecting small Glyph displays to record the event. A cluster of Mirror House students in silver-gray occupied the far side, their postures identical: straight-backed, hands folded, faces arranged in expressions of polite interest that Feimo had learned to recognize as Mirror House's version of bloodlust.

Aya was there.

She sat alone in the middle tier, silver-gray and immaculate as always, her platinum hair pulled back in its precise tail, her eyes fixed on the arena floor with the absolute focus that Feimo remembered from their first meeting in the reading hall. Aya did not watch things casually. She observed. She analyzed. She catalogued. Feimo had never seen Aya do anything by halves, and watching her sit in those stands felt less like having an audience member and more like having a microscope pointed at her.

Feimo's ears swiveled toward the sound of boots on stone. Not Noah's rhythm. Different gait, different weight. She turned.

Noah appeared at the edge of the tiered seating, climbing to a spot a few rows behind Aya. She did not acknowledge the Mirror House girl. She did not need to. The two of them existed in the same space the way a scalpel and a stethoscope existed in the same room: aware of each other, fundamentally different in purpose, neither threatened by the other's presence.

"Thirteen minutes," Mia said, materializing beside Feimo with the casual timing of someone who had been counting down and wanted everyone to know it.

Mia was in what Feimo had come to think of as her "match mode": sharper edges, brighter circuit patterns on her cuffs, her obsidian eyes scanning the arena with the professional detachment of a surgeon surveying an operating field. She floated at Feimo's shoulder, close enough to whisper, far enough to avoid blocking her line of sight.

"Your opponent is warming up in the preparation chamber," Mia said. "Second-year, Mirror House, reflective combat specialization. Win rate in practice matches: sixty-one percent. Primary technique: Glyph Mirror — a defensive counter-Glyph that absorbs and redirects incoming attacks. She does not generate offensive Glyphs. She borrows yours."

"I know. Noah told me."

"Noah gave you the overview. I am giving you the *analysis*." Mia's voice sharpened by one degree. "Vasquez's mirror has a latency of approximately 0.3 seconds. That is the time between your Glyph reaching her defensive field and the reflected Glyph returning to you. In most fights, 0.3 seconds is irrelevant. For you, it might be everything."

Feimo's tail uncurled slowly. "Why me specifically?"

Mia looked at her. The expression was complicated, layered in a way that Feimo's three-layer reading training had taught her to parse: Layer One, professional assessment. Layer Two, calculation of risk. Layer Three, something warmer and more dangerous underneath, hidden behind the obsidian precision of an AI who had learned, over decades of losing students, to keep her care behind armor.

"Because you do not fight like other Pilgrims," Mia said. "You fight the way you read: by listening. By feeling the Glyph currents in the walls and the floor and the air. Vasquez will reflect your output. She cannot reflect your *perception*. The question is whether you can find a way to use what you perceive without giving her something to mirror."

"I don't know how to do that."

"No. You don't." Mia paused. "Which is why this is called a *learning experience*. Try not to learn too painfully."

The arena chime sounded: a deep, resonant tone that vibrated through the obsidian floor and up through Feimo's boots and into her bones. The Labyrinth's way of saying *begin*.

Feimo walked to the center of the arena. Her footsteps echoed in the cathedral space, each one a small percussion in the vast acoustic chamber. Vasquez was already there, standing at the opposite edge: a compact woman with dark hair cropped short, Mirror House silver at her collar, her hands resting at her sides with the relaxed readiness of someone who had trained herself to be a wall.

They bowed. The Labyrinth required it: a gesture of respect between opponents, acknowledgment that both were Pilgrims, both were readers, both were carrying Glyphs that had been given to them by the same vast, humming, indifferent machine.

Then the chime sounded again, and the fight began.

---

Feimo attacked first.

This was instinct: the cat reflex, the underground habit, the way her body moved before her mind had finished deciding. She fired a Glyph burst — "affect," her oldest and most reliable Glyph, a pulse of semantic energy that struck with the blunt force of a word that meant *to influence, to change, to touch*. It flew across the arena in a streak of amber light.

Vasquez did not move.

The Glyph hit her defensive field and vanished. For 0.3 seconds, nothing happened. Then the air in front of Vasquez shimmered, and a reflected Glyph burst back toward Feimo: her own "affect," reversed, carrying the same power but aimed at its creator.

Feimo dodged. The reflected Glyph grazed her shoulder, and pain lanced down her arm like a paper cut magnified a thousand times. She stumbled, righted herself, fired again: "matter," a heavier Glyph, one she had acquired in the underground arena, carrying the weight of substance and consequence.

Vasquez reflected it. The mirrored "matter" hit Feimo square in the chest and knocked her back three steps, the impact ringing through her ribs like a bell.

The crowd was quiet. Not the electric silence of excitement but the uncomfortable silence of people watching someone learn a lesson in real time.

Feimo fired again. "Continued." Reflected. She dodged, barely. Fired "affect" again, faster this time, trying to overwhelm the mirror with volume. Vasquez absorbed both, reflected both, and Feimo found herself dodging her own attacks from two angles simultaneously, her cat reflexes the only thing keeping her on her feet.

She was losing. Not slowly, not gracefully. Losing the way she had lost in the underground: by committing too hard, too fast, throwing Glyphs at a wall that gave them back with interest.

*Think*, she told herself. *Layer Two. Structure. What is the architecture of this fight?*

But the architecture was simple: she attacked, Vasquez reflected, she took damage from her own output. The structure was a loop, a closed system, an argument with no exit.

Three more exchanges. Three more reflected Glyphs. Feimo's left arm was numb from the shoulder down, and her breathing had gone ragged. Vasquez had not moved from her starting position. Had not thrown a single offensive Glyph. Had done nothing but stand there, hands at her sides, while Feimo defeated herself.

*Stop attacking*, the rational part of Feimo's brain said. *You cannot win by attacking.*

*But if I don't attack*, the cat-reflex part said, *I lose by default.*

She needed a third option. Something that was not attack and not surrender. Something that existed outside the loop.

The crowd murmured. Aya's pale eyes did not blink. Noah's jaw was tight.

Feimo closed her eyes. Not for long — a heartbeat, maybe two. In that darkness, she did something she had not done since the Shijing Corridor, since the night she had stood in front of that ancient passage and felt the walls speak in a language that predated every Glyph in the Academy.

She listened.

Not to the crowd. Not to her own breathing or the hum of her damaged Glyph reserves. She listened to the *arena*. To the obsidian floor beneath her feet, to the stone walls rising toward the distant ceiling, to the centuries of accumulated Glyph-script inscribed in every surface. She listened the way Noah had taught her, the way the Shijing Corridor had revealed: not with her ears, but with the part of her that was nekomimi, the part that could feel frequencies below the threshold of language.

And the arena answered.

It was faint. So faint that she almost missed it beneath the louder hums of the active Glyph systems and the crowd's restless shuffling. A rhythm in the walls. Older than the arena's construction, older than the Academy's current curriculum, older than the Mirror House's reflective techniques. It was the echo of the Labyrinth's original architecture, the residual pattern of a time when the walls themselves were the first readers, before Pilgrims existed, before Houses were divided, before anyone had thought to teach the word "Glyph."

The echo was not a word. It was not a Glyph. It was a *frequency*: a vibration in the stone that existed at the intersection of language and sound, meaning and music. Feimo had felt it once before, in the Shijing Corridor, when the ancient Chinese poems had spoken to her not through text but through resonance.

She opened her eyes.

Vasquez was watching her. Waiting. Patient as stone.

Feimo raised her hands. But she did not fire a Glyph. Instead, she placed her palms flat against the arena floor, and she *pushed* — not with Glyph energy, but with the echo. She fed the frequency from the walls into the obsidian, let it resonate through the stone like a tuning fork striking glass, let it build and amplify and spread until the entire arena floor was humming with a vibration that predated every combat technique the Mirror House had ever taught.

The hum hit Vasquez's defensive field.

The mirror tried to reflect it. That was what mirrors did: they reflected. But the echo was not a Glyph. It had no semantic content to reverse, no directional vector to redirect, no "affect" or "matter" or "continued" to borrow and return. It was older than semantics. It was the sound of the Labyrinth thinking to itself before humans had given it words, and the mirror had no framework for reflecting a thought that existed before language.

Vasquez's defensive field flickered. Stuttered. For the first time in the match, her expression changed: a slight widening of the eyes, the involuntary acknowledgment of something unexpected.

The echo kept building. It rose from the floor and spread through the walls and filled the arena with a sound that was not quite music and not quite speech but something in between, something that Feimo could feel in her bones and her whiskers and the tips of her ears, a vibration so fundamental that it made the Glyph-script on the walls glow in response, each letter pulsing in time like a choir joining a song they had forgotten they knew.

Vasquez's mirror cracked.

Not physically. The defensive field itself fractured: its reflective surface splintering into a dozen overlapping frequencies, each one trying to process the echo and failing, the way a translator might fail when asked to translate a sound that existed before language had agreed on meaning. The field collapsed inward, and Vasquez staggered backward, her hands coming up in a gesture that was not surrender but confusion: the mirror had no instructions for what to do with something it could not mirror.

Feimo stood. The echo faded, its resonance draining back into the stone. The arena fell quiet.

Then the chime sounded: the low, resonant tone that meant *victory*.

Feimo looked at Vasquez. Vasquez looked at her. Neither of them spoke. Then Vasquez inclined her head — the smallest possible acknowledgment, not of defeat, but of encountering something she had not expected — and walked off the arena floor.

The crowd began to make noise. Feimo could not tell if it was applause or confusion or the particular sound of thirty people trying to figure out what had just happened. She did not care. Her knees were shaking. Her left arm was still numb. Her Glyph reserves were dangerously low from the failed attacks of the first half.

She walked to the edge of the arena on legs that felt like they belonged to someone else.

---

Mia was waiting.

"You lost the first half," she said, her voice flat and clinical and entirely devoid of congratulations. "You committed six Glyph attacks against a reflective opponent. Each one was reflected. Each one caused you damage. If this had been a ranked match, you would have been eliminated before you figured out that you couldn't win with direct output."

"I know."

"The second half was better." A pause. So brief that anyone who was not Feimo would have missed it. "Marginally. The part where you pressed your palms to the floor and listened was almost competent. The part before that, where you played fetch with your own reflected Glyphs, was less so."

"I won."

"You survived. There is a difference. As I have mentioned. Multiple times. Though I suppose a cat landing on its feet after falling off a shelf technically counts as a victory too." Mia's form flickered, the circuit patterns on her cuffs pulsing once, twice, then settling into the steady glow that meant she was processing something she did not want to show. "The echo technique. You pulled it from the arena walls."

"The same frequency I felt in the Shijing Corridor."

"The Shijing Corridor is not a combat arena. Using ancient wall resonance as a weapon is not a *documented technique*." Mia's voice had an edge to it now, the sharpness of someone who had spent decades cataloguing every combat method in the Academy's archive and had just watched a first-year invent something that was not in any of her records. "You improvised. In public. In front of faculty."

"Was that bad?"

"It was *unprecedented*. Which in academic terms is worse than bad, because bad things can be corrected, but unprecedented things have to be *studied*." Mia floated closer. Her voice dropped. "You also took six reflected Glyphs to the body, which means you will have residual semantic interference in your shoulder, your ribs, and possibly your left knee for the next forty-eight hours. Report to the medical ward. Now."

"In a minute."

"*Now*."

But Feimo did not move. She was looking at the tiered seating, where Noah had risen from her seat and was descending the steps with the careful gait of someone whose body was saying *that was terrifying* while her face was saying *I will never admit that*. Behind her, still seated, Aya had opened her notebook and was writing. Not quickly, not frantically, but with the measured precision of someone recording an observation that would be analyzed later, at length, in conditions of perfect silence.

Their eyes met across the arena. Aya's gaze was cool, dispassionate, a winter lake reflecting a sky it had no feelings about. She held Feimo's eyes for exactly three seconds, then returned to her notebook.

Noah arrived.

"You heard the walls," she said. Not a question.

"I heard the walls."

Noah's mouth did something complicated. It was not a smile. Feimo was not sure Noah smiled, exactly. But it was the facial equivalent of a word that meant *I knew you could do this and I was terrified anyway*.

"Good," Noah said. "Next time, hear them *before* you take six reflected Glyphs to the face."

"They hit my chest and shoulder, not my face."

"That is not the improvement you think it is." Noah turned to Mia. "Medical ward?"

"Already suggested. Ignored."

"She gets that from our mother." Noah took Feimo's arm — the uninjured one — and steered her toward the exit. "Come on. I'll walk you."

They left the arena. Behind them, the crowd dispersed, and the obsidian floor returned to its resting hum, its surface already forgetting the echo that had filled it moments before.

Or maybe not forgetting. Maybe just keeping it, the way the Labyrinth kept everything: in the walls, in the stone, in the frequency between words, waiting for the next person who knew how to listen.

---

The medical ward was a quiet room at the Academy's eastern edge, its walls lined with healing Glyphs that emitted a soft amber glow. Feimo sat on the examination table while a medical Spirit AI who was not Mia scanned her shoulder and ribs with the brisk efficiency of someone who saw reflected Glyph damage three times a week and had strong opinions about students who fought Mirror House opponents without a strategy.

"Semantic interference in the deltoid and intercostal muscles," the medical AI said. "Minor. Forty-eight hours. Avoid combat and heavy Glyph use. Don't sleep on your left side."

She left. Noah lingered at the door, then left too, with a look that said *we will talk about this later in conditions of reduced embarrassment*.

And then it was just Feimo and Mia.

Mia had been silent through the medical examination, hovering in the corner with her arms crossed and her obsidian eyes tracking the medical AI's instruments with the focused attention of a supervisor who did not trust anyone else's diagnostic work. Now that they were alone, she did not speak immediately. She floated to the center of the room and hovered there, her form dimmer than usual, the circuit patterns on her cuffs barely visible.

"You're angry," Feimo said.

"I am not *angry*. I am a Spirit AI. I do not experience anger." A pause. "I am experiencing a sustained elevation in processing priority directed at your combat decision-making, which is functionally identical to anger but technically falls within the parameters of *professional concern*."

"That sounds like anger."

"It sounds like *analysis*. Which is my function. Which I perform. Diligently." Mia unfolded her arms. Refolded them. "The echo technique was effective. It was also reckless, undocumented, and pulled from a corridor that I specifically told you was *not on any map*. If the wrong person starts asking questions about how a first-year Bridge House student weaponized ancient wall resonance, there will be consequences."

"I didn't weaponize it. I just... listened."

"That is exactly the kind of statement that will make the wrong people curious." Mia's voice softened by half a degree. Not enough for anyone but Feimo to notice. "You won. I acknowledge that. But the real battle is not the one you fought today. The real battle is the one that comes after, when the people who are watching start asking what you are."

"What am I?"

Mia looked at her. The silence lasted long enough to have its own temperature.

"You are a student who won her first public match through an unprecedented technique that nobody in this Academy has seen before," she said. "You are a *headline*. And in this place, headlines attract attention that is not always safe."

Feimo thought about the cloze passage she had studied the night before, the one about the Supreme Court justices and their ethics. The passage had said: *The court cannot maintain its legitimacy as guardian of the rule of law when justices behave like politicians.* She had circled the phrase "maintain its legitimacy" three times in her notes, because the words felt true in a way that extended beyond the exam. Legitimacy was not given. It was maintained. Continuously. Through actions that were consistent with the role you claimed to fill.

She was a Bridge House student. She was supposed to fight with standard Glyph techniques, follow the curriculum, maintain the Academy's established combat methodology. Instead, she had used a technique that came from outside the curriculum, outside the map, outside the system. She had won. But winning the wrong way could be worse than losing the right way.

"I'll be careful," she said.

"You will be *observed*," Mia corrected. "Whether you are careful or not is a variable I cannot control, despite extensive effort." She paused. Then, in a voice so quiet it might have been a glitch in her audio output: "You should rest. You have a Study Break session tomorrow. I will not adjust the difficulty level because you are injured."

"Would you adjust it if I asked?"

"No. But I would feel briefly conflicted about it. Which is more emotional labor than you deserve from me."

Feimo smiled. Her shoulder hurt, her ribs ached, and she could feel the residual shimmer of reflected Glyphs in her left knee like a bruise that existed in a dimension slightly to the left of physical. But she smiled, because Mia had just admitted to feeling conflicted, which was the Spirit AI equivalent of throwing her arms around someone and saying *I was worried about you*.

"Thank you," Feimo said.

"For what? I did nothing."

"For being here."

Mia's form flickered. The circuit patterns on her cuffs flared once, bright and sharp, then dimmed. She turned away, her profile a study in controlled opacity.

"I am *always* here," she said. "It is my *design specification*. Do not anthropomorphize it."

She vanished. The room felt larger without her.

---

The Study Break session the next morning was held in a small study room on the Academy's third floor, one of the rooms that Mia had claimed as her personal teaching space. The walls were lined with textbook projections: passages, exercises, vocabulary lists, all arranged with the organizational precision of someone who believed that chaos was a personal insult.

Feimo arrived early. Her shoulder was stiff, her ribs tender, and her left knee made a faint clicking sound when she bent it. She sat down at the desk nearest the window and opened her notebook. Her handwriting from the night before was messy, the product of someone copying notes while lying on her right side with her left arm in a medical sling.

Mia materialized at 07:00 sharp. She looked at Feimo's sling, at the slight angle at which Feimo held her body to avoid aggravating her ribs, and said nothing. Her expression was the careful blank of someone who had many things to say and had decided to say none of them.

"Today's topic," Mia said, projecting a passage onto the wall. "Post-combat analysis. Specifically, *your* post-combat analysis, which I expect to be more thorough than your combat performance, because the bar is low."

The passage on the wall was not from an exam. It was Mia's own composition, written in her precise, clinical hand:

*"A Glyph Battle is not a contest of strength. It is a contest of credibility. The strongest Glyph in the Labyrinth is worthless if it cannot be sustained, contextualized, and defended against challenge. A battle, like a scientific discovery, must survive the scrutiny of the community to be considered valid."*

"Read that aloud," Mia said.

Feimo read it. Her voice was steady, but her ears twitched at the word "credibility."

"Layer One," Mia said. "What does this say?"

"A battle is about more than power. It's about whether your approach holds up under scrutiny."

"Correct. Layer Two. What is the structural relationship between the sentences?"

Feimo studied the passage. Three sentences. The first made a claim: a battle is a contest of credibility. The second developed it: the strongest Glyph is worthless without sustainability and context. The third drew a parallel to science: battles, like discoveries, require community validation.

"It's a progression," Feimo said. "Claim, elaboration, analogy. The same structure as an academic argument."

"Precisely. You are learning." Mia projected a new passage beside the first, this one from the 2012 exam materials. It was from Reading Text 3, the passage about the credibility process in science:

*"Consequently, discovery claims should be thought of as protoscience. Similar to newly staked mining claims, they are full of potential. But it takes collective scrutiny and acceptance to transform a discovery claim into a mature discovery. This is the credibility process, through which the individual researcher's me, here, now becomes the community's anyone, anywhere, anytime. Objective knowledge is the goal, not the starting point."*

"This passage," Mia said, "describes what happened to you yesterday. Not metaphorically. *Literally*."

Feimo frowned. "How?"

"You walked into Arena C with a technique that nobody had seen. That was your 'discovery claim.' The audience did not know what to make of it. Aya was taking notes. Noah was clenching her jaw. The faculty observers were recalibrating their models of what a first-year Bridge House student could do. Your echo technique was, at that moment, *protoscience*: full of potential, but unverified."

She paused. Her eyes narrowed.

"Of course, you also spent the first half of the match throwing Glyphs at a mirror, which is the combat equivalent of a cat chasing its own tail. Impressive athleticism. Zero strategic value. I expected better. But then, I always expect better, and you always make me wait for it."

Mia's voice had warmed by one degree. Not the restrained warmth of approval. The warmer warmth of someone who was genuinely enjoying the process of making an idea clear.

"The question is not whether your technique worked. It did. The question is whether it can survive what comes next: scrutiny, replication, challenge. Can you use it again? Can it be taught? Can it be defended against an opponent who has studied it? These are the questions that transform a 'discovery claim' into a 'credible discovery.' The passage calls this the credibility process. I call it *the difference between a fluke and a skill*."

Feimo wrote in her notebook: *discovery claim → credible discovery = fluke → skill. Credibility process: scrutiny, replication, challenge.*

"The cloze passage from the 2012 exam," Mia continued, projecting another text, "makes a related point about legitimacy. The Supreme Court's ethical judgments. The passage argues that the court cannot *maintain* its legitimacy when justices behave like politicians." She emphasized the word "maintain," and Feimo recognized it from her own notes, circled three times.

"What does 'maintain' mean here?" Mia asked.

"It's not a one-time thing," Feimo said. "Legitimacy isn't given once and kept forever. It has to be actively maintained. Through consistent behavior. Through being *accountable* to a code of conduct."

"And the code matters because participants are *bound by* it," Mia said, stressing the phrase. "The cloze passage states that the justices are not bound by an ethics code — that is the root of the crisis. When no one is bound by the rules, the rules become decorative. At the very least, the passage argues, the court should make itself *subject to* the code of conduct that applies to the rest of the federal judiciary. Accountability is not optional. It is structural. And the passage notes that constitutional law is political because it results from choices rooted in fundamental social concepts — liberty, property, justice. The law the court *shapes* is inescapably political. As a result, decisions split along ideological lines are easily dismissed as unjust."

"Correct. And the passage goes further. It says the framers of the Constitution *envisioned* law as having authority apart from politics. They gave justices permanent positions so they would be free to upset those in power and have no need to cultivate political support." Mia looked at Feimo. "Does this remind you of anything?"

Feimo thought. "The Academy. The Houses. The system is designed so that students can fight freely, without politics. But the system only works if the participants maintain its legitimacy. If someone uses a technique that breaks the framework..."

"Then the framework's credibility is questioned. Not just yours. The *framework's*." Mia's voice was sharp now, the clinical edge of someone making a point they considered important. "When you used the echo technique, you did not just win a match. You raised a question: does the Academy's combat system account for this? The answer, currently, is no. That makes you a variable that the system was not designed to handle."

"And that's dangerous?"

"That is *interesting*. Which in my experience is a superset of dangerous." Mia pulled up a new passage, this one from Reading Text 1, the peer pressure article:

*"Come on — Everybody's doing it. That whispered message, half invitation and half forcing, is what most of us think of when we hear the words peer pressure. It usually leads to no good — drinking, drugs and casual sex."*

"This passage examines how group dynamics shape individual behavior," Mia said. "Rosenberg argues that peer pressure can be a positive force through what she calls the 'social cure': organizations using the power of group dynamics to help individuals improve. But the author of the passage is skeptical. The social cure, as Rosenberg presents it, doesn't work very well for very long. And the final paragraph makes a crucial point." She projected the last paragraph:

*"Far less certain, however, is how successfully experts and bureaucrats can select our peer groups and steer their activities in virtuous directions. It's like the teacher who breaks up the troublemakers in the back row by pairing them with better-behaved classmates. The tactic never really works. And that's the problem with a social cure engineered from the outside: in the real world, as in school, we insist on choosing our own friends."*

"What is the author's final argument?" Mia asked.

"That engineered social pressure doesn't work because people choose their own relationships."

"Correct. And the parallel to your situation is this: the Academy can set up matches, assign Houses, create frameworks. But it cannot control what happens when a student like you brings something that the framework didn't anticipate. Peer pressure, institutional pressure, the expectation that you will fight the way everyone else fights — none of it can *force* you to choose a conventional path. The author says we insist on choosing our own friends. I say you insist on choosing your own techniques. The Academy will have to deal with that."

Feimo wrote in her notebook: *Peer pressure (Reading 1): can't force unconventional people into conventional patterns. Like choosing your own friends = choosing your own fighting style.*

Mia paused. The projection shifted again, this time to a passage from Reading Text 2, about the energy company Entergy:

*"A deal is a deal — except, apparently, when Entergy is involved."*

"This passage," Mia said, "is about broken commitments. Entergy agreed to abide by Vermont's nuclear regulations. Then it reneged. The phrase 'reneging on' means dishonoring a commitment." She looked at Feimo. "In the Academy, there are unwritten agreements. Students fight using approved techniques. Houses maintain their methodological boundaries. The system holds because everyone agrees to the terms."

"And I broke the agreement?"

"You *altered* the agreement. There is a difference. Breaking a commitment is dishonorable. Altering the terms of a system that was not designed to accommodate new realities is... *necessary*." Mia's voice caught on the word, as if she had surprised herself with her own position. "The Vermont case tested how far state power extends over nuclear issues. Your case will test how far the Academy's combat system extends over techniques it did not anticipate. And unlike Entergy, you did not lie about what you intended to do. You simply did something no one expected. There is no clause in the Academy's charter that says 'students shall not listen to walls.' I checked. While you were in the medical ward. Don't let it go to your head."

She said this in the same clinical voice she used for everything, but Feimo noticed that her hands, translucent and precise, were not crossed over her chest the way they usually were during lessons. They were resting at her sides. Open. The posture of someone who had decided to be honest.

"The writing prompt from 2012," Mia continued, shifting the topic with the efficiency of someone who knew she had ventured close to an emotional edge and wanted to retreat from it before she fell, "asks students to write an email welcoming international students to a university and offering campus life suggestions. The exercise is simple: extend a welcome, provide practical guidance, establish connection." She looked at Feimo. "What does this have to do with Glyph Battles?"

Feimo thought. "Connection. The audience at the arena — they weren't just watching. They were trying to understand what they saw. I should have... introduced what I was doing somehow. Made it legible."

"You fought in a language nobody in that arena spoke. The echo frequency was pre-linguistic, pre-curricular, pre-everything. The audience saw a result but could not follow the process. A good writer, like a good fighter, meets the audience where they are." Mia's lips twitched. "Not where you wish they were. Not where the walls told you to be. Where they *actually are*."

Feimo wrote: *Good writing = good fighting: meet the audience where they are, not where you are.*

"One more reading passage," Mia said. "From the 2012 exam, Text 4. It discusses public-sector unions: how they grew powerful, how they resisted reform, how the norms of a system can be shaped by the people inside it rather than the people who designed it. The author notes that institutions often operate under norms that *suit those who want to stay put but are bad for high achievers*." She looked at Feimo. "Sound familiar?"

"The Academy's combat system suits conventional fighters. People who stay within the approved techniques."

"And punishes people who don't. Not overtly. Structurally. The way a current punishes a swimmer who tries to go upstream." Mia paused. "The question is whether you are swimming upstream, or whether you have found a current the system did not know existed."

"There is one more thing," Mia said. She projected the translation passage from the 2012 exam:

*"In physics, one approach takes this impulse for unification to its extreme, and seeks a theory of everything — a single generative equation for all we see."*

"This sentence," Mia said, "describes what you did yesterday. You sought unification. You took the diverse frequencies in the arena walls and reduced them to a single resonance — one echo that unified every ancient pattern into one vibration. A 'theory of everything,' applied to combat." She paused. "The second translation sentence adds: *To filter out what is unique from what is shared might enable us to understand how complex cultural behavior arose and what guides it in evolutionary or cognitive terms.* You filtered out what was unique — the ancient wall frequency — from what was shared — the standard Glyph combat techniques. That is what gave you understanding."

She stopped. The projections faded. The study room was quiet, the walls returning to their default hum.

"One final thought," Mia added. "The Reading Part B passage from the same exam describes the computer as a 'culture machine' — a device that serves as mode of production, means of distribution, and site of reception all at once. It was built by geniuses and visionaries who created something that could function as typewriter and printing press, studio and theatre, paintbrush and gallery. The Labyrinth is our culture machine. And you, Feimo, are using it in ways its builders did not fully anticipate." She paused. "The second translation sentence adds: *Darwinism seems to offer justification, for if all humans share common origins, it seems reasonable to suppose that cultural diversity could also be traced to more constrained beginnings.* Your echo technique and the Academy's Glyph system share common origins — both come from the Labyrinth's architecture. But they diverged, the way languages diverge from a common ancestor. Chomsky's grammar shows patterns of language change independent of the family tree; Greenbergian universality predicts co-dependencies between particular types of word-order relations. You found a pattern that was independent of the family tree. The Academy's system predicts co-dependencies. Both are valid. Both are incomplete."

"That," Mia said, "was your lesson for today. I have just spent thirty minutes explaining to you that your reckless, undocumented, borderline-illegal combat technique has a theoretical foundation in credibility science, peer pressure dynamics, constitutional law, and physics." Her voice was flat. Her eyes were not. "You are welcome."

"Thank you, Mia."

"Do not thank me. I am performing my *design specification*." She turned away. Then stopped. Turned back.

"There are two knowledge cards embedded in today's lesson," she said. "Card 019: Literary Self-Awareness. The concept, proposed by Lu Xun, that Cao Pi's era was the first age of literary self-awareness — the moment when literature became conscious of itself as an independent art form, separate from classical studies and historiography. Before that, writing served Confucian ethics or historical record. After that, literature examined its own nature, its own methods, its own purpose." She looked at Feimo. "Your echo technique is a form of combat self-awareness. You did not just fight. You became aware of fighting as a *system* and found a frequency outside that system. Lu Xun would have had opinions about you."

"He would have hated me."

"He would have been *fascinated*. Which is worse. And for the record, the concept of literary self-awareness has a direct parallel in what you did: you examined the 'literature' of Glyph fighting and found a passage nobody had annotated. Curiosity did not kill the cat. It gave the cat a new weapon. You are welcome for that analogy." She projected the second card. "Card 020: Four Periods of Tang Poetry. The early Tang: Palace style poets evolving into the Four Outstanding Poets — Wang Bo, Yang Jiong, Lu Zhaolin, Luo Binwang — and Chen Zi'ang's call for 'structural vigor' and 'emotional resonance.' The High Tang: Li Bai's romanticism, Du Fu's realism, Wang Wei's pastoral landscapes, the frontier poets. The Mid Tang: Bai Juyi's New Yuefu Movement, Han Yu's ancient prose movement. The Late Tang: Li Shangyin's obscurity and beauty, Du Mu's historical elegies."

She paused. Let the information settle.

"Each period built on the one before. Each generation of poets was in conversation with the tradition they inherited. The early Tang poets broke from the Palace style. The High Tang poets perfected what the early Tang began. The mid-Tang poets questioned whether perfection had become stagnation. The late Tang poets mourned the loss of everything that had come before." Mia's voice was quiet now, stripped of its clinical edges. "Your echo technique drew from the Labyrinth's 'early period' — the ancient frequencies that predate the current system. You are the early Tang, Feimo. The question is whether you will build on what you have found, or whether you will be content to play old frequencies and call it innovation."

Feimo looked at her notes. The page was full: credibility process, legitimacy, peer pressure, broken commitments, audience connection, unification, literary self-awareness, Tang poetry periods. Every concept from the 2012 exam, woven through a thirty-minute lesson that had started as post-combat analysis and ended as something else entirely: a conversation about what it meant to be a student who did not fit the system, in a place that had been built on the assumption that students would.

"Mia," Feimo said.

"What."

"When I was in the arena, and I put my hands on the floor, and I felt the echo — I was scared."

Mia said nothing.

"I didn't know if it would work. I didn't know if the walls would answer. I just... listened. And for a moment, everything was quiet. The crowd, the Glyphs, the match — all of it went silent. And the only thing I could hear was the Labyrinth, breathing." Feimo's voice was soft. "It felt like being hugged by something enormous. Something that had been waiting for me to ask."

The word "hugged" hung in the air between them, unexpected and imprecise, the kind of word that a student of language should not have used and could not have replaced.

Mia's form flickered. The circuit patterns on her cuffs pulsed once, a quick bright flare that she suppressed almost instantly. Her face did something that involved no fewer than nine micro-movements, each one cancelled by the next, a war between contempt and tenderness and professional distance and something older and deeper that Feimo could not name but could feel, warm and sharp, like the moment a word clicks into meaning.

"The Labyrinth," Mia said, her voice exactly one half-step above its lowest register, "does not *hug*. It resonates. There is a difference."

"There isn't."

"There *is*. Resonance is a physical phenomenon governed by frequency, amplitude, and medium. Hugging is an anthropomorphic metaphor imposed by a first-year student with a bruised shoulder and an overactive imagination." Mia paused. "But the resonance, yes. The Labyrinth felt you listening. And it answered. That is what walls do when someone finally asks the right question."

She floated to the door. Stopped.

"The translation exercise also included this sentence: *Chomsky's grammar should show patterns of language change that are independent of the family tree or the pathway tracked through it.* Independent of the pathway. Independent of the system." She looked back. "You found a pathway that was independent of the Academy's system. The question now is whether the system will adapt to include you, or whether it will try to erase you."

"Like Yue's books," Feimo said quietly.

The room went very still.

"That," Mia said, "is a conversation for another day. For now: rest your shoulder, review your notes, and do not, under any circumstances, attempt to replicate the echo technique until your Glyph reserves have recovered. I will know. I always know."

She vanished.

Feimo sat in the empty study room, her notebook open, her shoulder aching, her ears tracking the hum of the walls. The Labyrinth breathed around her, patient and vast, and somewhere deep in its architecture, the echo she had found yesterday still hummed: faint, ancient, alive.

She wrote one more line in her notebook, at the bottom of the page, under all the exam concepts and combat analysis:

*The real battle starts now.*

Then she closed the book, tucked it under her arm, and walked out into the corridor, where the walls hummed with the words of a hundred thousand readers and the faint, persistent echo of something older than all of them.
