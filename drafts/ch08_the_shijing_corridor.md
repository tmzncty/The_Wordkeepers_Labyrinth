# Chapter 8: The Shijing Corridor

Feimo should have been in bed.

It was 2 a.m. by the clock on her Glyph interface, and the Bridge House dormitory hallway was empty in the particular way that institutional corridors are empty at 2 a.m.: not peaceful, but abandoned, as if the building itself had exhaled its last occupants and was now holding still, waiting for something it could not name. The overhead lights had dimmed to their nighttime setting, casting everything in a gray-blue wash that made the walls look like deep water.

Her ears were the reason she was awake. They always were. Cat ears do not have a snooze button. Three hours ago, she had been lying in her narrow dormitory bed, her tail curled around her ankles, drifting toward a sleep that had been building all day. The underground arena session had left her body a catalog of minor bruises and one significant ache in her left shoulder where she had hit the wall after a Glyph blast gone sideways. Noir had laughed. Mia had said nothing, which was worse.

Then the sound had started.

Not loud. Not even a sound, exactly. More like a vibration that only her ears could decode: a rhythmic pattern in the sub-bass frequencies that the Labyrinth's walls hummed with constantly. Most students heard the hum as white noise, the background radiation of a building made from living language. Feimo heard it the way a radio antenna hears static: as a carrier wave, modulated by faint signals that most receivers could not detect.

Something in the hum had changed.

The pattern had shifted. Where before there had been uniformity, now there was modulation: a repeating sequence of pulses, like a heartbeat, like footsteps, like someone knocking on a door from very far away. It came from below. Always from below. Feimo had pressed her ear to the dormitory floor and felt the vibration travel through her skull, through the bones of her jaw, into the soft tissue where her body kept its oldest instincts. Her tail had gone rigid. Every hair on her arms had stood up.

So now she was walking. Not toward the sound, exactly. But not away from it either. She was following the hum the way a cat follows a scent: not because it has decided to, but because something deeper than decision has already committed.

The Bridge House dormitory connected to the main academic wing through a service corridor that Feimo had used exactly once, on her first day, during orientation. It was not on the official student maps. It was not on the unofficial student maps either, the ones the upper-years traded like contraband. It was simply there, a gray concrete passage that smelled of old paint and pipe insulation, and it connected two parts of the building that the designers had apparently decided should not be connected, and then connected them anyway, as if the building had insisted.

Feimo turned a corner and stopped.

The corridor ahead was not the service corridor. She was certain of this because she had walked this route three minutes ago, and it had been a plain concrete passage with fluorescent lights and a mop bucket at the far end. It was no longer any of those things.

The concrete walls were gone. In their place stood surfaces of a material Feimo could not identify: dark, matte, with the grain of aged wood but the coolness of stone. The fluorescent lights had been replaced by something that glowed from within the walls themselves, a warm amber luminescence that cast no shadows. The floor tiles, previously institutional beige, had become deep slate, their surfaces worn smooth by what looked like centuries of foot traffic.

And the walls were covered in paintings.

Not painted decorations. Not murals in the modern sense, with their planned compositions and their artist's statements. These were something older: ink-wash scenes that seemed to grow out of the wall material itself, as if the images had not been applied but had emerged, the way fossils emerge from sedimentary rock. Water buffalo in rice paddies. Bamboo groves in mist. Mountains that rose and fell in the endless rhythms of landscape scrolls, each peak a breath, each valley an exhalation.

Feimo's ears flattened against her skull. Not in fear, exactly. In recognition. Her body knew something her mind had not yet processed. This corridor was not the Labyrinth's. It did not have the warm, word-alive hum of the Academy's constructed passages. It had something else: a silence that was not empty but full, like a held breath, like a sentence that has been written but not yet spoken.

She stepped forward. The slate was smooth under her bare feet. She had left her shoes in the dormitory, another decision her body had made before her mind had caught up.

The paintings changed as she walked. The pastoral scenes gave way to something stranger: figures in ancient robes gathering in fields, holding vessels, their faces turned toward a sky full of birds. The brushwork was different here, more controlled, more deliberate, each stroke laid down with the precision of someone who understood that every mark was permanent and every empty space was a choice. The figures were not individuals. They were types: the farmer, the scholar, the singer, the mourner. They stood in their painted world and did not look at Feimo, but their presence filled the corridor with a weight that she could feel in her chest.

She kept walking. The amber light deepened. The paintings grew more complex.

A scene of a court ceremony: figures in formal dress arranged in rigid hierarchies, their robes embroidered with patterns that Feimo's eyes could not quite resolve into symbols. A sacrificial rite: offerings arranged on an altar, smoke rising in curling tendrils that the artist had rendered with a single, perfect brushstroke. A woman standing alone in a field, her back to the viewer, her hand extended toward something beyond the frame of the painting.

Each image carried a frequency. Feimo did not know how else to describe it. The pastoral scenes hummed at a low, warm register, the way a lullaby hums. The court ceremonies vibrated at a higher pitch, formal and structured, like the measured intervals of a bell. The sacrificial rite carried a frequency so deep it was almost subsonic, the kind of sound you feel in your bones before you hear it with your ears.

The woman in the field made no sound at all. She simply was. And her silence was louder than everything else.

Feimo stopped in front of her. The painting was small, no larger than a sheet of paper, tucked between two larger scenes as if the artist had tried to hide it. The woman's robes were rendered in a single wash of gray ink, darker at the edges, lighter at the center, with no detail and no ornament. Her hand was extended, palm up, and in her palm was nothing. The emptiness was deliberate. The space where an object should have been was painted with such precision that it became its own presence, its own statement, its own word.

Feimo felt something move inside her chest. Not an emotion she could name. Not sadness, not wonder, not fear. Something that existed before names, before language, before the first human mouth shaped the first human sound into meaning. It was the feeling of standing in front of something that had been waiting for you, specifically you, for a very long time.

She reached out and touched the wall.

The surface was warm. Not room-temperature warm. Body-warm. As if the wall had a pulse, a metabolism, a life that ran parallel to hers but on a different timescale, in a different direction, through a different medium. Her fingers rested on the empty space in the woman's palm, and the vibration she had been following all night intensified, traveling up her arm, across her collarbone, into the hollow of her throat where words are born.

And then she heard it.

Not with her ears. With something else. Something that lived in the space between hearing and feeling, in the territory where sound becomes meaning without passing through the checkpoint of language. The painted woman's silence resolved into a pattern that was not a word, not a sentence, not a thought. It was a frequency: rising, falling, rising again, like breath, like waves, like the rhythm of a poem that has been spoken so many times it has become the rhythm of the land itself.

Feimo understood nothing. And yet she understood everything.

The poem was in Chinese. She knew this not because she recognized the language but because she felt its shape: the tonal contours of a language that carries meaning not just in its words but in the pitch of each syllable, in the rise and fall of each phrase, in the silences between sounds. She could not read a single character. She could not parse a single grammatical structure. But the feeling was unmistakable: this was a language that had been sung before it was written, that had been felt before it was understood, that had existed in the bodies of people who worked the land and buried their dead and celebrated their harvests long before anyone thought to carve its sounds into stone.

*Fu*, said a voice that was not a voice. Direct narration. The laying down of what is. *Here is a woman in a field. Here is her hand. Here is the empty space in her palm.*

*Bi*, said the voice. Metaphor. The transformation of one thing into another. *The empty space is a seed. The seed is a word. The word is a thing that has not yet been named.*

*Xing*, said the voice. Evocation. The awakening of feeling through the presence of something else. *The field is not a field. The woman is not a woman. The silence is not silence. Everything in this painting is a door that opens onto something the painting cannot contain.*

Feimo's hand was still on the wall. Her ears were trembling. Her tail had gone completely still, which in the grammar of her body was the equivalent of a scream.

The Six Principles — Card 016 in her study deck. She recognized them now, the way you recognize a song you have heard in a dream: not through memory but through resonance. *Feng*, *Ya*, *Song*: the three categories of content. Folk songs, court hymns, sacrificial odes. The paintings on the wall were not random. They were arranged in the order of the Shijing, the oldest collection of Chinese poetry, three thousand years of human feeling compressed into three hundred and five poems and organized not by chronology or theme but by the kind of truth each poem was trying to tell.

The pastoral scenes were *Feng*: the songs of the common people, the sound of a civilization in its infancy, learning to speak by describing what it saw. The court ceremonies were *Ya*: the music of governance, of ritual, of a society that had grown complex enough to need structure and formal enough to express its complexity through ceremony. The sacrificial rite was *Song*: the hymns of the sacred, offered not to audiences but to the dead, to the gods, to the forces that a civilization acknowledges but cannot control.

And the woman in the field was none of these. She was the principle beneath the principles: the moment before the poem, when the feeling exists but the words have not yet arrived. *Xing*. The awakening. The thing that happens when you see a bird on a bare branch and something inside you stirs, and you do not know why, and the not-knowing is the point.

The word *civilization* surfaced in Feimo's mind, not as a definition but as a weight. She thought of all the people who had sung these poems: farmers, soldiers, lovers, mourners, each one adding their voice to a chain of sound that stretched back three millennia, each one trusting that the feeling they carried was worth transmitting, worth preserving, worth carving into the memory of a language that would outlive them and their children and their children's children. This was what the word meant. Not monuments. Not empires. Not technology. Civilization was the decision to trust that a feeling, once spoken, could survive the silence that followed.

She pulled her hand away from the wall. The vibration faded, but slowly, like a bell that has been struck continues to ring in the bones of the listener long after the metal has gone still.

"I need you to come here."

Her voice was steady. She was proud of that, later, when she thought about it. Her voice did not shake, even though her hands were trembling and her ears were flat and her tail had wrapped itself around her left leg in a defensive coil that she would have been embarrassed about if anyone had been watching.

No one was watching. She was alone in a corridor that should not exist, at 2 a.m., talking to an AI who lived in her Glyph interface.

Mia materialized slowly. Not her usual instantaneous projection, crisp and sharp-edged and radiating professional disdain. This was a gradual coalescence: first a shimmer in the amber air, then a form, then features, then the full holographic presence of a woman made of light and circuit patterns, hovering three inches above the slate floor and looking around with an expression that Feimo had never seen on her face before.

Mia looked afraid.

"Where are we?" Feimo asked.

Mia did not answer immediately. She turned in a slow circle, her projection flickering slightly as it passed through the darker patches of wall, her eyes moving across the paintings with the rapid, systematic scanning of someone who processes information at speeds that human cognition cannot match. Her circuit-pattern cuffs, usually a steady silver, were pulsing in irregular bursts, like a heartbeat under stress.

"This corridor is not on any map," Mia said.

"I know. That's why I asked."

"Not on the official maps. Not on the internal schematics. Not on the architectural blueprints that I have access to. Not on *any* blueprint." Mia's voice had lost its usual sardonic edge. What remained was something Feimo had not heard from her before: the careful, measured diction of someone who is choosing each word because the situation demands precision and precision demands restraint. "The Bridge House dormitory connects to the main academic wing through a service corridor built in 2003. That corridor is twelve meters long, two meters wide, and contains one mop bucket. This is not that corridor."

"Obviously."

"Do not say 'obviously' to me when we are standing in a place that cannot exist." Mia turned to face her. The fear in her expression was controlled but visible, the way a crack in a wall is controlled but visible: the structure holds, but you can see where the stress is. "This place has architecture. It has materials. It has a light source. It has paintings. But it does not appear in any record of this building's construction, renovation, or modification. It was not built. Or rather, it was built by someone who did not leave a record. Which, in a building managed by an institution that documents the placement of every brick, should not be possible."

Feimo looked at the paintings. The woman in the field had not moved, but her silence felt different now: less patient, more urgent, as if the painting had been waiting for a witness and was now anxious about what the witness might do.

"Mia. What are these?"

Mia drifted closer to the nearest wall. She did not touch it. Feimo noticed this. Mia could not touch anything, being a holographic projection, and she usually did not acknowledge this limitation. But now she hovered with a deliberate gap between her projected fingertips and the wall surface, as if proximity itself were dangerous.

"Chinese," Mia said. "Classical Chinese. Poetry. These are poems from the Shijing, the Classic of Poetry, the oldest existing collection of Chinese verse. Composed between the eleventh and seventh centuries BCE. Three hundred and five poems, organized into three categories: *Feng*, folk songs; *Ya*, court hymns; *Song*, sacrificial odes." She paused. "The artistic tradition that produced these poems is one of the oldest continuous literary civilizations in human history. The techniques used here, *fu*, *bi*, *xing*, direct narration, metaphor, and evocation, predate Western literary criticism by two thousand years."

"You're quoting a textbook."

"I am trying very hard not to panic, and quoting textbooks is how I do that. You should be grateful I am not reciting the periodic table."

Feimo almost smiled. Almost. The smile died somewhere between her mouth and her eyes, killed by the weight of the corridor's silence.

Mia continued her circuit of the space. Her projection dimmed and brightened as she moved through areas of stronger and weaker luminescence, and Feimo noticed that the walls seemed to respond to Mia's proximity: the paintings nearest to her projection glowed more intensely, as if the amber light were not a fixed property of the material but a reaction, a living response to the presence of an observer.

"The literary movements represented here span the entire arc of Chinese poetic tradition," Mia said, her voice recovering some of its analytical cadence. "The pastoral scenes evoke the folk song tradition, the *Guofeng*, which is to Chinese literature what oral tradition is to Western literature: the foundation, the source, the place where a civilization learns to speak by describing what it sees. The court scenes reflect the *Ya* tradition, where poetry becomes a tool of governance and social order. And the sacrificial imagery belongs to the *Song*, where language confronts the sacred and tries to say what cannot be said."

She paused, studying the newly appeared figures.

Feimo looked at the woman in the field. She had not changed. She was still there, still silent, still waiting.

"But the Shijing is different from Western literary movements in one crucial way," Mia continued. "Western traditions tend to produce individuals: single authors, single voices, single visions. Homer. Shakespeare. Tolstoy. The Shijing was composed by nobody. Or rather, it was composed by everybody. Three hundred and five poems, and we do not know who wrote a single one of them. The authorship is collective, anonymous, communal. It belongs to a civilization, not to a person."

She turned to Feimo.

"That is why these paintings disturb me. Not because they are old, and not because they are hidden. Because they are *authorless*. Every surface in the Labyrinth was designed by someone. Every corridor, every classroom, every text has a maker, a name, a record. These paintings have no maker. They emerged from the wall the way the poems emerged from the land: without authorship, without attribution, without anyone taking credit. The corridor does not belong to anyone. It belongs to itself."

Feimo considered this. "Like earned media," she said.

Mia's projection flickered. "Explain."

"The marketing passage. From the supplementary reading. The one about how consumers create 'earned' media by willingly promoting products to friends, while paid media is controlled by marketers and sold media is owned media whose traffic is strong enough to attract advertisers." Feimo spoke carefully, feeling her way toward the analogy. "The Labyrinth is paid media. It is an institution that controls how knowledge is transmitted: what is taught, how it is tested, what counts as understanding. The Shijing corridor is earned media. It was not created by the institution. It was created by the tradition itself, by the accumulated weight of three thousand years of people singing and writing and feeling. The institution did not make it. The institution found it and tried to sell it."

Mia was quiet for a moment. "That is a surprisingly good analogy."

She stopped in front of the woman in the field. Her projection flickered.

"This one is different," she said quietly.

"I know."

"This one is not *Feng* or *Ya* or *Song*. This is something else. This is the *xing* itself. The moment of awakening. The thing that happens before the poem, when the feeling exists but the language has not yet arrived to contain it." Mia's voice dropped. "This painting should not be here. Not because it is Chinese, and not because it is old, but because it is *incomplete by design*. The empty space in her palm is not a missing element. It is the point."

"The point of what?"

"Of everything. The Shijing's most radical innovation was not its content but its method. The *xing* technique means that a poem begins not with its subject but with something else: an image, a sound, a fragment of the natural world that *awakens* the feeling the poem is about to express. A bird on a branch. A reed in the wind. A woman standing in a field with an empty hand. The poem does not describe the feeling. The poem *creates the conditions* for the feeling to arise."

Mia turned to Feimo. Her expression had changed again. The fear was still there, but now it was layered with something that looked almost like respect.

"You felt it," she said. Not a question. A diagnosis. "When you touched the wall. You felt the *xing*. The evocation. The thing before language."

Feimo nodded. Her throat was tight.

"That should not be possible," Mia said. "You cannot read Classical Chinese. You have no formal training in Chinese poetics. You are, by every measurable standard, unqualified to experience what you just experienced." She paused. "And yet."

"And yet."

They looked at each other across the amber light. The corridor held its breath.

"It is worth considering," Mia said carefully, "that what we call 'reading' is not the only way to encounter a text. The Shijing was a sung tradition before it was a written one. The poems were composed to be performed, to be felt in the body through rhythm and pitch and the physical vibration of the human voice. The written characters came later, as a technology of preservation. But the *feeling* was always primary. The body knew the poem before the mind could read it." Her eyes met Feimo's. "You did not read those poems. You felt them. The physical came first. The meaning followed."

"So the poems were designed to be felt," she said. "Not read."

"Designed to be *experienced*," Mia corrected. "Reading is a cognitive act. Feeling is a somatic act. What the Shijing created was a third thing: a somatic-cognitive bridge, a way of knowing that uses the body and the mind simultaneously, that refuses to separate the thought from the sensation, the word from the breath that carries it."

The amber light pulsed. Somewhere in the walls, the paintings hummed at frequencies below the threshold of conscious perception.

"You are a walking demonstration of the body-before-mind principle," Mia said quietly. "Your body felt the poem. Your body produced the emotion. And now your mind is trying to catch up." Her projection dimmed slightly. "This is not a comfortable experience. I understand that."

"You understand that," Feimo repeated. "You're a hologram. You don't have a body."

"Which is precisely why I find this corridor so deeply unsettling." Mia's voice was quiet. "I process information. I analyze patterns. I am very good at understanding how texts work, how arguments are constructed, how meaning is produced through language. But this place does not operate through language. It operates through *feeling*, through physical vibration, through the body's ancient knowledge. And I do not have a body. I cannot feel what you feel. I can only watch you feel it and try to understand what it means from the outside."

She paused.

"This is, for me, the equivalent of a blind person being asked to describe a sunset. I can analyze the wavelengths. I can describe the atmospheric conditions. I can explain the physics of light scattering. But I cannot see it. And the not-seeing changes what I am able to know."

The honesty of the admission struck Feimo harder than any of Mia's usual sharp-edged commentary. Mia, who hid behind expertise and sarcasm and the absolute authority of someone who knows more than everyone else in the room, was standing in a place that made her feel small. Not ignorant. Small. There is a difference.

"Okay," Feimo said. "So what do we do?"

"We document what we can. We note the corridor's location relative to the dormitory. We record the visual content of the paintings. We attempt to identify which specific Shijing poems are represented." Mia's voice steadied. Professional footing. Analytical ground. This she could do. "And we do not tell anyone about this. Not yet. Not until we understand what we are dealing with."

Feimo nodded. She turned to take a final look at the paintings, memorizing their arrangement: the pastoral sequence, the court scenes, the sacrificial imagery, the woman in the field. Her eyes moved along the corridor, cataloging, and that was when she noticed the door.

It was at the far end of the corridor, where the Shijing paintings stopped and the wall became blank. Not bare. Blank: a surface so featureless that it seemed to reject the concept of surface altogether, as if it were not a wall but a refusal, a space that had been deliberately emptied of everything: texture, color, history, meaning. The door was set into this blankness, and it was blank too: no handle, no hinges, no frame, no markings, no indication that it was a door at all except that it was a rectangle in a wall and rectangles in walls are either windows or doors, and this one was too solid to be a window.

The blankness was the most disturbing thing Feimo had ever seen. Not because it was ugly or threatening but because it was *wrong*. Every surface in the Labyrinth had texture, had history, had been designed by someone with an aesthetic intention. The walls of the academic corridors were covered in analytical texts and decorative Glyphs. The underground arena walls were rough stone and carved warnings. Even the service corridor had its institutional paint and its mop bucket. Everything in this building had been made by someone who left a mark.

This door had no mark. No maker. No intention. It was not a door that had been built. It was a door that had always been there, and the wall had been built around it, and the wall had been built *away* from it, leaving the door untouched, unclaimed, as if no one who had ever encountered it had been willing to take responsibility for its existence.

Feimo's ears went flat. Every hair on her tail stood up.

"Don't," Mia said.

"I wasn't going to."

"You were going to. I could see it in your ears. Your ears point at things you want, and they were pointing at that door."

"I was pointing at it because it's *wrong*."

"Yes. It is wrong. And that is exactly why we are not opening it tonight." Mia's projection steadied, drawing herself up to her full holographic height, which was exactly the height of a woman who has decided that authority is a better response than terror. "This corridor was not built by the Academy. I am now certain of that. The Academy builds things that can be documented, that have blueprints, that appear in records. This place was not built. It was *already here*. And whatever was already here, the Academy found it and either could not remove it or chose not to. Either option is concerning."

Feimo took a step back from the door. Then another. She did not turn her back on it. Cats do not turn their backs on things that frighten them.

They retraced their steps in silence. The Shijing paintings watched them pass, their amber light dimming as Feimo moved away, as if the corridor were closing its eyes. The woman in the field was the last to fade, her empty palm glowing faintly in the darkness like a word that has been spoken but not yet heard.

The service corridor reasserted itself as they crossed the threshold back into the building's documented architecture: concrete, fluorescent light, the mop bucket. Feimo's feet found the institutional tiles, cold and familiar. Her ears slowly rose from their flattened position. Her tail uncurled, one vertebra at a time, and resumed its normal loose sway.

But the vibration was still there. Below the floor. In the bones of the building. That rhythmic pulse, like knocking, like footsteps, like a heartbeat. It had not stopped. It had never stopped. It had only been waiting for her to notice it.

"Mia," Feimo said, as they reached the dormitory hallway. "The Labyrinth is a monopoly. It controls how knowledge is transmitted, what counts as understanding. The corridor we just found is not on any map because the knowledge it contains is not the kind the Labyrinth was designed to transmit. It is older. It was felt before it was understood, shared between bodies before it was stored in books."

Mia said nothing for a long moment.

"You are making an argument," she said finally, "that the Labyrinth is an institution built on top of a thing it cannot contain."

"Is that wrong?"

"It is not wrong. It is also not safe. The difference between those two things is the reason we are not telling anyone about this corridor." Mia's projection was fading, the edges dissolving into the dormitory hallway's fluorescent light. "Go to bed, Feimo. Sleep. And do not dream about that door."

Feimo opened her door. Her room was small, plain, exactly as she had left it: narrow bed, desk covered in exam prep materials, a shelf of texts she had not yet read. The walls were bare. No paintings. No poems. No amber light. Just the institutional white of a student's room in a building that had been designed to be forgotten.

She lay down. She closed her eyes. Her tail curled around her ankles, a habit of comfort that she had never been able to break.

Below her, in the floor, in the concrete and the rebar and the foundation and the earth beneath the foundation, the vibration continued. Knocking. Rhythmic. Patient. The sound of something that had been buried and had not forgotten, that was digging its way up through the layers of civilization that had been built on top of it, one poem at a time, one century at a time, one silent woman in a field at a time.

*Tap. Tap. Tap.*

Feimo pulled the blanket over her ears.

*Tap. Tap. Tap.*

It did not stop. It had never stopped. And now that she had heard it, she would always hear it: in the floor beneath her bed, in the walls of every corridor, in the silence between words, in the vibration that lived in the body of the building like a pulse, like a heartbeat, like a door that has always been there, waiting to be opened.

She did not sleep for a long time.

When she did sleep, she dreamed of a woman standing in a field, her hand extended, her palm empty, and the silence around her so vast and so complete that it had weight, had color, had the shape of every poem that had ever been written in a language that the dreamer could not read but could feel, could always feel, in the deepest bones of her body, in the place where understanding begins before language arrives to name it.

The woman's hand was open. The space inside it was waiting.

And far below the dream, below the building, below the Labyrinth, below the civilization that had been built on top of everything it could not contain, something was still knocking.

*Study Notes: Card #013 — Literary Movements*

*Literary movements are broad intellectual currents that shape entire eras of writing. From Classicism through Romanticism, Realism, Naturalism, Modernism, to Postmodernism, each movement represents not just a style but a worldview: a shared set of assumptions about what literature is for and what it can do. A movement is broader and more abstract than a literary school. Romanticism is a movement; the Lake Poets are a school. One movement can contain many schools, the way one ocean contains many currents. The Shijing's folk song tradition, its court hymns, and its sacrificial odes are not merely categories: they are three currents within a single literary civilization, each carrying its own frequency, its own way of knowing, its own truth about what it means to speak and to be heard.*

*Study Notes: Card #016 — The Six Principles of Shijing*

*The Shijing's content is organized into three categories: Feng (folk songs, the voice of common life), Ya (court hymns, the voice of governance and ritual), and Song (sacrificial odes, the voice of the sacred). Its technique operates through three methods: Fu (direct narration, the laying down of what is), Bi (metaphor, the transformation of one thing into another), and Xing (evocation, the awakening of feeling through the presence of something else). Together, these six principles constitute the foundation of Chinese literary criticism. Xing is the most radical: it means that a poem does not begin with its subject but with something else entirely, and that "something else" creates the conditions for the feeling to arise. The poem does not describe the feeling. The poem makes the feeling possible.*
