# Chapter 3: The Girl Who Read Too Much

The morning after Yue said she could no longer dream, Feimo woke to the sound of her own tail knocking a textbook off the nightstand.

She lay still for a moment, staring at the ceiling of her Bridge House dormitory. The text drifting overhead had reorganized overnight, sentences from a dozen languages flowing past in their endless, gentle migration. Somewhere in the current she caught fragments of a French poem, a line of Arabic philosophy, a technical specification for bridge engineering that she suspected was not metaphorical. The hum of the Labyrinth was steady and warm, like the breathing of something vast and patient.

Yue's words lingered. *I can't dream anymore.* The way she had said it, not as a complaint but as a diagnosis, flat and factual, the way you might say *the faucet is leaking* or *we're out of milk.* Feimo had not known what to say. She still didn't.

Mia materialized at the foot of her bed with the subtlety of a thunderclap.

"You're awake. Good. Your cross-House observation session begins in forty minutes." Mia's holographic form was crisp this morning, her dark bob precise, her obsidian eyes already scrolling with data. "Mirror House. Orientation week requires first-year students to observe one session in each of the other three Houses. Today is Mirror."

"I know. You told me yesterday."

"I tell you many things. You retain approximately thirty percent of them, which is better than most Bridge first-years but still insufficient for academic survival." Mia paused. "Get dressed. Brush your hair. The fact that I have to say this every morning is a statistical indictment of your species."

Feimo sat up. Her ears were already swiveling, catching the ambient sounds of Bridge House waking up: footsteps in the corridor, the low murmur of students trading Glyphs, the particular hiss of someone's boba tea being opened. Normal sounds. Safe sounds. She had been here for less than a week, and already the warmth of Bridge House felt like something she could rely on.

"Is Yue coming?" she asked.

"Yue has her own schedule. She doesn't need a Spirit AI to escort her to class because she actually reads the notices that are posted." Mia turned and drifted toward the door. "Thirty-eight minutes. Move."

Feimo moved.

---

Mirror House was cold.

That was the first thing Feimo noticed, and the thing her cat instincts protested most strongly. Where Bridge House was warm amber and flowing text, Mirror House was silver-gray and sharp angles, every surface reflective, every wall a mirror that caught and multiplied the light into something precise and clinical. The corridors were narrower, the ceilings lower, the text on the walls compressed into dense, analytical blocks that did not drift or migrate but sat fixed in place, annotated and cross-referenced in neat columns of silver script. The air smelled of polished glass and intellectual superiority.

"The architecture reflects the ideology," Mia said, hovering at Feimo's shoulder. "Mirror House privileges interpretation over creation, analysis over synthesis. They believe that understanding a text is more important than producing one. Every surface is reflective because their methodology requires self-examination. You read, and the text reads you back. A comment from a Mirror House student is never casual; it is a precisely calibrated analytical product."

Feimo processed this for a moment. "So the mirrors aren't just decoration."

"They are ideology made architectural. Every surface forces you to reflect on what you're reading, and on how you're reading it." Mia's voice carried a note that might have been admiration, if admiration had been filtered through several layers of professional detachment. "Mirror House students are selected for their ability to tolerate cognitive dissonance. They're trained to hold contradictory interpretations simultaneously without collapsing into certainty. They're the Academy's closest approximation to literary critics. They argue about symbolism at breakfast." Mia drifted closer to the entrance. "Come along. You're in the observation gallery today. Try not to touch anything."

Other students moved through the corridors in small, quiet groups, their Mirror House badges catching the silver light. Their behavior, Feimo noticed, was a study in controlled precision: they walked differently from Bridge students, more upright, more deliberate, as if each step was a statement about the relationship between the individual and the institution. Feimo saw no cat ears, no animal features of any kind. Mirror House was exclusively human, and despite the Academy's official commitment to diversity, the absence of demi-kind students felt less like coincidence and more like policy. The attitude projected by the House was unmistakable: analysis is a serious business, and frivolity is a deficiency to be corrected.

She followed Mia through a series of interconnected halls until they reached the inner circle: a vast, circular reading room ringed by tiered galleries, each level lined with mirror-surface desks at which students sat in focused silence. The ceiling was a dome of reflective glass that doubled the room, creating the illusion that the space extended infinitely both above and below, students reading in endless recursion. At the center of the room, a single lectern held a large, open book, and the text from its pages was projected onto the dome in slow-scrolling silver letters that reflected and re-reflected until the words seemed to exist everywhere at once.

The passage on display was about the decline of arts criticism in English-language newspapers.

Feimo settled into a seat in the observation gallery, trying to take up as little space as possible. Mia hovered beside her, her form flickering slightly in response to Mirror House's anti-Spirit interference field, a precaution against unauthorized AI analysis that Feimo had not known was a thing until Mia complained about it for seven uninterrupted minutes on the walk over.

The session was already underway. A Mirror House senior student stood at the lectern, leading a close reading of the projected text. She was tall, sharp-featured, and spoke with the particular precision of someone who had rehearsed her analysis until it sounded spontaneous.

"The passage presents a historical argument," the senior said. "Of all the changes that have taken place in English-language newspapers during the past quarter-century, perhaps the most far-reaching has been the inexorable decline in the scope and seriousness of their arts coverage. Notice the word *inexorable*. The author does not say 'significant' or 'notable.' Inexorable means unstoppable, impossible to prevent. The decline is framed not as a choice but as a force of nature."

Feimo read the projected text as it scrolled. The passage described a time when high-quality arts criticism could be found in most big-city newspapers, a time that readers under forty could not imagine. It spoke of the most significant collections of criticism published in the twentieth century, many of which consisted largely of newspaper reviews. To read such books today, the passage argued, was to marvel at the fact that their learned contents were once deemed suitable for publication in general-circulation dailies.

The senior continued. "The passage then moves further back in time to the period between the turn of the twentieth century and the eve of World War II, when newsprint was dirt-cheap and stylish arts criticism was considered an ornament to publications. In those far-off days, critics of major papers would write in detail and at length about the events they covered. Theirs was a serious business."

Mia leaned toward Feimo. In a whisper that was technically unnecessary but felt appropriate to the atmosphere, she said: "This is the Mirror House method. They call it *close reading*. Every word is a choice, and every choice reveals intent. The senior is teaching the students to read not just what the text says, but what the text does."

The senior was discussing the critics now. George Bernard Shaw and Ernest Newman, who wore their learning lightly but could be trusted to know what they were about. These men believed in journalism as a calling and were proud to be published in the daily press. Newman had written: *So few authors have brains enough or literary gift enough to keep their own end up in journalism that I am tempted to define 'journalism' as 'a term of contempt applied by writers who are not read to writers who are.'*

"That's a beautiful insult," Mia murmured. "Newman is saying that the writers who call journalism contemptible are the ones who lack the talent to do it well. The contempt is sour grapes, dressed up as principle."

The passage moved to Neville Cardus, who wrote for the Manchester Guardian from 1917 until shortly before his death in 1975. During his lifetime, he was one of England's foremost classical-music critics, a stylist so widely admired that his Autobiography became a best-seller. He was knighted in 1967, the first music critic to be so honored. Yet only one of his books was now in print, and his vast body of writings on music was unknown save to specialists. The passage did not need to state the implication directly; the numbers spoke for themselves, and the process of decline, once recognized, was impossible to unsee.

Feimo felt something tighten in her chest. She thought about the Labyrinth, about words that stopped being read and grew thin, corridors eroding in silence because nobody visited them anymore. Cardus had been a giant in his field, knighted by the Crown, and now he was known solely as a writer of essays on cricket. His music criticism, the work that had defined his life, had vanished as thoroughly as if the Labyrinth's walls had crumbled around it.

"The question the passage poses," the senior said, "is whether Cardus's criticism will enjoy a revival. The author concludes that the prospect seems remote. Journalistic tastes had changed long before his death, and postmodern readers have little use for the richly upholstered Vicwardian prose in which he specialized. The amateur tradition in music criticism has been in headlong retreat. In other words: the state of literary journalism has deteriorated beyond the point of easy recovery." She looked up from her notes. "What does this growth of indifference, this slow erasure of critical culture, tell us about the relationship between readers and texts?"

A student raised her hand. "That texts don't survive without readers. A critic is only as relevant as their audience. If no one seeks out the work, it dies regardless of its quality."

"Correct. The text demands active engagement to remain alive." The senior's voice gained intensity. "And this connects to the broader Mirror House principle." "We do not merely analyze texts. We ensure they are not forgotten. The decline of arts criticism described in this passage is not just a historical observation. It is a warning. When we stop reading seriously, when we refuse to engage with the full range of literary and critical production, when we limit our attention to what is popular and ignore what is difficult, the texts die. The corridors collapse. The words fade. We reduce the Labyrinth itself, one unread page at a time."

*Just like the Labyrinth,* Feimo thought. The passage was doing something the Labyrinth did every day: preserving the memory of what had been lost so that it could, someday, be found again.

The senior moved to the next section of analysis, discussing how the passage functioned as an example of what the Russian formalist Viktor Shklovsky called *defamiliarization*. "The passage takes something we assume we understand, the role of newspapers, and makes it strange. We think we know what newspapers are for. But by showing us what they used to be, the passage reveals how much has been lost. It makes the familiar unfamiliar. Shklovsky argued that this is the fundamental purpose of literary language: to break through the automatization of perception, to restore our ability to see what habit has made invisible. The everyday becomes strange, and in that strangeness, we perceive it truly for the first time. He wanted to make the stone stony again."

Feimo's ears perked forward. Defamiliarization. She had encountered the term in her survey courses, filed it away under *things that will be on the exam*, and never thought about it again. But here, in Mirror House, surrounded by reflective surfaces that seemed designed to return every gaze, the concept felt different. The walls around her reflected her own image back at her from a dozen angles, and each reflection showed something slightly different, as if the mirrors were producing not copies but perspectives. Defamiliarization was not just a literary technique. It was a way of seeing. A way of refusing to let habit dull perception. The mirrors proved it: the same face, viewed from different angles, was never the same face twice.

"The formalists divided literary study into three branches," the senior continued. "Literary theory, literary history, and literary criticism. The three are interdependent: theory provides methodology, history provides temporal context, criticism provides close analysis. Aristotle further classified literature into three genres based on the mode of imitation: narrative, lyric, and drama. The Chinese tradition, however, uses four categories: poetry, fiction, prose, and drama. Both systems attempt to answer the same question: what kind of text is this, and how should we read it?"

*Three or four,* Feimo thought. *The number changes depending on where you stand.* She glanced at Mia, who was watching the senior with an expression of professional interest and personal disdain in equal measure.

"Adequate," Mia whispered. "She's leaving out the key insight: genre categories are not fixed. New literary practices produce new genres. The novel itself was once a genre violation. The power of genre theory lies not in its ability to classify but in its ability to provoke thought about why we classify at all. But for a Mirror House senior, this is competent work."

The session wound down. Students filed out in small groups, discussing the passage in low, intense voices. Feimo stayed in her seat, watching the text scroll across the dome, thinking about critics who were forgotten and words that grew thin.

Then the room changed.

It happened the way weather changes: not suddenly, but with a shift in pressure that made Feimo's ears rotate before her conscious mind registered the cause. The ambient conversation in the inner circle dropped by half. Students straightened in their seats. The air, already cold, acquired an edge of precision, as if someone had sharpened it with a scalpel.

A girl walked into the inner circle.

She was not tall. She was not physically imposing. She moved with the quiet economy of someone who had eliminated every unnecessary gesture from her body and found that most gestures were unnecessary. Her hair was silver-gray, cut to exactly chin length with a geometric precision that suggested either obsessive self-grooming or a stylist who understood the concept of a single correct answer. Her Mirror House badge was polished to a shine that seemed to exceed the standard issue. She carried a single book under her left arm, its cover worn soft from excessive handling.

But it was her eyes that Feimo noticed. They were the color of mirrors: pale, reflective, revealing nothing while appearing to reveal everything. When she looked at a surface, the surface felt inspected. When she looked at a person, the person felt measured, catalogued, and filed under a heading they would never see.

She walked to a desk in the center of the front row, sat down, opened her book, and began to read. She did not look up. She did not acknowledge the room. The room acknowledged her. Students who had been leaning back straightened. Conversations died mid-sentence. It was not that Aya demanded attention; it was that her presence made anything other than attention feel like poor behavior.

"Aya," Mia whispered. The single syllable carried a weight of context. "Mirror House. First in her year. She has held that position continuously since enrollment. Her analytical scores are the highest the House has recorded in a decade. Her close-reading speed is approximately three times the House average. Her social circle is nonexistent by choice."

"She's alone?"

"She's selective. There's a difference." Mia's voice was careful, measured. "In Mirror House hierarchy, Aya is not merely at the top. She redefines the top. Other students compete for second place. Her power within the House is absolute, and she achieved it not through politics but through pure intellectual dominance. She has never needed to claim authority; it was conceded to her the moment she scored her first perfect analytical result."

Feimo watched Aya read. There was something in the way she held the book, close to her body, angled so that no one could see the page. Not secretive, exactly. Possessive. The way you hold something you are afraid of losing.

Aya read for three minutes. Then she closed the book, placed it on the desk, and raised her head. Her gaze swept the room with the mechanical efficiency of a lighthouse beam, passing over students, desks, mirrors, and arriving at Feimo.

The gaze stopped.

Feimo felt it land on her like a physical weight. Not hostile, exactly. Not yet. Simply assessing. The pale eyes moved from Feimo's face to her ears, to the Bridge House badge on her lapel, to the tail that was, Feimo realized with a sinking feeling, wrapped protectively around her own ankle. A posture of anxiety. She willed it to stop. It didn't.

Aya looked at Feimo for five seconds. Then she spoke.

"A cat girl." Her voice was clear, low, and completely without warmth. Not directed at Feimo, exactly. Directed at the room. At the universe. A statement of fact offered to anyone who might benefit from hearing it. "In Bridge House." A pause, measured and deliberate. "What an insult to the Academy."

The words fell into the silence of the inner circle like stones into still water. No ripples. No response. The other students looked at their desks, their books, anywhere but at Feimo. The statement was not an invitation to discussion. It was a verdict, delivered and closed.

Feimo felt her ears flatten. She forced them back up, refusing to give the room the satisfaction. Her tail had tightened around her ankle, and she deliberately relaxed it, exerting the small, private control that was all she had in this moment. Mia's holographic form flickered, the edges going sharp with static, but Feimo shook her head. Not here. Not now. Responding to the insult would prove nothing except that the insult had landed.

Aya had already returned to her book. The conversation, such as it was, was over. She had observed, commented, and dismissed, all in the time it took to turn a page.

Feimo sat in the observation gallery for another ten minutes, pretending to review her notes, actually doing nothing of the kind. The passage about arts criticism scrolled overhead, and she read it again without seeing it, the words dissolving into the background hum of her own thoughts. A cat girl in Bridge House. An insult to the Academy. The sentence played on repeat, and each time it did, something inside her tightened a little more, not with hurt, but with the particular stubbornness of a cat that has been told it cannot jump to a shelf it has already decided to reach. There was a cost to being dismissed, but the cost was not paid in wounded pride. It was paid in the slow accumulation of proof that would, eventually, silence the objection entirely.

When she left the inner circle, Aya was still reading.

---

Feimo explored Mirror House alone.

Mia had been called away for what she described as "routine system maintenance" and Feimo suspected was actually a chance to recharge in a less Spirit-hostile environment. The anti-AI field in Mirror House was giving Mia a headache, or whatever the holographic equivalent of a headache was. Feimo had told her to go. Mia had protested for exactly the duration required by her programming and then left.

The corridors of Mirror House were quieter than Bridge House, the silence here not companionable but deliberate, enforced by a culture that considered unnecessary noise a form of intellectual weakness. Feimo walked with her footsteps barely audible on the polished stone, her cat instincts automatically adjusting her gait to minimize sound. She passed reading rooms, analysis chambers, and a vast hall where pairs of students sat across from each other in mirrored desks, deconstructing texts in a ritual that looked like chess played with words. Periodically, a student would look up from their work and track her with their eyes, a brief, assessing glance that registered her Bridge badge and dismissed her in the same instant.

She moved deeper into the building, past the well-maintained central areas and into a section that felt older. The mirrors here were cloudier, their surfaces no longer perfectly reflective but instead showing a faintly distorted version of the corridor, as if the reflections themselves had aged. The text on the walls was denser, more compressed, written in scripts she did not recognize alongside passages in English that seemed to be from a much earlier era. Some of the text was annotated in silver Mirror House script, cross-references and analytical notes layered over the original words like a net of interpretation. But the annotations were old, their ink faded, and in some places the original text had begun to erode beneath them, the foundation crumbling while the analysis remained.

Her ears caught something.

It was subtle. Not the hum of the Labyrinth, which was always present, a constant background frequency that she had learned to filter out the way you filter out the sound of your own breathing. This was different. A thin current of air, moving through a space that should have been solid wall. She felt it before she heard it: a faint coolness against her left ear, the one that was angled toward the wall, carrying a scent she recognized from the restored corridor in Ch.1's Weave challenge. Old text. Unread text. The particular smell of words that had not been accessed in a very long time.

She stopped walking.

Her ear rotated, triangulating. The air current came from a specific section of wall, roughly at shoulder height, between two mirror panels that were cloudier than their neighbors. She stepped closer and pressed her ear flat against the stone.

The wall was hollow.

Not entirely hollow. Behind the stone, perhaps two or three feet deep, there was space. She could feel it the way you feel an empty room in a house you know well: a subtle difference in resonance, a faint echo where there should be solid mass. And beneath that, carried through the hollow like a whisper through a pipe, she heard text. Not the structured hum of maintained Labyrinth corridors, but something rawer, more fragmented. Sentences being spoken by no one, syllables overlapping and decaying, the sound of words losing their meaning from lack of attention. It was the audio equivalent of watching paint peel: slow, patient, irreversible.

She pulled back from the wall and looked at it. The surface was seamless, covered in the same compressed text as every other section. But when she ran her fingers along the stone, she found it: a hairline seam, running vertically from floor to ceiling, barely perceptible beneath the layers of text. A door. Hidden in the writing, its edges concealed by the density of the surrounding script. She traced the seam with her fingertip. The stone was cool, slightly rougher than the polished surfaces elsewhere, and when she pressed against it, she felt movement. Not much. A fraction of a millimeter. But the wall was not solid. There was a passage behind it.

She pushed harder. The stone resisted, then gave slightly, then stopped. Sealed. Locked by something she could not see, mechanism or magic or simply centuries of disuse. But the gap was there. The passage existed. She pressed her palm against the wall one more time, feeling the faint vibration of air that had nowhere to go, trapped in a passage that had no way to release its secrets to the world beyond.

Feimo stood in the corridor, breathing carefully, her ears still picking up the faint murmur of unread text from behind the wall. She thought about the knocking beneath the floor on her first night. She thought about the Deep Archive, which Mia described as restricted, off-limits, not her concern. She thought about how the Labyrinth preserved everything but not everything was maintained, and how the words people stopped reading grew thin. She did not respond to the sounds, not yet. She simply stood and listened, cataloguing what she heard for the moment when listening would not be enough.

She was still standing there when she heard footsteps behind her.

---

"What are you doing in this section?"

Aya's voice reached Feimo before Aya did, the words arriving like ice crystals forming in midair. She was standing ten feet behind Feimo, her book still under her arm, her mirror-eyes fixed on Feimo with an expression of cold assessment.

"This area is restricted to upper-year Mirror students," Aya continued, stepping closer. "First-years from other Houses are not authorized to be here."

"I was exploring," Feimo said. She stepped away from the wall, putting a casual distance between herself and the seam. "Your building is hard to navigate."

"That's by design. Navigational difficulty discourages the unqualified." Aya's gaze moved past Feimo to the wall she had been examining. "What were you listening to?"

Feimo hesitated. She could lie. She could say she heard something and couldn't identify it, play the confused first-year, and leave. But the passage was real, and the sound behind it was real, and something about Aya's presence made dishonesty feel like a concession Feimo was not willing to make.

"There's a passage behind this wall," she said. "A hidden one. I can hear air moving through it, and text decaying. It's sealed, but it's there."

Aya's expression did not change. It was a face that had been designed by evolution specifically to not change in response to statements it found absurd. "These walls have been mapped. Every section. The Mirror House architectural survey is comprehensive and continuously updated."

"Then the survey missed something."

"You're suggesting that centuries of Mirror House analysis overlooked a hidden passage that a first-year Bridge student discovered by pressing her ear against a wall."

"I'm not suggesting it. I'm telling you."

For the first time, something shifted in Aya's gaze. Not warmth. Not respect. A recalibration, the faintest adjustment in the angle of contempt. She stepped forward and placed her palm flat against the section of wall Feimo had indicated.

Silence.

Feimo watched Aya's face. The girl was perfectly still, her hand pressed against the stone, her eyes focused on nothing visible. She was listening. Not with cat ears, but with the trained analytical attention of someone who had spent years learning to perceive what others missed.

Ten seconds. Twenty.

Aya's eyes widened. Not much. A fraction of a degree, a microexpression that would have been invisible to anyone who was not specifically watching for it. But Feimo was watching, and her cat eyes caught the shift: the moment when Aya felt it. The resonance. The hollow space behind the stone. The faint vibration of air moving through a passage that should not exist.

Aya pulled her hand back. Her composure reassembled itself in less than a second, the crack sealed as if it had never been. But something had changed in the air between them. Aya's certainty had been dented, and certainty was the foundation she built her entire world on.

"How did you hear this?" she asked. Her voice was level, analytical, stripped of everything except the question.

"I have good ears." Feimo twitched one of them for emphasis. "Cat hearing. It picks up things."

"Things." Aya repeated the word as if testing its structural integrity. "Air currents. Resonance patterns. The sound of text decaying in an unmonitored space." She paused. "Those are not things that human ears detect."

"No."

Aya looked at the wall again. Her hand did not rise a second time. She did not need to confirm what she had already felt. Instead, she looked at the seam, tracing it with her eyes the way Feimo had traced it with her fingers.

"This passage has been here for a very long time," Aya said. "The text covering this section dates to the seventh century. Some of the inscriptions are in dead scripts. The Mirror House annotation layer was added over centuries, but the underlying structure predates the House system entirely. It is related to nothing in our current architectural records." She paused. "And no one found it."

"Maybe no one was listening."

"Maybe no one needed to." The words were sharp, automatic, a reflex of dismissal. But they lacked the weight of conviction. Aya stared at the wall for another moment, and Feimo saw it again: the hunger behind the mirror-eyes. Not for Feimo. For the passage. For the unknown text that lay behind the stone, unread and unanalyzed, a text that had evaded the most rigorous reading methodology in the Academy for centuries.

Aya turned to face Feimo directly. "Don't tell anyone about this."

"Why not?"

"Because I will handle it. I'll report the structural anomaly to the House prefect. It will be investigated. Properly. By people who are qualified to assess what lies behind these walls. That is the service Mirror House provides to the Academy."

"People like you."

"People like me." No irony. No acknowledgment of the arrogance. A statement of fact.

"I found it," Feimo said. "Shouldn't I be part of the investigation? I could mention this to the Bridge House prefect as well."

"No." The word was immediate, flat, a wall thrown up without thought. "You will not mention this to anyone."

"Why not?"

Aya looked at Feimo the way you look at an equation that has produced the correct answer through the wrong method. "Because you are a first-year Bridge student with animal instincts and no analytical training. Your ears are not a qualification. They are a biological accident. To protect the integrity of the investigation, it must be conducted by those with the proper methodology."

Feimo felt her tail bristle. She controlled it. "An accident that found something your qualifications missed. Due to nothing more than good hearing."

The silence that followed was the kind of silence that has weight, that presses against the walls and makes the air feel thick. Aya's expression did not change, but something behind it shifted, a tectonic plate of pride grinding against an unwelcome truth she could not remove from her awareness.

"Influence," Aya said, "does not require competence."

"What?"

"A passage we analyzed last year. From a researcher named Gladwell. He argued that social epidemics are driven by a tiny minority of special individuals, people who are unusually informed, persuasive, or well-connected. He called them influentials. The theory suggests that if you can find and influence these select people, they do most of the work for you." Aya's tone was clinical, as if she were dissecting a specimen. "But subsequent research demonstrated that the theory was wrong. The data did not support the claim. Influentials have far less impact on social epidemics than is generally supposed. The real requirement for what the researchers called 'global cascades' is not a few powerful individuals but, rather, a critical mass of easily influenced people. The study of these dynamics is a statistical process, not a personality cult."

She paused, and Feimo had the uncomfortable sensation of being analyzed in real time.

"You are not an influential," Aya continued. "You are not even a properly trained Mirror student. And yet you found something we all missed. This is not because you are talented. It is because your perception has not been dulled by habit. You are different, and that difference produced a result that years of similarity could not. It does not relate to skill. It relates to perspective."

"Are you calling me defamiliarization?"

"I'm calling you an anomaly." Aya's voice was flat. "The formalist Shklovsky argued that the purpose of literary language is to break through what he called the automatization of perception. We stop seeing things that are habitual. We stop hearing things that are constant. Your ears hear what trained ears have stopped hearing because your ears have never been trained to ignore. They have not been subjected to the same assumptions that govern ours. The outcome is not a matter of ability. It is a matter of what state your perception is in."

"That's not a compliment."

"It's an observation. I don't give compliments." Aya held Feimo's gaze for three seconds, then looked away. Her mirror-eyes caught the light and reflected it back in a cold, perfect beam that illuminated nothing. "With regard to the passage: report this to no one. I will handle it."

She turned and walked down the corridor. Her footsteps were precise, measured, each one landing with the weight of a decision made and a conclusion drawn. She did not look back. She did not need to. The mirror-walls reflected her departure in infinite recursion, Aya walking away in a dozen overlapping reflections, each one slightly different, each one returning to the same cold, perfect posture.

Feimo stood in the corridor and let her go.

But Aya did not go far.

At the intersection where the old section met the newer corridors, Aya stopped. She took out her book, opened it to a page she had already memorized, and began to read. She stood there, motionless, her back to Feimo, her eyes on the page, her presence a cold constant in the silver-gray light.

She was not leaving.

Feimo watched her for a moment, confused. Aya had made her position clear: Feimo was an anomaly, an insult, a biological accident. And yet she stayed. She read her book in a corridor she could have left, near a passage she had already dismissed, within earshot of a first-year she considered beneath her notice. It was the behavior of someone who could not leave a question unanswered, even a question she refused to admit she was asking.

*Why?*

Feimo thought about the passage on social influence. About cascades and critical masses and the principal requirement for change being not the presence of extraordinary individuals but the readiness of ordinary ones to be influenced. Aya was extraordinary by any measure. But the passage behind the wall had not been found by an extraordinary person. It had been found by a cat girl with good ears and a habit of pressing her face against things. The discovery would demand a response from Mirror House eventually, and that response would have to deal with the fact that their methods had failed where a first-year's instinct had succeeded.

Maybe that was what Aya could not stop thinking about. Not Feimo, but the gap in her own methodology. The blind spot that years of rigorous training had not only failed to eliminate but had actively created. Mirror House trained you to read deeply, analytically, with perfect technique. But perfect technique meant consistent technique, and consistent technique meant that certain kinds of perception, the kind that came from a different angle, a different body, a different way of being in the world, fell outside the range of what the system could detect.

Three kinds or four. Narrative, lyric, drama, and maybe prose if you were Chinese. The categories mattered because they determined how you read. Aya read everything. She read too much. She read the way breathing works: involuntarily, continuously, without the option to stop. And she was standing in a corridor, reading a book she had already finished, because she could not stop thinking about a passage she had not yet entered, found by a person she had already dismissed.

*The girl who read too much,* Feimo thought. *And it still wasn't enough.*

---

Mia reappeared seventeen minutes later, flickering into existence at the end of the corridor with the slightly frazzled look of a Spirit AI who had been fighting with Mirror House's interference field.

"Finished exploring? Your heart rate is elevated. Were you running from something or toward something? With you it's genuinely ambiguous."

Feimo told her. About the passage. About the hollow wall. About the sound of unread text decaying in the dark.

About Aya.

Mia processed the information in silence for four seconds, which was three seconds longer than her usual processing time. "Aya of Mirror House. First in her year. Continuous since enrollment. Her analytical scores are the highest the House has recorded in a decade. She completed her first Mirror challenge in two minutes fifty-one seconds, which is still the House record. She reads approximately four hundred pages per day. She has not received a single grade below ninety-seven in any subject since enrollment. Her item-level precision on textual analysis is, frankly, inhuman." A pause. "She spoke to you."

"She called me an insult to the Academy."

"That qualifies as speech. She typically does not acknowledge Bridge students exist. Her usual interaction with non-Mirror students consists of looking through them as if they were made of low-quality glass." Mia's voice was unreadable. "The fact that she stopped to talk to you, even to insult you, is statistically unusual. It is worth stress-testing that observation: did she seek you out, or did she stumble upon you by accident?"

"She also told me to tell no one about the passage."

"And you agreed?"

"I didn't agree. I just didn't argue."

Mia gave her a look that said, very clearly, *we will discuss this later.* But she let it go for now. "We should return to Bridge House. You have a Glyph practice session in two hours, and you still haven't reviewed the three Glyphs you collected from the Weave challenge."

They walked back through Mirror House in something that was not silence, because Mia was incapable of true silence, filling the gaps with commentary on Mirror House architecture, Spirit AI interference fields, and the statistical probability of hidden passages existing in other Houses. Feimo listened with half her attention. The other half was still in the corridor with the cloudy mirrors, listening to the sound of air moving through a space that had been hidden for centuries.

Near the exit, Feimo passed a notice board. A small poster, printed in silver Mirror House script, caught her eye:

*VOLUNTEERS WANTED: International Conference on Inter-Labyrinth Exchange. Seeking students with cross-cultural communication skills for translation support during conference sessions. Applicants should demonstrate proficiency in at least two languages and a willingness to serve as cultural mediators between Houses. Admission to the program is competitive. Apply to the Office of Inter-House Affairs.*

She read it and kept walking. The notice related to a part of Academy life she had not yet encountered: the larger, institutional machinery that ran beneath the daily routine of classes and challenges. For now, she had a hidden passage to think about, and a girl with mirror-eyes who refused to leave her mind.

---

Bridge House was warm.

The transition from Mirror House's silver-gray precision to Bridge House's amber glow was like stepping from a laboratory into a kitchen. The text on the walls flowed freely here, sentences from a dozen languages weaving around each other in the constant, gentle migration that Feimo had already come to think of as normal. The hum of the Labyrinth was warmer, softer, tuned to a frequency that her cat instincts read as *safe*.

She dropped onto her couch in the common room. Her three active Glyphs pulsed faintly in her awareness: "affect," "matter," "continued." Small words, common words, each one carrying the weight of the Hawthorne corridor and the strange, unsettling lesson that observation itself could change the thing observed. She released a long breath, the tension of Mirror House draining out of her muscles.

Yue was in the corner.

She was curled in an armchair with her blank book, the one she always carried, the one that had no text on its cover and no writing on its pages. Her eyes were open but distant, focused on something Feimo could not see. She looked like a photograph of a person rather than the person herself, an image captured at a moment when the subject was not quite present.

"Yue," Feimo said.

A slow blink. "Feimo."

"Do you know what defamiliarization is?"

Yue looked at her. For a moment, the distance in her eyes sharpened into focus. "It's when something makes you see what you've stopped seeing. The formalists believed that habit turns perception into automation. We walk past the same wall every day and stop noticing it. Defamiliarization breaks the automation. It makes the wall strange again. It forces you to admit that your original understanding was incomplete."

"Have you ever experienced it?"

"I experience it every night." Yue looked down at her blank book. Her fingers traced the empty cover. "I close my eyes and try to dream, and instead of dreams I get nothing. The absence makes me aware of what dreaming used to be. I see it because it's gone." She paused. "I wish I could stop seeing. I object to the way loss teaches you to value what you had."

The hum of the Labyrinth filled the space between them.

Feimo did not know what to say to that, so she said nothing, and the silence was the right thing because Yue had already returned to her book, or to whatever she found in the blankness of its pages.

Feimo leaned back on the couch and closed her eyes.

She thought about the passage behind the wall. About Aya, standing in the corridor with her book, not leaving. About the sound of text decaying in the dark, words losing their meaning because no one was listening. She thought about Neville Cardus, knighted by the Crown, forgotten by everyone except cricket fans. She thought about the Hawthorne workers whose productivity changed simply because someone was paying attention to them.

She thought about what it meant to be the kind of person who hears what others have stopped hearing. She thought about the objects in the Labyrinth that existed at the margins: dead languages, forgotten dialects, critics whose work no one read. They were not less valuable for being ignored. They were less visible. There was a difference, and the difference mattered.

*Defamiliarization,* she thought. *Making the stone stony. Making the familiar strange. And sometimes, making the invisible impossible to keep ignoring.*

Her ears twitched, catching a sound from far below. Not knocking, this time. Something else. A low, sustained vibration, like a word being spoken very slowly in a language she almost but did not quite understand.

Then it stopped.

Feimo opened her eyes. The common room was quiet. Yue had fallen asleep in her armchair, her blank book open on her lap, its pages as empty as the dreams she could no longer have.

On Feimo's lapel, the Bridge House badge pulsed softly, warm against her chest, keeping time with the hum of the walls.

Somewhere in Mirror House, in a corridor lined with cloudy mirrors, a passage waited behind a wall of stone and centuries of text. And somewhere in that passage, words were decaying in the dark, patient and silent, waiting for someone to listen.

**Study Notes**

*From the Labyrinth Archives:*

> **Concept: Defamiliarization (陌生化)**
> The Russian formalist Viktor Shklovsky argued in "Art as Technique" (1917) that the purpose of literary language is to counteract the automatization of perception. Habitual actions become unconscious; we stop truly seeing the things we encounter daily. Literature's function is to make the familiar strange, to "make the stone stony," restoring the sensation of life as experienced rather than as recognized. This principle underlies all narrative innovation: every technique that disrupts reader expectation, from unreliable narrators to fragmented timelines, is an application of defamiliarization.

> **Concept: Literary Genres (文学体裁)**
> Aristotle's Poetics established the foundational three-part classification of literature by mode of imitation: narrative (epic), lyric, and dramatic. The Chinese literary tradition, recognizing that its prose tradition (散文) could not be contained within Aristotle's framework, developed a four-part system: poetry (诗歌), fiction (小说), prose (散文), and drama (戏剧). The three-part system is more theoretically rigorous; the four-part system is more practically useful. Genre categories are not fixed: new literary practices continuously generate new forms that challenge existing taxonomies.

> **The Two-Step Flow of Communication**
> Proposed by Lazarsfeld and refined by Katz (1955), this theory suggests that information flows from mass media to opinion leaders (influentials), and from them to the wider public. Gladwell's *The Tipping Point* (2000) popularized the idea that social epidemics are driven by a tiny minority of highly connected individuals. However, subsequent research by Watts and Dodds demonstrated that influentials have far less impact on cascading social change than is generally supposed. The critical factor is not the presence of extraordinary individuals but the readiness of ordinary people to be influenced and to pass that influence forward.

> **Discussion Questions:**

How does this mirror the Labyrinth's concern about words that stop being read? Is there a difference between a text that is forgotten and a text that is deliberately abandoned?
> 2. Aya argues that Feimo's discovery of the hidden passage is not evidence of talent but of "difference." Is she right? What is the relationship between perspective and insight? Can training ever be a substitute for the fresh eyes of an outsider?
> 3. Yue describes her inability to dream as a form of defamiliarization: the absence of dreams makes her aware of what dreaming was. What does this suggest about the relationship between loss and perception? Can we truly see something only after it is gone?
